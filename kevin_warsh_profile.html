Key Controversies & Concerns ▼

🔴 The Inflation Flip-Flop

Then (2009-2011): As Fed Governor during the Great Recession, Warsh repeatedly warned of inflation risks that never materialized. In April 2009, with 9% unemployment and negative inflation, he told colleagues: "I continue to be more worried about upside risks to inflation than downside risk." He voted against QE2 (quantitative easing) over inflation concerns.

Now (2024-2026): Warsh criticizes the Fed for fighting inflation too aggressively. In July 2024, he criticized the Fed's "hesitancy to cut rates" as "a mark against them." He argues AI will create productivity gains that allow lower rates without inflation.

"HuffPost characterized this as flip-flopping 'his way into Trump's heart,' noting the shift occurred only after Trump became the Republican standard-bearer favoring lower rates."

🔴 Fed Independence Under Threat

The DOJ Investigation: The Trump Justice Department launched a criminal investigation into Fed Chair Powell over headquarters renovation costs, serving Powell with a subpoena. Many view this as retaliation for not cutting rates fast enough.

Confirmation Obstacle: Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) has vowed to block Warsh's confirmation until this investigation is resolved, potentially preventing his appointment. Senate Majority Leader Thune acknowledged Warsh will "probably not" be confirmed without Tillis's support.

🔴 "Regime Change" Rhetoric

Warsh has called for "regime change" at the Federal Reserve, criticizing its:

  • Heavy reliance on economic data and models
  • Large balance sheet ($6.6 trillion in assets)
  • Focus on issues like climate change and inequality
  • Relationship with fiscal policy

Critics worry this rhetoric signals intention to fundamentally restructure the institution rather than maintain continuity.

Historical Parallel: Economists cite the 1970s when President Nixon pressured Fed Chair Arthur Burns to keep rates low before the 1972 election, leading to runaway inflation that peaked at 15% in 1980. It took Fed Chair Paul Volcker raising rates to punishing levels to finally control inflation.

Historical Record: 2006-2011 ▼

February 2006

Appointed to Fed Board

At age 35, becomes youngest Fed governor ever appointed. Faced criticism for youth and inexperience. Former Fed Vice Chairman Preston Martin said it was "not a good idea."

March 2008

Early Crisis Warning

Warned Fed colleagues the financial system was "vastly undercapitalized" and predicted investment bank business model would "not endure through this period." Proved prescient.

September 2008

Crisis Response

Granted waiver to deal with former employer Morgan Stanley. Helped convert Morgan Stanley to bank holding company, accessing Fed loans and effectively saving the firm. Attempted to engineer mergers between major banks (efforts failed).

2009-2010

Inflation Hawk Position

Repeatedly warned of inflation risks despite 9-10% unemployment and negative inflation. Expressed concerns about large-scale asset purchases and near-zero rates "distorting markets."

November 2010

Voted Against QE2

Cast dissenting vote against second round of quantitative easing. Argued Fed was "removing burden" from fiscal policymakers and putting too much "onus on ourselves." Unusual for Fed official to suggest withholding support to force other branches to act.

March 2011

Resigned from Fed

Left Fed board after 5 years (before end of 14-year term). Became critic of post-crisis Fed policies, arguing they went too far with stimulus and were "sowing seeds for further crises."

Ben Bernanke's Assessment: Former Fed Chair Bernanke wrote that Warsh's "political and markets savvy and many contacts on Wall Street would prove to be invaluable" and described him as a "most frequent companion" in crisis strategy calls. However, Bernanke also noted Warsh had "substantial reservations" about QE2.

Structural Constraints on Fed Chair ▼

Important Context: The Fed Chair does not set interest rates alone. Rates are determined by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC): 7 Fed governors + 5 rotating regional Fed presidents = 12 voting members. The chair needs majority support to change policy.

Economic Realities

  • Inflation still above 2% target
  • Aggressive cuts could worsen prices
  • Midterm elections November 2026
  • Voter concerns about affordability

Market Constraints

  • Trump historically responsive to market turmoil
  • Financial crisis risk constrains options

Institutional Factors

  • Fed staff consists of Ph.D. economists

  • Congressional oversight remains

The Tillis Blockade

🚧 The Critical Vote That Was Blocking Confirmation

Sen. Thom Tillis (R-NC) held the key to Warsh's confirmation. With Republicans holding a 12-10 majority on the Senate Banking Committee, a single Republican defection would block the nomination.

"I'm not going to ask you anything. I'm going to talk about what's preventing me from being in a position to vote for you... Let's get rid of this investigation so I can support your confirmation." — Sen. Thom Tillis at the hearing
"If we put everybody in prison in the federal government that had had a budget go over, we'd have to reserve an area roughly the size of Texas for a penal colony because of the way government projects work." — Tillis calling the DOJ investigation "bogus"

Trump's Response: In a CNBC "Squawk Box" interview on the morning of the hearing, Trump said he had no plans to drop the investigation. This appeared to doom Warsh's chances.

The Sudden Reversal (April 24, 2026)

⚡ DOJ Drops Powell Investigation

Just three days after the hearing, U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro announced on social media that the DOJ was closing its criminal investigation into Jerome Powell and the Fed's headquarters renovation.

"The IG has the authority to hold the Federal Reserve accountable to American taxpayers." — Jeanine Pirro, announcing investigation transferred to Fed Inspector General
"American taxpayers deserve answers about the Federal Reserve's fiscal mismanagement, and the Office of the Inspector General's more powerful authorities best position it to get to the bottom of the matter. The White House remains as confident as before that the Senate will swiftly confirm Kevin Warsh as the next Federal Reserve Chairman to finally restore competence and confidence in Fed decision-making." — White House spokesman Kush Desai

Key Detail: As recently as Wednesday April 23 (two days before), Pirro had vowed to continue the investigation and appeal a federal judge's ruling that quashed her subpoenas of Powell. The sudden reversal came immediately after it became clear Warsh's nomination was dead without it.
