I

The Grand Announcement

Make the boldest possible claim to dominate news cycles
Early January 2026
Trump demands Denmark turn over Greenland - the largest land acquisition in American history, slightly larger than the Louisiana Purchase.
The Maximalist Demand:
A small European ally that shed blood for the U.S. in Afghanistan must surrender vast territory "for U.S. national security." Testing boundaries of the Atlantic alliance.
Davos Speech - January 2026
Trump publicly pressures Denmark before global elite.
We want a piece of ice for world protection, and they won't give it. You can say yes, and we will be very appreciative. Or you can say no, and we will remember.
II

The Media Frenzy

Let coverage build, double down when questioned
Throughout January
Trump escalates with tariff threats against Denmark and European allies. Markets fall abruptly. Allies object openly.
The Formula:
Familiar to anyone tracking Trump's real estate approach - bullying and keeping opponents off-balance. "The strong can do what they can, and the weak must suffer what they must."
Two Weeks Before Reversal
Trump tells the New York Times that ownership is essential.
That's what I feel is psychologically needed for success.
III

The Reality Check

Complexity emerges, obstacles appear, walking back begins
Mid-January 2026
Trump discovers limits of coercive powers. Markets react negatively. European leaders express alarm.
The Breaking Point:
Denmark consistently says it has no interest in a deal at any price. Unlike Napoleon selling Louisiana to Jefferson for $15 million, no willing seller exists.
Reality Emerges
European leaders question the fundamental shift in how America envisions using military and economic power. First time since WWII the U.S. threatens military force to expand borders.
The prohibition against conquest is a key feature of the post-1945 world, and one that has helped keep the peace among great powers. Coveting Greenland and threatening to acquire it, possibly by force, upends that.

IV

The Reframe

Declare victory on partial progress, blame others for gaps
Wednesday Night - Late January
Trump announces on social media that a "framework of a future deal" has been reached - one that does NOT resemble full American ownership.
The Vague Victory:
Instead of ownership, Trump floats idea of lease for expanded bases with "time limit is infinity" where "we can do anything we want" including Golden Dome missile defense.
Thursday - Air Force One
When pressed about how this differs from existing 1951 treaty (which already gives U.S. almost unlimited rights to base troops, missiles, aircraft, Navy), Trump calls it "a much more generous deal."
The Existing Rights:
The 1951 treaty already provides extensive military access. The "new" deal appears to be repackaging existing arrangements while claiming victory.
Mark Rutte's Diplomacy
NATO Secretary General negotiates the "framework" that defuses immediate crisis.
In my private meeting with the president, American ownership of Greenland had never come up... the president was really focusing NATO again on how can we collectively save the Arctic from the Russians and the Chinese.
‚Äî Mark Rutte, NATO Secretary General
V

The New Show

Launch next dramatic announcement before accountability arrives
Immediately Following Greenland
Before Greenland outcome can be fully evaluated, Trump moves to new dramas: threatening Canada, Cuba predictions, Colombia warnings, Venezuela action.
The Pattern Continues:
Trump threatens Canada: "Canada lives because of the United States. Remember that, Mark, the next time you make your statements." The show moves on before accountability arrives.
The Perpetual Cycle
European leaders left asking: Why create drama only to back away? Was reward worth the risks? How did Greenland emerge as Washington's most urgent security concern?
Estranging allies is the cost, while the benefit of gaining ownership of Greenland seems minimal.

üé™ The Pattern Revealed

Governance as Performance Art: Where the announcement itself becomes the accomplishment, the promise becomes the policy, and moving to the next drama prevents reckoning with the last.

From his mentor Roy Cohn, Trump learned: any publicity is good publicity. Attack, deny, never apologize. Keep the spotlight moving.

The Greenland saga shows the pattern perfectly - but this time with permanent damage to the Western alliance that has kept the peace since 1945. The show moves on. The consequences remain.
