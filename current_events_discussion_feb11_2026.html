‚öñÔ∏è Bob Fishman: Presidential Power & Independent Agencies

‚ñº
Bob examined constitutional limits on the President's authority to fire heads of independent agencies, focusing on the Humphrey's Executor case and current threats to Federal Reserve independence

üèõÔ∏è The Central Question

Does the president have unlimited authority to fire members of independent agencies without cause, or must there be legitimate grounds? This question, first litigated in 1935, has profound implications for agencies like the Federal Reserve Board, Securities and Exchange Commission, National Labor Relations Board, and Federal Trade Commission.

üìú What Are Independent Agencies?

Independent agencies were established by Congress, starting with the Interstate Commerce Commission around 1887. Under its constitutional power to establish agencies, Congress created entities partially insulated from partisan politics for sound policy reasons. These are distinct from cabinet departments that serve at the president's pleasure.

Key Examples:

  • Federal Reserve Board (monetary policy, interest rates)
  • Securities and Exchange Commission (financial markets regulation)
  • National Labor Relations Board (labor disputes)
  • Federal Trade Commission (consumer protection, antitrust)

‚öñÔ∏è The Humphrey's Executor Case (1935)

This landmark Supreme Court case established that the president cannot arbitrarily fire members of independent agencies. The Court ruled that Congress has the constitutional authority to create agencies with members who can only be removed "for cause"‚Äîmeaning legitimate reasons such as inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance.

The Principle: This protection ensures these agencies can operate with some independence from direct presidential control, serving their statutory missions without fear of politically motivated dismissal.

üö® Current Threats: The Lisa Cook Case

The Situation: President Trump fired Federal Reserve Board member Lisa Cook based on an allegation that she lied on mortgage applications‚Äîa claim that has never been proven in court and which Cook denies.

The Constitutional Problem: The president made an unsubstantiated allegation. According to Justice Kavanaugh's comments during oral arguments, this reason has never been considered or validated by a court of law. Under the Humphrey's Executor framework, a valid "cause" for removal must be established, not simply alleged.

The Investigation: Bob noted that someone at a relevant agency (Pulte) was actively looking for "dirt" on Cook‚Äîsuggesting a politically motivated search for justification rather than legitimate oversight.

The Legal Process: Before such an allegation could constitute legitimate cause for firing, it would need to be determined by a court whether she actually lied on mortgage applications. The allegation alone does not satisfy the "for cause" standard established in precedent.

üí≠ Implications for Federal Reserve Independence

The Federal Reserve's independence from political pressure is fundamental to sound monetary policy. If the president can arbitrarily fire Fed members who make unpopular but economically necessary decisions (like raising interest rates to combat inflation), the central bank's credibility and effectiveness would be severely compromised.

As the group noted, successfully defending this constitutional limitation would at least spare Americans from hearing Trump claim he "decreased the interest rate by 2,000%" every day‚Äîa reference to his tendency toward hyperbolic and factually questionable economic claims.

üéØ Discussion Questions Raised

  • How do independent agencies differ from cabinet departments? (Established by Congress with statutory insulation from partisan control)
  • What happens when allegations are made but not proven? (The Lisa Cook case demonstrates the problem with unsubstantiated claims)
  • Can courts protect agency independence? (The Humphrey's Executor precedent provides the legal framework)

üåê South Africa in Global Context: BRICS Membership

South Africa is a member of BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa), with Argentina having applied to join. This group is:

  • Somewhat anti-Western and anti-American
  • Seeking to change the global financial structure away from dollar reliance
  • Coordinating foreign affairs positions
  • Also concentrating on positive items like humanitarian help and climate change

This membership shapes South Africa's international alignments, placing it in a coalition that often challenges Western hegemony.

üìä South Africa's Demographics (1922 Census)

  • Blacks (Bantu Africans): 81% of the population
  • Coloreds: 8% - Originally descended from Dutch settlers (from 1652) and Dutch East Indies immigrants, with a large Muslim population living in the Cape Town area/Western Province where few Blacks lived until much later
  • Whites: 7.5% - "really a minority"
  • Indians: Nearly 2 million - Came from India to work in Natal's sugarcane fields (a job few others wanted due to poisonous snakes)
  • Jews: 120,000 in the 1970s; 75,000 in 1925 (only 50% affiliated with synagogue/temple); current population includes 10,000 Israelis in Johannesburg area

‚ú°Ô∏è The Jewish Community's Origins

The Jewish population originated mainly from:

  • Lithuania: The majority
  • Germany: Small minority
  • 1820 British settlers: In Michael's hometown of Port Elizabeth

This community faced complex moral challenges during the apartheid era, with the relationship between Israel and South Africa creating particular tensions.

‚ö´ Apartheid: 1948 and Beyond

The Shock of 1948: To the shock of all demographics and half the population, the Afrikaner Nationalist Party won the election with a plan to impose apartheid to prevent "the Black danger" to whites.

Apartheid vs. Previous Segregation: Apartheid differed from previous "national segregation" (similar to the American South). Apartheid was "from the cradle to the grave"‚Äîcomprehensive, very strongly enforced, and legal.

Legal Framework: 13 laws were passed by the South African Parliament. By the end, opposing apartheid could result in house arrest or imprisonment.

Enforcement Apparatus:

  • The government had a "special branch" - comparable to Gestapo methods
  • Black anti-apartheid activists simply disappeared‚Äîthey were assassinated quietly

ü§ù Israel-South Africa Relations: "Deliberate Ambiguity"

Thomas Friedman's Assessment: The Brandeis graduate described Israel's policy as "deliberate ambiguity"‚Äîpublicly condemning apartheid while privately maintaining pragmatic and mutually beneficial commercial and military relationships.

The Relationship Characterized: According to Wikipedia (which Michael noted contains inaccuracies), the relationship has been "on and off, negative, positive, negative, and ambivalent"‚Äîa problem for the Jewish community throughout.

Military Suppliers in the 1980s: South Africa initially received military supplies from the United States (Reagan's tolerance of apartheid) and UK (Margaret Thatcher "couldn't care less about apartheid, as long as she could supply South Africa with military hardware").

UN Security Council Ban: After a mandatory ban on military aid to South Africa, only two countries continued supplying large quantities of sophisticated military equipment:

  • Israel
  • Collaboration even involved nuclear capability (though Michael corrected Wikipedia's claim about atomic bomb tests in the Indian Ocean‚Äîthey never occurred)
  • Taiwan

üíù The Apartheid Government's "Love" for Israel

The apartheid government, initially under Prime Minister Malan, felt a kinship with Israel because they believed Israel had a similar problem with Arabs as South Africa had with Blacks.

The Weitzmann Hall Banquet: Michael witnessed this closeness firsthand as an usher at a banquet given by the Jewish community in honor of the South African government at Weitzmann Hall in Seapoint, Cape Town. The entire cabinet, including the Prime Minister, attended.

Prime Minister Malan visited Israel, and the closeness between Israel and South Africa was very tight, despite Israel at times criticizing apartheid.

Military Innovation: South Africa was the first country in the world to weaponize drones as an offensive weapon.
