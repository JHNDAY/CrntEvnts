SCOTUS Rules 6–3: IEEPA Is Not a Tariff Statute ▶
Supreme Court Delivers Major Blow to Trump Tariff Authority — Learning Resources v. Trump, Feb. 20, 2026
In a landmark 6–3 ruling, the Supreme Court struck down the sweeping tariffs President Trump imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), holding that the 1977 law does not authorize the president to levy tariffs. The case, Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump, consolidated challenges to the so-called "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs first imposed in April 2025 and the earlier fentanyl-related tariffs on Canada, Mexico, and China.
NBC News reported that the ruling invalidated tariffs covering most of the world's goods, representing what the administration had projected as its primary revenue vehicle — an estimated $1.4 trillion over the period 2026–2035 — to offset deficits generated by the 2025 tax legislation.

SCOTUSblog provided the most detailed breakdown of the coalition. Chief Justice Roberts wrote for six justices on the core holding — that IEEPA's grant of authority to "regulate…importation" does not include the power to impose tariffs, a word that never appears in the statute. The major questions doctrine, invoked in sections joined by Roberts, Gorsuch, and Barrett, required "clear congressional authorization" for a power of this economic magnitude. Justices Thomas, Alito, and Kavanaugh dissented.
How Each Bloc Reasoned
  • 6-justice majority (Roberts, Gorsuch, Barrett + Sotomayor, Kagan, Jackson): IEEPA doesn't mention "tariffs" or "duties." The ordinary meaning of "regulate" doesn't include the power to tax. Congress has always used explicit language when delegating tariff authority — its absence here is decisive.
  • Roberts/Gorsuch/Barrett additionally: Major Questions Doctrine requires Congress to speak clearly when authorizing vast economic consequences. It had not done so in IEEPA.
  • Kavanaugh (dissent): Focused on the "mess" of administering refunds; conceded this ruling "is not likely to greatly restrict Presidential tariff authority going forward" in other contexts.
  • Thomas/Alito (dissent): Would have upheld on statutory grounds.
"When Congress grants the power to impose tariffs, it does so clearly and with careful constraints. It did neither in IEEPA."
— Supreme Court majority, Learning Resources v. Trump (Feb. 20, 2026)
Within hours of the ruling, Trump signed a proclamation imposing a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — capped at 150 days by statute. He announced the following day his intention to raise it to the statutory maximum of 15%. Ropes & Gray noted that Treasury Secretary Bessent stated the new combination of Section 122, Section 232, and Section 301 tariffs would yield "virtually unchanged tariff revenue in 2026."
On the refund question, Penn Wharton Budget Model projected up to $175 billion in potential refunds. Nearly 2,000 importers had already filed cases at the Court of International Trade before the ruling. Retailers like Walmart and Costco have filed refund claims; individual consumers who absorbed higher prices as pass-through costs are unlikely to see individual recovery. Hedge funds have been buying up importer claims at a discount — a phenomenon previously reported in the Wall Street Journal.
The Petard Problem: The Major Questions Doctrine was Roberts's own creation, first deployed to invalidate Biden-era EPA rules and student loan forgiveness. Justice Gorsuch applied it symmetrically here, as noted in session discussion: you cannot invoke the doctrine against one administration's broad statutory claims and refuse to apply it to another's. The doctrine that conservatives invented to constrain the administrative state became the instrument of Trump's tariff defeat.

Operation Epic Fury: U.S.–Israel Strike on Iran, Khamenei Killed ▶
U.S. and Israel Launch Joint Strike on Iran — Supreme Leader Killed, February 28, 2026
In the early morning hours of February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a coordinated surprise attack on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, dozens of senior military commanders, and other officials. The operation was codenamed "Epic Fury" by the Pentagon and "Roaring Lion" by Israel.
The Washington Post and CNN reported that Khamenei was killed in an Israeli strike on his Tehran compound while attending a morning meeting with senior military officials. The CIA had gathered intelligence on the meeting and shared it with Israel, accelerating the timeline of the strikes.

NPR reported that the Israeli military struck over 500 military targets in the first 24 hours, using approximately 200 fighter jets — the largest combat sortie in Israeli Air Force history — while U.S. forces fired Tomahawk missiles and carried out airstrikes on hundreds of additional targets from carriers and regional bases.
Senior Officials Confirmed Killed (per Al Jazeera, NBC News, Wikipedia)
  • Ali Khamenei — Supreme Leader (confirmed March 1 by Iranian state media)
  • Mohammad Pakpour — IRGC Commander
  • Aziz Nasirzadeh — Defense Minister
  • Ali Shamkhani — Head of National Defense Council
  • Abdolrahim Mousavi — Chief of Staff, Iranian Armed Forces
  • Seyyed Majid Mousavi — Commander, IRGC Aerospace Force
  • Hossein Jabal Amelian — Chairman, SPND (nuclear weapons research)
Iran responded with waves of missile and drone strikes against Israel, U.S. military bases across the region, and Gulf states including Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, and Kuwait. CNBC reported Iranian strikes on the port of Jebel Ali in Dubai and near Doha, and the UAE ordered all schools nationwide to remote learning.
Congressional Backlash and War Powers: Sen. Tim Kaine called the conflict "an illegal war" and introduced a War Powers resolution to force a pullback. Congress was not consulted in advance; the Gang of Eight was notified after the fact. Even Trump-aligned legal commentators acknowledged the president bypassed the War Powers Act. The resolution was considered largely symbolic as Trump was expected to veto it.
Iranian reaction was sharply divided. While some Iranians celebrated in cities including Isfahan, Karaj, and Tehran — with a statue of Khamenei toppled in Dehloran — others mourned publicly. The government declared 40 days of national mourning and seven days of public holiday. On March 8, Mojtaba Khamenei, the son of Ali Khamenei, was elected as the new Supreme Leader by the Assembly of Experts.
Analysts and commentators debated the strategic logic. The Atlantic's Karim Sadjadpour described Khamenei as "a rigid guardian of a revolution increasingly disconnected from much of Iranian society." A widely-cited internet meme named the operation "Operation Epstein Fury," reflecting widespread skepticism — discussed at length in the session — that the timing was driven by domestic political calculations: the Epstein files, bad inflation data, and declining poll numbers, rather than a mature strategic plan.
Sources: Washington Post  •  CNN  •  NPR  •  NBC News  •  CNBC  •  Al Jazeera  •  Wikipedia: 2026 Iran War
Regime Change: Analysts Say Prospects Are Slim
Trump called for regime change within hours of the opening strikes. Coverage across mainstream outlets — including The Washington Post and The Atlantic — reflected broad analytical skepticism. Historians and former national security officials noted the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps is deeply embedded in Iranian society and will suppress street uprisings. There is no organized democratic opposition ready to govern. The Afghanistan and Iraq precedents loom large.
"For those predicting disaster, the odds are in your favor — but you can't be sure, and you should hope not to be right. For those celebrating: the odds are definitely not on your side."
— Tom Nichols, The Atlantic (cited in session discussion)
The session also noted the destruction of UNESCO-protected Iranian heritage sites — some more than a thousand years old — by U.S. and Israeli airstrikes, prompting formal condemnations from UNESCO and the UN that produced no material response.
The Drone Math: Asymmetric Warfare and America's Ammunition Problem
Coverage in The Wall Street Journal, The Atlantic, and defense publications highlighted a structural problem the Iran conflict has made stark: U.S. Patriot missiles cost approximately $4 million each. Iranian drones cost approximately $35,000. Sustaining a war of attrition at that cost ratio is arithmetically impossible. Iran has publicly stated it fired over 500 ballistic missiles and nearly 2,000 drones in the first week alone.

Ukraine as the Answer: Defense reporting noted the irony that the U.S. military is now consulting Ukraine — the country the Trump administration has wavered on supporting — for help developing low-cost counter-drone technology. Ukrainian forces, under battlefield pressure, developed a drone capable of intercepting Shahed-class drones at roughly 1/40th the cost of a Patriot engagement.
The Iran War's Silver Lining? Experts Say It May Accelerate the Shift Away from Fossil Fuels ▶
The Largest Oil Supply Disruption in History — and What It Reveals About Fossil Fuel Dependency
The closure of the Strait of Hormuz following Operation Epic Fury produced what analysts are calling the largest oil supply disruption in recorded history — surpassing even the 1956 Suez Crisis. Roughly 20% of global oil and petroleum products, plus all LNG exports from Qatar and the UAE (about 20% of global LNG trade), effectively went offline within days of the February 28 strikes.
OilPrice.com, citing consulting firm Rapidan Energy, reported the disruption was double the previous record set during the Suez Crisis. Brent crude rose from roughly $70 to over $100 per barrel within two weeks. European natural gas prices nearly doubled from €30/MWh to a peak above €60/MWh in early March before retreating.

The Chicago Council on Global Affairs framed the crisis starkly: "The Trump administration's attack on Iran has roiled energy markets... sending a fifth of the world's oil and natural gas supplies offline almost overnight." The piece noted a deep irony: Trump had previously gutted the Inflation Reduction Act's renewable energy buildout and scrapped EV incentives — decisions that deepened American and global vulnerability to exactly the disruption he then helped trigger.
Could the Crisis Accelerate the Green Transition? Analysts Say: Possibly Yes
The argument made in session by John and Lois — that rational business and policy planners may respond to this shock by accelerating the shift to renewables — has found substantial support in expert coverage. The core logic: wind, solar, and water-based energy generation is domestic, geopolitically inert, and non-depletable. It has no Strait of Hormuz.
"There is a clear solution to this fossil fuel cost chaos — renewables are now cheaper, safer and faster-to-market, making them the obvious pathway to energy security and sovereignty."
— Simon Stiell, UN Climate Chief, quoted in OilPrice.com, March 2026
TIME Magazine noted the parallel to earlier shocks: the Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022 drove European investment in domestic renewables, and the 1973 Arab Oil Embargo spurred the nuclear buildout that produced over 400 operating reactors worldwide today. "The war in Iran has shattered much of the remaining confidence in the global energy trade," TIME concluded, adding that the response "will certainly help renewables" — countries want to build whatever energy sources they can deploy quickly and domestically.
The Council on Foreign Relations made a related point: the crisis may be "a validation of China's long-term energy security strategy," which has focused on electrifying its economy and building massive domestic renewable capacity precisely to reduce Hormuz exposure. China holds approximately 1.4 billion barrels in strategic petroleum reserves and has been building out solar and wind at a rate no other economy has matched.
The Renewable Advantage Lois Named in Session: Wind and solar energy are not consumed — they "refurbish." Oil is a one-way drawdown. The session's spontaneous observation maps precisely onto what energy security analysts have been arguing for years: fossil fuel dependence is not just a climate problem, it is a strategic vulnerability. Every barrel burned is gone; every kilowatt of wind or solar is structurally self-renewing. The Hormuz crisis has given that abstract principle a $100-per-barrel price tag.
The Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS) noted that the crisis will likely prompt "import-reliant economies all over the world" to look with new urgency at "building out endogenous generating capacity (renewables in the near term, nuclear power over time) and accelerating the electrification of end-use sectors." Whether that actually happens, CSIS cautioned, will be "proportional to the duration and scale of the crisis."
The Columbia University Center on Global Energy Policy launched a dedicated podcast series on the crisis as of March 11, noting that as of that date the strait was "effectively closed to tanker traffic, stranding almost a fifth of world supplies of crude oil, oil products, and liquefied natural gas." The IEA was on the brink of releasing 300–400 million barrels from strategic reserves — the largest such release in history — covering perhaps 20–25 days of losses at then-current disruption rates.
The Countervailing Force — Trump's Domestic Policy: As Chicago Council on Global Affairs and TIME both noted, the same administration that struck Iran had previously dismantled domestic renewable energy incentives, halted wind and offshore projects, and pushed "drill, baby, drill" as its energy doctrine. The session noted this dimension too: Geoffrey Kent raised the example of a French offshore wind company offered $1 billion by the Trump administration to abandon wind turbine construction in Virginia and North Carolina and pivot to gas production. The geopolitical case for renewables is strengthening; the domestic political will to act on it is absent under the current administration.

UPenn vs. the EEOC: Refusing to Compile a List of Jewish Community Members ▶
Trump Administration Demands Names of Jewish Faculty, Students, and Campus Group Members — Penn Refuses
In July 2025, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission subpoenaed the University of Pennsylvania, demanding it compile and turn over the names, personal contact information, and affiliations of Jewish employees, students, members of Jewish campus organizations, and faculty in the Jewish Studies Program. Penn refused to comply with the identifying-information demands.
Inside Higher Ed reported that in November 2025, the EEOC filed suit against Penn to compel compliance. Penn escalated its formal response in January 2026, filing a brief calling the demand unconstitutional and invoking what it described as "the frightening and well-documented history of governmental cataloging of people with Jewish ancestry."
"Providing the requested information would not aid in the fight against antisemitism, and would in fact gravely endanger and violate the privacy of the very people the EEOC purports to protect."
— Penn AAUP Chapter statement, November 2025
A federal court hearing in Philadelphia on March 10, 2026 — reported by The Philadelphia Inquirer — saw Penn and intervening Jewish faculty and student groups argue that the subpoena would have "a chilling effect" on First Amendment rights and on Jewish life on campus. An EEOC lawyer called the request "garden-variety." The judge had not yet ruled as of the session date.
What the EEOC Demanded
  • Names and contact information of all employees in Penn's Jewish Studies Program
  • Full membership rosters of all Jewish campus clubs and organizations, including Hillel
  • Names and contact information of employees and students who filed antisemitism complaints
  • The same, extended to Jewish student employees
The Broader Pattern: Inside Higher Ed reported that the Trump administration launched similar probes at the University of California and California State University systems. UC Berkeley and Cal State LA released some employee information to federal officials — triggering faculty union lawsuits. Penn, as of the session date, remained the only institution to take its refusal to federal court.
Legal commentators cited the 1958 Supreme Court ruling NAACP v. Alabama as the precedent most directly on point: the government cannot compel an organization to produce its membership list absent a compelling justification, because doing so infringes on the right to associate freely. An Inside Higher Ed op-ed by Amherst's Austin Sarat argued the EEOC's effort was "clearly unconstitutional."
The session discussion added a dimension not widely covered in the press: Penn's strategic vulnerability. Even if it prevails legally, the administration can cut federal research and grant funding as retaliation — a tactic already used against Brown University and others. The Brown precedent, raised by Ralph Loomis in session, illustrates the gap between legal victory and practical leverage: lower courts sided with Brown; the administration cut $800 million in funding anyway.

The Pardon-for-Pay Industry: Joshua Nass Arrested in Extortion Scheme ▶
Lobbyist Arrested After Hiring Enforcer to Collect $500,000 Pardon Fee — March 13, 2026
Joshua Nass, 34, a South Carolina political consultant and attorney who ran a firm called Merkava Strategies, was arrested outside a Midtown Manhattan hotel on March 13, 2026, by FBI agents and charged with attempted Hobbs Act extortion. CNN reported that Nass had been in the hotel meeting with other clemency seekers when agents took him into custody.
According to court filings cited by CNN and AP/WSLS, Nass had received $100,000 from a client — widely identified in press reporting as Joseph Schwartz, a nursing home executive convicted of a $38 million payroll tax fraud scheme — to lobby for a presidential pardon. Trump pardoned Schwartz on November 14, 2025. Schwartz had agreed to pay Nass a total of $600,000; he paid $100,000 and refused to pay the remaining $500,000.
$600,000
Fee Schwartz agreed to pay Nass for pardon lobbying services
The Alleged Scheme (per FBI affidavit)
  • Beginning January 2026, Nass hired a cooperating witness (who communicated in English and Russian) to threaten and physically intimidate Schwartz and his son into paying the $500,000 balance
  • Nass paid the witness $3,000 in cash; offered $15,000 for physically assaulting the son and $5,000 for additional hired men
  • Nass instructed the witness not to behave "like a human being" with the son
  • Discussions included forcing the son into a car with masked men
  • Nass faces up to 20 years in prison if convicted; released on $5 million bond
The case drew wide coverage as a concrete illustration of what the New York Times had previously described as a growing "pay-for-pardon industry" around the Trump administration. Pardon brokers have advertised White House access with reported rates ranging from $1 million to $6 million per clemency application, depending on the complexity of the case and the access claimed.
"Rather than honestly representing his client, Joshua Nass allegedly chose to shake him down by hiring an enforcer to extort payment."
— James Barnacle Jr., FBI Assistant Director in Charge, New York Field Office
Context noted in session: The Nass arrest arrived in the same week the session discussed hedge funds buying up tariff-refund claims at a discount. John drew the parallel: both represent the monetization of legal and governmental processes — one through the courts, one through the executive clemency apparatus — by intermediaries operating in the gray zones of legality.
