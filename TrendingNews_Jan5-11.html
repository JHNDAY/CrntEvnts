Tariff Case - "Most Important Case Ever"
Learning Resources v. Trump: November arguments completed, ruling expected early 2026. Justices appeared skeptical of Trump's power to impose tariffs under 1977 International Emergency Economic Powers Act. Loss could curtail power, require refunds of $100+ billion. Trump January 2 Truth Social: ruling against tariffs would be "terrible blow" to US. January 6: "If Supreme Court rules against United States on this National Security bonanza, WE'RE SCREWED!" January 9: Supreme Court did not issue ruling - next opinion day January 14. Kevin Hassett: administration has "other legal authorities that can reproduce deals" if ruling goes against them.
Trump v. Cook - Federal Reserve Independence
Arguments scheduled January 21, 2026. Whether Trump can fire Lisa Cook from Fed Board of Governors. October temporary ruling allowed Cook to remain on job. Federal Reserve Act: president can only remove members "for cause." Cook started serving 14-year term in 2023. Trump tried to fire Cook this year, alleging mortgage fraud before her appointment.
Transgender Sports Ban Case
Little v. Hecox: Arguments January 14, 2026 (next week). Challenges Idaho law prohibiting transgender girls/women from participating in female sports. Combines lawsuits from Idaho and West Virginia. Plaintiffs argue laws violate federal civil rights laws prohibiting sex discrimination and 14th Amendment equal protection. Decision against athletes would mark major setback for LGBTQ+ community, adding to Trump's 2025 actions limiting protections.
Birthright Citizenship Challenge
Barbara v. Trump: Arguments expected spring 2026, ruling by June-July. Trump's EO seeks to bar children of undocumented immigrants born in US from citizenship - would overturn right guaranteed by 14th Amendment upheld for over century. Lower courts already ruled against administration. Administration argues birthright citizenship applies only to formerly enslaved people granted citizenship under 14th Amendment after Civil War.
2025 Record: Overwhelming Support for Trump
SCOTUSblog January 5: "Supreme Court overwhelmingly sided with Trump administration" during first year. Court ruled in favor of administration in one case decided after briefing/oral argument. Emergency docket: 25 decisions concerning administration through end of 2025 - 20 ruled for administration at least partially; 7 not accompanied by any written explanation. December 23: First real loss - rejected Trump's effort to deploy National Guard in Illinois in 6-3 decision. As of January 6: Court issued 29 emergency orders in cases related to second Trump administration, two pending, three withdrawn. Key wins: letting Trump fire federal workers, proceed with mass deportation, ban transgender people from military service.
Other Major Cases Pending
Louisiana v. Callais: Court appeared "poised to severely curtail Section 2 of Voting Rights Act" - would reshape how legislative/congressional maps are drawn nationwide, potentially reducing minority representation. Overturning could allow Republicans to lock in additional seats. Chiles v. Salazar (conversion therapy): Whether therapists' conversations with patients about conversion therapy protected by First Amendment or considered medical treatment states can regulate. 23 states had full bans as of late 2025, 5 states partial ban. Trump v. Slaughter (FTC): December 8 arguments completed - whether Trump can fire FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter, overturn 90-year Humphrey's Executor precedent. Win would give Trump far greater control over FTC, FCC, NLRB, other independent agencies.

Wage Growth and Job Market Weakness
November paycheck growth: lowest since May 2021. Average hourly wages $36.86, up only 3.5% annually - barely keeping pace with 3% inflation. RSM US chief economist: "Wage growth is easing into sustained affordability crisis." 2025 on pace for worst job growth since pandemic (average 76,000 jobs/month). Voluntary quit rates at 5-year low in October; employers have less incentive to give raises.
Consumer Financial Strain
Marist poll (December, 1,400+ adults): 70% say cost of living not very affordable or not affordable at all. NPR/PBS News/Marist: 71% say income just matches or falls short of monthly expenses. Conference Board December: consumers' views of current financial situation "collapsed" into negative territory for first time since July 2022. McKinsey Q4 2025: 29% dipped into savings in last 3 months; 29% reduced savings rate; 28% using credit cards more. Credit card debt rose to $1.23 trillion Q3 2025.
Specific Cost Increases
CPI risen 26% in past 6 years - about twice as fast as Fed's optimal rate. Categories rising faster: housing (up 14% Sept 2023-Sept 2025), groceries (18% higher Sept 2025 vs. Jan 2022), medical costs (up nearly 7%), services (up over 8%). November 2025: coffee prices up 19% from previous year, beef up around 15%, bananas up 7%. Almost half say harder to afford groceries than year ago. Roughly 14% of US households reported food insecurity Jan-Oct 2025, up from 12.5% in 2024. In NYC, 40% of families can't afford weekly food costs.
Political Impact
53% trust Democratic Party over Republican Party on handling rising prices. Democrats won recent elections in NYC, New Jersey, Virginia by highlighting affordability. NPR/PBS/Marist: Only 36% approve of Trump's economic stewardship - lowest in 6 years. Trump called "affordability" a "con job by the Democrats" despite it being issue No. 1 in polls. Rep. MTG: Trump's "gaslighting the people and trying to tell them that prices have come down is not helping." Ben Shapiro: "When President Trump says things are becoming more affordable, people don't believe that because they aren't really more affordable."

Enhanced Subsidies Expired
Enhanced premium subsidies for 22 million Americans enrolled in ACA marketplace expired December 31, 2025. Congress failed to extend subsidies despite December votes. December 11: Senate voted on ACA measures but neither Democrats' 3-year extension nor Republicans' alternative advanced. Without extension: average premiums will increase 75% for 2026. KFF analysis: low/middle-income households will see premiums rise from average $888 in 2025 to $1,904 in 2026. Four out of five consumers found 2025 plans for $10 or less monthly - that ends January 1.
Coverage Loss Projections
CBO estimates: without enhanced subsidies, 14+ million more uninsured by 2034. Combined with One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) cuts, 10-15 million could lose coverage. Johns Hopkins estimates up to 15 million more uninsured by 2034 from OBBBA alone. OBBBA includes over $1 trillion in healthcare cuts through 2034 - "biggest cut to our social safety net in history." New sign-ups dropped considerably in Pennsylvania and California during enrollment period; 21 state-based exchanges saw 18% decrease in new customers to 210,500.
January 1, 2026 Changes
More frequent Medicaid redeterminations. Work requirements for Medicaid expansion enrollees. Restrictions on lawfully present noncitizens - refugees, asylees, TPS recipients no longer eligible for ACA subsidies. DACA recipients ineligible for ACA Marketplace coverage. New enrollees must prove eligibility before receiving subsidies - no more 90-day grace period. Fiscal incentives for Medicaid expansion end, leaving 10 non-expansion states with less motivation. Urban Institute projects uninsured rates to jump 65% in Mississippi, 50% in South Carolina.
Broader Healthcare System Impact
Century Foundation survey: 250 million Americans will face health insurance premium increases in 2026 far exceeding inflation, wage growth, Social Security adjustments. Nearly 3 in 10 Americans (29%) report delaying or going without medical care in past year. One in four people (24%) delayed or skipped buying doctor-prescribed medication due to finances. Employer-based health costs projected to rise as much as 7% for 2026 plans. Medicaid represents ~20% of hospital revenue nationwide. Johns Hopkins warning: "If hospitals see increase in uninsured people who can't pay bills, they're going to increase rates for other payers."
