Questions to Bring to the Table
On the Polling
When a president is at 20/76% with voters under thirty, what does that suggest about the durability of the coalition that elected him? Is this a temporary storm or a structural shift?
Democrats lead generic congressional polling by 11 points. In 2018, a +8 lead yielded a 41-seat gain. History as guide — or not?
On the Iran War
If the public is not being told the true cost of the war — 228 assets destroyed, half the THAAD stockpile depleted — at what point does the information deficit itself become the story?
Lydia Polgreen: "For all his projection of unbound command, the man has revealed extraordinary weakness at the core of his presidency."
On the Retribution Primaries
Of the seven Republican senators who voted to convict in 2021, only two can remain in office after this year. What does a party that has purged nearly all its dissenters actually become?
Cassidy's concession: "Our country is not about one individual, it is about the welfare of all Americans and it is about our Constitution."
On the DOJ Settlement
A president files a lawsuit against the government, then settles it with his own DOJ to fund pardons for his supporters. Is there a name for that kind of governing structure — and does it matter what we call it?
Republican senators called the Blanche meeting "incredibly hostile" — yet the $70B ICE bill delay was their chosen lever. What does that tell us about the limits of Senate pushback?
The Underlying Pattern — and What It Requires of Us
The Pattern
Every story this week — the plummeting polls, the undisclosed war costs, the self-dealing settlement, the purge of dissenters — points toward the same structure: a leader who performs strength precisely because structural power is slipping. Edifice Rex builds the arch, renovates the ballroom, paints the Reflecting Pool — and sues the government he controls. The question history keeps asking of moments like this is not whether the walls are crumbling. It's how fast, and who is paying attention.

The numbers tell a story that no bully pulpit can rewrite. A new NYT/Siena poll finds the president's overall approval rating at 37% with 59% disapproving — a net of negative 22 points. The damage cuts across every demographic. Among Hispanics the split is 21/70%; among Independents, 26/69%; and among voters aged 18–29, a staggering 20/76% disapproval.

A WAPO/IPSOS poll reinforces the portrait. Fifty-five percent of respondents believe the president is not in adequate physical health to serve; 59% question his mental sharpness; 67% say he does not carefully consider important decisions; and 71% find him neither honest nor trustworthy.

Among Democratic respondents, 47% believe the party should move toward the center, 28% favor a move to the left, and 19% feel the party is already positioned correctly.

The president, consulting his own one-respondent Truth Social poll, arrived at a different conclusion — declaring himself, in characteristic capitalization, "WITHOUT A DOUBT THE GREATEST PRESIDENT IN AMERICAN HISTORY."

The Iran War

The war grinds on, at considerable cost to American credibility, materiel, and the lives of allies. Emerging reporting indicates that Iran has damaged U.S. military assets to a far greater extent than the administration has disclosed: a Washington Post article reported 228 structures or pieces of equipment damaged or destroyed; a subsequent Times report noted that Iran retains roughly 70% of its missile launchers and prewar inventory. Meanwhile, the United States has depleted approximately half its supply of THAAD interceptors, deployed largely in defense of Israel.

"Iran wants to go back to the Seventh Century and Trump is willing to meet them about halfway."— Christopher Bates

On Capitol Hill, the Senate advanced a war-powers resolution to floor debate by a 50–47 vote, with four Republicans — Cassidy, Collins, Murkowski, and Paul — crossing the aisle, while Democrat Fetterman dissented. In the House, Republican leadership withdrew a parallel resolution when it became clear it would unexpectedly pass.

Our Year of Independence Times One Million Dollars

In January, the president filed a $10 billion lawsuit against the IRS over the inadvertent release of his tax information — along with that of 400,000 other taxpayers — by a contractor. On Monday, two days before the federal court's deadline for justification, he agreed to drop the suit. In exchange, the DOJ — led by his former defense attorney Todd Blanche — negotiated the following settlement:

  • Establishment of a $1,776 million "Anti-Weaponization Fund" to compensate alleged "victims of lawfare," including those convicted in connection with the January 6 Capitol assault.
  • A formal apology to Trump and his family, with no further monetary payment beyond the fund.
  • A DOJ addendum permanently barring prosecution of "any and all claims" connected to Trump, his family, and prior tax returns — signed by Blanche himself.
"This is pure fraud and highway robbery. No one can be both plaintiff and defendant in the same case."— Rep. Jamie Raskin (D-MD)

Donald Sherman of Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington called the deal "one of the single most corrupt acts in American history." Republican senators held what was described as an "incredibly hostile" meeting with Blanche afterward, delaying a $70B ICE funding bill in protest. Mitch McConnell offered perhaps the sharpest rebuke: "So the nation's top law enforcement official is asking for a slush fund to pay people who assault cops. Utterly stupid, morally wrong."

Context for the Midterms

History and polling together sketch a compelling forecast for 2026. In the last thirteen federal elections, the House and/or Senate have flipped eleven times. Democrats gained 31 seats in 2006 winning the national popular vote by 6%; 41 seats in 2018 winning by 8%. They currently lead generic congressional polling by 11 points. Since 2024, Democratic candidates have outperformed Kamala Harris in 85% of state legislative races (193 of 226).

Former Republican Jennifer Rubin has proposed a "Six for 2026" platform focused entirely on reversing Trump's actions:

  1. End massive corruption
  2. Reclaim tariff power
  3. Reverse cuts to Medicaid and the ACA
  4. Cut off offensive operations in Iran
  5. Rein in ICE
  6. Regulate massive data centers
