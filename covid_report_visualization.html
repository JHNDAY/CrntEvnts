National COVID-19 Deaths

▼
Key Insight: Annual deaths peaked in 2021 at 462,000, driven by the Delta variant and pre-vaccination spread. The emergence of Omicron (highly infectious but less severe) combined with increasing immunity through vaccination and prior infection has led to a steady decline. Deaths dropped 66% from 2022 to 2023, and continued falling to just 16,000 in 2025—a 97% reduction from the peak.

By the end of 2021, nearly 80% of adults, including 90% of seniors, had been vaccinated and the highly infectious Omicron strain became dominant, going on to cause 150k deaths during the first two months of 2022. Since then, the number of deaths has decreased steadily as vaccines and illness have induced the emergence of a degree of herd immunity, Omicron and its descendants have produced less severe illness than earlier strains, (most recently the XFG variants), and vaccines have mitigated the severity of illness.

Although the trend has been favorable, the 1.23 million Covid deaths so far is comparable to the 1.3 million deaths the U.S. has sustained from all its wars (equally divided between foreign wars and the Civil War). Looking forward, we remain vulnerable as virulent new Covid strains which overcome our defenses could emerge, especially because our vaccination rates have decreased and the timely development of new vaccines may be deterred by RFK Jr. and his cronies. Although the anti-vax community complains that the vaccines are not fully effective in preventing the disease, its adherents blithely ignore that their main benefit is reducing the severity of disease.

Covid Vaccination Rates

For the 2023-24 and 2024-25 Covid vaccination seasons, the CDC reports that about 23% of adults received the new vaccines each year. So far this season according to the CDC, 17% of adults, including 34% of seniors, have already been vaccinated, 8% plan to be vaccinated, 26% are unsure, and 49% are likely to decline vaccination. Although the 2025-2026 vaccination rate could then fall between 25-30%, a surprising increase, this pales in comparison to the 80% rate achieved at the end of 2021. Moreover, given the anti-vaccine messaging from HHS and the CDC as well as restrictions placed upon vaccine access there may only be a minimal increase in inoculation, if any, this year.

Wastewater Covid Monitoring

Since 2022, the CDC has been monitoring the Covid viral load in wastewater across the country. Because many, if not most, cases are not reported, this data is the best available measure of the prevalence, not the severity, of Covid. The CDC uses a non-intuitive logarithmic scale to measure relative viral loads, but their metric effectively shows us how Covid prevalence is trending. Seasonal Flu

Over the 9-year period spanning the 2011-20 flu seasons, which run between October thru May inclusive, annual deaths ranged between 12k and 52k, with an annual average of about 35k deaths.

Since the pandemic, annual deaths have been as follows (there was no data collection during the 2020-21 season as Covid was the overriding focus):

The 4-year average for 2022-2026 is approximately 28,000 deaths. Some decrease from the pre-pandemic 35,000 average was to be expected because some flu deaths were likely subsumed by being classified as Covid-related.

Through late January there have been 11,000 deaths so far this season (2025-2026). Given that we're nearly halfway through the winter, which is when the flu peaks, and allowing for late reporting, I estimate roughly 25,000 deaths for the full season.

With regard to vaccinations, over the course of the last two years the population flu vaccination rate including children decreased from the prior 3-year average of 52% to 48%. So far this season, the vaccination rate has been 46%, a 2% increase over the rate at this point last season. I estimate that the rate for the full season will be around 50%. Given the HHS messaging, this small increase since last year is a pleasant surprise.

Seasonal Flu

There have been 193 deaths due to influenza in MA so far this season, 1.75% of the National total, which means that we are faring 1/6 better than our 2.1% population share.

So far this season, 37% of MA residents have been vaccinated against influenza, which compares unfavorably to the national rate of 46% and also falls below the 38% achieved in MA by this time last year and 40% the year before.

Although DPH characterizes the 4.32% of provider office visits last week attributable to influenza as being High, nearly double the regional baseline, this share did decrease from 5.85% the prior week.

On the other hand, DPH characterizes the 2.63% share of hospitalizations associated with influenza, down from 10.94% just two weeks ago, as being Low.

This past week, there were 1420 ER visits related to influenza compared to 6865 for the same week last year and 1677 the year before. The DPH Dashboard characterizes this level of ER visits as being Low.
