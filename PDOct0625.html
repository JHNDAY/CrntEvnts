Historical Progressive "Deals"

üî∑ Square Deal (Theodore Roosevelt)

First major progressive presidency with forward-looking ideas about citizen-government relationships. Addressed issues coming out of the Gilded Age when common folks were treated poorly by big business.

üî∑ New Deal (Franklin D. Roosevelt)

Fundamentally changed the relationship between citizens and government. Created a 60-year coalition coming out of the Depression. Conservatives never forgave FDR, viewing him as a "traitor" to the privileged class.

üî∑ Fair Deal (Harry Truman)

Continued progressive reforms in the post-war era, building on the New Deal foundation.

üî∑ "Bum Deal" (Editorial - Current Era)

John's term for the current situation, where Project 2025 represents the latest attempt to dismantle the foundations of the New Deal.

Historical Debate: Alan challenged this framing, noting that presidents like Lincoln pursued progressive economic policies (transcontinental railroad, land-grant universities, canal improvements) despite being consumed by the Civil War.
2

Political Realignment of the 20th Century

1920s Coalition

Democrats: Manual laborers, union members, working class

Republicans: College-educated, Ivy League, business class

1950s Shared Prosperity

Extraordinarily productive period for U.S. wellbeing. Europe was decimated, leaving the U.S. intact to dominate economically.

Post-1960s Transformation

Technology created an "education premium." Automation decimated manual labor value and fundamentally changed labor markets.

üìö Key Source: Daron Acemoglu

2024 Nobel Prize Winner in Economics

John based much of the analysis on Acemoglu's work, particularly his upcoming book "Remaking Liberalism" and a recent lecture transcript incorporated into the discussion materials.

Educational Context Discussion: Alan noted that in the 1950s-70s, education was more valued in other parts of the world than in the U.S. The Sputnik crisis (1957) caused renewed focus on technical education through the National Defense Education Act, which directly benefited John's educational path.

Working Class Liberalism & Economic Populism

We have to get rid of the idea that everybody in the country has the same aspirations that we do... the Democratic Party has done a very bad job of not understanding that, and presenting a face that a lot of people don't think about, don't relate to. That's one of the fundamental ways that the Democrats keep losing elections that they should really win.

Three Promises of Liberalism (from Acemoglu)

  • Analysis of when liberalism worked and when it failed
  • Understanding of how promises were made and kept (or broken)
  • Blueprint for working-class liberalism going forward

üí° Democratic Path Forward

Economic Populism Strategy:

  • Recognize that not all voters share the same aesthetic values around education and the arts
  • Avoid presenting an elitist face that alienates working-class voters
  • Focus on economic issues that matter to everyday citizens
  • Understand that cultural disconnect is a fundamental problem
Alan's Zero-Sum Observation: Conservatives operate with a zero-sum mindset - if someone wins, someone must lose. The liberal approach fundamentally rejects this framework.

Government Guardrails: The Central Tension

The extent to which [Trump and allies] have been ignoring guardrails and getting things done... the question is, what's a responsible set of guardrails? Because I take away from Trump's success that we've overdone it.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem

Excessive bureaucratic impediments slow down beneficial government action. Too many procedural checks and balances create paralysis.

üö® Trump's Approach

No responsible checks and balances. Gleefully ignores all norms. Gets away with pushing boundaries constantly.

‚úÖ The Solution?

Find middle ground: streamline processes while maintaining essential safeguards. Codify norms into law.

üéØ Key Questions Raised

  • How to codify norms so future "bad players" cannot simply ignore them?
  • How to speed up government processes responsibly while providing safeguards?
  • What was actually valuable in the various barriers to action (e.g., construction permitting)?
  • How to avoid putting "so much gunk in the machine that it can't operate"?

üìä Two Axes of Political Discussion (Alan's Framework)

Operational Axis: How to get things done within existing constraints

Goal-Oriented Axis: What should a liberal society achieve through government

Both are legitimate and necessary, but often in tension with each other.
