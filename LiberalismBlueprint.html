Liberalism's Historical Success

The Three Promises of Liberalism

Over the past 150 years, liberalism delivered on three essential promises that transformed Western societies:

ü§ù Sharing

Creating economic growth and sharing that progress broadly. "Universal suffrage is a knife and fork question" - ensuring prosperity reaches all segments of society.

üèõÔ∏è Public Services

"Democracy and proper drains" - building infrastructure, education, healthcare. Illiteracy disappeared, health improved tremendously.

üó£Ô∏è Voice

Protecting free speech and enabling political participation. Bottom-up self-government from communities to national level.

The Industrial Compact (1945-1975) Markets enabled firms to expand with new technology, creating a natural pathway for shared prosperity. As firms grew, they hired more labor across diverse skill levels - engineers, clerical workers, manual workers. Rising wages became the surefire way to create shared prosperity. This was bolstered by trade unions and democratic self-government.

The Crisis of Liberalism

Liberalism is in crisis not just because of misinformation and social media. It's really that liberalism hasn't lived up to its promises and hasn't adjusted to being in power.
üíª Post-Industrial Economy
Computers destroyed the industrial compact:
  • Digital technologies created wedge between educated and less educated
  • Automation severed link between growth and jobs
  • Firms can expand without hiring more labor
  • Robotics and AI automate manual and routine office work
üìä Baumol Cost Disease
Structural economic shift:
  • Manufacturing sheds labor (high productivity growth)
  • Healthcare, education expand (low productivity growth)
  • Manual workers lose jobs
  • New sectors require cognitive workers
  • Creates fewer opportunities for working class
üéì Post-Industrial Politics
The educated class transformation:
  • College-educated grew from 10% (1940) to 50% (today)
  • Gained income, social status, political power
  • Experiences diverged from non-college educated
  • Different neighborhoods, marriages, values
  • Political realignment: educated vote left, working class vote right
üè¢ Cognitive Elite vs. Mass College-Educated
Division within the educated class:
  • Cognitive Elite: High earnings, postgraduate degrees, Ivy League, believe markets work well
  • Mass College-Educated: Similar aspirations but less economic success, more left-leaning
  • United by cultural values, not economic policy
  • Coalition excludes working class concerns
üåê Cultural Politics Dominance
Sins of commission:
  • Tech sector promoted individualism and universal values
  • Cultural values became common denominator
  • Went against less-educated communities' values
  • Undermined local community participation
  • Created major backlash
üìâ Loss of Community Solidarity
Broken shared experiences:
  • Cannot build solidarity without common experiences
  • 1950s: all classes lived together, married similarly
  • Today: complete segregation by education
  • 85%+ say "politicians don't speak for people like me"
  • Voicelessness and disempowerment
‚ö° Anti-Liberal Ideas Rising
Not just Thatcher-Reagan conservatism:
  • Reversing political participation
  • Strong leaders and executives (Schmittian ideas)
  • Freedom of speech only for some groups
  • Tech sector breeding ground for extremism
  • It takes an idea to beat an idea
üîª Decline in Trust
Institutional erosion:
  • Decline in trust in Congress, courts
  • Decline in trust in science
  • Loss of trust within and between communities
  • Social media amplifies disagreements
  • Unable to adapt to new challenges
The Central Problem: Educated elites failed to develop strategies for addressing inequality and technological change (sins of omission), while cultural politics alienated working-class communities (sins of commission). The result: all three promises of liberalism - sharing, public services, and voice - have failed in the eyes of many citizens.

Blueprint for Remaking Liberalism

Working Class Liberalism

A new vision that brings communities together, focuses on job creation, and restores self-government

1

Return to Community

Go back to early liberalism's emphasis on community-level organization and solidarity

  • Communities are essential for knowledge sharing
  • Mutual respect and trust enable adaptation
  • Local experimentation drives innovation
  • Different communities can have different values
2

Focus on Job Creation

Shift focus to economic growth and job opportunities that communities desperately want

  • More job opportunities across skill levels
  • Belief that children will be better off
  • Bottom-up economic participation
  • Not UBI - meaningful contribution to economy
3

Pro-Worker Technology

Redirect AI and technology toward augmenting workers rather than replacing them

  • Pro-worker AI instead of pure automation
  • Make crafts workers more productive (electricians, plumbers, nurses)
  • New goods and services, not just cost reduction
  • Efficiency through innovation, not labor-saving
4

Self-Government

Restore bottom-up political participation and decision-making

  • Liberalism and democracy are symbiotic
  • Communities make choices about governance
  • Local participation in politics essential
  • Not imposed top-down solutions
5

Cultural Economics

Accommodate different cultural values across communities within liberal framework

  • Different communities have different values
  • Cannot have experimentation with cookie-cutter communities
  • Respect diversity while protecting individual freedoms
  • Apply multiculturalism principles to all communities
6

Business Leadership

Companies must create new services and markets in expanding sectors

  • Baumol sectors (healthcare, education) will expand
  • Create good jobs for diverse skill levels
  • Not just automation strategies
  • New tasks and services for demographic/climate challenges
7

Targeted Regulation

Shift regulation from procedures to critical issues

  • Regulate AI direction, not just safety
  • Social media regulation
  • Protect individual freedoms
  • Focus on what matters most
8

Policy Realignment

Listen to what working-class communities actually want

  • Not just tax and redistribute
  • Minimum wage, unions, job protections
  • Job programs and training
  • Policies that create good jobs directly

Key Insights & Path Forward

The Direction of Technology is Not Fixed
The future of AI is not like a weather report - it won't just happen to us. It depends on choices that companies and societies make. We can have pro-worker AI that augments human capabilities rather than pure automation.
Efficiency ‚â† Labor-Saving
Despite 50 years focused on automation, total factor productivity growth has been slower than ever. The industrial compact worked because companies created new markets and products, not because they cut costs. Economic vibrancy comes from innovation, not automation.
Community-Level Experimentation is Essential
Social adaptation requires community-level experimentation. New institutions and norms cannot be developed individually - they require collective action within trusted communities. Different communities must be allowed to try different approaches.
Demographic Change Can Help
Slowing population growth and aging will create labor scarcity, which helps the agenda of creating good jobs and high wages. Immigration conflicts can be better managed in aging societies with the right policies.
The Baumol Opportunity
The Baumol cost disease can be turned from a problem into a solution. Healthcare, education, and business services will expand and need workers. Creating good jobs for diverse skills in these sectors is the key to shared prosperity.
Authoritarianism is Not the Answer
Authoritarian systems cannot cultivate the individual autonomy, experimentation, and information sharing needed for social adaptation. They have no track record of building collective knowledge or dealing with complex modern challenges.
Liberalism Must Be Tolerant of Diversity
Liberalism has become less tolerant of dissent and critical thinking. It must return to respecting people regardless of who they are and what they say, while maintaining core protections for individual freedoms. The principles of multiculturalism should apply to all communities, not just select ones.
Social Media Must Be Regulated
While AI capabilities are still limited, social media is amplifying disagreements that were already built into post-industrial politics. Regulation of social media is urgent and necessary, though our adaptation challenges go beyond just technology.

The Path Forward: It Takes an Idea to Beat an Idea

For 80 years, all new ideas came from the liberal left. Now completely anti-liberal ideas are gaining traction worldwide. Working class liberalism offers a new vision that:

  • Restores the three promises: sharing prosperity, delivering public services, and giving people voice
  • Brings communities back to the center of liberal philosophy
  • Creates pathways for meaningful work and economic participation
  • Respects cultural diversity while protecting individual freedoms
  • Redirects technology toward human flourishing
  • Rebuilds trust through bottom-up self-government

The change may not happen, and social media may amplify tensions even if it does. But we have no other option but to try.
