Former special counsel Jack Smith sat down with Nicole Wallace on MSNBC's Deadline: White House for his first television interview since resigning from the Justice Department — the same program that began broadcasting the day James Comey was fired in 2017. The occasion, timed just ahead of the Fourth of July, was framed by Smith as a chance to honor the career prosecutors and FBI agents he worked alongside, many of whom he says have been fired, smeared, or driven out for doing their jobs.

Smith spent nearly thirty years as a federal prosecutor under both parties, including a stint as acting U.S. Attorney in Nashville during the first Trump administration. He led the special counsel investigations into Trump's efforts to overturn the 2020 election and his handling of classified documents after leaving office. Throughout the interview he returns again and again to a single throughline: that the standards he applied were never meant to shift with the administration in power, and that what has changed since is not his conduct but the willingness of institutions to defend the people who do this work.

The conversation ranges from the personal — an agent fired days after his wife's death from cancer — to the structural: judges who say they can no longer trust DOJ attorneys, law firms that capitulated versus those that didn't, and whether a legal doctrine like the presumption of regularity can survive when the people who are supposed to uphold it stop believing in it. Smith ends where he began: not afraid, and "completely optimistic" that the department can be rebuilt.

Deep dives
The purge of career agents and prosecutors
A recurring theme of the interview is the personal toll on the FBI agents and DOJ prosecutors who worked Smith's cases. He describes one agent fired shortly after his wife died of cancer, and another, Brian Driscoll — a decorated counterterrorism and hostage-rescue veteran — dismissed for defending colleagues who were being improperly targeted.
"I just can't believe that if people knew the character of these people I worked with, that they could ever stand for the demonizing that's happened to them."

Smith frames the firings as part of a broader campaign designed to make career officials feel isolated, and says his central goal since leaving office has been to publicly support them.

Law firms: who "ran toward the fire"
Smith draws a sharp line between Covington & Burling, which represented him without hesitation after the administration targeted the firm, and larger firms — he names Paul, Weiss specifically — that struck deals with the administration or later did work for Trump's Justice Department.
"You get to find out who's who, right? You get to find out who runs toward the fire, who kind of backs away."

He credits Judge Beryl Howell's description of the administration's executive orders against law firms as "chilling," and argues firms that fought back have "batted a thousand" in court.

From Mueller to Smith: communicating facts in a hostile media environment
Smith, self-described as "a courtroom guy," reflects on how the Mueller report was misrepresented within a single news cycle because, in his view, no one explained or defended it effectively. He says he didn't think about communications strategy while writing his own report, but has since concluded that career prosecutors need outside help translating dense legal findings into terms the public can absorb — without abandoning the DOJ's traditional reluctance to try cases in the media.
"No one's tried to communicate with them. No one's tried to give them the facts in a way that's actually easy for them to digest."

He praises Robert Mueller's career of service while suggesting Mueller's more buttoned-up, "different era" communication style is part of what allowed the report's findings to be misrepresented.

Is the rule of law dead? Smith's case for optimism
Pressed directly on whether the rule of law in America is "dead," Smith says no — pointing to the large number of career prosecutors, unnamed and unrecognized, still working inside the department who want to follow the facts and the law. He compares the moment to Watergate, arguing the country has historically responded to institutional crises by getting better, not worse.
"Recovering character is a lot harder than gaining it in the first place. But I think we're totally capable of doing that. I'm completely optimistic."

He frames his own posture — and that of colleagues who've stood up in similar moments — around a simple test: acting in a way that, in retirement, he can look back on and say he did things right.
