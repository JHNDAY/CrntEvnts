China Turns CrisisInto Leverage
How the Iran War Handed Beijing the Regional Influence It Has Long Sought
Source: The New York Times · Alexandra Stevenson & Murphy Zhao · May 7, 2026  ·  Visualized Against the AI Elements of Style
Move I · The Trigger
I
The Strait Closes
The U.S. and Israel attack Iran. The Strait of Hormuz shuts. China bans most oil-product exports — starving Asian nations that relied on its refineries for jet fuel, gasoline, and diesel.
Move II · The Lever
II
Selective Relief
Countries with improving diplomatic ties — Vietnam, Australia — receive partial fuel shipments. Others petition Beijing. The ban was never complete; good relations earned exceptions. Scarcity creates sway.
Move III · The Offer
III
Green Energy as Future
China's message: it did not start this war and offers an alternative — domestically sourced renewables. Solar panel exports doubled in March. EV exports rose. A crisis becomes a sales platform.
The One-Pager Protocol
Issue · Question · Evidence
The Issue
China leverages its neighbors' energy desperation to expand regional dominance
Asia's fuel-starved nations are petitioning Beijing — the world's largest crude importer, holder of enormous reserves — for relief. China responds selectively, exacting diplomatic concessions and commitments to future clean-energy cooperation in return.
Scarcity in others + surplus in China = soft power at scale.
Core Question for Discussion
Is China's energy diplomacy genuine leadership — or calculated dependency?
Beijing casts itself as the leader of a renewable-powered future, in contrast to Trump's embrace of fossil fuels. But it also quietly dangles reunification at Taiwan in exchange for "resource security." The carrot and the stick travel together.
A position of strength never stops being a position of power.
Strategic Context
Belt and Road ran into blowback; clean energy offers a cleaner image
China has spent an estimated $1 trillion in Belt and Road loans — often to heavily indebted nations, often with reputational cost. The crisis allows influence without that baggage. "Chinese officials see clean energy as a remedy to the bad image of Belt and Road." — Eurasia Group
Same reach. Better optics. No balance-sheet risk.
Taiwan Dimension
The Thinly Veiled Bargain
Beijing to Taiwan: reunification buys energy security
Taiwan imports over 96% of its energy. Around 60% of its oil transits the Strait of Hormuz. China's Taiwan Affairs Office floated the prospect of "better resource security after peaceful reunification, with a strong motherland as its backing."
Energy dependence made geopolitical.

The Longer Argument
What This Moment Accelerates
Strategic Pattern
China is writing the rules for who powers Asia's recovery
The Belt and Road Initiative spent $1 trillion over decades to build infrastructure influence. The Iran war has handed China a faster, cleaner mechanism: energy scarcity as a door, green technology as the threshold, diplomatic alignment as the price of entry. Every country that accepts Chinese solar panels or fuel shipments becomes more embedded in Beijing's emerging energy architecture.
Infrastructure took twenty years. This took three months.
The Overcapacity Reversal
Criticism of Chinese dumping has quietly softened
Before the war, Western and Asian governments complained bitterly about Chinese manufacturers flooding global markets with cheap EVs and solar panels. "But when you are in the midst of a crisis, that doesn't look so bad anymore." The crisis launders the overcapacity problem — for now.
Crisis changes the vocabulary of complaint.

  • Do Asian nations diversify away from Middle Eastern oil — or deepen ties with China instead?
  • Does the softening of overcapacity criticism outlast the crisis?
The Frame in One Sentence

A war China did not start, in a strait China needs open, has given Beijing the most consequential expansion of regional soft power since the founding of the Belt and Road Initiative — at no direct cost and with maximum deniability.

"Good diplomacy never announces itself as power. It arrives as relief."  —  Discussion Group, Turner Street
