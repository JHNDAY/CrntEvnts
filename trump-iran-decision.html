The Decision

President Donald Trump launched Saturday's wide-ranging attack on Iran after a weeks-long lobbying effort by an unusual pair of U.S. allies in the Middle East — Israel and Saudi Arabia — according to four people familiar with the matter. The decision was made despite U.S. intelligence assessments that Tehran was unlikely to pose a direct threat to the American mainland within the next decade.

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, who had spent years pressing Washington on the Iranian threat, continued his longstanding push for a decisive strike. Meanwhile, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman made multiple private phone calls to Trump over the past month, advocating military action — even as Riyadh maintained a public posture of supporting diplomatic resolution.

"I can go long and take over the whole thing, or end it in two or three days..." — President Trump, via Axios, on "off-ramps" after the strikes

The Strike

The coordinated U.S.-Israeli operation — reportedly codenamed "Epic Fury" or "Lion's Roar" — targeted Iran's ballistic missile array, air defenses, and nuclear-adjacent infrastructure. Explosions were reported in Tehran, Qom, Isfahan, and Kermanshah. The strikes were timed to coincide with a meeting Khamenei was holding with top aides; his death was subsequently confirmed by Israeli and U.S. officials.

⚠ Intelligence Disconnect

U.S. intelligence agencies assessed that Iran was unlikely to pose a direct threat to the U.S. mainland within the next decade. The decision to strike proceeded despite this finding, raising immediate questions about the weight given to intelligence versus geopolitical lobbying in the final deliberations.

The Fallout

Iran responded swiftly, launching missiles and drones against Israel and neighboring Gulf states, including the United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar. Dubai International Airport — the world's busiest hub for international travel — sustained damage and was closed. Airspace across Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Israel, and Bahrain fell largely empty as airlines cancelled or rerouted flights globally.

Iran's exiled Crown Prince Reza Pahlavi welcomed the strikes, writing in a Washington Post op-ed that the moment could mark a turning point against the Islamic Republic, while cautioning that the final outcome would depend on Iranians themselves. Iranian authorities were expected to convene the 88-member Assembly of Experts to name a successor to Khamenei — a process complicated by wartime conditions.

Constitutional Questions

Trump ordered the strikes without seeking Congressional approval, immediately intensifying the decades-old War Powers debate. While presidents hold broad authority as commander-in-chief for certain military actions, the Constitution explicitly vests Congress with the power to declare war — a tension that critics moved quickly to highlight on Capitol Hill.
