ü§ù The Strongman Diplomacy Factor

EDITORIAL SPECULATION

By John Day

  • L'√âtat, C'est Moi: Trump's success may stem from Arab leaders who identify with his strongman approach - autocrats who concentrate power and can deliver deals unilaterally
  • Personal Relationships: Egypt's Sisi, Saudi's MBS, UAE's MBZ, and Qatar's Emir - all authoritarian leaders who prefer dealing directly with powerful counterparts
  • Transactional Leadership: Strongman leaders appreciate Trump's direct, transactional style over traditional diplomatic bureaucracy
  • Mutual Recognition: Autocrats see in Trump a leader who prioritizes decisive action and personal authority over institutional processes
  • Speed of Decision-Making: When power is concentrated, leaders can guarantee commitments quickly - no need for parliamentary approval or broad consensus
  • Qatar's Role: Despite hosting Hamas leadership, Qatar's Emir could pressure Hamas knowing Trump would recognize and reward such moves
  • Egypt's Leverage: Sisi controls Gaza border and can enforce terms; responds to Trump's preference for dealing with "strong" leaders
  • The Mussolini Paradox: This approach mirrors the appeal of Mussolini "making the trains run on time" - authoritarian efficiency can deliver short-term results and quick deals. However, history suggests that governance built on concentrated personal power and strongman dynamics ultimately proves unsustainable, often leading to institutional decay, accountability deficits, and long-term instability

Note: This analysis represents editorial speculation and is not part of the original Washington Post reporting.
