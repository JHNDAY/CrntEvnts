08 AI & the Liberal Arts: What AI Can't Do — and Who Now Has the Advantage Emerging story ▼
The Dowd Series: Liberal Arts Rising
Maureen Dowd's ongoing NYT series on AI and the humanities captures a counterintuitive labor market shift: demand for programmers is declining as AI handles implementation, while demand for people skilled in literature, history, and interpretive analysis is rising. The scarce resource is now the person who knows what question to ask — and how to read the answer.
NYT (Dowd)Wired
Acemoğlu: What Happened to Liberal Democracy?
MIT Nobel laureate Daron Acemoğlu's forthcoming book addresses the same territory — AI's gains are not automatically distributed; institutions and policy determine who benefits. Acemoğlu has argued that the liberal arts provide the kind of contextual, adaptive intelligence AI cannot replicate, and that societies underinvesting in humanistic education will fall behind in AI deployment, not ahead.
The AtlanticNYTWired
Anthropic Leadership: The English Major VP
WaPo profiled Anthropic's founding story: CEO Dario Amodei (biophysics PhD, departed OpenAI over strategic disagreements with Sam Altman) and his sister Daniela (president). The VP of Anthropic holds an English degree — described by the CEO as a strategic hire, and cited across multiple outlets as the emblematic case for the liberal arts–AI thesis.
WaPo TechWiredThe Atlantic
Salary Data: STEM Premium Eroding
Recent salary studies for college graduates showed engineers continuing to outperform, but mathematicians and hard-science majors performing no better — sometimes worse — than communications and humanities majors. The gap between STEM premium and liberal-arts penalty is narrowing in ways the labor market did not predict five years ago.
