Supreme Court Poised to Expand Presidential Powers â–¶
Court Appears Ready to Overturn 90-Year Precedent Limiting Executive Authority
On December 8, 2025, the Supreme Court heard oral arguments in Trump v. Slaughter, a case that could fundamentally reshape the balance of power between the president and independent federal agencies. NPR reported that the conservative majority appeared poised to allow President Trump to fire Rebecca Kelly Slaughter, a Democratic member of the Federal Trade Commission, purely for policy reasons.
At Stake:
  • Humphrey's Executor (1935): Established that presidents cannot remove heads of independent agencies without cause
  • Impact: Decision affects approximately two dozen bipartisan agencies designed to be insulated from political interference
  • Agencies at Risk: FTC, EEOC, Merit Systems Protection Board, Consumer Product Safety Commission, Federal Reserve
  • Conservative Theory: "Unitary executive" holds that any person in executive branch is appendage of president
"You're asking us to destroy the structure of government and to take away from Congress its ability to protect its idea that the government is better structured with some agencies that are independent."
â€” Justice Sonia Sotomayor to Solicitor General D. John Sauer
ABC News noted that all six conservative justices indicated during oral arguments that a president should have absolute control over leadership of any government body carrying out executive functions. Solicitor General Sauer responded to concerns by saying "The sky will not fall. The entire government will move toward accountability to the people."
Justice Elena Kagan warned of a "slippery slope," telling Sauer: "The result of what you want is that the president is going to have massive unchecked, uncontrolled power not only to do traditional execution [of the laws] but to make law," referring to agencies' regulatory authority.
Chief Justice Roberts' Pattern:
The Washington Post reported that Chief Justice John Roberts has led the Supreme Court's conservative majority on a "steady march" of increasing presidential power, starting well before Trump's time in the White House. The justices have already allowed Trump to fire almost everyone he wanted in his second term, despite the 1935 precedent.
The Bulwark characterized the stakes dramatically: "What's at stake is nothing less than the wholesale reshaping of the federal government through another massive transfer of largely unaccountable power to a single man uniquely and dangerously unqualified to wield it."
Sources: NPR, ABC News, Washington Post, The Bulwark
Texas Redistricting Battle: Supreme Court Clears GOP Map â–¶
Court Allows Mid-Decade Gerrymander That Could Give Republicans Five More House Seats
On December 4, 2025, the Supreme Court cleared the way for Texas to use a controversial new congressional map that could help Republicans win five additional U.S. House seats in the 2026 midterm election. NPR reported that the decision came despite a lower federal court finding that the map is likely unconstitutional due to racial gerrymandering.
Timeline of Events:
  • July 2025: DOJ letter alleges four Texas districts are "unconstitutional racial gerrymanders"
  • August 2025: Texas Legislature passes new map at Trump's request; Democrats walk out
  • November 18: Federal judges block map, finding evidence of racial gerrymandering
  • December 4: Supreme Court reinstates map for 2026 elections
  • December 8: Candidate filing deadline with map uncertainty resolved
The Texas Tribune reported that Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton celebrated the ruling, saying the GOP-drawn map "reflects the political climate of our state and is a massive win for Texas and every conservative who is tired of watching the left try to upend the political system with bogus lawsuits."

Houston Public Media explored the legal dichotomy at the heart of the case: the difference between gerrymandering for purely partisan reasons (which the Supreme Court has said federal courts cannot interfere with) and gerrymandering for racial reasons (which remains illegal under the Voting Rights Act).
Houston's Dramatically Altered Districts:
  • 9th District (Al Green): Redrawn to reduce Democratic advantage
  • 18th District: Reconfigured, creating potential primary clash
  • DOJ Letter Impact: Three of four districts flagged were in Houston area
  • "Cracking and Packing": Classic gerrymandering techniques applied to minority communities
Justice Elena Kagan dissented strongly, writing that the Court's order "disrespects the work of a District Court that did everything one could ask" and "disserves the millions of Texans whom the District Court found were assigned to their new districts based on their race," according to SCOTUSblog.
Votebeat reported that the "redistricting wars" are "truly unprecedented in modern politics," with chaos in multiple states including Texas, Indiana, and North Carolina. The article noted that 11 state senators in redistricting debates have been targets of swatting attempts, bomb threats, or other threats, which many lawmakers decried as intimidation tactics.
Sources: NPR, Texas Tribune, Houston Public Media, SCOTUSblog, Votebeat
Research Funding Crisis: NIH Cuts Shake Scientific Community â–¶
Trump Administration's Science Policy Threatens Academic Medical Centers
Throughout 2025, the Trump administration has implemented dramatic changes to federal research funding that have rattled the scientific community. STAT News launched a comprehensive series titled "American Science, Shattered," describing 2025 as "the year their path to a career in science was closed off" for a substantial group of U.S. researchers.
$3 Billion
Total frozen or terminated NIH and NSF grant funding in 2025
Scale of Disruption:
  • NIH: ~$2.3 billion in unspent funds across nearly 2,500 grants frozen or terminated
  • NSF: Over 1,300 grants with ~$700 million in unspent funds cut
  • Total Impact: More than 3,800 research grants terminated or frozen
  • Geographic Shift: Funds diverted from major research hospitals to institutions in red states
Science News reported specific impacts, including the largest single hit to any NIH grant: $77 million in remaining funds frozen for Northwestern University's Lurie Cancer Center, a national hub for cancer research, care and community outreach.
The geographic redistribution of research dollars has become a contentious political issue. Major Boston-area hospitals have complained that funds traditionally allocated to them are being diverted to colleges in Iowa and Nebraska, according to discussions referenced in current events coverage. This follows administration policy from Jay Bhattacharya (mentored by Harvard's Alan Garber) aimed at distributing research funds more broadly across the country rather than concentrating them in traditional research hubs.

The administration implemented a controversial 15% cap on indirect costs for research grants in February 2025, which was upheld by the Supreme Court in August. This cap represents a dramatic reduction from the typical 27-28% rate that institutions had negotiated to cover overhead costs like building maintenance, equipment, and support staff. Advisory Board reported that 22 states filed a lawsuit challenging this policy.
Impact on Specific Research Areas:
  • Cancer Research: National Cancer Institute facing 38.6% reduction in research grants
  • Aging/Alzheimer's: National Institute on Aging proposed 45.5% cut in grant funding
  • Diversity Initiatives: NSF's INCLUDES coordination hub ($9M) terminated
  • COVID Research: Vaccine hesitancy study ($200K) among terminated grants
  • High-Risk Research: Innovative grants dropped from 406 in 2024 to 364 in 2025
"Make no mistake. This announcement will mean less research. Lights in labs nationwide will literally go out."
â€” Association of American Medical Colleges (AAMC)
Brookings Institution analysis estimated that the proposed cuts to NIH direct research activities constitute a 40.4% reduction, which could result in eight fewer drugs approved for every $1 billion cut in research grants over a three-to-ten-year period.
Sources: STAT News, Science News, Advisory Board, Brookings Institution
Ken Burns' "The American Revolution": Reexamining Founding Myths â–¶
Documentary Series Addresses Slavery, Native Dispossession, and Revolutionary Violence
Ken Burns' six-part, 12-hour documentary "The American Revolution" premiered on PBS November 16-21, 2025, arriving just before the nation's 250th anniversary in July 2026. NPR described the series as making viewers "think differently about U.S. history" through its frank treatment of the revolution's complexities and contradictions.
Documentary Scope:
  • Time Period: May 1754 (French and Indian War) through 1789 (Washington's election)
  • Production: Eight years in production, directed by Ken Burns, Sarah Botstein, and David Schmidt
  • Narration: Peter Coyote, with voices by Tom Hanks, Meryl Streep, Samuel L. Jackson
  • Critical Reception: 96% positive on Rotten Tomatoes (24 critics)
Smithsonian Magazine reported that Burns described the series as forcing him to "revisit everything he thought he knew." The documentary portrays the war as "much more than the oft-told tales of the founding fathers. It was a bloody, gritty affair, a tragic civil war and a global conflict as much as it was a war for independence."
"The American Revolution is one of the most important events in human history. We went from being subjects to inventing a new concept, citizens, and set in motion democratic revolutions around the globe."
â€” Ken Burns
The series arrives, in the words of a New York Times review, "in the middle of a culture war," with its frank discussions of slavery and Native American dispossession drawing both praise and criticism. The documentary depicts the Revolution as a "hyper-violent civil war that divided families and communities" and presents Native Americans as "members of powerful nations faced with complex choices."
Key Themes Emphasized:
  • Violence of War: Focus on guerrilla warfare, brutality often sanitized in history
  • Loyalist Perspective: Humanizes both Patriots and those faithful to the crown
  • Native American Agency: Shows tribes making strategic choices to protect their lands
  • Slavery's Role: Addresses the contradiction between liberty rhetoric and enslaved people
  • Women's Contributions: Highlights often-overlooked female revolutionaries
NPR interviewed Burns, who emphasized that the Founding Fathers "talked about you and me. They talked about the unborn millions," referring to how Jefferson's pursuit of happiness was about "lifelong learning in a marketplace of ideas" rather than material pursuits.
Burns noted the relevance to contemporary divisions: "When you look at the revolution, we were really divided then, really divided. People were killing their neighbors." The series, he said, serves as a reminder of why America's democratic experiment is special and the obligations current generations have to "hand that off to our posterity."
Conservative Criticism:
Some MAGA commentators have objected to the series, calling it "woke" for its inclusion of perspectives from enslaved people, Native Americans, women, and Loyalists. The criticism reflects broader debates about how American history should be taught and whether acknowledging difficult truths diminishes patriotic pride.
"We think that if we acknowledge the violenceâ€¦ that somehow it will diminish those big ideas. In fact I think that those big ideas are even more inspiring when you understand that they come out of a revolution that is bloody, just guerrilla warfare at times and unbelievably brutal, a revolution that is also a civil war."
â€” Ken Burns on the series' treatment of Revolutionary War violence
The documentary uses innovative cinematography, including drone footage capturing battles from high above and close-up shots of re-enactors (showing hands, boots, but avoiding faces) filmed at actual historical locations. Viewer reviews praised this approach as avoiding "cheesy reenactments" typical of History Channel productions.
Sources: NPR, Smithsonian Magazine, Wikipedia, NPR Interview, PBS
