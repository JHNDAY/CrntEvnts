Constitutional Challenge · February 17, 2026

Can Congress Rein In the Pardon Power?

A proposed constitutional amendment — backed by the first Republican co-sponsor — would let Congress nullify presidential pardons. The odds are long, but the bipartisan discontent is real.

Pardon Integrity Act · Rep. Johnny Olszewski (D-MD) & Rep. Don Bacon (R-NE) · Source: Axios, Feb. 17, 2026
Pardons this term (est.) 1,500+ Jan. 6 defendants alone
GOP co-sponsors so far 1 Rep. Don Bacon (R-NE) · retiring centrist
States needed to ratify 38 of 50 · constitutional requirement
How the Pardon Integrity Act Works
1
Trigger
A presidential pardon is issued that draws congressional concern.
2
Force a Vote
20 House members  +  5 Senators must sign to trigger a nullification vote.
3
Override the Pardon
Both chambers must vote to nullify — each requiring a ⅔ majority to succeed.
4
Why This Is Hard to Abuse
The high threshold is intentional — designed as a "narrow, commonsense guardrail," not a routine political weapon.
"Frankly, it is clear to me the pardon authority has been abused. I'm pleased to cosponsor Rep. Olszewski's Pardon Integrity Act."
— Rep. Don Bacon (R-NE), first Republican co-sponsor

Reality Check
Amending the Constitution is intentionally difficult. Even with bipartisan discontent, the Pardon Integrity Act faces near-impossible odds in the current Congress — and ratification by 38 states adds a second mountain to climb. Its significance today is more political than legislative: it signals that the president's own party has limits to its tolerance of pardon power use.
