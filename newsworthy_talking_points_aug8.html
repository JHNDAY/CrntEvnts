Stephen Miran, Chair of the Council of Economic Advisers and key architect of Trump's tariff policies, has been nominated to fill a vacancy on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Miran will serve temporarily through January 2026, replacing Biden appointee Adriana Kugler who resigned early.
Sources: NBC News, CNN, The Washington Post (August 7-8, 2025)
Context: Miran's nomination gives Trump his first opportunity to shape the Fed's board since returning to office. His appointment comes amid Trump's criticism of Fed Chair Jerome Powell for not cutting interest rates more aggressively.
üìÑ The Paper That Started It All: "A User's Guide to Restructuring the Global Trading System"
Miran's non-peer-reviewed November 2024 paper laid the intellectual foundation for Trump's sweeping tariff policies. The 41-page essay argues that persistent dollar overvaluation prevents trade balance and proposes "sweeping tariffs" to narrow trade gaps.

Critical Reception

The paper has faced significant criticism from economists and analysts who question its theoretical foundations and practical feasibility. Critics argue the paper's approach to currency manipulation and trade deficits is fundamentally flawed.
Sources: Hudson Bay Capital Management, Financial Times, France24 (November 2024 - April 2025)
Note: The paper was published by Hudson Bay Capital where Miran served as senior strategist before joining the Trump administration.

Pending litigation argues that Trump's use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose broad tariffs is illegal. Multiple federal courts are considering challenges to the administration's tariff authority.

Key Legal Arguments

Plaintiffs argue that IEEPA requires an "unusual and extraordinary threat" to justify emergency powers, and that trade deficits don't constitute such a threat. Courts have ruled that IEEPA has never been used to impose tariffs in the law's 47-year history.
Key Cases: V.O.S. Selections v. Trump (Federal Circuit), 12-state coalition lawsuit (Court of International Trade)
Sources: NPR, CNBC, Liberty Justice Center, Peterson Institute for International Economics
Status: Appeals pending; temporary stays keeping tariffs in effect during litigation

Looking Ahead

The convergence of Miran's Fed nomination, ongoing legal challenges, and mounting economic evidence of tariff costs creates a complex policy environment. With Senate confirmation hearings for Miran likely in September and court decisions on tariff legality pending, the coming weeks will be crucial for the future of U.S. trade policy.
