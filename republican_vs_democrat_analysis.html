Analysis of Partisan Questioning Approaches
Jack Smith Deposition - December 17, 2025

Overview

The deposition of former Special Counsel Jack Smith revealed starkly different questioning strategies between Republican and Democratic members of the House Judiciary Committee. The transcript shows a highly partisan divide in both the substance and tone of questions posed to Smith, with fundamentally different narratives about the legitimacy of his investigations.

Republican Questioning Strategy

Core Themes and Accusations

Central Narrative: The investigations were politically motivated "weaponization" of the Justice Department designed to interfere in the 2024 election and target Trump for partisan purposes.

1. Allegations of Political Targeting

  • "Picking the Man, Then Finding the Crime" - Republicans extensively quoted Robert Jackson's 1940 speech about prosecutorial abuse, focusing on the danger that prosecutors will "pick the man and then search the law books...to pin some offenses on him"
  • Questioned whether Smith's office was "only interested in prosecuting President Trump because an election is coming up"
  • Emphasized that Smith works for a "Democratic President" and "Democratic Attorney General"
  • Suggested the goal was to "prevent him from...being the party's nominee or being a successful party's nominee...or at the very least, keeping him off the campaign trail"

2. Congressional Toll Records Controversy

This was the Republicans' most sustained and detailed line of attack:

  • Focused extensively on subpoenas for phone records of Members of Congress, including Speaker McCarthy and Chairman Jordan
  • Argued these violated Speech or Debate Clause protections
  • Questioned why Smith's office didn't simply ask Members' lawyers for the records voluntarily
  • Highlighted that magistrate judges were not informed that subpoenas involved Members of Congress
  • Raised concerns about DOJ Inspector General's office serving subpoenas (potential conflict of interest)
  • Questioned the timeframe for Jordan's records (going back to January 2020, a full year before the relevant events)
"There's no evidence that Representative Perry or Speaker McCarthy was interested in obstructing your work, was there?"

3. Gag Order Criticism

  • Questioned the legitimacy of seeking protective orders limiting Trump's public statements
  • Asked Smith to identify specific witnesses who would be intimidated
  • Noted that the D.C. Circuit ultimately narrowed the gag order from what Smith requested

4. Justice Manual Violations

  • Challenged Smith's definitive statements about Trump's guilt in his final report
  • Argued the Justice Manual prohibits prosecutors from asserting defendant guilt before a jury determination
  • Contrasted Smith's report language with Special Counsel Hur's more cautious phrasing
  • Noted Hur used words like "jurors could find" or "might conclude" whereas Smith's language was "pretty definitive"

5. Evidence and Timing Questions

  • Questioned whether Smith had evidence Trump directly instructed people to "crash the Capitol"
  • Challenged whether Trump's statements about election fraud could be criminalized
  • Asked about "disputed elections" throughout American history (1800, 1824, 1876, 2000)
  • Questioned the timing of indictments relative to the 2024 election

Smith's Responses to Republican Questioning

Defensive but Unwavering Strategy

On Political Motivation Allegations:

"All of that is false... The evidence here made clear that President Trump was by a large measure the most culpable and most responsible person in this conspiracy. These crimes were committed for his benefit... I would never take orders from a political leader to hamper another person in an election. That's not who I am. And I think people who know me and my experience over 30 years would find that laughable."
  • Firmly rejected all suggestions of political motivation
  • Stated he "did not consider politics, anyone's politics, in charging these cases"
  • Emphasized his 30-year career and that he began the investigation with an open mind about whether to indict
  • Noted he would have been "perfectly comfortable" not indicting if facts and law didn't support it

On Toll Records:

  • Disagreed that subpoenas violated Speech or Debate protections
  • Insisted records were obtained "in a manner that was consistent with the law and consistent with Department policy"
  • Stated "there was nothing improper about how we got these records"
  • Could not speak to decisions made before his appointment (Perry phone seizure, Jordan records timeframe)
  • Acknowledged current DOJ practice would notify judges when subpoenas involve Members of Congress (but this wasn't done at the time)

On Gag Orders:

  • No apologies: "I make no apologies for that"
  • Detailed Trump's threatening statements: "If you come after me, I'll come after you"
  • Cited Trump calling General Milley a "traitor" and mentioning people would be "put to death" in "olden times"
  • Noted judges received "vile death threats" due to Trump's statements
  • Emphasized both District Court and Court of Appeals found Trump's actions were "causing witnesses to be intimidated and endangering people"

On Strength of Evidence:

  • Maintained he had "proof beyond a reasonable doubt in both cases"
  • Stated "we had a very strong case...if we went to trial we would prevail"
  • On January 6: Trump "caused it," "exploited it," and it "was foreseeable to him"
  • Described Trump creating "a level of distrust" with false fraud claims, inviting angry supporters to Capitol, then refusing to stop the violence

On Prosecutorial Standards:

  • Agreed with all of Robert Jackson's principles about prosecutorial ethics
  • When asked if he lived up to those standards: "I do"
  • Maintained professional composure but was firm in defending his decisions

Democratic Questioning Strategy

Core Themes and Defense

Central Narrative: Smith conducted a legitimate, evidence-based investigation into serious crimes that threatened democracy, following all DOJ protocols while facing unprecedented witness intimidation.

1. Establishing Smith's Credibility and Experience

Rep. Raskin's approach:

  • Asked Smith to detail his extensive prosecutorial career
  • "Do you have any idea how many cases you've actually handled and prosecuted in your career?"
  • Smith: "I'm sure it's in the thousands"
  • "Have you ever been prosecuted for selective prosecution, vindictive prosecution, or partisan discrimination?" - "Never"
  • "Have you ever fired a prosecutor because they refused to engage in a prosecution on grounds that there was not sufficient evidence?" - "No"

2. Bottom-Line Questions on Evidence

Rep. Goldman's direct approach:

"Let's just start with the bottom line up front. One of your investigations focused on whether any person violated the law in connection with efforts to interfere in the lawful transfer of power following the 2020 election. Is that correct?"
  • Asked directly: Did you have sufficient evidence to obtain and sustain a conviction?
  • Did you have proof beyond a reasonable doubt?
  • Were there substantial Federal interests in prosecuting?
  • Smith answered "Yes" to all three questions for both cases

3. Witness Intimidation Focus

Multiple Democrats emphasized this theme:

  • Raskin: "Will you speak a bit about the problem of witness intimidation and what that does to the rule of law and justice?"
  • Smith: "Witness intimidation is probably the most corrosive thing that can happen to the rule of law"
  • Rep. Crockett: "Do you have any regrets about the steps that you took as it relates to seeking gag orders?"
  • Smith: "I have no regrets about that"
  • Rep. Crockett: "Have you, yourself, been intimidated...threatened?"
  • Smith: "I'm not going to be intimidated" / "Yes" (to threats)

4. Democracy and Accountability

Rep. Jayapal's questioning:

"Do you think that it's important for our democracy to hold free and fair elections without interference or obstruction?"
  • "What happens if there is election interference and the people who are responsible for that are not held accountable?"
  • Smith: "It becomes the new norm"
  • "What would [the toll on our democracy] be?"
  • Smith: "Catastrophic"

5. Transparency and Public Hearing Requests

  • Jayapal highlighted that Smith requested to appear voluntarily in a public hearing
  • Chairman Jordan instead issued a subpoena for closed-door deposition
  • Smith testified he wanted to speak publicly because "we conducted our investigation in a way that I'm proud of"
  • Even after the deposition, Smith confirmed he was still willing to testify publicly

6. Pushing for Case File Release

Goldman's strategy:

  • If case files were released, would they show political considerations? "We did not consider politics... It never happened"
  • DOJ gave Smith "unrestricted testimony" authorization - shouldn't committee get unrestricted access to case files?
  • Smith: "I have no objection to people looking at the case file"

7. Following DOJ Protocol

  • Asked whether Smith followed DOJ protocols and consulted with Public Integrity Section, Office of Legal Counsel, etc.
  • Smith: "Yes"
  • Were consultations memorialized in writing? "Yes"
  • Emphasized Smith consulted regularly with Solicitor General

8. Defending Against "Attacking the Good Guys"

Jayapal on witness intimidation tactics:

Smith: "I think it drives people away from public service... When we get to attacking the people who are the good guys, I just don't know where that goes. It's counter to how I was raised."

Smith's Responses to Democratic Questioning

Cooperative and Expansive Strategy

Provided Detailed, Substantive Answers:

  • Willingly expanded on witness intimidation experiences throughout his career
  • Spoke about murder cases where witnesses were murdered
  • Described witness intimidation as "the most corrosive thing that can happen to the rule of law"
  • Explained prosecutorial tactics to protect witnesses and shorten timeframes to limit obstruction opportunities

Expressed Strong Views on Democracy:

  • Called the toll on democracy from unpunished election interference "catastrophic"
  • Stated that without accountability, interference "becomes the new norm"
  • Emphasized free and fair elections are fundamental

Showed Vulnerability About Threats:

  • Acknowledged he has been threatened
  • Described impacts on his staff, court staff, witnesses
  • But maintained: "I'm not going to be intimidated"

Transparent About His Work:

  • "I'm proud of" the investigation
  • "I'm particularly proud of the people I conducted the investigation with"
  • Repeatedly expressed willingness to testify publicly
  • Had "no objection to people looking at the case file"

Key Contrasts in Approach

Republican Focus

  • Process violations
  • Political motivations
  • Constitutional concerns re: Congress
  • Targeting Trump specifically
  • Election interference by DOJ
  • Overreach and abuse of power

Democratic Focus

  • Strength of evidence
  • Professional qualifications
  • Threats to democracy
  • Witness intimidation
  • Following DOJ protocols
  • Accountability for crimes

Republican Tone

  • Skeptical and challenging
  • Accusatory at times
  • Lengthy setup questions
  • Technical legal arguments
  • Emphasis on procedural irregularities

Democratic Tone

  • Supportive and defensive (of Smith)
  • Direct, bottom-line questions
  • Emphasizing bigger picture
  • Building credibility record
  • Focus on threats to witnesses and democracy

Notable Patterns in Smith's Response Style

To Republicans:

  • Firm but controlled - Never apologized, but remained professional
  • Limited elaboration - Answered questions directly without volunteering extra information
  • Defended decisions vigorously - Particularly on gag orders and evidence strength
  • Invoked professional standards - Referenced 30-year career and past experience
  • Acknowledged limitations - Couldn't speak to pre-appointment decisions
  • Stood his ground - Disagreed with Speech or Debate arguments, refused to back down on political motivation claims

To Democrats:

  • More expansive and detailed - Provided context and examples
  • Emotionally engaged - Showed passion about democracy, witness protection
  • Personal revelations - Discussed being threatened, effects on public service
  • Philosophical reflections - Spoke about values, what motivated him
  • Cooperative tone - Offered to testify publicly, had no objection to file release
  • Validated their narrative - Confirmed evidence was strong, followed all protocols

Overall Assessment

The deposition revealed a fundamental partisan divide that was never bridged:

Republicans operated from the premise that the investigations were politically motivated "lawfare" designed to interfere with Trump's 2024 campaign. They focused on process questions, constitutional concerns about congressional privileges, and technical violations of Justice Department guidelines. Their questioning suggested Smith picked Trump as a target and then found crimes to charge.

Democrats operated from the premise that Smith conducted legitimate investigations into serious crimes threatening American democracy. They emphasized Smith's credentials, the strength of evidence, proper DOJ procedures, and the importance of accountability. Their questioning reinforced a narrative of Trump as a criminal who intimidated witnesses and attacked democracy itself.

Smith maintained throughout that he followed the evidence, adhered to DOJ protocols, made decisions based solely on facts and law (not politics), and had strong enough cases to prevail at trial. He showed no regrets about his decisions and expressed willingness for maximum transparency through public testimony and case file release.

Neither side appeared to persuade the other, and the transcript reads as two competing realities existing in the same room.
