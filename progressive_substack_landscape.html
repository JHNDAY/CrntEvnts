üéØ Origin of the Company Name (2017)

Substack, Inc. was founded in October 2017 by Chris Best, Jairaj Sethi, and Hamish McKenzie. The name appears to derive from "sub(scription) + stack" ‚Äî referencing both:

  • The subscription model at its core
  • The tool stack the founders wanted to provide writers

The founders' explicit goal was to give writers the complete "tool stack" they needed to go independent. As co-founder Chris Best said: "You just have to be a great writer and we can take care of the rest." The platform would handle payments, email publishing, analytics, and community tools ‚Äî the full stack.

üìß Early Usage: The Platform (2017-2019)

2017-2019

Initially, "Substack" referred solely to:

  • The company/platform itself
  • The service that hosted newsletters
  • Writers would say they were "using Substack" or "publishing on Substack"

üîÑ Linguistic Shift: From Platform to Publication (2019-2021)

2019-2021

As the platform grew, a metonymic shift occurred where "Substack" began referring to the individual publications themselves:

  • "My Substack" = My newsletter/publication hosted on Substack
  • "Her Substack" = Jennifer Rubin's publication "The Contrarian"
  • "Substacks" (plural) = Multiple newsletters on the platform

This is similar to how:

  • "blog" became the name for individual sites (not just the platform)
  • "tweet" became the individual post (not just Twitter as a whole)

üìù Official Terminology Evolution

The company itself contributed to this evolution:

  • Publications - The official term for individual newsletters
  • Substack publications - How the company formally refers to them
  • But writers and readers commonly just say "Substacks"

üöÄ Expansion of Meaning (2022-Present)

2022-Present

As Substack added features beyond email newsletters, the meaning expanded:

  • 2017-2019: Email newsletters only
  • 2019: Added podcasts and discussion threads
  • 2022: Added video, Substack Chat (group chats), Notes (Twitter-like feed)
  • 2023: Substack Reader app

Now a "Substack" can mean:

  • A multimedia publication (newsletter + podcast + video + community)
  • A personal media empire
  • A direct creator-to-audience relationship

üåê Genericization

The term has become genericized in journalism circles, much like "Kleenex" or "Google" ‚Äî where "Substack" sometimes means any independent subscription newsletter, even those not hosted on Substack.

üîß The "Tool Stack" Legacy

The original concept of providing a complete "tool stack" for writers remains central ‚Äî but now that stack includes far more than email:

  • Video hosting
  • Podcasting infrastructure
  • Payment processing
  • Community features
  • Discovery tools

The name "Substack" continues to capture this all-in-one nature.
