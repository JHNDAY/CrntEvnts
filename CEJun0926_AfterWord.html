01 Colombia's Runoff: The Center Eliminated Itself High coverage ▼
Three-Way Race, Two Extremists in the Final
Colombia's May 25 first round produced no majority winner. Center-right Senator Paloma Valencia was eliminated; the runoff pits far-left ex-guerrilla Iván Cepeda (allied with Maduro and outgoing President Petro) against far-right lawyer Nicolás De La Espriella. Analysts described the outcome as a textbook case of centrist collapse through strategic abstention.
ReutersEl País América
The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
BOLLI member Francesca Nannetti — who left Colombia the day before the election — reported a striking pattern on the ground: voters who thought Valencia was the best candidate declined to vote for her because they believed she couldn't win. Her youngest son worked on the campaign and concluded it was also poorly run. The dynamic produced exactly the outcome it predicted.
El País AméricaThe Atlantic
Trump Backs De La Espriella; Petro Was Sanctioned
Trump publicly praised far-right candidate De La Espriella and previously froze President Petro's financial accounts in a trade dispute — giving the administration a clear stake in the outcome. Outgoing Petro raised the minimum wage 23%, potentially swinging working-class votes to Cepeda. The runoff is scheduled approximately two weeks after the first round.
ReutersWaPo
What's at Stake for the Region
Colombia has been the U.S.'s most durable ally in Latin America. A Cepeda victory is widely forecast to trigger capital flight and professional emigration. The election fits a regional rightward trend (Milei in Argentina, Bukele in El Salvador) but with an unusual twist: the right candidate is the U.S.-backed one while the left candidate inherits the incumbent's wage-boost popularity.

02 CBS News: The Bagel Breakfast Massacre & Murrow's Network Surrenders High coverage ▼
Three Journalists Fired; Pelley Speaks Out, Then Goes Too
CBS News president Barry Weiss fired three senior journalists in June 2026. When veteran anchor Scott Pelley went public with his objections, he was subsequently dismissed as well. Weiss reportedly offered the first round of departing staff bagels and cream cheese — an attempt at mollification that became the session's defining image of institutional cowardice. The New York Times covered Pelley's departure extensively.
NYTWaPo
Weiss Was Hired to Do This — It's Not an Accident
George's framing in session: Barry Weiss was not an incompetent person accidentally placed in the role — she was installed by David Ellison to do exactly what she is doing. The Ellison family's acquisition of Paramount gave them both the vehicle (CBS) and the shield (deep pockets) to execute a full editorial alignment with the administration, regardless of viewership consequences.
WaPo TechThe Atlantic
Murrow's Network: The Historical Irony
CBS was the network of Edward R. Murrow's 1954 confrontation with McCarthy — the gold standard of broadcast journalism courage. Every serious TV journalist since has aspired to that lineage. Weiss reportedly declared that CBS's mission is to "drive the news" rather than report facts — a direct inversion of Murrow's ethos. 60 Minutes had seen a 9% audience uptick before the firings; that gain has now been reversed.
NYTThe New Yorker
Elise Stefanik as Mirror: The Cost of "Going Over"
The session used Stefanik — once a respected legislator with a Harvard-adjacent profile, now Ambassador to the UN after her MAGA pivot — as the parallel to Weiss. Both followed Petro's ambassador to the dark side. Both paid a reputational price invisible to themselves. John: "It didn't end well for her." The group cited Lord Acton: power corrupts, absolute power corrupts absolutely.

03 Monuments as Spectacle: The Octagon, the Arc de Trump & the Kennedy Center Name Developing story ▼
UFC Octagon on the South Lawn — Lawsuit Filed
The administration proposed a permanent UFC octagon on the South Lawn, with fighters entering through the Lincoln Memorial and walking through the Oval Office. A legal challenge has been filed arguing the plan constitutes inappropriate use of public grounds for a for-profit commercial event. The group noted Trump has also floated extending the Lincoln Memorial grounds all the way to the Potomac.
WaPoPolitico
Arc de Trump: 250 Feet, Blocking Arlington Sightline
George flagged in session: plans for a 250-foot triumphal arch — dubbed "Arc de Trump" by the group — are in development. If built, it would permanently obstruct the historic sightline from the Lincoln Memorial toward Arlington National Cemetery. The East Wing ballroom construction was cited as precedent: legal challenge came too late to stop the damage.
NYTWaPo
Kennedy Center: Court Orders Name Removed
A federal court ordered the Kennedy Center to remove Trump's name from all references. The Center has complied online and in promotional materials; physical building signage has not yet been changed. George cited a commentator who described the full erasure of references as a metaphor for the presidency. The group expected the administration to delay or resist the physical removal as long as legally possible.

04 U.S. Demographics: Fertility at 1.6 — Immigration Soon the Only Engine of Growth High coverage ▼
The Numbers That Don't Add Up — Until They Do
Larry Schwirian brought a puzzle to session: reports show more Americans leaving the country than immigrants arriving, while the fertility rate is 1.6 — yet U.S. population still grew ~0.8% in 2025. The resolution: net immigration still exceeds net emigration even at suppressed levels; and fertility rate (children per woman of childbearing age) and birth rate (births per 1,000 population) are different statistics, frequently conflated.
Pew ResearchAxios
Post-Session Resource: Immigration Becomes the Only Source
Larry shared this key link after the session: within four years, immigration is projected to become the sole source of U.S. population growth — a finding that crystallizes the stakes of current immigration policy precisely at the moment the administration is dismantling legal immigration pathways. Read the full article →
MSN / Larry Schwirian
The Smartphone Thesis — Boston Globe
Michael Berger cited a Boston Globe article arguing the primary driver of birth rate decline since the late 1980s is the smartphone: people are on their phones at night rather than doing what produces children. The article also noted the U.S. fertility decline cannot be modeled cleanly because the country is too heterogeneous — racial, economic, and geographic gaps in maternal and infant mortality skew aggregates significantly.
Boston Globe
Construction, Services, and the Pipeline Being Dismantled
Up to 40% of construction workers in California and Texas are immigrants, legal and illegal — and housing construction is already badly lagging demand. Hilary Kent: service industry jobs least substitutable by AI will face the sharpest labor shortages. John: a non-trivial share of major U.S. corporations are now led by immigrants or their direct descendants; immigrant crime rates are well below those of native-born citizens.
Pew ResearchAxios
Life Expectancy Gap: U.S. 79, Peers 82.7
George cited the 3.7-year gap between U.S. life expectancy and that of comparable developed nations — a gap attributable partly to the heterogeneity of health outcomes across racial and economic groups, and likely to widen as Medicaid restrictions take effect. Michael Berger, a physician: "The lack of access to competent medical care is the problem. And it's gonna get worse."
Pew ResearchNYT
Global Picture: Even India Now Shrinking
Larry noted declining fertility is a worldwide phenomenon. Even India — which recently surpassed China as the world's most populous nation — now registers a negative population growth rate. Africa is the sole continent with robust growth. Sally's conclusion: in the face of demographic decline, Europeans and Americans will simply have to work longer. "If you're going to live to 98, you shouldn't feel entitled to retire at 65."

05 Maine 2026: The Plattner Question — Principle, Strategy, or Abstention? Developing story ▼
The Allegations and the Candidate
Democratic Senate candidate David Plattner faces a near-uncontested primary (Janet Mills dropped her active campaign) and a general election against incumbent Susan Collins. Allegations include sexual texting, roughing up a girlfriend (denied), and a troubling tattoo. His wife, Amy, preemptively told a campaign staffer about the texting; that staffer left and went public. Sally Fleschner cited a recent article by a former campaign aide calling him a "loose cannon."
PoliticoMaine Public
The Group's Straw Poll: 6 For, 3–4 Abstain, 0 for Collins
John polled the group: six hands for Plattner; three to four said they wouldn't vote; nobody said they'd vote for Collins. Hal Shear argued most passionately for Plattner — he knows veterans who did three tours, came back with PTSD and addiction, and recovered, and he doesn't believe past crisis behavior should define fitness for office. 06 The Thread That Ran Through Everything — & What Comes Next Emerging story ▼
"How Do Societies Choose When All the Choices Are Bad?"
Colombia's runoff and Maine's Senate race posed the same underlying question: what do citizens do when the viable center has been eliminated and the remaining options are all imperfect? In Colombia, voters who liked the center candidate decided she couldn't win, and made their prediction come true. In Maine, Democrats face the same logic — vote for the flawed candidate who can win, or protect your conscience and cede the field.
The AtlanticForeign Policy
Hal's Benchmark: "No Bar Too Low"
Hal Shear's recurring observation — "Every time it can't get worse, it does" — became the session's dark north star. The octagon at the Lincoln Memorial, the bagel breakfast firings, Trump at the Knicks game claiming mostly cheers while being booed near-unanimously, 25,000 first-term tweets the presidential library says it can't locate — each item individually absurd; collectively, a systematic erasure of norms.
WaPoThe Atlantic
The Wins Are Real, Even When Narrow
John's counterweight: the Kennedy Center name-removal order, the octagon lawsuit, the symbolic House vote blocking further Iran strikes, the Ukraine loan passage — these are genuine institutional responses, even if partial and slow. "Take the wins when you can. Keep as steady a course as you can. The country can start rebuilding — but only if it gets past this first."
