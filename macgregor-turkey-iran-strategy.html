Colonel Douglas Macgregor (Ret.)

Background: Career military officer with combat command experience in Desert Storm and the Balkans. Strategic advisor and policy commentator on military and foreign affairs.

Expertise: Long-term critic of sustained U.S. military interventionism in the Middle East. Advocates for strategic restraint, realistic assessment of regional power dynamics, and prioritization of domestic U.S. priorities over open-ended commitments abroad.

Key Position: Argues that conventional military power cannot solve Iran's challenge; that Turkey—not Iran—represents the enduring existential threat to Israel and regional stability; and that the U.S. faces inevitable fiscal and political pressures that will force disengagement from the Persian Gulf regardless of policy preference.

Central Argument: Washington's effort to contain Iran while simultaneously strengthening Turkey through defense technology sales is strategically incoherent. Turkey—historically the region's dominant power—poses a far greater long-term threat to Israeli and regional stability than Iran ever could. Trump's current Middle East policy is driven by political pressure ("duress") from special interests demanding Iran's destruction, but this strategy is militarily infeasible, economically ruinous, and ultimately unsustainable.

What Washington Is Doing Wrong

Proxy Strategy Folly

Using Sunni militant factions against Hezbollah inadvertently creates a Turkish-aligned footprint on Lebanon's doorstep—exactly the regional dominance structure the U.S. should oppose.

Military Misplaced Confidence

Washington believes conventional air power can destroy Iran's military-industrial complex. Iran's massive geography, mountainous terrain, and heavily fortified underground facilities make this belief naive.

Strategic Incoherence

Arming Turkey (the greater long-term threat) while trying to destroy Iran (the more contained regional power) is backwards from any coherent regional strategy.

Why This Matters Now

The "Duress" Factor

Trump's Political Pressure: Mega-donors and special interest groups are demanding total Iran dismantlement. Trump fears losing financial backing and facing renewed domestic attacks if he pulls back.
The Cost of Resistance: Refusal to comply could trigger impeachment threats or personal safety concerns tied to volatile foreign policy decisions.
The Outcome: Policy driven by political coercion rather than strategic reality, leading to overcommitment and eventual failure.
The Inevitable Endgame

U.S. Fiscal Crisis

A major domestic financial crisis will force Washington to end military operations in the Persian Gulf and focus resources inward.

Regional Restructuring

Within 5–10 years, artificial Sykes-Picot borders will dissolve. Turkey will dominate northern Iraq and Syria; Iran will control Shiite-heavy areas in the south and Gulf.

Historical Pattern Returns

The region will revert to its thousand-year pattern of alternating Turkish-Persian dominance—the natural structure, not the artificial post-colonial borders.

1 Geography Defeats Air Power

Iran's vast territory (over 600,000 square miles), mountainous terrain, and deeply buried military-industrial infrastructure cannot be destroyed by conventional bombing campaigns. Lessons from Afghanistan and Iraq demonstrate this.

2 Economic Insulation Works

Russia-China partnerships provide Iran with economic alternatives, reducing the impact of Western sanctions as coercive tools.

3 Regional Retaliation Escalates Costs

Iran can attack infrastructure across the region, close the Strait of Hormuz, or activate proxy networks. The cost of escalation exceeds what the U.S. public will tolerate.

4 Fiscal Crisis Will Force Withdrawal

U.S. domestic debt and entitlement obligations will eventually force a withdrawal of military resources from the Persian Gulf region.

5 Borders Will Be Redrawn

When U.S. power withdraws, artificial post-colonial borders will dissolve, and regional powers will reorganize the map along historical ethnic and religious lines.

✓ Accept Regional Realities

Accept that Turkey and Iran are the historical regional powers, that their dominance over post-colonial spaces is inevitable when external powers withdraw, and that pretending otherwise wastes resources and lives.

Stop Proxy Warfare in Syria

Acknowledge that Syria will be dominated by Turkey, Iran, and local actors, not by U.S.-backed militias.

Accept Iraqi Reorientation

Accept that Iraq's Shiite majority will orient toward Iran regardless of U.S. efforts to maintain influence.

Focus on U.S. Energy Independence

Rather than trying to control Middle Eastern energy through military presence, invest in U.S. renewable energy and reduce dependence on Gulf oil.
