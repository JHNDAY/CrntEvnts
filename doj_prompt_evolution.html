The Anatomy of a Prompt

Five iterations — from topic list to operational brief
This document traces how a single inquiry about the DOJ evolved across five coaching sessions. Each iteration added a new dimension of context, transforming a bare subject into a fully specified research and facilitation brief. The final comparison shows exactly what changed — and why it matters.

· Reduction of DOJ employees due to resignations and firings

✦ What's working

The word "profound" sets the right register — serious, analytical, not tabloid. The final bullet (future litigation) shows sophisticated forward-looking thinking that goes beyond current-events summaries.

→ What's missing

This is a topic outline, not a narrative. Claude has no idea who is asking, why they're asking, or what they'll do with the answer. It can only return a generic encyclopedia entry.

✦ What's working

"Analyzing and understanding numbers in complex systems" tells Claude exactly how this mind works — it wants patterns, data, and systemic analysis, not anecdote. The phrase "anti-science and unfactual" signals concern without outrage, which is a disciplined register.

→ What's still missing

We know who is asking and how they think — but not what for. Without purpose, Claude still can't calibrate depth, format, or tone. Is this for personal understanding? A presentation? A written piece?

✦ What's working

"Fairly but honestly" is a compressed statement of intellectual integrity — acknowledging the tension between balance and candor. The facilitator role gives Claude a dual mandate: analytical depth and audience translation.

→ What's still missing

We know the purpose — but not the audience. "A Current Events group" could be anything from a Fox News watching demographic to a faculty seminar. Audience determines tone, balance, and how to anticipate pushback.

"Mostly from the progressive liberal faction" is candidly self-aware — not pretending the audience is a blank slate. The parenthetical "(not emotionally)" signals that emotional reaction is a real risk the facilitator is actively managing.

→ What's still missing

We know the audience disposition and desired mode — but not what success looks like in practice. "Objectively respond" is still abstract. Claude needs a concrete success criterion to design toward.

Version 5 — Success Criterion Added

I want to investigate the profound changes that occurred at the DOJ in Trump's second administration.

· Reduction of DOJ employees due to resignations and firings
· The shift in the DOJ to becoming the personal lawyer of the president
· The impact of vindictive prosecutions
· The sharp decrease in legal expertise and the attendant reversal in the courts
· Pending lawsuits against the DOJ for malfeasances
· Possible litigation against the current DOJ after the Trump administration is over

I am 75 years old and retired. I have a PhD in physics and spent my career analyzing and understanding numbers in complex systems. I have always read widely in history and current events. I am concerned about the anti-science and unfactual approach to governance of the current administration.

I facilitate a Current Events group and want to present this fairly but honestly. My group is mostly from the progressive liberal faction. I want them to objectively (not emotionally) respond to the results.

I'd consider it a success if participants left asking better questions rather than feeling their existing views confirmed.
✦ What's working

This single sentence is the most important in the entire prompt. It's countercultural (you're not building a case — you're building curiosity), it's measurable, and it perfectly encodes a Socratic rather than rhetorical pedagogy. Claude now knows exactly what to optimize for.

✦ Now complete

The arc from "analyzing complex systems" to "asking better questions" creates an intellectual through-line that a physicist would recognize: premature closure is the enemy of understanding. The prompt now embeds that wisdom structurally.

The Coaching Principle This Sequence Demonstrates

A prompt is not a search query. It is a conversation brief — a specification of who you are, what you need, who will receive it, and what success looks like. The subject matter barely changed across five iterations. What changed was everything surrounding it: the intellectual frame, the social context, and the pedagogical goal. Each of those additions didn't just improve the prompt — it transformed what kind of document Claude could responsibly produce. Version 1 could only yield a news summary. Version 5 yielded a structured inquiry document organized as a physicist's lab report, built for a specific group, calibrated to a specific risk (emotional confirmation bias), and anchored to a specific definition of success. The topic was always the same. The narrative made it real.
