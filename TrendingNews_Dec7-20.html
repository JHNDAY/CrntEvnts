Institutional Resilience (186 mentions)
Courts block Trump orders; Just Security tracks 186 plaintiff wins vs. 113 government wins
▼
Courts Push Back on Executive Overreach
Just Security's litigation tracker (updated daily through December 19) shows 186 total plaintiff wins against Trump administration actions: 50 government actions blocked, 103 temporarily blocked, 28 blocked pending appeal, 5 cases closed in favor of plaintiff. This compares to 113 total government wins, with 214 cases still awaiting court rulings. December 19: Supreme Court dealt Trump administration its "first real loss" since April, rejecting request to block 4th Circuit ruling in immigration judges case (law professor Stephen Vladeck). December 12: 7th Circuit became first federal appeals court to reject Trump's mandatory detention argument. November 21: District Court permanently blocked Trump's executive order dismantling Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS)—ruling actions were "arbitrary and capricious and contrary to federal law."
Judges Enforcing Compliance with Court Orders
Judge John McConnell (Rhode Island) found Trump administration violated "plain language" of his "clear and unambiguous" order to unfreeze federal grants/loans. After government argued order was "ambiguous," McConnell laid out in greater detail the funds government must restore, stopping short of contempt but warning administration. December 9: Judge Jamal Whitehead (Washington) rejected government request to pause discovery in refugee lawsuit, ordering it to proceed despite administration's claim it "would experience hardship" if forced to turn over documents. Brennan Center analysis noted judges have "range of tools for enforcing orders" including civil contempt (fines accruing daily until compliance) and criminal contempt.
Career Officials and Whistleblowers Fighting Back
Hampton Dellinger, head of Office of Special Counsel (whistleblower protection agency), sued Trump over his February firing and was temporarily reinstated by federal judge. Government Accountability Project is suing Trump over unconstitutional rollback of federal worker protections with partner organizations. 200 DOJ employees who left wrote open letter December saying administration is making "coordinated effort" to undermine career staff, transferring career leaders "to work on menial activities" unrelated to experience. Whistleblower Aid's Andrew Bakaj: "This is not accountability, this is retaliation masquerading as reform." The Conversation reported whistleblower groups are "organising... to shore up workers' rights, challenge threats in the courts, and inform the public."
State Attorneys General Leading Resistance
21 state Attorneys General filed lawsuit that resulted in permanent blocking of IMLS dismantling. American Library Association President Sam Helmick: "This isn't just a win for the 21 states who filed the case--it's a win for every library user and every American in every state." Multiple state AGs filed lawsuits challenging birthright citizenship order, refugee suspension, and other executive actions. State-level litigation has been critical component of institutional pushback, with states using their legal standing to challenge federal overreach where individual plaintiffs might lack resources.
December Specific Resistance Moments
December 15: Federal appeals court judges scrutinized Trump's national security basis for collective bargaining rollback during oral arguments. Judge Neomi Rao (Trump appointee) noted statute "provides certain criteria" for presidential determinations. Judge Bradley Garcia (Biden appointee) said court "ought not to second-guess" but expressed concern if record suggests "president didn't make those determinations." December 19: IMLS reinstated all agency grants to libraries after court ruling. December 12: Indiana rejected redistricting plan seen as favoring Trump's priorities. Federal judges around the country continued to issue restraining orders throughout December despite administration's claims of judicial overreach.
Institutional Memory and Expertise Preserved
Despite mass firings, career officials who remained continued professional work and resisted political pressure. Career prosecutors at DOJ recommended against indicting James Comey (ABC News, September), though political appointees proceeded anyway. Erik Siebert, predecessor to current prosecutor, refused to indict Comey "due to lack of evidence"—a decision looking increasingly correct as case proceeds. Former DOJ employees noted political appointees conducting litigation directly are being "chide[d]" by judges "for their shortcomings." The resilience comes from career officials' commitment to professional standards, legal procedures, and institutional norms even under extreme pressure to abandon them.

Escalating Death Toll and Military Operations
As of December 18, at least 104 people killed in 28 US strikes on 29 vessels in Caribbean Sea and Eastern Pacific Ocean. December 18: US killed 5 people in strikes on two boats—third attack that week. Operation Southern Spear uses fleet with robotics and autonomous systems. Administration provided only grainy footage as evidence boats carried drugs, describing victims as "narco-terrorists." Legal experts argue killings likely violate US/international law and amount to extrajudicial executions.
War Crime Allegations: "Double Tap" Strike
Washington Post revealed Defense Secretary Hegseth gave verbal order to SEAL Team Six to leave no survivors in September 1 strike. Two men surviving initial strike were killed in subsequent "double tap." Admiral Bradley told lawmakers he understood mission was to kill all 11 men on boat. Trump initially opposed second strike but later reversed to support "knocking out the boats."
Shifting Justifications and Regime Change Motives
December 16: White House Chief of Staff Susie Wiles revealed in Vanity Fair: "He [Trump] wants to keep on blowing boats up until Maduro cries uncle"—contradicting official anti-drug narrative. Administration's story about first boat's destination changed multiple times. Senator Van Hollen: tanker seizure shows "their whole cover story—that this is about interdicting drugs—is a big lie. This is really about regime change—by force."
December 10 Oil Tanker Seizure
December 10: Trump announced US seized "Skipper" oil tanker off Venezuela—"largest one ever seized." Vessel carrying Venezuelan crude seized in international waters under federal warrant for alleged Iran links. Naval historian called it "very unusual" and "provocative," noting it will deter ships and that "if you have no maritime traffic... then you have no economy."
Congressional Scrutiny and Expanding Threats
December 19: House rejected resolutions challenging Trump's military actions. Defense bill signed December 19 withholds quarter of Hegseth's travel budget until unedited strike videos released to Armed Services Committees. Trump refused to rule out ground invasion, declared Venezuela's airspace "closed in its entirety," ordered "total and complete blockade" of sanctioned tankers. US deployed USS Gerald R. Ford carrier and armada—largest regional military presence in decades.

Affordability Crisis (62 mentions)
Wage growth slumps to lowest since 2021; 71% say income doesn't cover expenses
▼
Wage Growth Collapse
November paycheck growth hit lowest since May 2021: average hourly wages $36.86 (up only 3.5% annually)—barely keeping pace with 3% inflation. RSM US chief economist: "Wage growth is easing into a sustained affordability crisis." US economy lost jobs in three of past six months. 2025 on pace for worst job growth since pandemic. Voluntary quit rates at five-year low in October.
Public Perception and Trump Approval Plunge
NPR/PBS News/Marist poll (December): Only 36% approve of Trump's economic stewardship—his lowest rating in six years, matching Biden's 2022 low. Staggering 71% say income just matches or falls short of monthly expenses. 45% said high prices are biggest economic worry vs. only 10% worried about job security. University of Michigan's Consumer Sentiment Index showed "broadly somber" tenor despite small December rise.
Food Insecurity Jumps
14% of US households reported food insecurity January-October 2025, up from 12.5% in 2024 (Purdue). In NYC, 40% of families can't afford weekly food costs. Grocery prices 18% higher in September than January 2022. Nearly half of Americans say it's harder to afford groceries than a year ago. Beef steaks surged 17% annually.
White House vs. Reality
Trump claimed December 11: "prices are coming down tremendously." But inflation stood at 3% in September (same as January inauguration). Rep. Greene told Sean Spicer that Trump's "gaslighting the people and trying to tell them that prices have come down is not helping." Ben Shapiro: "When President Trump says things are becoming more affordable, people don't believe that because they aren't really more affordable."

Government Sponsored Health Care (58 mentions)
Enhanced ACA subsidies expire Dec 31; millions face doubled premiums
▼
Enhanced Subsidies Expiration Crisis
Enhanced ACA premium subsidies expire December 31, 2025. Congress failed to extend them. December 11: Senate voted on ACA measures but neither Democrats' three-year extension nor Republicans' alternative advanced. Without extension, average premiums will increase 75% for 2026. Low/middle-income households will see ACA premiums rise from $888 (2025) to $1,904 (2026). Four out of five consumers found 2025 plans for $10 or less monthly—that ends January 1.
Coverage Loss Projections
CBO estimates without enhanced subsidies, 14+ million more uninsured by 2034. Combined with "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBBBA) cuts, 10-15 million could lose coverage. Johns Hopkins estimates up to 15 million more uninsured by 2034 from OBBBA alone. New sign-ups dropped considerably in Pennsylvania and California. 21 state-based exchanges saw 18% decrease in new customers to 210,500.
OBBBA Medicaid/ACA Restrictions (Effective January 1, 2026)
July 2025 "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" includes over $1 trillion in healthcare cuts through 2034—"biggest cut to our social safety net in history" (Johns Hopkins). January 1, 2026 changes: More frequent Medicaid redeterminations, work requirements for expansion enrollees, restrictions on lawfully present noncitizens (refugees, asylees, TPS recipients no longer eligible for ACA subsidies), DACA recipients ineligible for ACA Marketplace coverage.
Medicaid Gap States Face Crisis
Ten states have not expanded Medicaid. This "Medicaid gap" expected to cause uninsured rates to jump 65% in Mississippi, 50% in South Carolina (Urban Institute). Millions will turn to Federally Qualified Health Centers, hospital charity care, and prescription assistance. Emily Sory of Tennessee planned to be uninsured in 2026, forced to make difficult tradeoffs.

Trump v. Slaughter: Presidential Firing Power (December 8)
Supreme Court heard oral arguments December 8 on Trump's firing of FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter. Justices seemed open to overruling 90-year Humphrey's Executor precedent preventing presidents from removing independent agency members at will. Trump appointed Slaughter in 2018 but fired her March 2025 without citing legal grounds ("inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance"). September: justices voted 6-3 to allow firing pending case. Win for Trump would give him far greater control over FTC, FCC, NLRB, and other independent agencies.
Immigration Judges Speech Case (December 19)
December 19: Supreme Court sided with immigration judges, rebuffing Trump administration in case with implications for federal workers. Justices let stand ruling raising questions about Trump's handling of federal workforce related to his firings of top complaint system officials. Court declined to quickly freeze ruling but said administration could return if lower courts moved too fast. Decision comes after Court has sided with Trump administration about two dozen times on emergency docket issues.
