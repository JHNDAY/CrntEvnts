For as long as American courts have existed, they have extended the federal government a quiet courtesy: the presumption of regularity. It instructs judges to assume, absent clear evidence otherwise, that executive officials have acted lawfully and in good faith. That courtesy is not symbolic — it narrows discovery, limits judicial review of agency reasoning, and shapes who wins close cases.

Since January 2025, that courtesy has been tested more openly than at any point in modern memory. A Just Security tracking project — featured on 60 Minutes — has now catalogued more than 200 instances in which federal judges voiced concern over the administration's noncompliance with orders, distrust of its representations, or arbitrary conduct. A federal judge in Maryland told a government lawyer outright: "You have taken the presumption of regularity, and you've destroyed it."

Yet the institutional story is not one-directional. The Supreme Court — the backstop of last resort — has handed the administration genuine defeats on tariffs, the National Guard, and birthright citizenship, while simultaneously dismantling a 90-year-old precedent that protected independent agencies from at-will removal. The picture that emerges is neither collapse nor business-as-usual, but an uneven, case-by-case contest whose outcome is still being written.

Deep dives
Removal power: from Wilcox to Slaughter
For ninety years, Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935) let Congress shield "quasi-legislative" or "quasi-judicial" agency heads — FTC, NLRB, MSPB — from being fired without cause, preserving a measure of independence from the White House. The second Trump administration tested that wall almost immediately, firing FTC commissioner Rebecca Slaughter, NLRB's Gwynne Wilcox, and MSPB's Cathy Harris within its first months.
"What text, history, and structure settle, our precedent confirms — the President may remove his subordinates at will."

— Chief Justice Roberts, Trump v. Slaughter majority opinion, June 29, 2026

Justice Kagan's dissent in the earlier Wilcox shadow-docket stay called the move an effective repeal of Humphrey's "by fiat." Notably, the Court has so far carved out the Federal Reserve and spared two other officials — Fed governor Lisa Cook and the Register of Copyrights — pending further litigation, leaving open exactly how far the new removal doctrine reaches.

"Presumption of irregularity" — how judges describe the shift
The phrase that gives this session its title was coined not by a critic but by a Trump-appointed Fifth Circuit judge, Andrew Oldham, dissenting in Alien Enemies Act litigation. His complaint — that the majority was treating the administration with extra suspicion — was meant as criticism of the courts. Legal commentators have since adopted his phrase approvingly, arguing it accurately names what has happened: courts that once gave the executive the benefit of the doubt are now doing the opposite, deliberately.
"You have taken the presumption of regularity, and you've destroyed it."

— U.S. District Judge Paula Xinis (D. Md.), addressing a DOJ attorney in open court

A Just Security study — now in its fourth edition, updated through March 19, 2026 — catalogs the pattern across three categories: court findings of non-compliance with orders, judicial distrust of government factual representations, and rulings that agency action was arbitrary or pretextual.

Tariffs and the power of the purse
Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump was, in the Court's own framing, the first case to reach the substantive merits of a major second-term Trump policy, rather than just resolving procedure or emergency relief. The administration had used IEEPA — a sanctions statute never before used to impose tariffs — to levy duties that pushed the average U.S. effective tariff rate to nearly 17%, the highest since the early 1930s.
"A tariff is a tax levied on imported merchandise" — and the taxing power belongs to Congress, not the President.

The administration's response illustrates the "whack-a-mole" pattern that recurs across these cases: within hours of losing on IEEPA, Trump pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, a narrower but still-available authority, capped at 150 days absent congressional extension — itself now also under legal challenge.

What "withstanding" looks like in practice
Two things are simultaneously true. District and circuit judges — across red and blue states, Republican and Democratic appointees alike — have been willing to call out noncompliance, contradictory representations, and pretextual reasoning in unusually blunt terms, and to use contempt referrals, sanctions, and disciplinary referrals when warranted. That is the system functioning as designed.

At the same time, the Supreme Court itself has been inconsistent: handing the administration major substantive losses on tariffs and birthright citizenship while, on a separate and arguably more structural question — who controls the machinery of the executive branch itself — siding decisively with expanded presidential power. Whether that asymmetry reflects principled doctrinal lines or a Court calibrating which fights to have is, itself, a fair question for the room.
