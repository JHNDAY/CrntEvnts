The Central Issue
President Trump has ordered military strikes killing 43 people suspected of drug smuggling on boats off South America's coast. Legal experts across the board say these extrajudicial killings are illegal and constitute murder under both domestic and international law.
43
People killed in 10 military strikes without due process
1
No Legal Justification Provided
The administration invokes terms like "self-defense" and "armed conflict" but has offered zero substantive legal analysis explaining how drug trafficking constitutes an armed attack warranting lethal military force. Even in closed congressional briefings, officials provide no detailed legal reasoning.
2
Reveals Structural Weakness of Law
This situation exposes that the rule of law in the White House depends primarily on norms rather than enforceable constraints‚Äîon lawyers willing to object and resign, and on presidents who want to appear law-abiding. With presidential immunity and partisan loyalty in Congress, these norms are the only remaining check.
Then vs. Now: Presidential Accountability

üïê Obama Administration (Drone Strikes)

Developed lengthy, detailed legal memos citing Supreme Court precedents. Wrestled with legal boundaries. Delivered speeches and white papers to Congress explaining their reasoning. Made efforts to justify controversial actions within existing legal frameworks.

‚ö° Trump Administration (Drug Boat Strikes)

Refuses to provide legal analysis. Acts with "audacious transparency" about the attacks themselves while remaining silent on legal justification. Simply asserts presidential "determination" that an armed conflict exists without explaining how drug smuggling meets that legal threshold.

3
The "Two-Part Hack" System
Trump has created a system that short-circuits legal analysis: (1) He declared that his and the Attorney General's legal opinions are "controlling on all employees," preventing lawyers from objecting. (2) He simply "determines" that factual/legal scenarios exist to justify extraordinary powers. Together, this creates a loophole where Trump dictates his own legal reality.
"Nixon tried to keep his criminality secret, and the Bush administration tried to keep the torture secret, and that secrecy acknowledged the norm that these things were wrong. Trump, as he often does when he is breaking law or norms, is acting publicly and without shame or unease. This is a very successful way to destroy the efficacy of law and norms."
‚Äî Jack Goldsmith, Harvard Law School Professor

Key Legal Questions Unanswered
Why is carrying drugs on a speedboat 1,500 miles from Florida an "imminent threat"? Why is cocaine trafficking (not even fentanyl) an "armed attack"? Why does crewing a drug boat make someone a targetable combatant rather than a criminal? The administration has provided no theory for transmuting acts of crime into acts of war.
5
Pattern of Presidential "Determinations"
This isn't isolated. Trump has used similar "determinations" to: nullify the TikTok ban despite Supreme Court approval; deport Venezuelan migrants to Salvadoran prisons; deploy federal troops to Democratic cities. He argues courts must defer to his assertions without reviewing whether they're factually accurate.
6
Get-Out-of-Jail-Free Cards
The Justice Department has produced a memo apparently blessing the killings. Even if it lacks proper legal analysis, its mere existence essentially forecloses future prosecutions‚Äîit's hard to prove someone intentionally committed a crime when DOJ said it was lawful at the time.
7
Impact on Service Members
Legal experts express concern about military personnel ordered to carry out these strikes. As one retired judge advocate stated: "The men and women who volunteered to serve this nation...have a right to know the nation will not order them to engage in that deadly endeavor unless it is genuinely justified both legally and morally."
