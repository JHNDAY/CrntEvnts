Judge Appears Receptive to Trump Demand for List of Penn's Jewish Faculty

A federal hearing tests how far the government can go in subpoenaing information about Jewish employees under the guise of an antisemitism investigation.

The New York Times Alan Blinder & Michael C. Bender March 10, 2026
Law & Courts Trump Administration Higher Education
Overview

A federal judge in Philadelphia signaled openness to the Trump administration's demand that the University of Pennsylvania hand over names and contact information for Jewish faculty, staff, and students — even as Jewish community members on campus warned the tactic echoes the list-making of Nazi-era Germany. No ruling was issued at the close of the hearing.

Key facts
The judge
Gerald J. Pappert — Obama-appointed, former Republican PA attorney general — said the government's case "seems to clear the low bar" for a valid subpoena.
The investigation
EEOC chair Andrea Lucas filed the original case in 2023, alleging a "pattern or practice" of antisemitic harassment at Penn, based largely on news reports and congressional testimony.
What's subpoenaed
Rosters of Jewish campus organizations, membership lists, Jewish Studies Program employee records, and notes from an antisemitism task force — all since Nov. 2022.
Penn's response
Called the subpoena unconstitutional and "disconcerting." Offered to notify employees directly — an offer the judge castigated as interfering with the investigation.
Broader stakes
The outcome will signal how aggressively the administration can pursue campus antisemitism inquiries nationwide. EEOC jurisdiction extends well beyond universities.
Timeline
Nov 2022 Scope of subpoena's records window begins — covering protests, complaints, and organizational activity.
2023 EEOC commissioner Andrea Lucas files the antisemitism case against Penn based on news reports and congressional testimony.
2024 Pro-Palestinian encampment on Penn campus heightens tensions; $175M federal funding paused over transgender swimmer dispute, later settled.
Mar 2025 EEOC requests organizational records and membership rosters. Penn balks. Commission issues formal subpoena with expanded demands.
Mar 10, 2026 Three-and-a-half-hour federal hearing in Philadelphia. Judge signals receptiveness. No ruling issued.
Voices from the courtroom

"We're not looking for a list of Jews or registry of Jews — how about a spreadsheet that identifies the contact information for potential victims and witnesses?"

Debra Lawrence · EEOC Philadelphia district counsel

"We know very well the history of governments assembling lists of Jews does not end well."

Jacob Naimark · Co-president, Penn Jewish Law Students Association

"I don't have a list, and I shouldn't have a list. There shouldn't be a list!"

Lorena Grundy · VP, Penn chapter of AAUP

"It's good for governments to be concerned about prejudice — but the tactics from the Trump administration have been too coercive."

Steven Weitzman · Director, Penn's Katz Center for Advanced Judaic Studies
Why it matters

The case crystallizes a central tension in the Trump administration's university campaign: using civil rights enforcement as a lever against institutions it views as ideologically hostile — while the very communities ostensibly being protected object to the methods being used on their behalf.

Perhaps most striking, the subpoena has temporarily unified a campus deeply divided by Gaza-related protests. As one faculty union leader put it: "The EEOC has managed to find the one thing in the last three years that at least everybody I've talked to agrees on." No one — Jewish or otherwise — wants the list released.

The EEOC's broad jurisdiction means a ruling in the government's favor wouldn't stop at Penn, or at universities. The same logic could be applied to any employer — making this hearing a bellwether for how far religious profiling can go in the name of protecting religious minorities.
