Remaking Liberalism with Daron Acemoglu

A Lecture at the Wheeler Institute for Business and Development

Delivered by Daron Acemoglu, Institute Professor of Economics at MIT and 2024 Nobel laureate, at the London Business School

The Crisis of Liberalism

Acemoglu argues that while liberalism has historically been responsible for major improvements in individual rights and rule of law 05:31, the system is now in crisis because it has failed to live up to its promises 09:18. He defines the liberalism he discusses as a "left liberalism," which includes a commitment to redistribution and helping the less well-off 06:05.

Historically, liberalism delivered on three key promises:

  • Shared Progress: Economic growth was coupled with a commitment to sharing that progress, as captured by the 19th-century Chartist slogan, "universal suffrage is a knife and fork question" 07:24.
  • Public Services: The early phases of democracy led to the development of better public services, including improved health, education, and infrastructure 07:58.
  • Voice: Protecting people's ability to speak and enabling them to participate in politics was a very important part of the agenda 08:51.

The Industrial Compact and Its Collapse

The success of post-war liberalism was tied to the "industrial compact" 10:14. This model relied on new technologies and expanding mass markets, which created a natural pathway for shared prosperity by necessitating the hiring of diverse labor, thereby pushing up wages across the board 10:33. This compact was supported by key institutions like trade unions and local self-government through democracy 11:50.

Two Primary Forces of Destruction

  • Computers and Automation: The shift to a "post-industrial economy" began with computers and was accelerated by digital technologies designed for automation. This process "severs the obvious link" between firm expansion and the need to hire more labor, instead displacing manual and routine clerical workers 18:59.
  • Post-Industrial Politics: The rise in the number and status of the college-educated created a divergence in values and social experiences between the educated elite and less-educated workers 1:22:12. This culminated in a change in political alignment, where the college-educated now predominantly vote for the center-left, and manual workers increasingly vote for the right 1:24:41.

The Liberal Elites' "Sins"

Acemoglu attributes the current crisis to 1:31:21:

  • Sins of Omission: Failing to develop strategies to deal with the technology-driven rise in inequality.
  • Sins of Commission: Adopting a "cultural politics agenda," often in coalition with the tech sector, which alienated non-educated, conservative communities, leading to a major backlash and a widespread feeling of being "voiceless" 1:31:39.

Remaking Liberalism

To address this crisis, Acemoglu advocates for a "working-class liberalism" that focuses on reconciliation and economic opportunity 1:32:41.

This new liberalism must:

  • Bring Community Back: Re-emphasize the importance of community and local self-government, which are essential for creating the trust and solidarity needed for "social knowledge" and collective adaptation 1:32:41.
  • Focus on Pro-Worker Progress: Shift the technological direction towards "pro-worker AI and technology" that creates new job opportunities for workers across skill levels, especially in expanding service sectors like healthcare and education 1:33:52.
  • Embrace "Cultural Economics": Accommodate the diverse values of different communities and allow for "community level experimentation" as long as fundamental individual freedoms are protected 1:35:50.

Acemoglu concludes that liberalism is "completely symbiotic with self-government" and that the path forward requires returning to these core republican-philosophical roots to build solidarity and cooperation 1:35:18.
