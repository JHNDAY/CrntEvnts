Operation Absolute Resolve: January 3 Strike
January 3, 2026: US launched 150+ aircraft strikes on Venezuela starting 2:00 AM local time, capturing President Nicolás Maduro and First Lady Cilia Flores. Delta Force conducted raid; both flown to USS Iwo Jima. Trump posted photo showing Maduro blindfolded, wearing Nike Tech sweatsuit, holding water bottle. Attorney General Pam Bondi announced indictments in Southern District of New York on narcoterrorism charges, superseding 2020 indictment. Trump at press conference: "We are going to run the country until such time as we can do a safe, proper and judicious transition." Strikes reported in Caracas, La Guaira, Miranda, and Aragua states. Maduro's Vice President Delcy Rodríguez denounced capture as kidnapping, elevated to acting president. Trump claimed "most stunning, effective and powerful displays of American military might" in US history, threatened "second and much larger attack."
Escalation Leading to Strike
December 30: Trump claimed first known US land strike on Venezuelan dock "used to load boats up with drugs"—marking shift from maritime to shore-based operations. December 30: Pacific strike killed 2 people; Southern Command described victims as "two male narco-terrorists." Total death toll from September through December: 107 people killed in 30 strikes on vessels. December seizure of "Skipper" oil tanker carrying Venezuelan crude in international waters—"largest ever seized." Naval historian called seizure "very unusual" and "provocative." US deployed USS Gerald R. Ford carrier and 15,000+ troops—largest regional military presence in decades. September: US began attacking alleged drug boats in Caribbean. Trump announced total blockade of sanctioned tankers entering/leaving Venezuela, declared Venezuelan airspace "closed in its entirety."
International and Domestic Response
Widespread international condemnation: Colombia's President Gustavo Petro rejected "aggression against sovereignty of Venezuela and Latin America," deployed military to Venezuelan border. Cuba's President Miguel Díaz-Canel called it "criminal attack" and "state terrorism." Brazil's Lula da Silva called for UN response; Uruguay, Bolivia, Trinidad and Tobago issued condemnations. Ecuador's President Daniel Noboa urged Venezuelan opposition to "reclaim your country." Within US: Venezuelan migrants in Doral, Florida, Santiago, Madrid, Lima, and Quito celebrated. Cúcuta, Colombia crowds danced in streets. Within Venezuela: CNN reported mixed reactions—celebrations and protests demanding Maduro's release. International affairs analyst Dominic Waghorn: capture was "unprecedented event" even accounting for past US removals of heads of state.
"Trump Corollary" to Monroe Doctrine
December 5 National Security Strategy introduced "Trump Corollary" emphasizing US "control" over Western Hemisphere politically, economically, commercially, and militarily. Trump at press conference characterized action as application of Monroe Doctrine, styled as "Donroe Doctrine": "American dominance in the western hemisphere will never be questioned again." Strategy warns migration flows will lead to "civilizational erasure" in Europe. Deputy Chief of Staff Stephen Miller and VP JD Vance framed mass migration as "theft of the American Dream." Secretary of State Marco Rubio's "Americas First" foreign policy pledged to end decades of US focus on other regions. Policy combines migration control, tariff threats, military deployment against alleged drug trafficking, rewarding political allies, cutting foreign aid, punishing adversaries. CSIS analysis: signals "Trump Corollary is not mere bluster."

Argentine President Javier Milei embraced Trump, won $20 billion peso support package boosting party in midterm elections. Colombian President Gustavo Petro initially rejected US deportation flights in January, reversed after Trump threatened punitive tariffs on flower exports. Mexico's President Claudia Sheinbaum and Brazil's Lula da Silva offered moderate compromises. US intervened in Brazil's domestic politics, attempted to meddle in Bolsonaro trial. March: Suriname's Albert Ramdin elected to lead OAS—opponent had signaled strong Trump alignment. Panama: Rubio threatened action if Hong Kong's CK Hutchison didn't sell port shares; company acquiesced month later. Regional GDP growth: 2.4% through 2025, ahead of IMF April projection of 2%. Foreign Policy: "The biggest single event to affect Latin American foreign policy this year was Trump's January inauguration."
Legal Questions and War Crime Allegations
Legal experts, human rights groups, Colombian and Venezuelan governments say strikes violate US and international law, amount to extrajudicial executions. Chatham House International Law Programme: Some authority exists for force to defend democracy abroad (African Union precedent), but only applies to counter-constitutional coups or clear election results refused. Venezuelan 2024 elections were "not free and fair" but insufficient to justify military force even under pro-democratic intervention standard. US Attorney General Pamela Bondi announced charges against Maduro and wife: "face full wrath of American justice on American soil in American courts." Trump claimed—without evidence—operations reduced maritime drug trafficking by 97%. Venezuelan government denies drug trafficking involvement, insists Washington seeks regime change to seize world's largest oil reserves. María Corina Machado (2025 Nobel Peace Prize recipient) managing "difficult balancing act" as Trump uses "increasingly aggressive tactics."

Affordability Crisis (149 mentions)
Wage growth hits 3.5% as inflation stays at 3%; 71% say income falls short of expenses
▼
Wage Growth at Multi-Year Low
November paycheck growth: lowest since May 2021. Average hourly wages $36.86, up only 3.5% annually—barely keeping pace with 3% inflation. RSM US chief economist Joe Brusuelas: "Wage growth is easing into sustained affordability crisis." Annual wage growth topped out at 5.9% in March 2022, has been steadily falling since. US economy lost jobs in 3 of past 6 months; 2025 on pace for worst job growth since pandemic (average 76,000 jobs/month vs. 2024's rate). Voluntary quit rates at 5-year low in October; employers have less incentive to give raises if not losing workers. Fed Chair Jerome Powell: "We are going to need to have some years where real compensation is higher for people to start feeling good about affordability issue." Thursday December CPI report expected to show annual inflation rose to 3.1%, further narrowing gap between paycheck growth and price increases.
Consumer Financial Stress Indicators
Conference Board December poll: consumers' views of current financial situation "collapsed" into negative territory for first time since July 2022 (month after pandemic-era inflation peaked). Marist poll (December, 1,400+ adults): 70% say cost of living in their area not very affordable or not affordable at all for average family. NPR/PBS News/Marist: Staggering 71% say income just matches or falls short of monthly expenses. 45% said high prices biggest economic worry vs. only 10% worried about job security. McKinsey Q4 2025 research: 29% dipped into savings in last 3 months (up from 26% Q3); 29% reduced savings rate (up from 26%); 28% using credit cards more (up from 26%). Credit card debt rose to collective $1.23 trillion in US as of Q3 2025 (New York Fed). 32% of Americans expect things to get worse in 2026 (Bankrate). Credit Karma survey: consumers hopeful about 2026 goals but many struggling.
Category-Specific Price Increases
CPI has risen 26% in past 6 years—about twice as fast as Fed's optimal rate (Mark Zandi, Moody's chief economist). Categories rising faster than overall inflation: housing (up 14% Sept 2023-Sept 2025), groceries (18% higher Sept 2025 vs. Jan 2022), medical costs (up nearly 7%), services (up over 8%), dining out, car repairs, used cars. November 2025 specifics: coffee prices up 19% from previous year, beef up around 15%, bananas up 7%. Almost half say it's harder to afford groceries than year ago (Axios/Harris Poll, September). Only 19% said food prices cheaper than year earlier. Roughly 14% of US households reported food insecurity Jan-Oct 2025, up from 12.5% in 2024 (Purdue). In NYC, 40% of families can't afford weekly food costs (Robin Hood/Columbia University). Utility costs: Americans now pay average $265/month in utilities, up 12% since last year (Century Foundation/Protect Borrowers). 124+ million Americans expected to see energy bill rate increases in 2025 (PowerLines).
Housing and Healthcare Costs
Almost 3/4 of Americans said housing has grown more unaffordable in communities in recent years (YouGov/U.Florida Center Oct poll). Mortgage rates stuck around 6.2% since September despite Fed cuts; expected to stay above 6% in 2026. Redfin: 75%+ of homes unaffordable for typical households. Rents may rise 2-3% year-over-year by end of 2026 (Redfin). Trump teased "most aggressive housing reform plans" in US history for 2026; National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett suggested focus on housing regulations. Health insurance: Enhanced ACA premium subsidies expired Dec 31, 2025. Without extension, low/mid-income households will see premiums rise from average $888 (2025) to $1,904 (2026) (KFF). Four out of five consumers found 2025 plans for $10 or less monthly—that ends Jan 1. Employer-based plans: costs likely to rise as much as 7% for 2026 plans (Mercer). 29% of voters delayed/skipped medical care in past year due to cost; 24% delayed/skipped buying doctor-prescribed medication (Century Foundation survey Oct 14-24).
Political Dimensions and Trump Response
Democrats won recent elections in NYC, New Jersey, Virginia by highlighting affordability issues. NPR/PBS/Marist: Only 36% approve of Trump's economic stewardship—lowest in 6 years, matching Biden's 2022 low. AP-NOPC December 11: 7 in 10 US adults described current state of US economy negatively. Trump December speech called "affordability" a "con job by the Democrats" despite it being issue No. 1 in poll after poll. Rep. MTG told Sean Spicer: Trump's "gaslighting the people and trying to tell them that prices have come down is not helping." Ben Shapiro: "When President Trump says things are becoming more affordable, people don't believe that because they aren't really more affordable." Trump claimed Dec 11: "prices are coming down tremendously" but inflation stood at 3% in September (same as January inauguration). Trump tariff agenda has pushed prices on essentials (bananas, coffee) and discretionary items (toys, electronics). Leanna Haakons (Black Hawk Financial): "Consumers balancing many pressures including tariffs, sticky inflation and softening labor market."
2026 Economic Outlook
Inflation should cool to about 2.4% in 2026 (Federal Reserve December forecast), but Fed projects only one additional rate cut for 2026 (down from earlier expectations). Fed Chair Powell: disinflation appears continuing in services; goods inflation "should come down in back half of 2026" as firms finish dividing tariff costs. Goldman Sachs December 22: US economic growth likely to accelerate in 2026 due to reduced "tariff drag," tax cuts, improved financial conditions. Projects 2.6% real GDP growth (higher than previously expected 2%). Hiring could improve: average payroll gains could rise to 70,000/month in 2026, more than double 2025's 32,000/month average. Wages projected to climb 2.3% in 2026 (accelerating from 1.9% in 2025). However: some economists warn job market could remain muted as companies turn to AI. Oxford Economics chief economist Michael Pearce: "I think it'll be better year. Tax cuts will be center stage, and we'll see broadening out of economic strength." But many households won't feel relief immediately: "2026 might not feel all that different than end of 2025—it's slow process that might take five or six years."

Cook (Federal Reserve): January 21, 2026 oral arguments. Whether Trump can fire Lisa Cook from Fed Board of Governors. October temporary ruling allowed Cook to remain on job. Federal Reserve Act: president can only remove members "for cause." Trump v. Slaughter (FTC): December 8 arguments completed. Whether Trump can fire FTC Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter and overturn 90-year Humphrey's Executor precedent. Win would give Trump far greater control over FTC, FCC, NLRB and other independent agencies.

ACA Enhanced Subsidies Expiration
Enhanced premium subsidies for 22 million Americans enrolled in ACA marketplace expired December 31, 2025. Congress failed to extend subsidies despite December votes. Shutdown ended without agreement to extend subsidies. December 11: Senate voted on ACA measures but neither Democrats' 3-year extension nor Republicans' alternative advanced. Without extension: average premiums will increase 75% for 2026 (previous reporting). KFF analysis: low/middle-income households previously qualifying for tax credits will see premiums rise from average $888 in 2025 to $1,904 in 2026. Four out of five consumers found 2025 plans for $10 or less monthly—that ends January 1. ACA marketplace premiums projected to rise by median of 18% nationwide in 2026—an 11 percentage point increase compared with 7% average annual growth rate seen from 2023 to 2025. Typical 40-year-old couple earning $82,000 annually (401% FPL) would see monthly premiums for silver plan nearly double—rising by $412, from $581 to $993 per month, resulting in annual increase of approximately $4,946.
Projected Coverage Losses
CBO estimates: without enhanced subsidies, 14+ million more uninsured by 2034. Combined with One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) cuts, 10-15 million could lose coverage. Johns Hopkins estimates up to 15 million more uninsured by 2034 from OBBBA alone. OBBBA includes over $1 trillion in healthcare cuts through 2034—"biggest cut to our social safety net in history" (Johns Hopkins). New sign-ups dropped considerably in Pennsylvania and California during enrollment period. 21 state-based exchanges saw 18% decrease in new customers to 210,500. Roughly 22 million people in 2025 had premiums reduced because of enhanced tax credits. Century Foundation survey October 14-24: 250 million Americans will face health insurance premium increases in 2026 far exceeding inflation, wage growth, and Social Security adjustments. Nearly 3 in 10 Americans (29%) report delaying or going without medical care in past year—37% of Hispanics, 33% of women, 32% of Black Americans. Among adults 18-29, nearly half (49%) delayed or skipped medical care. One in four people (24%) delayed or skipped buying doctor-prescribed medication in past year due to finances; women twice as likely as men (32% vs. 16%).
January 1, 2026 Changes
More frequent Medicaid redeterminations required. Work requirements for Medicaid expansion enrollees. Restrictions on lawfully present noncitizens: refugees, asylees, TPS recipients no longer eligible for ACA subsidies. DACA recipients ineligible for ACA Marketplace coverage. New enrollees must prove eligibility before receiving subsidies—no more 90-day grace period. Fiscal incentives for Medicaid expansion end, leaving 10 non-expansion states with less motivation. Medicaid gap in 10 states that have not expanded: Urban Institute projects uninsured rates to jump 65% in Mississippi, 50% in South Carolina. Millions will turn to Federally Qualified Health Centers, hospital charity care, prescription assistance programs. Emily Sory of Tennessee planned to be uninsured in 2026, forced to make difficult tradeoffs between healthcare and other necessities.
Systemic Healthcare Cost Impacts
Medicaid represents ~20% of hospital revenue nationwide. Johns Hopkins warning: "If hospitals see increase in uninsured people who can't pay bills, they're going to increase rates for other payers. It's not possible for 10-15 million people to lose health insurance and not have effects on rest of system." Employer-based health costs projected to rise as much as 7% for 2026 plans (Mercer). California attempted to mitigate: allocated $190 million to replace enhanced subsidies for lowest-income enrollees—far short of $2.5 billion in federal enhanced subsidies Californians received in 2025. Jeremy Tolbert (Kansas web developer): family's monthly premiums set to rise 18% to $2,600 in 2026. Planning to cut back in other areas: "Our food budget is going to go down—we're not talking about eating beans and rice, but going from comfortable middle-class lifestyle to eating how we did when we first got out of college."
