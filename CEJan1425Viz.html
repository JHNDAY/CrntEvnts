Current Events Discussion

January 14, 2025 Session

Participants: John Day, George Model, Hal, Susan Wagner, Ronald Marcus, Geoffrey Kent, Beth Davis, Ralph Loomis, Mark Seliber, Sally Fleschner, Francesca Nannetti

Session Focus: Trump's governance style, Minneapolis incident, Venezuela policy, media commentary analysis

Session Overview

This session examined Trump's governance through the lens of performative announcements versus substantive policy, with particular focus on the Minneapolis incident and its national impact. The group explored the growing influence of independent commentators and discussed strategies for accessing diverse perspectives while maintaining critical analysis.

Key themes included the contrast between announcement-driven governance and actual policy implementation, the intersection of law enforcement and political accountability, and the emerging role of Substack and independent media voices in shaping political discourse.

‚ñº
Trump's pattern of making splashy announcements that fade without follow-through, except for retribution and authoritarianism

John's Analysis

John presented his piece "Governance is the Art of Announcement," explaining how Trump follows one announcement after another, making a big splash, but soon the intended actions are lost and he's on to something else. This pattern is constantly visible in what he does, including the recent investigation of Powell that generated blowback.

"He makes this big splash, doesn't think about anything, just puts it out there... that'll just fall into the category of Act 5 of a bad play that he keeps constantly inflicting upon us."

The Exception

George identified two major exceptions to this pattern: "His retribution campaign and internal authoritarianism are unrelenting."

Incremental Progress

John acknowledged: "They continue to fail, except that he scores points here and there, and he makes incremental progress towards destroying our government. And each time, I'm sure he considers it to be a victory."

Discussion of the fatal shooting and its potential to become a watershed moment like Emmett Till

Historical Parallel

John drew a comparison to Emmett Till: "This terrible incident with the murder of the woman in Minneapolis is going to have some real repercussions and blowback. The mother insisted upon an open casket viewing of the body, and it really made an impression on the nation."

"This murder of this unfortunate woman may fall into that category. Been a lot of protest across the nation."

Victim Profile

George noted the complexity: "She was middle class and white. They're trying to leverage her being a lesbian as a justification."

The recording captured the victim saying to at least one officer: "Hey, I have no problem with you." Her wife's comment was merely "Hey, big boy, why don't you have another sandwich?" After the shooting, one officer said "effing bitch."

Political Response

Geoffrey: "Trump just called her disrespectful. Since when does disrespectful get you killed?"

Ronald highlighted the investigation focus: "They're saying they have to investigate her, the late woman, her wife. Not the officer, but the widow."

John noted the consequences: "As a result of that, 6 people in DOJ have resigned."

Broader Questions

Geoffrey raised the contradiction: "Why is it okay for U.S. military troops, or whatever you want to call these people, killing people, and yet in Iran, when they're doing the same thing, it's terrible?"

The New Feature

John introduced the section: "I started about a week or couple weeks ago this section called Commentators of Note. And it seems to be growing. The attempt here is simply to make what these authors write accessible and visible to us."

The portal gives links to recent articles from featured commentators like Jennifer Rubin, does comparative analysis of five commentators, and talks about what they focus on, how they approach stories, and why this is a good mix.

Current Featured Voices

  • Jennifer Rubin (Washington Post)
  • Joyce Vance (suggested by Francesca, added January 11)

The Challenge

Hal asked: "How about dissenting views? Is it unreasonable to put up the Fox people and all the rest, so you get some sense of that?"

George added: "Jennifer Rubin used to be an old-school conservative back in a more reasonable time. We don't really have a conservative voice in today's parlance. I can't think of one, but it might be good if we could come up with somebody who's somewhat reasonable."

John noted: "The Bulwark is populated by people who mostly used to be Republican operatives. They were active in the Republican Party during the Bush campaign. Tim Miller, Sarah Longwell, and others made the change when they were some of the few Republicans who really opposed what Trump was doing."

The Convert Problem

George identified the dilemma: "The trouble is, these recent converts, just like it is with religion, are the most zealously anti-Trump. So we really need pro-Trump voices as abhorrent as I find that to be."

Echo Chamber Concern

George earlier pointed out: "It's a bubble. We like to hear what Jennifer Rubin has to say... We know all the places where we're gonna like what they say. I don't think that the Trumpies read Heather Cox Richardson like I do every day."
