Governance

The Memo of Understanding (MOU), which gave Iran virtually everything they could have hoped for in exchange for minimal concessions, was ironically signed at Versailles, the same venue as was used for signing the treaty ending WWI. The harsh terms that the earlier treaty imposed on Germany are widely regarded to have fomented WWII. Moreover, Trump's self-congratulatory statements after the signing of the MOU evoke Neville Chamberlain's "Peace for Our Time" speech.

At the G7 summit with our six key (former?) allies, Trump stood out, in Sesame Street parlance, as "One of These Things is Not Like the Others". This was exemplified by a photo of Trump standing alone facing the camera while the other leaders were off to the side engaged with each other. Perhaps the demand for control of Greenland, the suggestion that Canada should become the 51st state, and frequent contretemps with the leaders of these countries have exacted a price. Andrew Tanenbaum commented on how our allies' leaders dealt with Trump, writing that they "now understand that if they treat Trump the way they would treat a 3-year-old, that helps a lot".

The 12-month CPI, which was 2.6% right before the 2024 election, and 2.9 or 3.0% as Biden was leaving office, had essentially stayed in that range until the start of the Iran War, but since then rose to 4.2% last month. Let's recall that Trump's promise to curb inflation greatly helped propel him to a second term. Trump responded to the sharp increase by saying "I love the inflation".

A YouGov poll taken last month found that 15% of respondents thought that the U.S. economy was getting better, 17% that it was staying about the same, and 63% that it was getting worse.

Notwithstanding the recently disclosed six meetings in the White House Situation Room last year to strategize on how to bring L'affaire Epstein to a close, widespread anger about it still lingers across the political spectrum. Last week, Trump attended Game 3 of the NBA Championship Series in NYC, requiring attendees, many paying $1,000s for their tickets, to arrive three hours early and tempering the public celebration in anticipation of the Knicks' ending their 53-year championship drought. When he appeared at the bullet proof window of the owner's box, Trump was roundly booed although he characterized this hostile reception as being "loud and enthusiastic, mostly cheers". Jennifer Rubin characterized his appearance at the game as a "big blubbery man, enraged at his ebbing power...an old man clearly in profound decline". Coming into the game, the Knicks had compiled a remarkable 13 straight wins, but they went on to lose this game to the Spurs before winning the next two games to secure the championship. Without Trump in attendance, the Knicks had a 15-0 record after game 3 of the first round, but with him present they were 0-1.

The extent of Trump's "winning" internationally, domestically and personally has not been prolific.

The Iran War

When I became aware of the salient details of the MOU to end the war, the word which immediately came to mind was "capitulation".

Here are the key details and a few comments with language from the document in italics:

  • The parties and their allies declare the immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon. Given that Israel is not a signatory and that Netanyahu's political and personal future is on the line, this may be difficult to comply with and could ultimately jeopardize the agreement. In this regard, as I was about to press "Send", Iran just announced that it was closing the Strait of Hormuz again in response to Israel's continuing attacks in Lebanon.
  • The two parties will refrain from interfering in each other's internal affairs. Given that the initial rationale given for the February 28 attack was to facilitate regime change, it is clear that this ambition has been thwarted.
  • A 60-day negotiation period has been set forth. Given the complexity of the nuclear issue, 60 days is a mere pittance.
  • The U.S. will end its blockade and Iran will open the Strait of Hormuz with no charge for 60 days only.
  • The U.S. and its partners will develop a plan to make $300B available to Iran for reconstruction and economic development. By way of contrast, the U.S. provided Iran $1.7B (the so-called "palettes of cash") under the JCPOA to settle a pre-existing economic dispute over undelivered arms purchased from the U.S.
  • The U.S. undertakes to terminate all kinds of sanctions against Iran. The sanctions related to human rights, ballistic missiles and support for militants had remained intact under the JCPOA.
  • The Islamic Republic of Iran reaffirms that it shall not pursue or develop nuclear weapons. This language is nearly identical to that in the 2015 JCPOA which Trump withdrew from in 2018.
  • The U.S. will make fully available for use the frozen or restricted funds and assets to Iran after the MOU is signed. The U.S. holds roughly $2B in frozen liquid assets and $50B of real estate-related assets, with a like amount held by other countries worldwide. The $104B combined total compares to the $50–$100B of assets made available under the JCPOA. If we use the $75B midpoint and account for 40% inflation, the combined funds made available under the two arrangements are nearly identical.

Comparing the MOU to the JCPOA, the major differences are then the establishment of the $300B fund and full vs. partial sanctions relief. Let's also bear in mind that over the course of the last eight years that Iran has significantly increased its supply of highly enriched uranium and made other progress toward nuclear weapons. Moreover, the JCPOA provided for intensive oversight of Iran's nuclear activities by the International Atomic Energy Agency which found Iran to largely be in compliance during the short life of the agreement. Now a detailed oversight process needs to be developed before any new agreement is finalized.

The war has cost the U.S. a disclosed $132B so far, probably considerably more, and resulted in 13 U.S. military deaths and 400 other casualties, with Iran suffering 3,500 deaths and Israel 26 more. Had Trump not withdrawn from the JCPOA, Iran would not have made eight years of progress toward developing a nuclear weapon, there may well have been no war, and Iran would not have access to a $300B "reconstruction and development fund". It appears that the deal outlined in the MOU will clearly be less favorable for the U.S. than the JCPOA was.

It took 20 months to develop the 18-page JCPOA agreement whereas the MOU is only 1 1/2 pages. A lot of work remains to be done and it is especially vexatious that prior to our attack on February 28, good progress had apparently been made toward resolving the nuclear issue. During the signing event, Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei (or his surrogate) said that Trump was signing the MOU "out of desperation" and that Iran would not yield to "excessive demands" during the forthcoming negotiations. The next day, Trump posted in reaction to bipartisan U.S. and international criticism "These fools, who said I haven't been tough enough on Iran, when the Stock Market just hit a RECORD HIGH, and oil prices are 'tumbling' down, are either jealous, bad people, or stupid". Apparently the two leaders use different metrics to measure success.

Election Results and Commentary

Let's look at election results and emerging information over the course of the last two weeks.

Here are recent key primary results:

  • The results for the 6/02 CA jungle primaries are virtually complete and the runoff candidates have been identified. In the gubernatorial primary, Democrat Xavier Becarra got 28.1% of the vote, Trump-endorsed Republican Steve Hilton got 24.7%, and Democrat Tom Steyer and $215MM of his money got 22.8%. All told, the Democrats got 62% of the vote compared to 58.5% in the 2024 presidential election. The Trump claim that Hilton would be eliminated because of massive electoral fraud was not borne out. The basic disconnect here is that CA wants to count each and every vote whereas Trump channels Stalin who said "It doesn't matter how many people vote, only who counts them". In the LA mayoral primary, two Democrats did make the runoff as incumbent Karen Bass got 34.1% of the vote, progressive Nitha Raman 29% and Republican reality TV entertainer Spencer Pratt 25.5%.
  • In the ME Democratic primary, Graham Platner got 72% of the vote and will oppose 5-term Republican incumbent Susan Collins. Although Platner's outsider status and populist appeal are formidable, he will have to deal with a plethora of unfavorable disclosures. Per the Globe's James Pindell, Platner faces a "purgatory summer". This said, Platner leads Collins by an average of 4% in the latest polls.
  • In the SC Republican gubernatorial primary, Lt. Governor Pamela Evette will face AG Alan Wilson in the runoff. Representative Nancy Mace, a leader in the call for the release of the Epstein documents, only finished fifth.
  • In NV, Democrat AG Aaron Ford will be running for governor against Republican incumbent Joseph Lombardo.
  • In the GA Republican senatorial primary, Trump-endorsed Representative Mike Collins defeated Brian Kemp-endorsed former football coach Derek Dooley by 10%. He will be running against incumbent Democrat Jon Ossoff. Former Republican strategist Sarah Longwell said "if you were cooking up an ideal 2028 (presidential) candidate in a lab, he would probably look a lot like Jon Ossoff". After the primaries, Cook has changed its Toss-up rating to Leans D. In the Republican gubernatorial runoff, however, Trump-endorsed Lt. Governor Burt Jones lost to businessman Rick Jackson. Jackson will run against former Atlanta Mayor Keisha Lance Bottoms in the general election. In the Republican runoff for Secretary of State, Brian Kemp aide Tim Fleming beat 2020 election denier and former Democrat Vernon Jones. Turning to national political information:

  • Nate Cohn's analysis for the NYT indicates that the Democrats will need to win the national popular House vote by 3.1% or more to take control of the chamber. The Silver Bulletin today has the Democrats ahead by 6.2%, which compares to a 6.8% lead for at this point in 2018.
  • Kyle Kondik of the UVA Center for Politics has looked into the impact of redistricting. In all, ten states, including CA and TX, changed their House maps this year and 42% of the House races will be affected. Although the number of solid Red and Blue districts has hardly changed, the number of competitive districts in his analysis has been reduced from 85 to 70, and the number of highly competitive ones from 26 to 21. The quid pro quo is that the number of somewhat competitive districts, where either party has between a 5–10% advantage has increased by 32, 22 Republican ones and 10 Democratic. This is what happens when voters are shifted around to gain advantage under gerrymanders. Given a Blue wave election, some of these new moderately competitive districts could swing to the Democrats. Taking all of this into account, except perhaps the wave dynamic, Kondik predicts that the Republicans will net 5–9 additional seats from their shenanigans.
  • An AP/NORC poll indicates that support for Trump among Independents has declined from 43% during the 2024 campaign to 26%.
  • A CNN poll indicates that voter identification is now 27% Democratic, 26% Republican and 47% Independent.
  • According to Cornell Belcher, 81% of likely Black voters are highly motivated for the midterms.
  • An NBC News poll found Trump's approval among Latinos was underwater at 34%/64% and among 18–29 year-olds at 21%/77%.
  • Republican strategist Rob Stutzman opined about Republicans "It's a party without anyone, without the leader, on message".

Some Depressing Numbers
I

According to the CBO, U.S. deaths will exceed births for the first time in our history by 2030, ten years earlier than thought. This reflects our fertility rate having declined to 1.53 births per woman.

II

The Social Security retirement trust fund (OASI) is projected to face a funding shortfall in late 2032, a year earlier than projected last year and exacerbated by our declining birth rate. The Social Security Board of Trustees estimates that incoming taxes will only be sufficient to cover 78% of scheduled benefits from that point on.

III

In the first quarter of 2026, the employee compensation share of U.S. Gross Domestic Income was 51%, which compares to 58% in 2001.
