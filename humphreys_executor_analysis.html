Humphrey's Executor v. United States

Presidential Removal Power and Independent Agencies

A Balanced Analysis of Constitutional Authority and Institutional Independence

The Landmark Case

Humphrey's Executor v. United States, 295 U.S. 602 (1935)

Decision: Unanimous (9-0)

Justice: George Sutherland

Holding: Congress may limit the President's power to remove commissioners of independent regulatory agencies to removal "for cause" only.

The Facts

In 1933, President Franklin D. Roosevelt attempted to remove William E. Humphrey from the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) because Humphrey, a conservative Republican appointed in 1931, opposed Roosevelt's New Deal policies. The FTC Act allowed removal only for "inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office." When Humphrey refused to resign, Roosevelt fired him anyway. Humphrey died five months later, and his estate sued for back pay.

Central Question: Can Congress constitutionally restrict the President's power to remove members of independent regulatory commissions, or does the President possess unlimited removal authority over all executive branch officers?

The Court's Reasoning

"We think it plain under the Constitution that illimitable power of removal is not possessed by the President in respect of officers of the character of those just named. The authority of Congress, in creating quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial agencies, to require them to act in discharge of their duties independently of executive control cannot well be doubted."
Humphrey's Executor v. United States, 295 U.S. 602 (1935)

The Court distinguished between:

  • Purely Executive Officers (e.g., postmasters) - subject to unlimited presidential removal
  • Quasi-Legislative/Quasi-Judicial Officers (e.g., FTC commissioners) - may be protected by for-cause removal provisions

Evolution of Removal Power Doctrine

1926

Myers v. United States

The Supreme Court held that the President has unlimited power to remove purely executive officers. Chief Justice William Howard Taft wrote that removal power is essential to the President's constitutional duty to execute the laws.

Wikipedia - Humphrey's Executor
1935

Humphrey's Executor v. United States

Unanimously ruled that Congress can limit presidential removal of independent agency heads to "for cause" only. Distinguished quasi-legislative/judicial agencies from purely executive ones.

Justia - Full Text
1958

Wiener v. United States

Reinforced Humphrey's Executor, holding that even without explicit statutory protection, the President cannot remove officials from agencies requiring independence.

Center for American Progress
1988

Morrison v. Olson

By 7-1, upheld for-cause protections for inferior officers. Justice Scalia dissented, arguing for unlimited presidential removal power based on unitary executive theory.

SCOTUSblog Analysis
2010

Free Enterprise Fund v. PCAOB

Struck down "dual for-cause" removal protections as unconstitutional, but left Humphrey's Executor intact for single-layer protections.

Constitution Annotated
2020

Seila Law v. CFPB

By 5-4, narrowed Humphrey's Executor, holding that single-director agencies wielding substantial executive power cannot have for-cause protections. Preserved exception for multimember commissions.

Cornell Law - Unitary Executive Theory
2021

Collins v. Yellen

Similar to Seila Law, struck down for-cause protections for single-director Federal Housing Finance Agency.

Cornell Law
2025

Trump Administration Removals

President Trump fired multiple independent agency commissioners without cause, leading to legal challenges. Cases pending before the Supreme Court include Trump v. Slaughter (FTC) and Trump v. Cook (Federal Reserve).

Wikipedia - Recent Developments
December 2025

Trump v. Slaughter - Oral Arguments

Supreme Court heard arguments on whether FTC commissioners' removal protections are constitutional. Decision could overturn or narrow Humphrey's Executor after 90 years.

Arguments For Unlimited Presidential Removal Power

The Unitary Executive Theory Perspective

1. Constitutional Text - Article II Vesting Clause

The Constitution states "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States." Proponents argue this language grants all executive power to the President alone, making removal authority inherent in the office.

"Article II grants the President conclusive and preclusive power to remove executive officers."
Solicitor General's Brief, Trump v. Slaughter
Wikipedia - Unitary Executive Theory

2. Democratic Accountability

The President is the only nationally elected official (along with the Vice President) and should be accountable to voters for all executive branch actions. If the President cannot control agency officials, there is no democratic accountability.

  • Voters elect the President to implement their policy preferences
  • Unelected bureaucrats should not make policy independent of the elected President
  • Clear lines of authority make government more responsive to citizens
The Regulatory Review

3. Take Care Clause

Article II requires the President to "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." Proponents argue this duty cannot be fulfilled if the President cannot remove subordinates who fail to execute laws properly.

"The President's removal power is the rule, not the exception."
Chief Justice John Roberts, Seila Law v. CFPB (2020)
Wikipedia

4. Separation of Powers

Independent agencies exercising executive power but insulated from presidential control violate the constitutional separation of powers by creating a "headless fourth branch" of government.

  • The Constitution creates only three branches, not four
  • Executive power must reside in the executive branch under presidential control
  • Congressional limits on removal improperly intrude on executive authority
Holland & Knight Analysis

5. Efficiency and Unified Policy

A single executive ensures coherent, unified policy implementation rather than conflicting actions from independent agencies pursuing their own agendas.

  • Reduces bureaucratic gridlock and conflicting regulations
  • Enables rapid response to changing circumstances
  • Prevents agencies from being captured by special interests
Public Knowledge

6. Historical Practice - The Decision of 1789

Early Congresses, including many Framers, debated and affirmed presidential removal power during the creation of executive departments in 1789. This represents an early constitutional interpretation.

Stanford Law Review - Historical Analysis

7. Modern Administrative State Problems

Independent agencies have grown far beyond what the 1935 Court envisioned, now wielding enormous regulatory, enforcement, and quasi-judicial powers that affect every aspect of American life.

"The CFPB Director exercised executive power, possessing the authority to promulgate binding rules fleshing out 19 federal statutes, issue final decisions awarding legal and equitable relief in administrative adjudications, and to seek daunting monetary penalties in enforcement actions."
Seila Law v. CFPB (2020)
Yale Journal on Regulation

Arguments For Limited Removal Power / Agency Independence

Defending Humphrey's Executor and Independent Agencies

1. Congressional Authority Under Article I

Article I grants Congress broad power to create offices and agencies. This includes authority to structure agencies in ways that serve their statutory purposes, including removal protections.

"The authority of Congress, in creating quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial agencies, to require them to act in discharge of their duties independently of executive control cannot well be doubted."
Humphrey's Executor v. United States (1935)
The Regulatory Review

2. Expertise and Apolitical Decision-Making

Independent agencies are staffed by nonpartisan experts who make decisions based on technical knowledge rather than political pressure. Removal protections enable evidence-based policy.

  • Technical fields (finance, communications, consumer safety) require specialized expertise
  • Protection from political pressure enables unpopular but necessary decisions
  • Long-term planning beyond election cycles
  • Consistent enforcement of laws regardless of administration
Constitutional Accountability Center

3. Preventing Politicization of Critical Functions

Some government functions—like election administration, civil service protection, and financial regulation—must be insulated from partisan politics to maintain public trust.

"The legitimacy of American democracy—and the public's confidence in the democratic system—comes from the belief that our elections are protected from powerful political figures who would benefit by manipulating them."
Campaign Legal Center
  • Federal Election Commission must be nonpartisan
  • Merit Systems Protection Board protects federal employees from political retaliation
  • Federal Reserve can make economically sound but politically unpopular decisions
Center for American Progress

4. Stability and Predictability

Removal protections and staggered terms prevent complete policy reversals with each new administration, providing stability for regulated industries and the public.

  • Businesses can plan based on consistent regulatory frameworks
  • Prevents whipsaw policy changes every four years
  • Institutional memory and continuity of expertise
Constitutional Accountability Center

5. Historical Precedent and Reliance

Independent agencies have existed since the Interstate Commerce Commission (1887). The modern administrative state and economy have been built around this 140-year structure.

"For almost 140 years, removal protections have been the hallmark of independence for multimember commissions like the Federal Trade Commission."
Lawfare

Overturning this framework would create massive disruption and uncertainty.

Notre Dame Law Review

6. Founding-Era Practice Supports Limitations

Historical research shows early Congresses created independent structures and used removal protections, contradicting unitary executive claims.

  • Sinking Fund Commission included Vice President and Chief Justice not removable by President
  • Early Congresses used multimember decision-making structures
  • First and Second Banks of the United States had removal restrictions
Notre Dame Law Review - Historical Analysis

7. Protecting Against Abuse of Power

Independent agencies serve as checks on presidential power, preventing corruption and ensuring laws are enforced even when politically inconvenient.

"For it is quite evident that one who holds his office only during the pleasure of another cannot be depended upon to maintain an attitude of independence against the latter's will."
Humphrey's Executor (1935)
  • Prevents presidents from using agencies to target political opponents
  • Enables investigation of executive branch misconduct
  • Protects whistleblowers and ensures accountability
Public Knowledge

8. Balanced Accountability

Independent agencies remain accountable through multiple mechanisms beyond presidential removal:

  • Congressional oversight and appropriations
  • Judicial review of agency actions
  • Public participation in rulemaking
  • Presidential appointment power (with Senate confirmation)
  • Removal "for cause" for genuine misconduct
Columbia Law Review

9. "For Cause" Still Permits Removal

The standard removal language ("inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office") already permits presidents to remove officials who fail to faithfully execute the law—it just prevents purely political firings.

"Neglect of duty and malfeasance in office, as traditionally interpreted, encompass what we call a failure of 'faithful execution': the official misbehavior that the Take Care Clause purportedly obliges the President to prevent."
Columbia Law Review
Columbia Law Scholarship

Current Legal and Political Status (2025-2026)

⚠️ Critical Juncture

The 90-year-old precedent of Humphrey's Executor is under direct challenge. The Supreme Court is currently considering cases that could fundamentally reshape the structure of the federal government.

Pending Supreme Court Cases

Trump v. Slaughter (FTC Commissioner)

  • Oral Arguments: December 8, 2025
  • Issue: Whether statutory removal protections for FTC commissioners violate separation of powers, and whether Humphrey's Executor should be overruled
  • Status: Rebecca Slaughter was removed by President Trump in March 2025 for policy disagreements. Lower courts ruled removal illegal; Supreme Court stayed the injunction
  • Stakes: Could affect 24+ independent agencies with similar structures
Lawfare Analysis

Trump v. Cook (Federal Reserve Governor)

  • Oral Arguments: January 21, 2026
  • Issue: Whether Federal Reserve governors' removal protections are constitutional
  • Unique Factor: Conservative justices have expressed special concern about Federal Reserve independence
  • Potential Outcome: Court might create exception for Federal Reserve while limiting other agencies
SCOTUSblog

Trump v. Wilcox (NLRB Member)

  • Status: Gwynne Wilcox became the first NLRB member ever fired since the board's creation in 1935
  • Significance: Tests removal protections for labor agencies
Wikipedia

Other Recent Removals

  • Hampton Dellinger - Office of Special Counsel
  • Cathy Harris - Merit Systems Protection Board Chair
  • Susan Grundmann - Federal Labor Relations Authority Chair
  • Alvaro Bedoya - FTC Commissioner
Constitutional Accountability Center

What Oral Arguments Revealed

Conservative Majority Skepticism: During December 2025 oral arguments in Slaughter, Chief Justice Roberts called Humphrey's Executor "just a dried husk," suggesting the conservative majority is prepared to narrow or overturn it.
Federal News Network

Key Questions from Oral Arguments

  • Scope: How far would the ruling extend? Would it affect all 24+ independent agencies?
  • Federal Reserve: Is the Fed different from other agencies and deserving of special protection?
  • Congressional Power: Can Congress restructure executive departments as independent agencies to evade presidential control?
  • Workable Standards: What distinguishes "executive power" from "quasi-legislative/quasi-judicial" functions?
PBS NewsHour

Possible Outcomes

Narrow Ruling

  • Court could rule on FTC specifically without overturning Humphrey's Executor broadly
  • Could create exception for Federal Reserve
  • Might maintain protections for some agencies based on their functions

Broad Ruling

  • Complete overturn of Humphrey's Executor
  • All multimember commission protections eliminated
  • Fundamental restructuring of administrative state
  • Massive legal and operational uncertainty

Potential Impacts

Political

Increased presidential power over entire federal bureaucracy; potential for partisan control of previously independent functions

Economic

Uncertainty in financial markets; changes to Federal Reserve independence could affect monetary policy credibility

Regulatory

Policy whipsaws every 4-8 years; reduced long-term planning by businesses; potential regulatory capture

Legal

Thousands of agency actions potentially subject to challenge; questions about past decisions made by now-removable officials

Institutional

Loss of expertise as career officials replaced with political appointees; reduced bipartisan cooperation

Democratic

Questions about election administration, civil service protections, and checks on executive power

"The fate of the administrative state turns on the 1935 case Humphrey's Executor v. United States."
Yale Journal on Regulation
