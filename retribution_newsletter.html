President Trump executed a sweeping purge of government oversight on January 24, 2025, firing at least 17 inspectors general across multiple federal agencies in what critics dubbed a "Friday night coup." The dismissals, which came via email citing "changing priorities," removed independent watchdogs from agencies including the Department of Defense, State Department, HHS, Veterans Affairs, and Transportation. Eight of the fired inspectors general have since filed a federal lawsuit challenging the legality of their terminations, arguing that Trump violated federal law by failing to provide Congress with the required 30-day notice.

Trump's campaign to intimidate major law firms has extracted nearly $1 billion in pledged pro bono services from nine of America's most prestigious firms, including Kirkland & Ellis, Latham & Watkins, and Paul Weiss. Using executive orders to threaten security clearance revocations, contract cancellations, and building access bans, Trump successfully coerced firms that previously represented his critics. However, four firms—Perkins Coie, Jenner & Block, WilmerHale, and Susman Godfrey—are fighting back in court and winning. The corporate capitulation has backfired spectacularly, with at least 11 major companies including Microsoft, Oracle, and McDonald's shifting legal work away from firms that "bent the knee," questioning whether firms that couldn't defend themselves could effectively represent clients.
