Topic II · Extended Exchange
The $1.776 Billion Weaponization Fund: "Utterly Stupid, Morally Wrong"
What the Fund Is — and Isn't
The session's first major flashpoint: Trump's proposed $1.776 billion "weaponization fund" — a DOJ settlement vehicle intended to compensate January 6th participants, including those convicted of crimes. The fund was never part of the Big, Beautiful Bill legislation; it was an executive action, never debated or voted on. When the White House's Todd Blanch presented it to the Republican Senate conference, the meeting reportedly degenerated into a yelling match. The Senate refused to vote on the measure before Memorial Day — a significant act of pushback.
Republican Reaction
The condemnations came from across the Republican aisle. Mitch McConnell: "The nation's top law enforcement official is asking for a slush fund to pay people who assault cops. Utterly stupid, morally wrong." Ron Johnson called it "a galactic blunder and a giant mistake." Tillis: "Stupid on stilts." Even Ted Cruz and Mike Johnson reportedly weighed in critically. John's read: "These people haven't suddenly found civic duty. They're afraid for their jobs."
The Deeper Logic: Rewriting January 6th
John tied the fund to a larger pattern: Trump pardoned the J6 participants, then called them "J6 Patriots," and now wants to pay them. "He's trying to rewrite history and make people believe that what they were doing was really patriotic." Mary Trump's book title — Too Much and Never Enough — was cited as the essential character sketch: always had too much, but it was never enough. The group debated whether the fund could be implemented without congressional approval. Alan's assessment: Trump will take the money from an existing settlement account and argue legality after the fact. Beth's observation: "By the time it gets through the Supreme Court, it will already be a fact."

The Two GOP Bets on the Big, Beautiful Bill

Republican Calculation
Voters will feel the tax cuts before they feel the Medicaid losses — which are strategically delayed. If the narrative can be held long enough, the bill becomes a political asset.
Democratic Counter-Bet
4.5 million people have already lost healthcare coverage. Gas station signs and grocery receipts don't lie. Medicaid losses will be felt viscerally — and voters will hold Republicans accountable, regardless of what Newsmax says.
Topic III · Extended Exchange
Iran, the War, and a President 22 Points Underwater
Michael's Nuclear Ledger
Michael Berger — ten months in remission from lymphoma, and still the group's sharpest voice on nuclear affairs — laid out what was lost when Trump abandoned the Kerry agreement in 2018. The original deal held uranium enrichment to 3.5%, monitored by the UN's Atomic Energy Agency, with a 15-year sunset clause. Trump's proposed replacement sets a 20-year ceiling. The original deal was, in Michael's view, the worst presidential decision of modern times — and the current war the predictable consequence. As of two weeks ago, Iran has retained 70–75% of its missile launchers and manufacturing capacity. 228 U.S. aircraft and structures have been destroyed, a figure the administration refuses to acknowledge.
George's Ledger: Edifice Rex & the Nero Parallel
George opened with a sustained set piece. Trump, pressed on anything, retreats to his vanity projects — rebuilding the National Mall, reflective pool, Arc of Triumph renamed Arc of Trump. George's classical frame: Oedipus Rex (Edifice Rex), and Nero fiddling while Rome burned. The Nero version landed as a group touchstone: "Trump fiddles with vanity projects while the economy and our international reputation burns." Jennifer Rubin's framing was also cited: Trump is making America "the dispensable country" — with China's influence growing and Europe becoming genuinely independent.
The Polling Numbers
New York Times–Siena poll: Trump 22 points underwater. Even on the Times' averaged model (which includes Rasmussen), he is 20 points below water. With voters under 29: 20% approval, 76% disapproval. With Hispanics — a group where he won 45% in 2024 — approval is now roughly 20%, disapproval 71%. Generic congressional ballot: Democrats +11%. In 2018, Democrats won 40 House seats on an 8-point advantage. George noted that gerrymandering since then complicates the math — but the direction of travel is unmistakable. Walter Reed: Trump was hospitalized the day of the session, "even though he's primed to live to age 200."
"Trump always makes America look embarrassing." TAMALE — George's political acronym of the session
Topic IV · Extended Exchange
2026 Midterms: Democratic Strategy, the Corruption Message, and What Happens If They Win
The Corruption Message
Alan argued that corruption — not just economic pain — is starting to break through to even nominal Trump supporters, and that Democrats should keep pounding it. George's caution: Republicans will draw a false equivalence with Biden's record. Alan's counter: "Old stories fade away in a world where Trump gives us a new story every day, or several a day. And the magnitude is so dramatically different." John's economic addendum: voters fill their tanks every week. They don't get their gas from Newsmax.
The Populist Opening
John on Democratic positioning: the serpentine survival instincts of Trump should not be underestimated, but the headwinds are real. If Democrats push populist issues artfully — high costs, broken promises, the Iran war, the Epstein files — "they're in very good shape." Reference was made to a candidate in Maine running a red-blooded populist campaign, and to the New York mayor. "You're starting to see them on the Democratic side." The crucial step: shed the elitist persona and the over-the-top progressive associations that made the party a sitting duck.
Trump's All-Women Cabinet Purge
Michael raised a striking data point: of the cabinet members Trump has fired, all four — Kristi Noem, Tulsi Gabbard, Pam Bondi, and Chavez DeRuiner — were women. Three female cabinet members remain, all in departments Trump wants to abolish or marginalize (Education, Agriculture, Small Business). Not one man has been fired. Michael's concern: the female Republican electorate has shown zero visible reaction. Michael's broader prescription: Democrats need a Tony Blair — someone who can reinvent and reinvigorate the party with a credible governing promise, not just opposition.
What If Democrats Win? Hal's Warning
Hal Shear articulated the group's sharpest forward-looking concern: if Democrats take the House, they may spend the entire two years on impeachment — and thereby squander the chance to actually legislate. Mark Seliber agreed that with a veto-wielding Trump still in the White House through 2028, the practical ceiling on legislation is low regardless. Hal's longer horizon: looking toward 2028, structural reforms — expanding the House (capped at 435 since 1929, now 775,000 constituents per seat), Supreme Court expansion, immunity reform — need to be part of the public conversation before the election, not after.
Alan's "Golden Opportunity" Question
Alan's question, first raised many months ago, resurfaced: assuming Democrats get full political control, how do you actually unwind the mess? John's measured answer: a mix of restoring order and pursuing accountability on the most egregious abuses — not as a vendetta, but as basic institutional maintenance. Larry's reform proposal: Congress should revisit the rules on executive orders, given how dramatically they've been abused. John added that the presidential immunity precedent, set by the current Supreme Court, urgently needs revisiting. George's wry summary: the Trump administration has used artillery pieces as weaponization; the Democrats would only need peashooters. "There are so many rich targets."

Israel & Gaza: Sally's Dispatch Before Boarding
The Kristof Article and What It Documents
Sally Fleschner, calling in from her airport gate with earbuds, en route to Bosnia, brought the group's attention to a Nicholas Kristof piece documenting alleged abuses of Palestinian detainees, including accounts from 14 people, some involving sexual violence. Sally noted an earlier, separately documented incident: five guards who videotaped themselves assaulting a Palestinian prisoner were arrested; the prosecutor who released their own video as evidence subsequently lost her job. "That tells me this practice may be fairly prevalent — and nobody seems interested in stopping it." Hal urged caution about sourcing on specific allegations; John acknowledged that some of the most extreme claims appeared thinly documented.
The Political Reaction — and the Flotilla
Sally's central frustration: Israeli officials are not denying the reports so much as dismissing them as blood libel or antisemitism — "they're not taking it seriously." The military's interception and detention of flotilla protesters in Greek waters was cited as another instance. President Herzog — whose role is largely ceremonial — has publicly excoriated some of these behaviors; the Netanyahu–Ben Gvir coalition shows no sign of changing course. Alan noted that Israeli elections are scheduled for October. Chris Van Hollen had an op-ed in the Times the same day arguing Democrats must reconsider unconditional military support for Israel.
Topic V · Closing Presentation
What AI Can't Do — and Why the Liberal Arts Are Rising
The Maureen Dowd Thread
George had flagged a series of Maureen Dowd columns on AI's relationship to the liberal arts; John built a short presentation around the theme. The central argument: demand for programmers has declined sharply as AI handles more and more of the technical implementation. What is rising, counterintuitively, is demand for people fluent in language, literature, history, and interpretation — people who know what the right questions are, who can read and compare different types of text, and who understand how to direct a powerful tool toward a meaningful problem. "We're cutting out the middleman. AI does the heavy lifting of the programming, and people in literature, history, psychology are doing the work of using the tool to solve problems." The title — What AI Can't Do — also echoed Kant and the ethics of autonomous judgment.
Hal's Reaction and Anthropic's Leadership
Hal Shear offered the session's most expansive praise: John's earlier AI material was "just first rate — a great, in-depth look at everything." The Kant reference had been good enough that Hal shared it with a bioethics group he facilitates. What had genuinely astonished him was the Anthropic leadership story: the CEO (Dario Amodei) and his sister as second-in-command, with her husband leading the foundation. "I was just absolutely dumbstruck." John filled in the biography: middle-class origins in the Bay Area, father a leather artisan, PhD in biophysics, departure from OpenAI over the direction Sam Altman was taking the company, and Anthropic's subsequent meteoric rise. Hal added: they have committed 80% of their success to philanthropic use. John's closing note: the VP of Anthropic holds an English degree — "a strategic decision on the part of Anthropic's leaders."
George's Data Point on STEM Salaries
George offered a corroborating data point: salary studies for college graduates show engineers doing well, but mathematicians and hard-science graduates performing no better — sometimes worse — than communications and humanities majors. "My mathematics confreres didn't do well at all." The transition is real, even if not yet fully visible in the labor market.

435 — A Historical Sidebar
The House Cap: Fixed Since 1929
Where the Number Comes From
Mark Seliber looked it up: the House has been effectively capped at 435 since 1913, made official by statute in 1929. In 1913, that worked out to roughly 225,000 constituents per seat. Today, the figure is approximately 775,000 per seat. The original constitutional concept was something closer to 50,000–100,000 per representative. Hal's point: expanding the House — and the Supreme Court — needs to be part of the pre-2028 Democratic platform, not an afterthought.

The Thread That Ran Through Everything
From redistricting wins to the weaponization fund to Iranian missile capacity to the liberal arts and AI: this session was animated by a single underlying tension — between the excesses of unconstrained power and the slowly gathering forces of accountability.

George's frame — Edifice Rex, Nero fiddling — gave the session its classical spine. The courts are pushing back. Republican senators are yelling at the White House. Trump's numbers with Hispanics, young voters, and independents are in freefall. The generic congressional ballot is +11. And yet: the Senate may not stop the slush fund, Iran has retained most of its arsenal, and the Democrats still risk squandering a historic opportunity on impeachment theater rather than governance.

John's recurring note: the Democrats must learn to speak to the people who fill their tanks and pay for their groceries — not to the party's internal audiences. They can't do anything if they don't win first. And they won't win first without being real.
