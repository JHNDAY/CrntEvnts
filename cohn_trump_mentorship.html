The Cohn Doctrine

How Roy Cohn Shaped Donald Trump's Approach to Media and Power

Roy Cohn

The Mentor (1927-1986)

Background: McCarthy era prosecutor, political fixer, and aggressive attorney known for his ruthless tactics and media manipulation.

Philosophy: "Attack, attack, attack. Never defend. Never apologize. Never admit wrongdoing."

Reputation: Master of the legal system's gray areas, expert at using media to shape narratives.

Donald Trump

The Protégé (1946-)

Background: Young real estate developer seeking to expand his father's business into Manhattan.

Challenge: Needed legal and strategic guidance to navigate complex business and political landscapes.

Adoption: Embraced and amplified Cohn's teachings throughout his career.

Core Teachings of the Cohn Doctrine

1. Constant Media Presence

"There's no such thing as bad publicity - only publicity."
Cohn taught Trump that staying in the news cycle was essential for maintaining relevance and power. Whether positive or negative, media attention kept one's name in the public consciousness and created the perception of importance and influence.

2. Never Admit Wrongdoing

"Admission of guilt is admission of weakness - and weakness is death."
A cornerstone of Cohn's philosophy was that any acknowledgment of error or fault would be weaponized by opponents. Instead, one should always maintain innocence and shift blame elsewhere, regardless of the evidence.

3. Attack When Attacked

"Don't just hit back - hit back harder than they hit you."
Rather than playing defense when criticized or challenged, Cohn advocated for immediate and overwhelming counterattacks. This strategy aimed to make opponents think twice before engaging in future conflicts.

4. Control the Narrative

"If you don't tell your story, someone else will tell it for you - and you won't like their version."
Cohn emphasized the importance of being proactive in shaping public perception through strategic leaks, press conferences, and media relationships. The goal was to frame stories before opponents could do so.

5. Use Legal Process as Warfare

"The legal system is a tool - use it to exhaust your enemies."
Cohn showed Trump how to use lawsuits, countersuits, and legal delays not necessarily to win in court, but to drain opponents' resources and create leverage in negotiations.

Key Moments in the Mentorship

1973
First Meeting: Trump family hires Cohn to defend against Department of Justice housing discrimination lawsuit. Cohn's aggressive countersuit strategy impresses young Donald.
1975-80
Apprenticeship Years: Trump regularly consults Cohn on business deals, legal strategy, and media relations. Cohn introduces Trump to influential political and media figures.
1980s
Media Mastery: Trump applies Cohn's teachings to build his public profile, courting gossip columnists and positioning himself as a larger-than-life business figure.
1986
Cohn's Death: Roy Cohn dies of AIDS-related complications. Trump distances himself from Cohn in his final months but continues to apply his teachings.

Long-term Impact of Cohn's Teachings

Business Career

Trump's approach to real estate deals, media coverage of his projects, and handling of business controversies consistently reflected Cohn's influence.

Entertainment/Reality TV

The persona Trump developed for "The Apprentice" embodied Cohn's teachings about projecting strength and never showing weakness.

Political Career

Trump's political communication style, from campaign rallies to social media presence, demonstrated the Cohn doctrine of constant media engagement.

Legal Strategies

Throughout various legal challenges, Trump's approach consistently reflected Cohn's teachings about never admitting fault and using legal processes strategically.
