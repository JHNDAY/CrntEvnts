The Week's Thesis
The framework agreement isn't remarkable for what it includes — it's remarkable for what it leaves out. Every stated American and Israeli war aim is absent from the final text, while Iran's core positions are untouched. The bigger story underneath, in Rabbani's phrase, is "Washington's Suez Moment" — a confirmation of declining reach rather than a single battlefield result. What's still genuinely open is whether Iran can convert this victory into durable regional leverage while managing serious discontent at home.

The Suez Moment

Mouin Rabbani on Defeat, Reckoning & a Region in Flux
Analysis of the June 20, 2026 Interview
The Unambiguous Defeat

Mouin Rabbani, speaking with Mondoweiss's Michael Arria, opens with an unambiguous claim: the United States has suffered a clear defeat in its confrontation with Iran. He immediately qualifies that any reading of the new framework agreement must stay tentative, since Washington has repeatedly used diplomacy as camouflage before striking — most recently launching two wars of aggression within a single year while talks were nominally underway.

To understand how a war could end this way, Rabbani traces the arc back to 2015, when the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action gave the United States everything it had asked for: a verified end to any path toward an Iranian nuclear weapon, paired with the most intrusive monitoring regime in the history of the nonproliferation era. Iran, by every independent account, scrupulously observed its commitments. Washington was slow to deliver the sanctions relief and trade benefits Iran was promised in return — and even that wasn't enough. In 2018 the U.S. unilaterally walked away and replaced the deal with "maximum pressure," a policy that, by 2021, had achieved none of its own goals: no regime change, no reduction in Iran's regional footprint, no end to its missile program. Freed from its own obligations under a deal the U.S. had renounced, Iran began enriching uranium to increasingly high levels.

"Washington's Suez Moment" — Mouin Rabbani, on the framework agreement

Rather than admit the mistake and rejoin the original agreement, Washington in 2021 tried to impose an entirely new one — folding in demands about Iran's missile program and its regional alliances that had nothing to do with the 2015 deal. Iran refused. That refusal, Rabbani argues, set the stage for everything that followed: an inconclusive 12-day war, and then, on February 28, 2026, an unprovoked war of aggression with explicit, openly stated aims — regime change, total dismantlement of Iran's nuclear program, removal of its enriched material to U.S. custody, comprehensive elimination of its missile arsenal, and an end to its regional alliances. None of it was achieved.

By April 8, recognizing both the military failure and the mounting global economic damage — an energy price shock threatening to become a supply shock, soaring maritime insurance rates, disrupted petrochemical and fertilizer exports — Washington accepted a ceasefire brokered by Pakistan and Saudi Arabia, with input from Egypt and Turkey. That ceasefire solved nothing structurally: Iran, long disconnected from the global economy by design, absorbed the pain more easily than a U.S. now also responsible for damage it had inflicted worldwide. Facing a choice between a settlement reflecting its weakened position or a far costlier resumption of full-scale war, Washington first tried a third path — blockading Iran and reopening the Strait of Hormuz by force. When Iran began retaliating with escalating intensity, the U.S. found itself back at the same fork, and this time chose the diplomatic exit. The resulting memorandum excludes every original war aim and leaves only Iran's enrichment levels — and its decades-old, never-broken pledge against weaponization — on the table.

Israel's Reckoning

Rabbani extends the same analogy to Israel, which since October 2023 has waged a series of wars aimed at remaking the regional order under its own unchallenged hegemony. The war on Iran was meant to be the capstone. Instead, Israel ran into the limits of its regional power — a reckoning Rabbani treats as parallel to, though distinct from, Washington's own.

Against that backdrop, Israel's recent strikes on Beirut's southern suburbs admit two plausible readings. The first: a desperate, unilateral attempt to sabotage a U.S.–Iran deal before it could be finalized. The second, equally plausible: a move undertaken with full American knowledge and backing, intended to carve Lebanon out of the agreement entirely — to tell Iran that Lebanon remains Israel's to handle bilaterally, under separate U.S.-sponsored negotiations, while Iran stays out of the theater. Either way, Rabbani notes, the attempt failed to hold.

What follows has been an Israeli political meltdown, with hawkish voices accusing Trump of betrayal, and Trump himself making statements — that Iran has a right to defend itself, a right to ballistic missiles "because everyone else has them," a right to civilian nuclear power — that would have been inconceivable only weeks earlier. Rabbani urges caution before reading too much into any single round of angry words between allies, which rarely translate into policy. But he sees reasons this time might differ: unlike his predecessor, Trump's backing of Israel has always been transactional rather than ideological, tied to campaign donors and perceived strategic benefit rather than fixed conviction. The war's damage to Republican-leaning constituencies is now producing an effect on the right comparable to what the Gaza war did to Democratic constituencies — lowering the political cost of distancing from Israel. And Trump, by Rabbani's account, excels above all at demagoguery and opportunism; faced with charges that he failed and capitulated, he would not hesitate to blame Netanyahu, or Israel itself, to protect his own standing. Layered beneath all of this, Rabbani points to a less-discussed factor: the growing influence Gulf Arab states now exercise in Washington. He stops short of calling it a rupture, but describes the beginnings of a transformation in the U.S.–Israel relationship comparable to shifts already visible in Washington's ties to NATO and South Korea.

A Region in Flux

Iran, Rabbani argues, has emerged from this confrontation indisputably victorious and stronger as a state — but not without serious challenges of its own. Winter protests, even accounting for foreign incitement, reflected genuine domestic discontent rooted in economic hardship that tens of billions of dollars in unfrozen assets will not, by themselves, resolve. How Iran's leadership manages that discontent will matter enormously, especially since the U.S., Israel, the Europeans, and likely some regional states as well, can be expected to keep probing for ways to destabilize Iran from within.

The wider "Axis of Resistance" presents a mixed picture. Hamas and Islamic Jihad have been severely battered by the Gaza genocide and the unprecedented violence and ethnic cleansing now underway in the West Bank — even as the Palestinian question has been forced back to the center of regional and international attention, undercutting any near-term prospect of Saudi normalization with Israel. Syria's Assad government has fallen, replaced by a regime hostile to Iran. The Houthis (Ansarallah) in Yemen, by contrast, appear strengthened. And Egypt's President Sisi, largely silent for years, is now publicly demanding Israel halt its expansion in Gaza — a sign, in Rabbani's reading, that he is feeling pressure from a shifting regional mood.

Lebanon, in Rabbani's assessment, may be the single most consequential test case. If Iran can leverage this agreement to halt Israeli aggression there and compel an Israeli withdrawal back to the positions it held in November 2024, that would be a major win for both Iran and Hezbollah — and a significant defeat for the current Lebanese government and its sponsors in Washington, Paris, and Brussels, reshaping domestic Lebanese politics in Hezbollah's favor.

Beyond Lebanon, Rabbani sees signs of a broader regional recalibration already underway: Saudi Arabia making overtures toward Iran, the UAE — for all its prior belligerence — seeking some form of non-aggression understanding, and the possibility that Iran and Oman could develop an arrangement charging "management fees" for transit through the Strait of Hormuz, though Oman is unlikely to pursue this in open defiance of Saudi Arabia. People across the region, Rabbani notes, have watched Iran negotiate in good faith in 2015, get that good faith answered with renunciation and two wars, and then achieve through resistance what diplomacy alone never delivered — a lesson regional populations are unlikely to forget when they look at their own governments' postures toward Washington and Israel.
