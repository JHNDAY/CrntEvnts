Presidential Power to Fire Officials

A Constitutional Battle at the Supreme Court
Supreme Court Arguments: December 2025

The Core Question

Can President Trump fire government officials for any reason — or no reason at all — despite laws meant to shield them from politics?

The Bombshell Development

A leading originalist scholar, whose work the Supreme Court frequently cites, has just released a provocative article arguing AGAINST the unitary executive theory — complicating the court's expected ruling.

The Two Competing Views

Unitary Executive Theory

Supported by: Supreme Court's conservative majority, Trump administration

Argument: The Constitution's original understanding demands letting the president remove executive branch officials as he sees fit.

Key phrase: "The Constitution vests the executive power in the president"

VS

Congressional Authority View

Supported by: Prof. Caleb Nelson (and historically, Congress)

Argument: The Constitution grants Congress broad authority to shape the executive branch, including limits on firing power.

Key concern: Presidential power without limits risks tyranny

Timeline of Key Developments

1935

Humphrey's Executor v. United States

Supreme Court rules Congress CAN limit the president's power to fire officials

2010-2020

Chief Justice Roberts begins "chipping away" at the 1935 precedent

Multiple decisions gradually expand presidential removal power

May 2025

Supreme Court lets Trump remove leaders of the National Labor Relations Board and Merit Systems Protection Board

September 2025

Supreme Court lets Trump remove Federal Trade Commission member; orders arguments on overruling Humphrey's Executor

Sept 29, 2025

💣 Prof. Caleb Nelson publishes article challenging unitary executive theory

December 2025

Supreme Court will hear arguments on whether to overturn Humphrey's Executor

Key Scholars & Players

Prof. Caleb Nelson

University of Virginia Law
Former clerk to Justice Thomas

Published the "bombshell" article arguing against unitary executive theory. His scholarship has been cited in over a dozen Supreme Court opinions.

Chief Justice John Roberts

Supreme Court

Has made expanding presidential removal power a "signature project" over his 20 years on the court.

Prof. William Baude

University of Chicago Law

Prominent originalist who called Nelson's article a "Bombshell!" on social media.

Prof. Richard Pildes

NYU Law School

Notes that Nelson's article "raises serious questions about an originalist justification" for the court's expected approach.

Nelson's Key Arguments

"Letting the president fire officials 'for reasons good or bad' would grant him an enormous amount of power — more power, I think, than any sensible person should want anyone to have."
"More than one member [of the founding generation] warned against interpreting the Constitution in the expectation that all presidents would have the sterling character of George Washington."
"The textual and historical evidence is far more equivocal than the current court has been suggesting... In the face of such ambiguities, I hope that the justices will not act as if their hands are tied."

The Expected Outcome

Despite Nelson's article, the Supreme Court's conservative majority is still expected to side with President Trump and adopt the unitary executive theory. However, the article may influence the reasoning and scope of the decision.

As Nelson acknowledges: "The Supreme Court appears to be moving toward a sweepingly pro-president position."
