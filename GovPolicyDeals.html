The Evolution of Government Support

A comparison of presidential policies and their impact on American citizens

Precursors: Lincoln to McKinley (1861-1901)

Early progressive reforms that laid the groundwork for the "Deal" era

Abraham Lincoln (1861-1865)

  • ▸ Homestead Act - Free land for settlers
  • ▸ Morrill Land-Grant Acts - Public universities
  • ▸ Transcontinental Railroad investment
  • ▸ National Banking Acts

Ulysses S. Grant (1869-1877)

  • ▸ Enforcement Acts - Protected voting rights
  • ▸ Civil Rights Act of 1875
  • ▸ 14th & 15th Amendments ratified
  • ▸ Supported Freedmen's Bureau

Chester Arthur (1881-1885)

  • ▸ Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act
  • ▸ Merit-based government employment
  • ▸ Reduced political patronage system

Grover Cleveland (1885-1889, 1893-1897)

  • ▸ Interstate Commerce Act (1887)
  • ▸ First federal regulation of railroads
  • ▸ Vetoed excessive corporate subsidies

Benjamin Harrison (1889-1893)

  • ▸ Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
  • ▸ First federal antitrust legislation
  • ▸ Attempted voting rights enforcement

William McKinley (1897-1901)

  • ▸ Gold Standard Act (1900)
  • ▸ Protective tariffs for American workers
  • ▸ Bridge to Progressive Era reforms

Note: While these presidents made important contributions, progress was often uneven. The post-Reconstruction era saw significant rollback of civil rights protections, and labor movements faced violent suppression. However, these early reforms established the precedent that the federal government could and should act to protect citizens and regulate powerful economic interests.

The Counter-Movement: Opposition to Progressive Governance (1930s-Present)

Organized efforts to roll back and dismantle progressive reforms

⚠️ 1930s-1940s: Initial Resistance

Key Opponents & Organizations:

  • ● American Liberty League - Corporate-funded anti-New Deal group
  • ● Business interests challenged Social Security in courts
  • ● Conservative Supreme Court struck down early New Deal programs
  • ● "Court-packing" controversy over judicial independence

📉 1950s-1970s: Conservative Coalition

Strategies & Tactics:

  • ● "States' rights" arguments against federal programs
  • ● Southern Democrats blocked civil rights & social programs
  • ● Barry Goldwater's 1964 campaign against "big government"
  • ● Richard Nixon's "Southern Strategy" realignment

🏛️ 1980s: Reagan Revolution

Major Initiatives:

  • ● "Government is the problem" ideology promoted
  • ● Massive tax cuts for wealthy individuals
  • ● Deregulation of industries and weakened enforcement
  • ● Attacks on labor unions and worker protections
  • ● Social safety net cuts while military spending soared

💰 1990s-2000s: Corporate Dominance

Institutional Changes:

  • ● Newt Gingrich's "Contract with America" (1994)
  • ● Welfare "reform" reducing support for poor families
  • ● Financial deregulation leading to 2008 crisis
  • ● Citizens United decision (2010) - unlimited corporate political spending
  • ● Koch Brothers network funding anti-government movements

⚡ 2010s: Tea Party & Beyond

Escalating Tactics:

  • ● Government shutdown strategies to block legislation
  • ● Attempts to repeal Affordable Care Act
  • ● Voter suppression laws targeting minorities
  • ● Federalist Society's judicial transformation
  • ● "Starve the beast" - deliberate deficit creation to justify cuts

🚨 2017-Present: Authoritarian Turn

Trump Era Escalation:

  • ● "Schedule F" plan to politicize civil service
  • ● Project 2025 blueprint for dismantling federal agencies
  • ● Plans to eliminate Department of Education
  • ● Attacks on independent regulatory agencies (EPA, FTC, etc.)
  • ● "Unitary executive theory" - unlimited presidential power
  • ● Promises of "retribution" against political opponents
  • ● Undermining electoral processes and democratic norms

The Pattern: What began as principled opposition to specific New Deal policies evolved into a systematic, well-funded campaign to dismantle progressive governance itself. Each generation of opposition built upon previous efforts, becoming increasingly radical in its rejection of the social contract established by the progressive era reforms. The current authoritarian approach represents the culmination of this 90-year effort to return to pre-New Deal governance—or worse.
