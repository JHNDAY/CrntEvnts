01 Middle East: US–Iran War & Regional Escalation 61 mentions ▼
War Enters Third & Fourth Weeks — Ground Operations Eyed
Now into its fourth week, the US–Israeli air campaign had struck more than 1,000 targets inside Iran by mid-March. The Pentagon publicly prepared plans for "weeks of US ground operations" in Iran's strategic regions, including considering seizure of Kharg Island, which handles 90% of Iran's oil exports. Trump threatened to destroy all Iranian power plants and oil wells if no deal was reached.
CNN Al Jazeera BBC
Strait of Hormuz Blocked — Biggest Oil Shock in History
Iran's effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of world oil passes — prompted the IEA to call it the biggest oil shock in history. Brent crude surged over 50% from pre-war levels, exceeding $120/barrel. Iran announced tolls for certain vessels and allowed 20 Pakistani-flagged ships to transit daily as a mediation gesture.
CNN Al Jazeera NPR
Iranian Retaliatory Strikes — Casualties & Nuclear Sites Targeted
Iran launched hundreds of missiles and drones against US bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE. By late March, 1,900 Iranians had been killed in strikes, along with 1,189 in Lebanon and 99 in Iraq. Israel struck two Iranian nuclear facilities, including a uranium processing plant. The IAEA called for restraint after Iran denied any radioactive leak.
CNN Al Jazeera Haaretz
Pakistan Mediates — 15-Point US Peace Proposal
Pakistan hosted Saudi, Turkish, and Egyptian foreign ministers in Islamabad to mediate, with its FM then traveling to Beijing. Special Envoy Witkoff confirmed Pakistan as the US–Iran back-channel. Trump said March 29 that Iran had agreed to "most of" a 15-point US demand list — a claim Iran's FM contradicted, calling the terms "largely excessive, unrealistic and unreasonable."
CNN Al Jazeera BBC
Hezbollah Enters War — Lebanon Toll Mounts
Hezbollah launched projectiles at Israel from Lebanon starting March 2, drawing a major Israeli ground operation. By late March, Israeli strikes in Lebanon had killed 1,189 people — including at least 121 children. On March 26, Trump announced a 10-day pause in strikes on Iranian energy sites until April 6, citing ongoing talks. 02 ICE Operations & the DHS Shutdown 47 mentions ▼
DHS Shutdown Becomes Longest in US History
The partial Department of Homeland Security shutdown — centered on a congressional standoff over ICE funding — crossed into record territory in late March, becoming the longest DHS shutdown in American history at 42+ days. The Senate passed a bipartisan bill funding most of DHS but excluding ICE and border patrol. The House then rejected it, passing its own 60-day extension bill that Democrats blocked in the Senate.
CNN NPR Politico
ICE Deployed to Airports — TSA in Crisis
Trump ordered ICE agents to major airports nationwide — JFK, LaGuardia, O'Hare, Atlanta, Houston, Newark — to assist with security as TSA workers called out in record numbers. On March 28–29, over 12% of TSA officers called out, the highest since the shutdown began. Border Czar Tom Homan said ICE agents might "remain" at airports even after TSA agents were paid.
CNN NPR NYT
ICE at Polling Locations? Voter Intimidation Fears
White House Press Secretary Leavitt said she couldn't "guarantee" ICE agents wouldn't be near polling places in November, prompting alarm from election administrators. CNN reported that Trump allies were eager to declare a national elections emergency to impose strict federal oversight of the 2026 midterms. Arizona's Secretary of State vowed to block any such deployment.
CNN NPR Politico
Detention Capacity Expands — Mega-Centers Planned
ICE detention surged to over 72,000 people by late March — up 75% from a year earlier — with 26 deaths in custody since October. An internal February memo revealed plans for eight mega-centers, each holding 10,000 detainees, funded by the $38.3B "One Big Beautiful Bill" appropriation. NPR's reporting documented community backlash across 20+ states.
NPR Guardian NYT
Third-Country Deportations — Costa Rica Joins
Costa Rica agreed to accept 25 deportees per week under a Trump administration agreement to deport migrants to third countries, joining South Sudan, Honduras, Rwanda, Guyana, and Caribbean island nations. Critics noted migrants are left in "legal black holes" in countries where they don't speak the language. Democrats demanded judicial oversight of all deportation warrants.
CNN NPR Guardian
Public Opinion Turns Against ICE — 65% Say "Gone Too Far"
The latest NPR/PBS News/Marist poll found 65% of Americans believe ICE has "gone too far" in enforcing immigration laws — an 11-point jump since last summer. A Quinnipiac poll showed Trump at just 28% approval among independents. Community protests in Merrimack (NH), Surprise (AZ), and Social Circle (GA) forced halts to planned ICE detention facilities.

Democrat Flips Mar-a-Lago District — Democrats Flip 30 Seats
Democrat Emily Gregory won a special election in Florida House District 87 — a deep-red district that includes Trump's Mar-a-Lago estate — defeating a Trump-endorsed Republican by 51%–49%. The district had been won by 19 points in 2024. It was the 10th GOP state legislative seat flipped by Democrats since Trump returned to office, part of a nationwide trend of 30 total seat flips since 2025.

The House passed the SAVE America Act (218–213) on Feb. 11 — requiring documentary proof of citizenship to register to vote and effectively banning no-excuse mail-in voting. Trump told House Republicans it would "guarantee the midterms." The Senate is filibustering it; Trump is pressing Republicans to eliminate the filibuster. Audit data from Georgia, Louisiana, and Utah found fewer than 100 noncitizen voter registrations combined.
CNN NPR Politico

In an irony widely noted by reporters, Trump cast a mail-in ballot in the Florida special election he lost — while simultaneously pushing Congress to sharply restrict mail-in voting through the SAVE America Act. NPR CEO Katherine Maher called it "a decisive affirmation of the rights of a free and independent press." The ruling doesn't restore the funding Congress rescinded, but is a significant First Amendment victory.

DOJ Whiplash on Law Firms — Drops Then Re-Ups Appeal
In a chaotic 24-hour reversal, the DOJ announced it was dropping appeals against four law firms (WilmerHale, Jenner & Block, Perkins Coie, Susman Godfrey) — then reversed course after Trump and Stephen Miller objected. NPR's Carrie Johnson reported the episode as emblematic of an administration incapable of coherent legal strategy and unwilling to accept judicial defeats.

IEEPA Tariffs Struck Down — Refund Process Mired
Following the Supreme Court's February ruling striking down most IEEPA tariffs, the Court of International Trade in March ordered Customs and Border Protection to begin refunding tariff payments.
