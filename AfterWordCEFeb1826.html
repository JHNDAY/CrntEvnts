🗂️ The Epstein Files: DOJ Review & Early Releases ▶
AG Bondi Launches Review; First Document Tranche Released
The Epstein files dominated political conversation throughout early February. The Department of Justice confirmed that Attorney General Pamela Bondi had ordered a comprehensive review of approximately 100,000 Epstein-related records at President Trump's direction — following Trump's two-year campaign promise to release the files. Bondi told Fox News on February 21 that Epstein material was "sitting on my desk right now to review," signaling an imminent release. The first formal DOJ tranche arrived at the end of February, but the period Feb 4–18 was marked by mounting public pressure and leaks of previously circulated documents.
Reporting synthesized across multiple outlets noted that the Epstein Files Transparency Act — signed by Trump in November 2025 — had already mandated a December 19, 2025 release of court documents, which included thousands of references to Trump, emails involving Prince Andrew (Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor), correspondence with Steve Bannon, and redacted grand jury testimony. The question heading into February was how much of the FBI's internal investigative files would eventually be made public.
What Was Known by Feb 18, 2026:
  • December 2025 release: 3+ million pages of court documents, heavily redacted in places, posted to the DOJ's Epstein Library website.
  • Trump references: Thousands of mentions in Epstein emails, largely gossip, news clippings, and social commentary — not direct evidence of misconduct.
  • Prince Andrew: Named several hundred times; photos and emails surfaced by PBS/AP showed him kneeling over an unidentified woman.
  • Elon Musk: Email exchanges in 2012–13 about potentially visiting Epstein's island; Musk said he refused the overtures.
  • Chomsky correspondence: NBC News reported Epstein used Chomsky to help rehabilitate his reputation — Chomsky communicated in good faith, unaware he was being manipulated.
  • Redaction controversy: At least 550 pages fully blacked out; key FBI interview notes and DOJ charging memos remained unreadable.
A Reuters/Ipsos poll released February 18 found that a majority of Americans believe the Epstein files confirm that powerful people in the U.S. are rarely held accountable. The group's discussion reflected this public mood — while cautioning that a name appearing in the files does not automatically establish wrongdoing.
Media Note: Many outlets cautioned that "appearing in the files" can mean anything from a passing email mention to direct participation. The New York Times and Wall Street Journal both ran explainers urging readers to distinguish levels of association before drawing conclusions.
Sources: DOJ Epstein Library · PBS NewsHour / AP · Wikipedia synthesis · NBC News

🎈 The El Paso Balloon Incident: A Case Study in Interagency Chaos ▶
Anti-Drone Laser Deployed Against Mylar Party Balloon; Airport Briefly Shut Down
On the night of February 11, 2026, the FAA abruptly closed all airspace above El Paso — up to 18,000 feet — issuing a restriction originally set to last ten days. The closure grounded commercial, cargo, and emergency flights for approximately seven hours before being lifted. CBS News and NBC News subsequently reported that Customs and Border Protection (CBP) had deployed an experimental high-energy anti-drone laser — on loan from the Department of Defense — against what officers believed was a cartel drone, but which turned out to be a mylar party balloon.
The Trump administration initially described the closure as a response to a "cartel drone incursion." Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy posted on X that the FAA and "Department of War" had "acted swiftly" and "the threat has been neutralized." Rep. Veronica Escobar (D-TX) publicly contradicted this account, saying it was "not what we in Congress have been told." NPR called the episode a failure of interagency coordination, noting the FAA closed airspace without alerting the White House, Pentagon, or DHS.
"Medical evacuation flights were forced to divert to Las Cruces. All aviation operations were grounded, including emergency flights. This was a major and unnecessary disruption, one that has not occurred since 9/11."
— El Paso Mayor Renard Johnson
The Breakdown in Brief:
  • CBP fired experimental DoD laser without coordinating with FAA
  • FAA Administrator closed airspace without alerting White House, Pentagon, or DHS
  • Army leadership at Fort Bliss was not informed of either the laser use or the airspace closure
  • Object shot down: a mylar party balloon, not a cartel drone
  • Administration publicly blamed Mexican drug cartels before correcting the record
The Bulwark published an analysis arguing the incident illustrated the danger of equipping domestic law enforcement agencies with battlefield-grade technology without the corresponding military discipline and coordination protocols.
Sources: CBS News · NBC News · NPR · The Hill · The Bulwark
🇧🇪 Ambassador Bill White & Belgium: Diplomatic Firestorm Over Circumcision Case ▶
Belgium Summons U.S. Ambassador After He Accuses Nation of Antisemitism in Mohel Case
In a story tailor-made for the group's "Adventures in Government Incompetence" segment, Trump-appointed U.S. Ambassador to Belgium Bill White generated an international incident just one day before the session. On February 16, White posted on X calling Belgian authorities' investigation of three mohels (practitioners of ritual Jewish circumcision) "ridiculous and antisemitic 'prosecution,'" copying President Trump, Vice President Vance, and Secretary of State Rubio in his post. Belgium summoned White to the Foreign Ministry on February 17.
The Washington Post reported that Belgian Foreign Minister Maxime Prévot called White's post "dangerous disinformation" and said it undermined "the real fight against hatred." Euronews noted Belgian law does permit ritual circumcision — but requires it be performed by a licensed physician, a standard the mohels in question allegedly did not meet.
Key Facts:
  • Belgian police raided three locations in Antwerp's Jewish Quarter in May 2025, seeking evidence of circumcisions performed without medical licenses
  • Three mohels were charged; prosecutors reportedly believed they had sufficient evidence for conviction
  • Ambassador White characterized it as "antisemitic prosecution" — Belgium said they were investigating, not yet prosecuting
  • White tagged Trump, Vance, and Rubio in the post; Rubio did not publicly respond
  • Belgium's summoning of an ambassador is described by diplomats as a rare and serious rebuke between allies
  • White's background includes a $1 million fine for financial fraud at a previous position
NBC News provided context on mohels and the theological significance of the practice, while CBS News pointed out that the clash underscores heightened tensions between the U.S. and European allies under Trump.
Sources: Washington Post · NBC News · Euronews · CBS News · The Hill

🏛️ Adventures in Bad Taste: Trump's White House Makeover ▶
Gold Chandeliers, Corinthian Columns, and a $250M Ballroom: Trump Reshapes the People's House
The New York Times visual comparison of the cabinet room under Obama, Biden, and Trump — which the group reviewed in its closing segment — is part of a sweeping ongoing story about the most dramatic physical transformation of the White House in generations. CNN reported that renderings of the new East Wing ballroom — the project's centerpiece — depict "a vast space with gold and crystal chandeliers, gilded Corinthian columns, a coffered ceiling with gold inlays, gold floor lamps and a checkered marble floor."
The East Wing was demolished in October 2025, despite lacking required approvals from the National Capital Planning Commission and the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation. Trump told reporters he was informed he needed no approvals. The demolition removed the East Wing — completed in 1942 under FDR — including two historic magnolia trees planted as memorials to Presidents Harding and Roosevelt. Construction documentation confirms the new ballroom will be 89,000 square feet — meaning the addition will substantially exceed the footprint of the original White House.
Trump White House Transformations (by Feb 2026):
  • Oval Office: Gold accents on ceiling, door frames, and cherub details; gold paint throughout
  • Cabinet Room: Three gold chandeliers added; Times visual showed transition from Obama → Biden → Trump
  • Rose Garden: Converted to a white limestone patio called the "Rose Garden Club," with umbrella tables mimicking Mar-a-Lago
  • Palm Room passageway: Highly polished book-matched marble floors
  • North/South Lawns: Two 88-foot flagpoles installed at ~$50,000 each
  • New Ballroom (East Wing): 89,000 sq ft; ~$200–250M estimated cost; privately funded per Trump
  • A federal arts panel approved the project 6-0 on February 19, 2026 — one day after the session
A PBS News analysis noted that a YouGov poll found 53% of Americans disapprove of the East Wing demolition. Sen. Chuck Schumer quipped that he doesn't "need a $200 million ballroom" to eat a cheeseburger at his desk. Historic preservationists and several lawsuits seek to challenge the project's legal basis.
Sources: CNN · NBC News · PBS News · Artnet News · White House State Ballroom (Wikipedia)
🏆 Presidents' Day: Historical Rankings & Trump's Place in History ▶
Historians Rank All 47 Presidents; Trump Lands Near the Bottom
Presidents' Day, observed February 17, 2026, prompted extensive media coverage of presidential rankings — including a YouGov survey in which the public ranked Trump 45th of 47 presidents, above only Joe Biden and Richard Nixon. CNN's presidential rankings — which the group discussed — showed Trump tied with Franklin Pierce at 41st place, with Andrew Johnson and James Buchanan at the bottom.
The group's discussion closely mirrored a Morning Joe Presidents' Day panel featuring historians Doris Kearns Goodwin, Douglas Brinkley, and Jon Meacham. Goodwin emphasized moral character and its inseparability from great achievement, citing Lincoln, the two Roosevelts, and LBJ. Brinkley focused on concrete achievements, singling out Kennedy's Moon program and Carter's Camp David Accords. Meacham offered that "we don't elect statues or icons, we elect human beings — and perhaps we've taken the human being thing just a little bit too far."
Consensus Top Tier (across major ranking systems):
  • #1 — Abraham Lincoln: Crisis leadership, moral courage, willingness to appoint rivals
  • #2 — FDR: Depression, World War II, activist government
  • #3 — George Washington: Setting democratic precedents, Cincinnatus model
  • #4–6 — Theodore Roosevelt, Truman, Eisenhower
  • Bottom tier: Pierce, A. Johnson, Buchanan — all Civil War–era failures. Buchanan perhaps the most credentialed and least effective president in U.S. history.
The group noted that no updated CNN/APSA rankings had been released covering the Biden presidency — a pattern observers attributed to corporate caution about provoking the Trump administration. The Mencken quote John cited — essentially that American democracy would one day produce an "absolute moron" in the White House — has circulated widely in commentary since 2017.
Sources: NewsNation / YouGov · Pew Research · MSNBC / Morning Joe · CNN Presidential Poll of Polls

📺 Media Landscape: Billionaire Ownership, Self-Censorship & AI Disinformation ▶
Billionaire Owners Muzzle Newsrooms; Podcast Misinformation Fills the Void
The group's observations about media capture echo coverage in several outlets. The decision by CBS to pull Talarico's Colbert interview was widely cited alongside Bezos's reported directive at The Washington Post and Patrick Soon-Shiong's moves at the Los Angeles Times as evidence of a pattern of billionaire media owners sidelining editorial independence to avoid friction with the Trump administration.
Key Media Ownership Concerns (Feb 2026):
  • CBS / Paramount: Colbert interview with Talarico suppressed; ongoing questions about editorial interference ahead of the Skydance merger review
  • Washington Post (Bezos): Shifted editorial policy away from presidential endorsements; reduced opinion section criticism of Trump
  • Los Angeles Times (Soon-Shiong): Reported staff departures and editorial tension over coverage direction
  • CNN: Presidential rankings page not updated to include post-Biden data, per group's observation
At the same time, the group noted the rise of misleading podcasts and AI-generated disinformation, which increasingly fill the vacuum left by cautious mainstream coverage. The Atlantic and The New Yorker have each published pieces in this period examining the epistemological challenge of navigating a fractured information environment — drawing parallels, as the group did, to the yellow journalism era of Hearst and Pulitzer.

🎿 Milan-Cortina Winter Olympics 2026: Sportsmanship & the Unexpected ▶
Opening Weeks Deliver Upsets, Sportsmanship, and Some Notable Moments
The XXVI Winter Olympic Games, hosted across Milan and Cortina d'Ampezzo in northern Italy, opened February 6 and were the immediate backdrop to the session. American and international coverage focused on two notable storylines the group touched on.
Figure skating produced one of the games' most-discussed moments: Ilia Malinin of the United States — who entered as a 99% gold medal favorite in men's figure skating — fell apart during the free skate, unable to handle the pressure of being the heavy favorite. George noted with appreciation that Malinin spoke openly with media about the experience, modeling a kind of emotional transparency rare in elite sport.
American alpine skier Mikaela Shiffrin, who had been in a prolonged results drought, won her race and was captured in what broadcasters described as a moment of pure relief. The extreme sports and snowboarding venues also drew coverage for the unusual camaraderie among competitors — a dynamic the group contrasted directly with the zero-sum ethos dominating Washington.
The 2026 Winter Olympics are the first to be held in Italy since the 2006 Turin Games. Coverage is available on NBC/Peacock. The games run through February 22, 2026.
