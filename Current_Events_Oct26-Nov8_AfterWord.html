Voice of America: Institutional Resistance to Dismantlement ▶
Federal Workforce Cuts Face Legal and Operational Obstacles
CBS News reported on August 30 that the Trump administration announced plans to cut 500-plus jobs at Voice of America and its parent agency. Kari Lake, senior adviser to USAGM, stated the cuts would "help reduce the federal bureaucracy" while maintaining the agency's "statutory mission."
Timeline of VOA Dismantlement Efforts:
  • March 15: Trump signs executive order targeting USAGM; nearly entire VOA workforce placed on administrative leave
  • May 30: Approximately 500 contractors terminated, positions eliminated
  • June 6: Kari Lake proposes reducing workforce from 1,300 to just 81 people
  • June 20-21: Mass layoff notices delivered to 639 employees across USAGM and VOA
  • September 30: Federal Judge Royce Lamberth blocks further job cuts
NPR analysis on June 6 revealed Lake's plan would reduce VOA to skeleton staffing levels: 11 people for Voice of America itself, 33 for Cuban broadcasts, and just 2 people each for foreign language services to Afghanistan, China, and Iran—down from 1,300 total employees.
"When you punch someone and leave them in the street for dead, how can you expect them to come back? It's humanly impossible."
— Kelu Chao, former VOA and USAGM official, on the devastating impact of workforce cuts
PBS News reported September 30 that U.S. District Judge Royce Lamberth temporarily suspended plans to eliminate 532 remaining jobs, accusing the administration of showing "concerning disrespect" toward the court. The judge noted the agency initiated cuts just hours after a hearing where government lawyers said a reduction was "merely a possibility."
85%
Percentage of USAGM/VOA workforce eliminated (1,400 positions)
Al Jazeera coverage on June 21 noted that VOA began broadcasting to Nazi Germany to deliver news about US democracy, and grew to deliver news in dozens of languages worldwide. One plaintiff attorney stated: "I believe that the destruction is permanent because we see no indication in the next fiscal year that Congress will rally to fund VOA."
Sources: CBS News, NPR, PBS News, Al Jazeera, Radio Free Europe
NYC Mayoral Race: Mamdani Defeats Cuomo ▶
34-Year-Old Democratic Socialist Makes History in NYC
NBC News called the race on November 4, projecting that Assemblyman Zohran Mamdani defeated former Governor Andrew Cuomo with 50.4% of the vote. The election featured the highest turnout for a NYC mayoral race in decades, with approximately 2 million votes cast.

CBS New York reported October 28 that a Suffolk University poll showed Cuomo had cut Mamdani's lead in half to 10 points (44%-34%), down from a 20-point deficit in September. The poll was conducted after Mayor Eric Adams endorsed Cuomo.
Age Demographics Revealed Sharp Divide:
  • 18-34 years: 64% backed Mamdani, 20% Cuomo
  • 35-49 years: 50% backed Mamdani, 25% Cuomo
  • 50-64 years: 41% backed Cuomo, 35% Mamdani
  • 65+ years: 39% backed Cuomo, 33% Mamdani
"My friends, we have toppled a political dynasty. I wish Andrew Cuomo only the best in private life, but let tonight be the final time I utter his name as we turn the page on a politics that abandons the many and answers only to the few."
— Zohran Mamdani, victory speech, November 4, 2025
Al Jazeera analyzed on October 28 that Cuomo sought to court conservative voters while being accused of "fear mongering" in the race's final stretch. Trump and Elon Musk backed Cuomo on the final day, though polls still showed Mamdani ahead.
Marist Poll released October 28 showed that if Sliwa dropped out, Mamdani's lead would shrink to 7 points (51%-44%). A majority of NYC likely voters had a favorable impression of Mamdani (57%), while Cuomo's favorable rating was "upside down" with 40% favorable vs. 55% unfavorable.
CNN reported that Mamdani became NYC's first Muslim mayor and first South Asian mayor, as well as its youngest since 1892. He received over one million votes, the first candidate to do so since John Lindsay in 1969.
Sources: NBC News, CBS New York, Quinnipiac Poll, Marist Poll, Al Jazeera, CNN
Democratic Strategy: Working-Class Politics Debate ▶
Post-Election Analysis Points to Messaging Failure on Economics
The Nation reported in October on polling by the Center for Working-Class Politics showing that Democratic messaging around "Trump as a threat to democracy" performed 9 percentage points worse than economic populism messages and was least favored among virtually all demographic groups.
60%
Percentage of US workforce without college degrees—the working class Democrats struggle to reach
The Democratic Strategist published analysis in early November noting that "Working-class voters perceive Democrats to be woke, weak, and out-of-touch, too focused on social issues and not nearly focused enough on the economic issues that impact everyone, every day."
Key Findings from Working-Class Voter Research:
  • 39 focus groups with 400 working-class voters across 21 states (Feb-Aug 2025)
  • Survey of 7,555 voters, including 2,179 self-identified working class
  • Working-class voters frustrated, anxious about inflation and paychecks keeping pace
  • Voters increasingly get information from YouTube and TikTok, not traditional media
  • Democrats who are "authentically relatable, clear, and respectful" connect most
Seattle Times published comprehensive analysis on the party's estrangement from working-class voters, noting that Democratic policies focused on people as consumers instead of workers. The article quoted former AFL-CIO political director Michael Podhorzer: "One of the things that has been frustrating about the narrative 'The Democrats are losing the working class' is that people are noticing it half a century after it happened."
"The Democratic Party has continued to fail to prioritize a strong, working-class message that addresses issues that really matter to workers. The party did not make a positive case for why workers should vote for them, only that they were not Donald Trump. That's not good enough anymore!"
— Jimmy Williams Jr., International Union of Painters and Allied Trades president
Washington Monthly published in November a package of essays providing "policy scaffolding of a new liberalism that addresses, not in word but in deed, the long-neglected interests of working-class Americans," arguing Democrats must offer more thoroughgoing change than previously considered.
New Labor Forum analysis emphasized that Democrats should not moderate core progressive principles for political expediency, citing examples like Arizona Governor Katie Hobbs who vetoed anti-immigration enforcement while maintaining strong border security messaging.
UnHerd profiled the emerging "populist moderate" model: candidates who are economically populist but moderate on culture-war issues, citing examples like Dan Osborn in Nebraska ("If Trump needs help building the Wall, well, I'm pretty handy") and Arizona Senator Ruben Gallego.
Sources: The Nation, The Democratic Strategist, Seattle Times, Washington Monthly, The Nation, New Labor Forum, UnHerd
