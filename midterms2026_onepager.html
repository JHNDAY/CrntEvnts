Context

The Political Environment

  • ▸The Supreme Court this week gutted the 1965 Voting Rights Act, ruling the new majority-Black LA-06 district an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. Justice Alito held that Section 2 does not limit partisan gerrymandering.
  • ▸Republicans are pursuing broad voter suppression measures: rigorous ID requirements, limits on mail-in and early voting, reduced urban polling places, and possible voter intimidation at the polls.
  • ▸Blue-state counter-gerrymanders (MD, IL, NY) may offset Republican gains from the VRA ruling — but at the cost of democratic norms.
  • ▸Trump's Latino approval has cratered from 48% to 31% in one year — creating unexpected vulnerability in South Texas redistricting targets.
House

House of Representatives

  • ▸Republicans hold a 218–212 margin. Democrats need a net gain of only 3 seats for control.
  • ▸Cook ratings: D Subtotal 217, Toss-ups 16, R Subtotal 202. Probabilistic projection: 221 D vs. 214 R.
  • ▸Historical precedent: the out-party gains ~30 seats in midterms. Even half that figure gives Democrats a double-digit majority.
  • ▸Republicans projected to net 2–3 seats from redistricting (TX, MO, NC, OH) after Democratic offsets — already reflected in forecasts.
  • ▸VRA ruling could allow up to 7 additional R gains from Deep South districts — but execution for 2026 ballot deadlines is uncertain.
Senate

United States Senate

  • ▸Current split: 53 R / 47 D. Democrats need a net +4 seats for control (or +3 if Murkowski flips).
  • ▸35 seats up in 2026. Cook ratings: D subtotal 13 · Toss-ups 3 (ME, MI, OH) · R subtotal 19. Probabilistic projection: ~52 R / 48 D.
  • ▸Key Democratic flip opportunities: Maine (Collins vs. Platner — Kalshi 69% D), North Carolina (Cooper leads Whatley by 9%), Alaska (Peltola leads Sullivan by avg. 2%).
  • ▸Wild cards if the wave is large: Iowa (Ernst retiring), Texas (Talarico leads both Paxton and Cornyn by 7–8%), Nebraska (Osborne rematch).
  • ▸The structural math demands winning all 7 competitive races — but a large enough wave makes the counting a formality. 2006 and 2018 precedent: "Lean R" seats fell without warning.
Wave?

Reading the Wave Signals

  • ▸+13-point Democratic overperformance in special elections and a +6 generic ballot lead are not the fingerprints of a close election — they suggest an electorate in motion.
  • ▸The coalition driving this: energized Democratic base (immigration, economy, democratic norms) plus alienated swing voters acting on revulsion rather than party loyalty.
  • ▸Voter suppression is a margin game. It can tip a close race but cannot neutralize a large-scale popular rejection of the governing party.
  • ▸Even a modest D gain of 1–2 Senate seats in 2025 could have blocked Hegseth and Patel confirmations and the "Big Beautiful Bill."
  • ▸"Six months is a long time in politics — and things could change markedly."
Discussion

Five Questions for the Group

  1. 1 The Supreme Court's dismantling of the Voting Rights Act has been compared to Dred Scott and Plessy v. Ferguson. Is that framing apt — and what does it suggest about the Court's long-term trajectory?
  2. 2 Special elections show Democrats overperforming by 13 points, yet prediction markets give them only 50% odds on the Senate. Are the markets pricing in structural Republican advantages — or something else?
  3. 3 The analysis distinguishes between "precision politics" (win exactly 7 toss-ups) and "wave politics" (the result becomes self-evident). Do you find that framing persuasive? What historical analogies come to mind?
  4. 4 Blue states responding to Republican gerrymanders with their own — MD, IL, NY — preserves seat parity but normalizes the practice. Is that a reasonable trade-off, or does it accelerate democratic erosion?
  5. 5 Trump's Latino approval has dropped from 48% to 31% in one year. What does that shift tell us about the durability of his 2024 coalition — and which other demographic "cracks" deserve watching between now and November?
