After sixteen years of systematic dismantling, Hungary's democracy stirred — and showed the world the Achilles' heel that all populists in power share.

Commentary by Max Boot  ·  Washington Post  ·  Analysis & synthesis: Current Events Portal
The Sixteen-Year Siege +

Viktor Orbán did not simply win elections. He rewrote the architecture of Hungarian democracy itself — gerrymandering constituencies, packing courts, muzzling media, and funneling public contracts to a loyalty-sworn oligarch class. For sixteen years he endured, and Europe's liberals watched in something between horror and despair.

He was Trump before Trump. He built the template: vilify immigrants, scorn globalist elites, invoke the nation, reward the faithful. Tucker Carlson pilgrimaged to Budapest. The Heritage Foundation declared modern Hungary "the model" for conservative statecraft worldwide.

"The model" has been repudiated — by the very people it was supposed to serve.

Max Boot · Washington Post

On April 13, 2026, Hungarian voters — in what can only be described as a landslide — ended Orbán's reign, handing power to center-right challenger Péter Magyar. The beau ideal of the illiberal right had fallen.

Hungary Endures — Then Decides +

There is something remarkable in the fact that democratic impulse survived sixteen years of Orbán's soft authoritarianism. He employed every tool short of open violence: electoral maps redrawn, independent judiciary slowly strangled, critical media starved of advertising, academic institutes quietly placed on the Fidesz payroll.

Yet the Hungarians endured. Civil society persisted in the cracks. And when the economic pain became undeniable — prices up 57 percent since 2020, nearly double the EU average; corruption visible in billion-euro contracts steered to cronies and Orbán's home village — the reservoir of grievance finally overflowed.

Hungary Under Orbán: The Ledger
  • 16 years of Fidesz rule, 2010–2026
  • 57% cumulative inflation since 2020, ~2× EU average
  • Hungary now among EU's poorest and most corrupt members
  • Billions in public contracts to Orbán allies & his home village
  • Independent media effectively eliminated or marginalized
  • Judiciary composition systematically packed by Fidesz

Péter Magyar, a telegenic former Orbán insider, made a fateful strategic choice: he said almost nothing about LGBTQ rights or immigration. He talked about corruption. He talked about prices. The electorate answered.

A Wider Retreat +

The Hungarian result did not arrive in isolation. The past month has produced a string of signals — modest but accumulating — that the European populist tide may have crested.

In Italy, Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni's attempt to expand executive power over the judiciary was rebuffed at referendum. In France, the National Rally underperformed in municipal elections, losing Marseille, Lyon, and Paris — where their candidate took just 1.6% of the vote. In Germany, the AfD surged in western state balloting yet still lost decisively in two major contests.

August 2025: far-right parties led in Britain, France, and Germany — for the first time ever. The fever since then may be breaking.

Washington Post · April 2026

None of these results is individually decisive. Collectively, they sketch an outline: the peak of the populist wave may have passed its crest, even as its structural causes — inequality, cultural anxiety, stagnant wages — remain stubbornly in place.

The Achilles' Heel: Why Populists Cannot Govern +

The structural paradox of populist governance is almost elegant in its cruelty: the very behaviors that propel populists to power reliably sabotage their performance once there. They shun credentialed experts in favor of loyalists. They undermine independent bureaucracies — including central banks — that exist precisely to cushion economies against political whim. They channel economic benefits to friendly executives in exchange for support, choking off the competitive dynamism that produces growth. They govern in mercurial, unpredictable bursts that destabilize investment and trade.

The result, reliably, is a stagnating economy overlaid with spectacular corruption. When growth is robust, the corruption can be tolerated — even embraced as theater. When growth falters and inflation bites, the corruption becomes intolerable. Jimmy Carter discovered this. Joe Biden discovered this. Now Orbán has discovered it. The political kryptonite of inflation does not ask for your party registration.

The Expert Problem

Populists dismiss professional competence as "elitist." In a complex modern economy, this produces predictable dysfunction: misallocated policy, institutional decay, regulatory capture by cronies. The bill arrives — slowly, then all at once.

The Corruption Trap

Power must be maintained through reward. Reward costs money. Money comes from public contracts. Public contracts go to the unqualified loyal. Infrastructure decays, services deteriorate, and the gap between promise and reality widens — visibly.

The Repression Ratchet

As performance disappoints, populists tighten political controls — gerrymandering, court-packing, press suppression. This buys time. But soft authoritarianism has a ceiling: when voter dissatisfaction passes it, no institutional adjustment can hold the dam.

The Mirror for America

Trump presides over flagrant corruption, rising inflation, and slowing growth — the same combination that ended Orbán. The differences between Hungary and America are vast. But the political physics of economic pain are universal.

The Bell Sounds in Washington +

Donald Trump did not merely admire Viktor Orbán. He studied him. The Heritage Foundation's Project 2025 — animated by the same Kevin Roberts who called Hungary "the model" — is in many respects an attempt to accelerate in America what Orbán achieved over sixteen patient years in Hungary: the capture of independent institutions, the subordination of expertise to loyalty, the weaponization of state resources for political reward.

Trump went further: he dispatched Vice President JD Vance to Budapest to campaign personally for Orbán in the final week. He offered Hungary financial support contingent on an Orbán victory. The investment in Orbán's survival was, at root, an investment in the proposition that the illiberal model works — that it can be exported, replicated, and vindicated.

In a YouGov poll, just 10 percent of Germans and 3 percent of Danes held a favorable view of Trump. Hungary's own Publicus Institute found 59 percent believe he contributes more to global conflict than to peace.

April 2026

The proposition has now been falsified — on its home ground, by Orbán's own electorate. The model's rejection carries a message that travels poorly through tariff walls and domestic propaganda: voters, when given a genuine choice, will ultimately price economic suffering into their ballots.

Trump's record-low international approval ratings — 14% favorability in Britain and France, 10% in Germany — compound the damage, suggesting that his intervention may itself have become an electoral liability for allied far-right candidates. The midterms are not far. Iran is not going well. Prices are not falling.

The architecture of illiberalism — courts bent, press suppressed, agencies packed with loyalists — takes years to build and, as Hungary shows, can still be dismantled when the economic pain becomes sufficient. The question for America is whether its elections, beginning with the 2026 midterms, remain free and fair enough for the same reckoning to arrive.

Coda: For Whom the Bell Tolls

"No man is an island entire of itself; every man is a piece of the continent, a part of the main... Any man's death diminishes me, because I am involved in mankind. And therefore never send to know for whom the bell tolls; it tolls for thee."

John Donne · Devotions upon Emergent Occasions, 1624

Orbán's fall is not merely the end of one man's tenure. It is the tolling of a bell for an entire model of governance — one built on grievance, sustained by corruption, and ultimately unable to deliver the prosperity it promised. The sound carries across the Atlantic.
