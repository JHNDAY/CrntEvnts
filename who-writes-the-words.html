When the portal summarizes a story,
who's really writing the words?

Every week, the Current Events portal distills dozens of news stories into crisp summaries, trending comparisons, and AfterWord reflections. The words read fluently — sometimes surprisingly well. But what is actually producing them? This question sits at the heart of what AI is, what it isn't, and what it means for how we consume and trust information.

The Short Answer

"A large language model generates the words — but the ideas, facts, and framing all originate with the journalists and analysts whose work it has read. The portal writes itself, but it couldn't write itself without them."

Discussion Questions

1

What does "generating" text actually mean? AI doesn't retrieve a stored sentence — it produces new text word by word, based on patterns in billions of documents. Does that feel like writing to you? Where does authorship begin and end?

2

What is the AI bringing — and what is it borrowing? The synthesis, the structure, the tone are AI-generated. The facts, the sourcing, the original reporting are human. Can you separate those two contributions? Should you have to?

3

What's missing that a human editor would catch? A seasoned journalist has instincts: something smells off, this source has an agenda, this framing buries the real story. Does AI have anything like that? Does it matter if it doesn't?

4

What happens to journalism if AI does the summarizing? If readers get polished AI summaries, do they still click through to original reporting? What does that do to the economics of newsrooms — and to the investigative journalism that AI depends on for its raw material?

5

Does it change how much you trust the summary? Knowing a summary was AI-generated — does that make you more skeptical? Less? The same? What would change your level of trust?

Something to Keep in Mind

AI has no stake in getting the story right. It won't lose its job, face a correction, or feel embarrassed if it misrepresents something. That's a fundamentally different relationship to truth than the one a journalist has. Whether that matters — and how much — is exactly the kind of question worth arguing about.

The Punchline Worth Sitting With

The portal summarizes journalism it could not have written. If AI erodes the economics of journalism, it eventually erodes its own source material. That's not a distant problem — it's already underway.
