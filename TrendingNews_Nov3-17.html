Trump Tariffs (42 mentions)
Economic policy, Supreme Court challenges, and global trade impacts
▼
Supreme Court Legal Challenges
Supreme Court heard oral arguments on November 5 regarding Trump's use of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to impose tariffs. Justices from both conservative and liberal wings expressed skepticism about the administration's legal authority, questioning whether Congress intended to grant the president such broad taxation powers.
Tariff Rollbacks and Food Prices
On November 14-15, Trump signed executive orders removing tariffs on beef, coffee, bananas, cocoa, tomatoes, spices, and other agricultural products in response to rising food prices and political pressure. This marked the most significant tariff backtrack of his administration.
$2,000 Rebate Proposal
Trump floated the idea of issuing $2,000 "tariff dividends" to middle and lower-income Americans using tariff revenue. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated this would require Congressional approval, and budget experts noted the plan would cost far more than tariff revenues generate.
Economic Impact
The average tax on U.S. imports has risen to nearly 18% from 2.4% before Trump's second term. Manufacturing employment has dropped by more than 40,000 jobs since April, while factory activity has declined for eight consecutive months. Ground coffee prices reached record highs of $8.41 per pound in July, up 33% year-over-year.
Trade Negotiations
The administration announced framework agreements with multiple countries including El Salvador, Argentina, Ecuador, Guatemala, Thailand, Vietnam, UK, EU, and Switzerland. China negotiations continued with temporary deals extending through November 9.

Hollowing Out of the DOJ (38 mentions)
Mass resignations, firings of career prosecutors, and dismantling of key divisions
▼
Eric Adams Case Mass Resignations (February 2025)
At least seven DOJ officials resigned in 36 hours after being ordered to dismiss corruption charges against NYC Mayor Eric Adams. Acting U.S. Attorney Danielle Sassoon and Acting Public Integrity Section head John Keller both resigned rather than comply. In resignation letters, prosecutors described a quid pro quo where Adams would help Trump's immigration enforcement in exchange for dropped charges. Assistant U.S. Attorney Hagan Scotten wrote: "I expect you will eventually find someone who is enough of a fool, or enough of a coward, to file your motion. But it was never going to be me." The event has been dubbed the "Thursday Night Massacre" or "Valentine's Day Seven," echoing Nixon's Saturday Night Massacre.
Civil Rights Division Exodus (70% Departure Rate)
As of May 2025, 70% of Civil Rights Division lawyers had left through quits, deferred resignation programs, or early retirements. Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon stated her vision to "turn the train around and drive it in the opposite direction," shelving the division's traditional mission. Attorneys were forced onto task forces focused on Trump priorities like antisemitism and transgender issues. The administration dropped investigations and withdrew statements in approximately 30 cases. Current and former officials describe this as "the dismantling of the civil rights division."
Immigration Judge Purge
The Trump administration terminated more than 80 immigration judges since taking office, with total numbers dropping from 700 to 600 nationwide. This occurred while Congress authorized hiring 100 new judges to address a 3.4 million case backlog. DOJ is training hundreds of military lawyers without immigration law backgrounds to serve temporarily as judges. Defendants increasingly fail to appear at court due to fear of ICE detention in hallways.
Career Prosecutor Firings and Intimidation
In late January 2025, multiple career prosecutors who worked on Jack Smith's team investigating Trump were fired. Former officials stated the firings were designed to "intimidate the Justice Department and FBI workforce and deter investigations of Trump's second administration." Senior career officials handling national security threats, from terrorist plots to Chinese cyberattacks, were removed or reassigned. One former FBI official asked: "Is anyone in DOJ or FBI really going to investigate that now?"
November 2025 Russia Investigation Resignations
In early November 2025, two DOJ prosecutors in the Southern District of Florida resigned after being assigned to a grand jury investigating the origins of the 2016 Trump-Russia investigation. One prosecutor told colleagues continuing would "violate their ethical responsibilities." Over 30 subpoenas were issued targeting former CIA Director John Brennan, FBI agents Peter Strzok and Lisa Page. U.S. Attorney Jason Reding Quiñones held an emergency meeting as the atmosphere became "pins and needles."
Strategic Removals and Reassignments
George Toscas, involved in the Mar-a-Lago FBI search, was reassigned to a sanctuary city task force. Eun Young Choi, who helped convict Ross Ulbricht (Silk Road founder), was similarly reassigned. The head of the Public Integrity Section was removed. Biden-appointed U.S. Attorneys across the country were terminated via email rather than being asked to resign, a departure from past practice.

Fractures in MAGA Coalition (35 mentions)

H-1B Visa Controversy
Trump's defense of H-1B visas during a November interview with Laura Ingraham sparked outrage from MAGA supporters. His statement that America doesn't have people with "certain talents" contradicted his America First base. Prominent supporters including Marjorie Taylor Greene, Jack Posobiec, Kevin Sorbo, and Laura Loomer publicly criticized the position, creating one of the first major MAGA splits since the 2024 election.
Israel Policy Divisions
Tucker Carlson's interview with Nick Fuentes and the Heritage Foundation's defense of Carlson exposed deep fractures over Israel policy. The Republican Jewish Coalition summit became a flashpoint, with signs reading "TUCKER IS NOT MAGA." The split pits pro-Israel "Israel first" Republicans against an emerging anti-Israel faction arguing US support contradicts America First principles.
Trump vs. Marjorie Taylor Greene
On November 15, Trump withdrew his support and endorsement of Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene, threatening to back a primary challenger. Greene had criticized Trump for "gaslighting the people" about whether prices have come down and for his stance on the Epstein files.
Economic Messaging Disconnect
MAGA media figures like Ben Shapiro openly acknowledged that Trump's claims about prices becoming more affordable don't match the reality many supporters face. Greene and others pushed back against Trump's economic messaging as prices remain high.
Tech Right vs. America First Wing
The divide between Silicon Valley tech figures (Elon Musk, Peter Thiel) and the paleoconservative America First wing has created tensions, particularly over H-1B visas and tariff exemptions for computer chips. Steve Bannon attempted to quell the backlash by calling Trump an "imperfect instrument" while urging supporters to stay focused.

Epstein Files (28 mentions)
Congressional release of documents, Trump's shifting position, and House vote
▼
Document Releases
On November 12, the House Oversight Committee released over 20,000 documents from Jeffrey Epstein's estate, including emails mentioning Trump. Key emails included: Epstein calling Trump "the dog that hasn't barked" and claiming Trump spent "hours at my house" with an alleged victim (2011); Epstein writing that Trump "knew about the girls as he asked Ghislaine to stop" (2019); and Epstein telling Lawrence Summers "I have met some very bad people, none as bad as Trump" (2017).
Trump's Position Reversal
On November 16, Trump dramatically reversed course and urged House Republicans to vote to release the Epstein files, calling it a "Democrat Hoax" after initially resisting the release. Speaker Mike Johnson indicated the House would vote on Tuesday, November 18, saying "We'll just get this done and move it on. There's nothing to hide."
Bipartisan Discharge Petition
Reps. Thomas Massie (R-KY) and Ro Khanna (D-CA) led a bipartisan effort forcing a House floor vote through a discharge petition. Republican support grew despite Trump's initial opposition, with expectations of around 40 GOP lawmakers voting for release.
Political Fallout
Trump withdrew his endorsement of Marjorie Taylor Greene over her support for releasing the files. Greene stated the rift "has all come down to the Epstein files" and questioned why Trump was fighting the release so hard. Right-wing media figures downplayed the document releases, with some claiming Trump was actually an FBI informant on Epstein.
Senate Uncertainty
Senate Majority Leader John Thune indicated the Senate may not take up the legislation, citing the Justice Department's existing document releases. The bill's fate in the Senate remains uncertain even if the House passes it.

Revenge Litigation by Trump Administration (22 mentions)
Criminal prosecutions of Trump critics, vindictive prosecution defenses, and DOJ controversies
▼
James Comey Prosecution
Former FBI Director James Comey was indicted in September 2025, fulfilling Trump's promise of revenge. Comey's defense argued the prosecution was vindictive, motivated solely by Trump's desire for retaliation. More than 100 former Justice Department officials, including ex-Attorney General Eric Holder, filed a brief supporting Comey's argument.
Letitia James Case
New York Attorney General Letitia James joined the growing list of Justice Department targets seeking to dismiss criminal charges by arguing prosecutions are motivated by Trump's revenge. She was charged in Virginia in November 2025.
Other Targets
The administration has directed prosecutors to investigate: Rep. LaMonica McIver, John Bolton (former National Security Advisor), Miles Taylor (who wrote warnings about Trump's authoritarian tendencies), and Chris Krebs (who assured voters the 2020 election was secure). Additionally, Trump cut deals with law firms to do free legal work rather than face penalties for hiring his opponents.
Vindictive Prosecution Defense
Multiple defendants are using the vindictive prosecution defense against Trump's Justice Department. One judge found "some evidence of vindictiveness" in these cases. The irony: When Trump was charged by special counsel Jack Smith, he claimed vindictive prosecution, but Judge Tanya Chutkan rejected his argument.
Broader Pattern
The administration has targeted institutions that defied Trump, including barring law firms from federal business, targeting universities for funding cuts, filing judicial misconduct complaints against judges who ruled against him, and revoking security clearances of about three dozen current and former officials. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth has fired military leaders perceived as critics.

Supreme Court Cases (18 mentions)
Tariff challenges, SNAP benefits, E. Jean Carroll appeal, and passport policy
▼
Tariff Cases (Learning Resources v. Trump)
The Supreme Court heard oral arguments on November 5, 2025, regarding Trump's use of IEEPA to impose tariffs. Both conservative and liberal justices expressed skepticism about the administration's legal authority. Chief Justice Roberts noted the statute says nothing about tariffs, while Justice Kagan sarcastically noted "we're in emergencies...all the time, about, like, half the world." The Court extended oral argument time to handle the complexity. A decision could come by year-end.
SNAP Benefits Dispute
Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson temporarily paused a lower court order on November 7 requiring the Trump administration to cover full SNAP food stamp benefits for millions of Americans during the government shutdown. The case involves whether the USDA must use emergency funds to pay full November benefits despite the lapse in Congressional appropriations.
E. Jean Carroll Sexual Abuse Case
On November 10, Trump asked the Supreme Court to review the $5 million civil case finding he sexually abused and defamed columnist E. Jean Carroll. Trump claims the trial judge made numerous errors, including allowing testimony from two other women who alleged assault and admitting the "Access Hollywood" tape. A separate $83 million defamation judgment was also affirmed by appeals courts.
Passport Sex Designation Policy
On November 6, the Supreme Court granted the administration's emergency request to stay a district court injunction blocking enforcement of a new passport policy requiring all passports to list an individual's sex assigned at birth. This followed Trump's January 20 executive order stating there are only two sexes determined by immutable biological factors at conception.
Hush Money Conviction Appeal
A federal appeals court on November 6 said Trump should be allowed to make a case for why federal courts should review his hush money conviction for possible conflicts with the Supreme Court's immunity decision. The panel sent the case back to district court to address questions about Trump's "good cause" for a second removal attempt.
