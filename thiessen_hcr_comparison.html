Comparative Analysis

Two Visions of 2025

Contrasting Philosophies of Governance and Social Contract in Speculative Trump Second Term Narratives

Understanding the Documents

Critical Context: Both documents analyzed here are speculative fiction written as if from late 2025/early 2026, describing Trump's hypothetical second term in past tense. Neither describes actual events—they represent predictive scenarios revealing each author's expectations, hopes, or fears.

Marc Thiessen (Washington Post)

Perspective: Former Bush speechwriter, American Enterprise Institute fellow, conservative columnist

Framing: Celebration of "accomplishments" - "objectively stunning string" of successes

Tone: Triumphant restoration of proper governance

Heather Cox Richardson

Perspective: Historian specializing in American political economy and democratic institutions

Framing: Warning of authoritarian dismantling of constitutional democracy

Tone: Alarmed but ultimately hopeful about democratic resistance

Analytical Approach: This comparison examines how fundamentally different philosophical commitments produce opposite interpretations of the same hypothetical actions. What one celebrates, the other condemns—revealing deep disagreements about the nature of legitimate governance, the social contract, and American constitutional principles.

1. Constitutional Authority & Separation of Powers
Thiessen's View: Results Trump Process
"Biden claimed he needed legislation from Congress to secure the border; Trump shut down the border in weeks with no new congressional authority."
Governing Philosophy:
  • Executive efficiency over legislative deliberation
  • Electoral mandate justifies unilateral action
  • "Getting things done" is primary measure of success
  • Congressional obstruction (even from own party) should be circumvented
  • Constitutional constraints are obstacles to overcome

Examples Celebrated:

  • Ending collective bargaining via executive order
  • Using tariff threats to circumvent trade negotiations
  • Forcing universities to change policies through federal pressure
  • Taking federal control of D.C. police
  • Deploying National Guard without state consent

Implicit Theory: Strong executive acting decisively produces better outcomes than messy democratic process. Popular mandate authorizes whatever president deems necessary.

HCR's View: Process Protects Liberty
"They have acted as if the laws, and the Constitution that underpins them, don't exist... The Constitution, judicial precedent, and the 1974 Impoundment Control Act all make it very clear that the power of the purse belongs to Congress."
Governing Philosophy:
  • Constitutional constraints protect against tyranny
  • Separation of powers is fundamental, not optional
  • Process matters as much as outcomes
  • No mandate justifies violating constitutional structure
  • Checks and balances are features, not bugs

Examples Condemned:

  • DOGE eliminating programs without congressional authorization
  • Nine emergency declarations to bypass Congress
  • Unilateral tariff war (Congress's constitutional authority)
  • Defying federal court orders on deportations
  • Federalizing state police against governors' wishes

Explicit Theory: Carl Schmitt's authoritarian philosophy—"There's no law, there's just power." Emergencies create exceptions allowing leaders to act without constitutional constraints.

The Fundamental Divide

Thiessen views constitutional process as impediment to necessary action. When Biden says he needs Congress to act, that's weakness or excuse-making. When Trump acts unilaterally, that's strength and efficiency.

Richardson views constitutional process as the essence of legitimate governance. Unilateral action isn't strength—it's authoritarianism. The process isn't obstacle but protection.

Core Question: Does democratic legitimacy flow from electoral victory (mandate theory) or from adherence to constitutional process (rule of law theory)?

2. Role of Federal Government
Thiessen's View: Government as Obstacle
"Eliminating USAID as an independent agency has been a conservative goal since the 1990s... Trump has folded its functions into the State Department, eliminated wasteful programs."
Vision of Government:
  • Smaller is better—10% workforce reduction celebrated
  • Federal programs are mostly "waste, fraud, and abuse"
  • Government services create dependency, not security
  • Regulatory state stifles freedom and prosperity
  • Private sector and individuals make better decisions than bureaucrats

Celebrated Reductions:

  • Ending collective bargaining (weakening federal worker power)
  • Shutting down USAID
  • Rolling back environmental regulations
  • Cutting Medicaid, SNAP, ACA subsidies
  • Eliminating DEI programs in universities

Underlying Philosophy: Pre-New Deal vision where federal government provides defense and basic functions, but individuals/states/markets handle most else.

HCR's View: Government as Protector
"That dismantling [of USAID] has caused the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people... Americans vocally objected to the dramatic cuts to the civil service... and the end of government services they had never imagined losing."
Vision of Government:
  • Essential services protect vulnerable populations
  • Expertise in agencies serves public interest
  • Regulations prevent harm, not just impose costs
  • Federal programs address market failures
  • Democratic government accountable to people, not just markets

Losses Mourned:

  • USAID (combating disease and malnutrition globally)
  • 10% of federal workforce (institutional knowledge lost)
  • Government services people "never imagined losing"
  • Privacy protections (DOGE access to privileged information)
  • Due process protections for immigrants

Underlying Philosophy: Post-New Deal vision where federal government actively promotes general welfare, protects rights, and provides social insurance.

The Federal Power Paradox

Thiessen's Contradiction: Celebrates both reducing federal government (USAID, regulations, workforce) AND expanding federal power (forcing universities to change, federalizing police, military deployments, tariff authority). The pattern: shrink government protections/services, expand government coercion/enforcement.

HCR's Observation: The administration claims to reduce government while actually centralizing unprecedented power in the executive—eliminating constraints on presidential authority while dismantling democratic accountability.

3. The Social Contract: Individual vs. Collective
Thiessen: Individual Responsibility Richardson: Collective Responsibility
Economic Security

Approach: Tax cuts, reduced benefits, market solutions

Examples:

  • Cut taxes on tips, overtime, Social Security
  • Extended 2017 tax cuts for wealthy/corporations
  • Cut Medicaid, SNAP, ACA subsidies
  • "Trump Accounts" ($1,000 trust funds)

Theory: Lower taxes → individual investment decisions → personal prosperity. Government programs create dependency. Markets allocate resources efficiently.
Economic Security

Approach: Social programs provide safety net

Concerns:

  • Cutting Medicaid/SNAP harms vulnerable families
  • Reducing ACA subsidies leaves people uninsured
  • Tax cuts for wealthy while cutting services for poor
  • $1,000 accounts don't address actual barriers to mobility

Theory: Markets fail to provide basic security. Collective insurance through government protects all citizens. Social contract means shared responsibility for general welfare.
Worker Rights

Approach: "Hire and fire based on merit"

Celebration: "He ended collective bargaining for a million federal workers... gutting public worker unions"

Theory: Unions distort labor markets, protect incompetence, reduce efficiency. Merit-based employment produces better outcomes. Individuals negotiate own terms.
Worker Rights

Approach: Collective bargaining essential protection

Concern: "Merit" can mask political loyalty tests; removing protections enables arbitrary treatment, politicizes civil service

Theory: Power imbalance between employer/employee requires collective action. Due process protects against arbitrary authority. Unions give workers voice in workplace conditions.
Immigration

Approach: "Virtual halt" to illegal migration = success

Celebration: "95 percent lower" border encounters; "no illegal migrants released for seven months"

Theory: Nations have right to control borders. Rule of law requires enforcement. "Illegal" immigrants broke law entering and don't deserve same treatment as citizens/legal residents.
Immigration

Approach: Due process applies to all persons

Concern: 230+ sent to terrorist prison (nearly half with legal status); defiance of court orders; sweeping raids grab integrated community members; asylum rights violated

Theory: Constitution protects "persons," not just citizens. Due process is fundamental right. Humanitarian obligations to asylum seekers. Integration into communities matters morally.
Healthcare

Approach: Individual/family responsibility

Position: Cut ACA marketplace subsidies; reduced Medicaid; personal health decisions

Theory: Healthcare is private matter. Government involvement creates inefficiency, raises costs, limits choice. Individuals should purchase coverage or bear consequences.
Healthcare

Approach: Universal access as collective goal

Position: Cutting subsidies leaves people uninsured; Medicaid cuts harm vulnerable; USAID dismantling causes deaths globally

Theory: Health is public good. Disease doesn't respect property lines. Society benefits from healthy population. Collective action solves market failures in healthcare.
Competing Visions of Social Contract

Thiessen's Contract: Government protects borders, enforces laws, defends nation, and gets out of the way. Citizens succeed or fail based on merit and effort. Safety net minimal—primarily private charity and family. Individual liberty maximized; collective obligations minimized.

Richardson's Contract: Government actively promotes general welfare through programs, regulations, protections. Citizens pool resources to address challenges beyond individual capacity. Social insurance provides security. Liberty requires material preconditions government helps secure.

Historical Context: These represent pre-New Deal vs. post-New Deal visions of American democracy's basic bargain.

4. Rule of Law: Means vs. Ends
Thiessen: Ends Justify Means
Governing Approach:
  • Results are primary measure of legitimacy
  • Legal technicalities shouldn't prevent necessary action
  • "Getting things done" trumps procedural niceties
  • Critics focus too much on process, not enough on outcomes
  • Electoral mandate authorizes expansive interpretation of authority

Celebrated Actions:

  • "Captured terrorist" in 6 weeks (without detailing process)
  • "Forced" Panama to crack down on China
  • Military strikes in Nigeria without congressional authorization
  • "Obliterated" Iranian nuclear program
  • Shut down border without congressional authority

Implicit Message: When goals are righteous, questioning means is pedantic. Quick, decisive action > slow, deliberative process.

HCR: Means Define Legitimacy
Governing Approach:
  • Process determines legitimacy, not just outcomes
  • Rule of law means law constrains even president
  • Ends don't justify unconstitutional means
  • Adherence to process distinguishes democracy from authoritarianism
  • Short-term efficiency may produce long-term tyranny

Key Violations Highlighted:

  • Deporting people after court orders not to
  • Refusing to obey federal judge AND Supreme Court
  • Missing legally-mandated deadline (Epstein files)
  • Nine emergency declarations to bypass Congress
  • Military operations without congressional authorization

Explicit Warning: "There's no law, there's just power" (Vance/Schmitt) represents abandonment of rule of law for rule by strongman.

Philosophical Foundations

Thiessen's Tradition

Intellectual Heritage:

  • Unitary executive theory (Cheney/Yoo)
  • Schmittian decisionism (sovereignty = decision on exception)
  • Hamiltonian strong executive
  • National security state liberalism

Core Belief: Vigorous executive action is American tradition. Washington, Lincoln, FDR all expanded presidential power when circumstances demanded. Separation of powers shouldn't paralyze government.

Richardson's Tradition

Intellectual Heritage:

  • Madisonian checks and balances
  • Republican anti-monarchism (1776)
  • Rule of law constitutionalism
  • Popular sovereignty through representation

Core Belief: American founding explicitly rejected concentrated power. Constitution designed to prevent tyranny through division of authority. Law must constrain rulers—that's what distinguishes republic from despotism.

5. Democratic Accountability & Popular Will
Thiessen: Mandate Theory
"Trump took the oath of office with a popular vote mandate to do everything on this list — and more."
Theory of Legitimacy:
  • Electoral victory = policy mandate
  • Winners empowered to implement agenda
  • Opposition should defer to election results
  • Voters understood and endorsed the program
  • Next election is accountability mechanism

Application: Since Trump won, he's entitled to:

  • Circumvent Congress (he has "mandate")
  • Override courts (voters spoke)
  • Ignore procedural constraints (elections have consequences)
  • Claim broad interpretation of powers (legitimized by victory)

Note on Victory: Thiessen claims "popular vote mandate" but acknowledges Trump won "less than 50%" of vote. Mandate claimed despite plurality, not majority, victory.

HCR: Continuous Accountability
"Over the course of 2025, the administration's refusal to recognize the tenets of American democracy has roused the American people to defend that democracy."
Theory of Legitimacy:
  • Elections don't grant unlimited authority
  • Constitution constrains even majority will
  • Democratic participation continuous, not just elections
  • Protests, lawsuits, organizing are legitimate resistance
  • Public opinion matters between elections

Application: Despite Trump's election, citizens exercise power through:

  • Lawsuits challenging illegal actions
  • Mass protests (7+ million on Oct 18)
  • November electoral backlash (Democratic sweep)
  • Bipartisan congressional resistance (Epstein files)
  • Economic pressure ("Tesla Takedowns")

Evidence: Democratic victories in November 2025 show people rejecting administration agenda. 21% of Republican-held seats flipped. "With disapproval at near-historic levels, voters strongly backed Democrats."

Competing Models of Democracy

Thiessen's Model: Electoral majoritarianism—winners take power and implement agenda until next election. Opposition's role is minimal; complaints about process are obstructionism. Democracy = periodic elections + executive action.

Richardson's Model: Constitutional democracy—winners govern within constitutional constraints, opposition maintains legitimacy, civil society actively participates, courts check executive, Congress remains coequal branch. Democracy = elections + continuous participation + institutional constraints.

Key Difference: Is democracy primarily about who governs (Thiessen) or how they govern (Richardson)?

7. The Proper Scope of Presidential Power
Expansive Executive Authority
Powers Celebrated as Appropriate:
  • Unilateral tariff imposition
  • Emergency declarations to bypass Congress
  • Military strikes without authorization (Nigeria, Iran)
  • Eliminating executive branch positions/programs
  • Forcing policy changes in universities
  • Taking control of local police forces
  • Deploying military domestically
  • Refusing congressional oversight

Theoretical Justification: President as unitary executive head with broad Article II powers, particularly in foreign affairs, national security, and law enforcement. Congressional micromanagement hamstrings effective leadership. Courts should defer to executive judgment.

Historical Models: Lincoln (Civil War powers), FDR (New Deal/WWII), Reagan (Iran-Contra latitude), Bush 43 (War on Terror authorities)

Constrained Executive Authority
Powers Exceeded:
  • Tariffs (Congress's Article I power)
  • Emergency declarations used to circumvent Congress
  • Military action without congressional authorization
  • Impoundment (spending decisions belong to Congress)
  • Coercing institutions through federal funding
  • Federalizing state/local police
  • Domestic military deployment
  • Defying court orders

Theoretical Justification: President is one of three coequal branches, with powers enumerated and limited. Congress makes laws, appropriates money, declares war. Courts interpret law and protect rights. Executive "takes Care" laws are faithfully executed—not that president makes law.

Historical Warning: Trump statement ("I have the right to do anything I want") plus Vance/Schmitt philosophy ("There's no law, there's just power") explicitly rejects constitutional constraints—the definition of authoritarianism.

8. National Security & Military Force
Decisive Action Saves Lives

Celebrated Uses of Force:

  • #1: "Obliterated the Iranian nuclear program" (Operation Midnight Hammer) - "possibly the most important and courageous foreign policy decision any president has made in the past half-century"
  • #5: U.S. airstrikes in Nigeria on Christmas Day against Islamic State
  • #6: "Eliminated dozens of suspected Venezuelan drug boats"
  • #20: Captured "top terrorist" responsible for Abbey Gate bombing

Philosophy: Strength prevents war. Decisive military action demonstrates resolve, deters adversaries, protects Americans. Hesitation invites aggression. Better to act preemptively than wait for attacks. Military power is moral when defending freedom.

Process View: Speed is virtue. Consulting Congress wastes time, leaks plans, ties president's hands. Commander-in-Chief should act when he judges necessary.

Unchecked Power Invites Abuse

Concerns About Force:

  • If "Operation Midnight Hammer" occurred, would be act of war without congressional authorization
  • Nigeria strikes violate sovereignty of ally
  • "Suspected" drug boats—were they confirmed? What about due process?
  • Domestic military deployments (LA, Portland, Chicago) against governors' wishes

Philosophy: Unconstrained military power is dangerous. Presidents must consult Congress (War Powers). Unilateral strikes create enemies, provoke retaliation, destabilize regions. International law matters. Domestic military deployment threatens liberty.

Process View: Constitutional checks exist precisely because war power is most dangerous authority. Requiring congressional authorization is protection, not impediment. Framers feared executive warmaking.

Competing Security Philosophies

Thiessen believes world is dangerous, adversaries respect only strength, and constrained president invites attack. Quick, decisive, sometimes secretive action prevents larger conflicts. Congress lacks expertise and decisiveness for national security.

Richardson believes unconstrained executive warmaking historically produces disasters (Vietnam, Iraq). Congressional deliberation may be slower but prevents rash action. Domestic military deployment is road to authoritarianism. International law protects Americans abroad.

9. Truth, Evidence, and Reality
Claims Without Evidence

Unsubstantiated Assertions:

  • "Obliterated the Iranian nuclear program" - No independent verification
  • "Greatest peacemaking records in U.S. history" - Resolving India-Pakistan Kashmir dispute, Congo-Rwanda conflict, etc.
  • "Brought war between Israel and Iran to an end after 12 days" - When did this war occur?
  • "Deaths of hundreds of thousands" from USAID dismantling - cited by HCR
  • Crime dropped "by double-digits in virtually every category" - No data sources

Methodological Approach: Claims are asserted confidently without evidence, sources, or verification. Article written as factual reporting of historical events despite being speculative fiction about future.

Epistemology: Political narrative shapes reality. If conservative movement believes something happened and should be celebrated, detailed evidence is unnecessary. Ideological allies will accept claims; opponents will reject regardless of evidence.

Detailed Chronology

Documented Assertions:

  • Specific dates for protests, deployments, legislation
  • Named individuals (Kilmar Ábrego García)
  • Specific institutional findings (Cato Institute on workforce cuts)
  • Concrete electoral results (Virginia, New Jersey, Miami margins)
  • References to specific laws (1974 Impoundment Control Act)

Methodological Approach: Builds detailed, chronological narrative with specific actors, dates, and institutional responses. While still speculative fiction, creates verisimilitude through specificity.

Epistemology: Historical method—even in speculative mode—requires specificity, chronology, institutional detail. Narrative must be plausible given known actors, institutions, and precedents.

The Verification Problem

Critical Reality: Both pieces are speculative fiction about 2025 presented as historical analysis. Neither has occurred. Comparing their "accuracy" is impossible because neither describes actual events.

What We Can Compare: Their plausibility given known patterns, institutional constraints, and political dynamics. HCR's specificity about legal/institutional resistance is more plausible because courts, Congress, and federal system have established procedures. Thiessen's claims about resolving century-old conflicts in months strain credulity.

Deeper Issue: These pieces reveal how political tribes increasingly inhabit separate information ecosystems where basic facts are disputed. If 2025 unfolds, will Thiessen and HCR even agree on what happened?

10. Vision of America's Future
Restoration of Traditional Order
Goals Achieved:
  • Smaller government (10% workforce reduction)
  • Weakened unions (collective bargaining ended)
  • Reduced regulations (environmental, labor, financial)
  • Lower taxes (especially for wealthy)
  • Strong borders (immigration "virtually halted")
  • Restored cultural norms (gender policy, university DEI)
  • Robust military (defense spending increased)
  • American dominance (tariffs, Iran, NATO spending)

Vision: America returns to pre-New Deal principles—minimal government, maximal individual freedom, strong national defense, traditional cultural values. Federal government protects borders, enforces law, maintains military, but otherwise lets markets and individuals determine outcomes. Merit and personal responsibility replace government programs. American power projects strength globally.

Historical Model: 1920s prosperity (before Depression), Reagan's morning in America, founding-era limited government

Defense of Constitutional Democracy
Democracy Preserved Through:
  • Legal resistance (Democratic AGs' lawsuits succeed)
  • Popular mobilization (7+ million march)
  • Electoral accountability (Democratic sweep November 2025)
  • Bipartisan congressional action (Epstein files)
  • Judicial independence (courts rule against administration)
  • Civil society resilience (protests, organizing)
  • Institutional resistance (career civil servants)

Vision: America reaffirms constitutional principles—rule of law, separation of powers, due process, democratic participation. Citizens actively defend democratic institutions against authoritarian encroachment. Government serves people through representative democracy constrained by constitutional structure. Approaching 250th anniversary of independence, Americans reject return to monarchy.

Historical Model: Founding rejection of King George III, Reconstruction amendments, New Deal response to Depression, Civil Rights Movement

Fundamentally Incompatible Futures

These aren't just policy disagreements. They represent incompatible visions of what America is and should be:

  • Thiessen: America is exceptional nation that thrives when government is constrained and individuals are free. Return to founding principles means limiting federal power (except in defense/borders). Traditional cultural values should prevail.
  • Richardson: America is experiment in self-government through constitutional democracy. Founding principles include rule of law constraining even majority will. Federal government legitimately promotes general welfare. Cultural pluralism reflects democratic values.

One sees Trump's actions as restoration; other sees them as revolution against constitutional order. One celebrates efficiency; other defends process. One wants pre-New Deal America; other wants to preserve post-New Deal settlement. These differences may be irreconcilable.

Methodological Note on Comparative Analysis

Methodological Note on Comparative Analysis

Challenge: Both documents are speculative fiction presented as factual analysis. This creates unique analytical problem—we're comparing not actual events but competing predictive narratives that reveal authors' expectations, fears, and values.

What This Comparison Reveals:

  • Not accuracy (neither has occurred)
  • Not which will happen (futures are uncertain)
  • But rather: How deep philosophical commitments shape interpretation of political actions

Key Insight: The same hypothetical actions (ending collective bargaining, deploying military, defying courts, cutting programs) are celebrated by Thiessen and condemned by Richardson. This isn't simply partisan disagreement—it reflects fundamental differences in:

  • What constitutes legitimate governance
  • The proper relationship between citizen and state
  • Whether process or results determine legitimacy
  • The role of constitutional constraints
  • The nature of democracy itself

Broader Implication: These incompatible frameworks make productive democratic discourse increasingly difficult. When sides can't agree on what constitutes legitimate governance, every action becomes existential battle—either salvation or tyranny, restoration or revolution. Finding common ground requires addressing these foundational disagreements about governance philosophy, not just policy details.

What Next? Speculating on Possible Futures

Given these fundamentally incompatible visions of governance, what are the likely trajectories for American democracy?

Both Thiessen and Richardson wrote speculative pieces imagining 2025. As we approach that date in reality, we can identify several plausible scenarios based on the deep philosophical divisions revealed in their contrasting narratives. These scenarios aren't predictions—they're explorations of possibilities that emerge from incompatible governance philosophies attempting to coexist within one political system.

Scenario 1: Partisan Oscillation (Business as Usual)
The Pendulum Keeps Swinging
Moderate Probability

How It Unfolds:

American democracy continues its recent pattern of sharp oscillations between parties. Each administration attempts to implement its philosophical vision, the other side resists through courts/states/Congress, and then power shifts in the next election. Trump's second term (if it occurs) implements some conservative priorities but faces significant institutional resistance. Democrats win back power in 2028 or 2032 and reverse course. The cycle continues.

Key Characteristics:

  • No side achieves decisive, permanent victory
  • Institutional guardrails (courts, federalism, Congress) constrain but don't prevent executive action
  • Each party gets periodic chances to implement agenda
  • Policies lurch back and forth rather than building cumulative change
  • Governance philosophy differences persist but system remains intact
  • Public exhaustion with constant policy reversals grows

Why It's Plausible:

  • This has been the pattern since 2000—power shifts regularly
  • Neither party has built durable electoral coalition
  • Institutional structures designed to prevent rapid change still function
  • Courts have shown willingness to check executive overreach from both parties
  • American system historically resilient to authoritarian capture

What It Means:

  • For Governance: Instability but not collapse; policies lack consistency; long-term planning difficult
  • For Citizens: Constant political combat exhausts civic engagement; policy uncertainty affects decisions
  • For Democracy: System survives but doesn't thrive; legitimacy questions persist; polarization continues
  • For the Divide: Philosophical differences unresolved; each side waits for next chance to implement vision

Critical Risk: This pattern might not be sustainable indefinitely. Each oscillation potentially damages institutions further. At some point, one side might succeed in permanently entrenching their governance philosophy, or the system might break under accumulated strain.

Scenario 2: Conservative Constitutional Revolution
Thiessen's Vision Realized
Moderate Probability

How It Unfolds:

Conservative movement achieves its century-long goal of dismantling New Deal state. Through combination of unified Republican control, sympathetic federal judiciary (especially Supreme Court), and state-level dominance, conservatives successfully implement their governance philosophy. Federal workforce dramatically reduced, regulatory state dismantled, social programs cut, union power eliminated, and executive authority expanded (when Republicans hold presidency) or constrained (when Democrats do).

Key Characteristics:

  • Constitutional interpretation shifts to pre-New Deal understanding
  • Supreme Court majority provides judicial backing for changes
  • Federal government's role in economy/society significantly reduced
  • States gain more autonomy (especially conservative states)
  • Market mechanisms replace many government programs
  • Individual responsibility emphasized over collective provision
  • Strong executive power in national security/borders

Why It's Plausible:

  • Conservatives have invested decades in building institutional capacity (Federalist Society, think tanks, media)
  • Supreme Court already showing willingness to overturn longstanding precedents (Roe, Chevron deference)
  • Republican structural advantages (Senate, Electoral College, gerrymandering) could enable sustained control
  • Project 2025 and similar initiatives provide detailed implementation plans
  • Economic disruption might create demand for "smaller government" solutions

What It Means:

  • For Governance: Dramatic shift in federal-state balance; regulatory rollback; reduced government capacity
  • For Citizens: Less government support but also less constraint; outcomes depend more on market position and individual resources; increased inequality likely
  • For Democracy: System remains electoral democracy but with different constitutional understanding; questions about whether process changes were legitimate
  • For the Divide: One side achieves decisive victory; opposition must adapt or wait generations to reverse

Richardson's Concern: This scenario risks authoritarian drift. Expanded executive power, weakened institutional constraints, and reduced democratic participation could enable strongman governance even within formally democratic structures. The question is whether constitutional minimalism leads to freedom or oligarchy.

Counter-Argument: Conservatives argue this restores proper constitutional balance, not undermines democracy. Rolling back administrative state and returning power to states/individuals enhances liberty. Federal overreach was the aberration; this is restoration.

Scenario 3: Progressive Resurgence and New Social Contract
Richardson's Hope Realized
Lower Probability (But Possible)

How It Unfolds:

Richardson's predicted popular resistance (7 million marching, electoral backlash) materializes and sustains. Younger voters, increasingly diverse population, and those experiencing economic precarity create durable coalition supporting expanded government role. Democrats achieve sustained congressional majorities, win presidency, and eventually shift Supreme Court composition. New social compact emerges addressing climate, healthcare, inequality, and democratic participation.

Key Characteristics:

  • Strengthened social safety net (healthcare, childcare, education)
  • Enhanced worker protections and union power
  • Robust regulatory state addressing climate, inequality, corporate power
  • Campaign finance reform and voting rights expansion
  • Constrained executive power through legislative checks
  • Active federal role in promoting general welfare
  • Constitutional interpretation emphasizing positive rights

Why It's Plausible:

  • Demographic trends favor Democrats (younger, more diverse electorate)
  • Popular support for many progressive policies (healthcare, climate action, higher minimum wage)
  • Economic inequality creating demand for government intervention
  • Climate crisis potentially forcing collective action
  • Younger generations show more skepticism of pure market solutions

What It Means:

  • For Governance: Expanded federal capacity; robust regulatory framework; greater government role in economy
  • For Citizens: More social insurance and support; greater equality; some increased constraints on market actors
  • For Democracy: Enhanced participation mechanisms; constrained executive power; stronger institutional checks
  • For the Divide: One side achieves decisive victory; conservatives must adapt or wait for pendulum swing

Thiessen's Concern: This scenario risks European-style social democracy with high taxes, reduced dynamism, dependency culture, and government overreach into private life. Expanding federal power and weakening market mechanisms could stifle innovation and freedom.

Counter-Argument: Progressives argue this addresses market failures and creates conditions for genuine freedom. Without economic security, healthcare, and education, liberty is illusory. Social democracy in practice delivers better outcomes than unregulated capitalism.

Why Lower Probability: Democratic coalition faces significant challenges—must hold together diverse constituencies with competing priorities; structural disadvantages in Senate and Electoral College; judicial obstacles from conservative court majority. Path to sustained power more difficult than for Republicans.

Scenario 4: Constitutional Crisis and Breakdown
When Incompatible Visions Collide
Lower Probability (But Catastrophic)

How It Unfolds:

The fundamental incompatibility between Thiessen and Richardson's governance philosophies becomes unsustainable. A constitutional crisis emerges—perhaps disputed election, president refusing to leave office, mass defiance of Supreme Court, state nullification of federal law, or breakdown of congressional function. Neither side accepts the other's legitimacy to govern. Institutional mechanisms for resolving disputes fail. Constitutional system strains to breaking point.

Possible Triggers:

  • Disputed Election: Close presidential race where both sides claim victory; competing slates of electors; no clear constitutional resolution; both sides claiming legitimacy
  • Executive Defiance: President openly refuses to comply with court orders (as Richardson imagined with CECOT deportations); Congress unable or unwilling to check; constitutional crisis over separation of powers
  • State Nullification: Red/blue states refuse to implement federal laws they view as illegitimate; federal government unable to enforce compliance; practical federalism breakdown
  • Congressional Paralysis: Repeated shutdowns, debt ceiling crises, inability to pass basic governance legislation; government functioning breaks down
  • Judicial Legitimacy Crisis: One party refuses to accept Supreme Court decisions as legitimate; court-packing threats or defiance; judicial branch loses authority

Why It's Possible (Though Unlikely):

  • Sides increasingly view each other as existential threats
  • Norms constraining behavior have weakened significantly
  • Trust in institutions at historic lows
  • Constitutional system relies on good faith actors; what if they disappear?
  • Information ecosystems so separate that shared reality doesn't exist
  • Political leaders willing to push boundaries for short-term advantage

What It Means:

  • For Governance: Breakdown of basic governmental functions; uncertainty about who has authority
  • For Citizens: Economic disruption, security concerns, inability to make long-term plans
  • For Democracy: Potential end of constitutional democracy as we know it; could lead to authoritarianism, secession, or reconstitution
  • For the Divide: Conflict becomes overt rather than contained within system

Historical Precedent: The only time American constitutional system fully broke down was 1861—when incompatible visions of federalism and slavery led to Civil War. That breakdown required specific conditions (slavery as moral crisis, regional concentration of interests, economic systems in fundamental conflict) that don't fully map to today. But it demonstrates that constitutional systems can fail when underlying disagreements become irreconcilable.

Why Still Lower Probability: Most Americans, including elites, have stake in system's continuation. Economic integration makes secession impractical. Military remains professional and apolitical. Most constitutional disputes still resolved through established procedures. Crisis would require cascade of failures and conscious decisions to break system rather than work within it.

Scenario 5: Muddling Through—Pluralism Without Agreement
Learning to Live with Deep Disagreement
Moderate Probability

How It Unfolds:

Americans gradually accept that fundamental philosophical agreement isn't necessary for system to function. Rather than one side achieving decisive victory or system breaking down, society develops ways to accommodate deep disagreement. This isn't "coming together" or "healing divisions"—it's acknowledging that Thiessen and Richardson represent irreconcilable but both legitimate visions of American governance, and creating structures that allow both to coexist.

Key Characteristics:

  • Stronger federalism allowing states to implement different models
  • Clearer boundaries on federal authority reducing scope for conflict
  • Constitutional amendments (unlikely but possible) clarifying disputed powers
  • Cultural acceptance that Americans disagree about fundamental questions
  • Reduced expectations that federal government will solve all problems
  • Greater tolerance for policy variation across jurisdictions
  • Focus on protecting process rather than imposing specific outcomes

Why It's Plausible:

  • Americans have history of accommodating deep disagreements (religion being prime example)
  • Federal system designed to permit policy diversity
  • Most people more pragmatic than ideological—care more about daily life than governance philosophy
  • Economic interdependence creates incentives for system maintenance
  • Neither side strong enough to fully defeat other
  • Cultural exhaustion with constant political combat might drive accommodation

What It Looks Like in Practice:

  • Blue States: Implement robust social programs, strong unions, extensive regulations, high taxes, expansive rights
  • Red States: Lower taxes, minimal regulation, right-to-work laws, market-based solutions, traditional cultural policies
  • Federal Government: Focuses on genuinely national issues (defense, interstate commerce, constitutional rights); reduced role in social policy
  • Supreme Court: Enforces constitutional minimums but allows states wide latitude in governance approaches
  • Culture: Accepts that Americans can't agree on basic questions; reduces moral intensity of politics

What It Means:

  • For Governance: More state autonomy, less federal uniformity, policy variation becomes norm
  • For Citizens: Where you live matters more; can "vote with feet" by moving to jurisdiction matching values
  • For Democracy: System persists by allowing disagreement rather than resolving it; stability through pluralism
  • For the Divide: Philosophical differences acknowledged as permanent feature, not problem to solve

Challenges:

  • Some issues genuinely national (climate, pandemics, national security) and can't be addressed state-by-state
  • Economic inequality between regions could increase
  • Civil rights protections might vary dramatically by location
  • People without resources to move stuck in places whose policies they oppose
  • Cultural sorting could further polarize regions

Historical Model: This resembles pre-Civil War federalism in some ways (states having fundamentally different social systems) but without slavery's moral crisis. Also resembles current European Union where member states maintain very different welfare/economic models while cooperating on shared interests.

Scenario 6: Realignment and New Consensus
Beyond Current Paradigms
Lower Probability (But Transformative)

How It Unfolds:

Current political coalitions break apart and realign around new dimensions entirely. The Thiessen vs. Richardson framework becomes obsolete as new challenges (climate crisis, AI/automation, global competition with China, demographic transformation) create different political cleavages. New consensus emerges that neither current side would fully recognize.

Possible New Cleavages:

  • Globalist vs. Nationalist: Not left/right but integration vs. sovereignty
  • Technocratic vs. Populist: Expert governance vs. direct democracy
  • Growth vs. Sustainability: Development vs. environmental limits
  • Human vs. Post-Human: Traditional life vs. technological enhancement
  • Universal vs. Particular: Common humanity vs. group identities

Why It's Possible:

  • Current coalitions are unstable—working-class voters shifting, college-educated realigning
  • Neither party has clear answer to emerging challenges (AI displacement, climate, China)
  • Younger generations don't fit neatly into current paradigms
  • Historical precedent: New Deal realignment replaced previous party system
  • Crisis (depression, war, pandemic, climate disaster) could force new thinking

What It Might Look Like:

  • New party system emerging around different issues
  • Current "conservative" and "progressive" labels become meaningless
  • Policy combinations that don't map to current left/right (nationalist welfare state? libertarian environmentalism?)
  • Different regional/demographic coalitions
  • Fresh approaches to governance questions

Historical Analogy: The Republican Party of 1936 would be unrecognizable to 1896 Republicans. The Democratic Party of 1968 bore little resemblance to 1928 Democrats. Major realignments do happen—usually catalyzed by crisis that makes old debates irrelevant.

Why Lower Probability: Realignments are rare, require major catalyzing events, and face resistance from entrenched interests. Current parties have strong institutional advantages. Would require both crisis and new political entrepreneurs offering compelling alternative vision.

Critical Variables Determining Which Future We Get
What Will Tip the Balance?

Which scenario unfolds depends on several critical variables:

1. Electoral Outcomes (2024-2032)

  • Does one party achieve sustained control of presidency + Congress + states?
  • Do structural factors (Electoral College, Senate apportionment) continue favoring Republicans?
  • Can Democrats overcome disadvantages to build durable majority?
  • Do close elections continue creating legitimacy crises?

2. Supreme Court Composition

  • Does 6-3 conservative majority persist or shift?
  • Will Court overturn more precedents (admin state, labor law, environmental protection)?
  • Does judicial legitimacy hold or erode further?
  • Will either party attempt court expansion?

3. Economic Conditions

  • Does inequality continue growing or stabilize?
  • How does AI/automation affect employment and wages?
  • Does economic growth continue or do we face stagnation/decline?
  • Can market-based systems address climate transition?

4. Crisis Events

  • Major climate disasters requiring collective response?
  • Economic crisis (financial collapse, depression)?
  • War or major security threat?
  • Pandemic or other public health emergency?
  • Political crisis (disputed election, violence, institutional breakdown)?

5. Demographic and Cultural Shifts

  • How do younger, more diverse generations vote as they age?
  • Do culture war issues intensify or fade?
  • Can parties adapt to changing electorate?
  • Does geographic sorting accelerate?

6. Institutional Resilience

  • Do norms constraining behavior recover or erode further?
  • Can courts maintain independence and authority?
  • Will military remain apolitical?
  • Do career civil servants preserve institutional knowledge?
  • Can Congress recover functional capacity?

7. Information Ecosystem

  • Do separate information bubbles persist or can shared reality emerge?
  • How does AI affect truth/misinformation?
  • Can journalism maintain credibility across political divide?
  • Do social media platforms become more or less polarizing?

8. Elite Behavior

  • Will political leaders choose system preservation or short-term advantage?
  • Can responsible actors in both parties constrain extremes?
  • Do economic elites prioritize stability or ideological victory?
  • Will military/civil service leaders uphold constitutional duties if tested?
Most Likely Future: A Synthesis Prediction
The Messy, Unsatisfying Reality

Highest Probability: Scenario 1 (Partisan Oscillation) morphing into Scenario 5 (Muddling Through)

The most likely future combines elements from multiple scenarios:

Short-Term (2025-2028): Partisan oscillation continues. If Trump wins, he attempts to implement Thiessen-style agenda but faces significant resistance from courts, states, and institutions. Policy wins are real but limited. If Democrat wins, they attempt progressive agenda but face similar constraints. No decisive victory for either philosophy. System remains stressed but functional.

Medium-Term (2028-2035): Exhaustion with constant political combat and policy instability drives gradual movement toward greater federalism. States increasingly go their own ways on social policy, healthcare, labor law, environment. Federal government maintains role in defense, interstate commerce, and constitutional rights but reduced capacity in social policy. Supreme Court allows greater state autonomy. Cultural acceptance grows that Americans disagree on fundamental questions and forcing uniformity is counterproductive.

Key Factors Driving This Outcome:

  • Neither side strong enough for decisive victory (geographic/demographic distribution prevents sustained dominance)
  • Institutional resilience stronger than extremists on both sides believe
  • Economic interdependence creates powerful incentives against breakdown
  • Most Americans more pragmatic than ideological—want system to work even if imperfectly
  • Federal system designed for precisely this kind of accommodation

What This Means Practically:

  • Greater policy divergence between states—California looks increasingly different from Texas
  • Federal government continues functioning but with reduced scope
  • Supreme Court acts as minimal floor for rights but allows state variation
  • Culture accepts (grudgingly) that Americans can't agree and that's okay
  • Geographic sorting continues as people move to jurisdictions matching values
  • National political combat remains intense but with lower actual stakes as more policy moves to states

Why This Isn't Satisfying to Anyone:

  • Thiessen's Side: Doesn't achieve full restoration of pre-New Deal America; blue states maintain expansive government
  • Richardson's Side: Doesn't prevent dismantling of federal protections; red states roll back rights and programs
  • Both Sides: Neither gets to impose their vision on whole country; must accept others living differently

But Why It Might Work:

This outcome represents not consensus but acceptance of persistent disagreement. It's unsatisfying precisely because it doesn't resolve the fundamental philosophical divide—it works around it. But that might be the most realistic path forward. Constitutional democracy doesn't require agreement on ultimate questions; it requires willingness to follow shared procedures despite disagreement. The alternative to muddling through might be breakdown—and most Americans, including elites, have stake in avoiding that.

Critical Unknown: Can genuine national challenges (climate crisis, great power competition, pandemic response, economic crisis) be addressed with this level of fragmentation? Some problems require coordinated national action. If we face crisis requiring federal response, will fragmented system prove adequate? This is the real test of whether "muddling through" is sustainable long-term or just delays reckoning.

Final Reflection: What These Scenarios Reveal
Beyond Prediction

These scenarios are not predictions in the traditional sense. No one can forecast political futures with confidence—too many variables, too much contingency, too much depends on individual decisions and chance events. The value of scenario planning isn't accuracy but preparation.

What This Exercise Reveals:

1. The Stakes Are Real

Thiessen and Richardson aren't just disagreeing about policy details. They represent incompatible visions of legitimate governance. What one considers freedom, the other sees as exploitation. What one calls efficiency, the other terms authoritarianism. These differences have concrete consequences for how power is distributed, who is protected, and what kind of society we build.

2. The Divide Is Philosophical, Not Just Partisan

Swapping partisan labels doesn't resolve these tensions. The questions at stake—individual vs. collective responsibility, means vs. ends, process vs. results, constraint vs. efficiency—are permanent features of democratic politics. They existed before current parties and will persist after them.

3. Multiple Futures Are Possible

Nothing is determined. Conservative victory isn't inevitable. Progressive resurgence isn't impossible. Breakdown isn't guaranteed. Accommodation might work. The future depends on choices people make—especially elites and activists who shape political possibilities.

4. All Outcomes Have Costs

There's no "good" scenario where everyone gets what they want. Thiessen's restoration leaves vulnerable populations without protections. Richardson's democracy requires extensive government that some view as oppressive. Muddling through leaves both sides unsatisfied and may fail to address collective challenges. Breakdown is catastrophic for everyone.

5. The System's Flexibility Is Both Strength and Weakness

American constitutional system has accommodated radical transformations—from agrarian republic to industrial power to global hegemon, from slavery to civil rights, from minimal government to New Deal to Reagan revolution. This flexibility is remarkable. But it also means the system can bend toward authoritarianism or dysfunction if enough actors push in those directions. Constitutional democracy requires not just structures but commitment to democratic values.

The Question Facing Americans:

Can people who disagree profoundly about governance philosophy nonetheless commit to shared democratic procedures? Can Thiessen and Richardson—representing millions with similar views—accept that the other side's victory in elections is legitimate even if they find the resulting policies abhorrent? Can they work within the system to advance their vision while respecting constitutional constraints?

Or have disagreements become so fundamental that one side must prevail and the other submit? Have Thiessen's "real American values" and Richardson's "constitutional democracy" become mutually exclusive?

The answer to this question will determine which future we get.

One Final Note:

Both Thiessen and Richardson wrote their pieces as if from the future, describing 2025 in past tense. This rhetorical choice is revealing. By presenting speculation as accomplished fact, both authors attempt to make their preferred future seem inevitable—whether triumph or catastrophe. But the future isn't written. It emerges from millions of individual decisions, from choices made in moments of crisis, from whether people in positions of authority choose constitutional process or personal power, from whether citizens engage or disengage, from contingency and chance as much as structure and strategy.

The value of comparing these visions isn't to predict which will come true. It's to understand the stakes, recognize the choices, and perhaps make better decisions about which future we want to build.
