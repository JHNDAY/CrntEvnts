Topic 1: The SCOTUS Tariff Ruling — Bob Fishman Presents

Context

The Supreme Court struck down the administration's IEEPA-based tariffs 6–3. Bob Fishman presented five big-picture points: the money at stake, the significance of the ruling, workarounds underway, the status of refunds, and the international response — "all in 5 minutes. 7."

The Money at Stake

$160–180B
Collected in tariffs to date under IEEPA
$1.4T
Revenue projected 2026–2035 to offset "Big Beautiful" Tax Law deficit increase
$1,000–$1,600
Estimated additional cost per average American household from tariffs
  • Trump used IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act) because it allowed executive action without investigations, hearings, or Administrative Procedure Act compliance — "he thought he could just do it"
  • The word "tariff" does not appear anywhere in the IEEPA statute — it had never been used to impose tariffs before
  • Administration lost in lower courts; case reached the Supreme Court, which ruled 6–3 against the tariffs

How the Justices Split

Three Liberal Justices — Strike Down

Sotomayor · Kagan · Jackson
Just read the statute. The word "tariff" isn't in it. It had never been used this way. Inquiry ends there — no need for elaborate doctrine.

Three Conservative Justices — Strike Down (Major Questions)

Roberts · Barrett · Gorsuch
The statute is ambiguous enough that under Roberts's Major Questions Doctrine — if Congress wants the President to have power with far-reaching economic effects, it must say so explicitly. As Gorsuch noted: you can't have two different sets of rules for Biden and Trump on the same doctrine.

Three Conservative Justices — Would Have Upheld

Kavanaugh · Alito · Thomas
Kavanaugh focused on the practical chaos of managing refunds (predicted accurately). Alito and Thomas would have upheld on the merits.
The Irony Bob Relished: The Major Questions Doctrine was invented by Roberts to knock out Biden's EPA Clean Power Plan and student loan forgiveness program. Now Gorsuch applied it symmetrically: "If you knocked out Biden-era policies on that basis, you've got to do the same thing here." The conservatives were hoisted by their own petard.

Is This a Turning Point?

Bob's View: Not Really

This was a statutory construction case — the words of the statute just didn't support the tariffs. The Major Questions Doctrine had never before been applied to foreign affairs / international trade, and some justices were uneasy about extending it there. It doesn't signal a broad judicial rebellion against executive power. The more telling test will be the Lisa Cook / Jerome Powell removal case — that goes to the heart of unilateral executive power.

John's Hope

This is still a significant ruling. The administration could not get what it wanted from the highest court. "Anytime some institution says no to Trump and is successful, I think it's a step in the right direction." It may at least give some justices more courage in coming cases.

The Emergency Gambit — and Its Collapse

Geoff's Question / Bob's Answer

Geoffrey raised the question: Trump invoked a national emergency under IEEPA. Now that the U.S. is at war with Iran — isn't that an emergency that could revive the tariffs? Bob's answer: maybe legally possible, but Trump's own head of counterterrorism had just resigned that very day, publicly stating there was no imminent threat to the United States from Iran — directly contradicting Trump's claims that Iranian drones were going to hit California.
Lois Sockol: The resignation letter was "very strong" — explicitly stating that the U.S. had become a partner with Israel and that Israel's actions initiated the war.

Workarounds & Refunds

  • 10% across-the-board tariff: Imposed immediately under a different statute — but capped at 150 days
  • New proceedings: Administration opened investigations under other statutes, particularly for steel and aluminum; these require hearings, could take a year. "Unlike IEEPA, where Trump just did it and said 'I dare you.'"
  • Refund claimants — three tiers: (1) Importers who paid directly; (2) Retailers like Costco and Walmart who filed claims; (3) Average citizens who absorbed higher prices — almost certainly no individual refunds
  • Hedge fund angle: Funds are buying tariff claims at a discount (e.g., 25 cents on the dollar from a Walmart) — "legalized gambling" on whether the claims succeed in court
  • Foreign countries: Most bilateral deals struck at ~10%; those countries are opting to honor the deals rather than risk a less predictable outcome

Tangent: The Pardon-for-Pay Racket

Another Form of Tariff Logic

John noted the parallel between hedge funds monetizing tariff claims and Trump monetizing pardons: brokers advertising White House access, standard rates of $1M–$6M per pardon, and in at least one case — broker Joshua Nass — actual mob-style pressure tactics when a client said he couldn't pay the balance. "Just another example of why these four years can't be over fast enough."

Topic 2: The Iran War — Military, Moral, and Energy Dimensions

Context: The discussion drew on material from Scott McKnight's "Power Lines" course on the energy transition, where the prior session had pivoted almost entirely to the Iran war and its implications. John had converted that transcript into a discussion guide for the portal.

The Energy Transition Lens

Could This War Accelerate the Green Transition?

The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20% of the world's oil supply — any disruption throws the global economy "into the ditch." John argued that rational business planners and policymakers, watching this, will increasingly recognize that renewable energy (wind, solar, water) eliminates the geopolitical vulnerability inherent in carbon-based fuels: you don't have to extract it from unstable regions, transport it, or defend sea lanes. The war may push decision-makers — not Trump, but the people who actually run energy businesses — toward accelerating the transition.
Lois Sockol's Insight: When you burn oil, it's gone. Wind and water "refurbish" — they don't get consumed. "I don't think people think of it that way, and that's too bad." Renewable energy is self-renewing; fossil fuels are a one-way drawdown.
Geoffrey & Hilary Kent: A French company contracted to build offshore wind turbines in Virginia and North Carolina was offered $1 billion by the Trump administration to abandon the wind project and pivot to gas. "That's how much he's going against this." And yet Congress hasn't pushed back.

Asymmetric Warfare: The Math Problem

The Missile–Drone Equation

U.S. Patriot missile: $4,000,000 each — used to shoot down a $35,000 Iranian drone. The math is simply unsustainable. You run out of Patriot missiles; the drones keep coming.

Ukraine's answer: Under existential duress, Ukraine developed a "Shahed Killer" drone costing $1,000–$2,000 that can intercept the enemy drones. The cost ratio is now manageable.

The irony John savored: The U.S. military is now asking the Ukrainians — the same Ukrainians it has been wavering on supporting — for help developing this technology. "The irony."
Note: John worked at Raytheon for 41 years and has hands-on familiarity with the underlying technologies. A MIT colleague of his pioneered a DARPA-funded swarm-vehicle concept a decade ago that has recently found real-world application in this exact problem.

Ralph Loomis: Why Regime Change Is Fantasy

The Historical Case Against

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard is deeply embedded in every sector of Iranian society and will suppress any street uprising immediately. Iranians celebrating in London or Boston are diaspora — not an organized opposition capable of governing 90 million people who have lived under autocratic rule for generations. Airstrikes only knock down infrastructure; the people who oppose you survive and reorganize. "You could go back hundreds of years where invaders had the same idea. Once we come in, there'll be a wonderful new country. It never has happened that way."
A Detail That Broke Ralph's Heart: Iran contains historical and archaeological sites up to a thousand years old that have been protected by all prior adversaries. These are now being bombed — by U.S. and Israeli strikes. UNESCO and the UN have protested loudly. "Of course, in all violations, it doesn't stop anything."
John's Coda: Afghanistan was called the graveyard of empires. The echoes here are unmistakable. And Iran has 90 million people, half of whom are deeply committed to the post-1979 revolutionary state — enough committed people to live in dire conditions and inflict sustained economic pain on the world. "They can produce drones for thirty-five thousand dollars."
Sally Fleschner's Reading List: Recommended King of Kings (journalist Tom [surname not recalled]) — a 500-page thriller-paced history of Iran from the 1953 CIA coup through 1981. Also mentioned two opposing analytical pieces she sent John: a Chris Hedges podcast with a military analyst arguing the U.S. has already lost the war, and a George Friedman piece supportive of the strikes — though Sally found Friedman's nuclear-bomb-on-a-boat scenario "a little racist" and notably alarmist compared to his usual standard.

Topic 3: UPenn and the Demand for Jewish Student Data

The Demand

The Trump administration has demanded that the University of Pennsylvania turn over records identifying Jewish students and faculty — ostensibly to monitor antisemitism. Penn, among other universities receiving similar demands, is reportedly the first to take the matter to court.

Sally's End-Run Strategy

The Opt-Out Gambit

Sally proposed a workaround: Penn (and other universities) could send all current and incoming students, faculty, and staff an opt-in/opt-out form — "Do you mind, or do you not mind, your religious, ethnic, or racial identity being disclosed to third parties, including the federal government?" If enough people check "I mind," Penn can truthfully tell the government: we don't have the data. She noted the same approach could apply to campus organizations like Hillel, which could vote on whether to disclose their membership lists.
Geoffrey & Hilary Kent: Surprised that universities would even have such lists — standard application forms explicitly offer the option not to answer identity questions. The data may be less complete than the government assumes.

The Funding Club

Ralph Loomis's Realism

The Brown University precedent: Brown went to court and won. The administration responded by cutting $800 million in funding anyway. Lower courts have consistently supported universities — the administration ignores them. The grants disappear fast. Once the money is gone, universities face an asymmetric negotiation where capitulation looks rational regardless of the legal merits. "They ultimately have the club here." Ralph compared the overall dynamic to a gangster mentality.
Sally (counter): But the government can't simply cut funds because students exercised their legal right not to disclose religious identity. "That's just apples and oranges." The administration would have no legal basis for the cut — only extralegal coercion.
John's Realism: "It's extortion, and they will do it. The funds will be cut down and shut down for some time, and it'll grind through the courts. But the administration will hurt Penn in the meantime. So what do administrators do — stand up, or say this is going to be too much pain for our graduate and research programs? That's the reality we're dealing with."
The DEI Angle (Sally): The administration eliminated DEI departments — the very departments that would have tracked identity data for affirmative-action purposes. Penn could argue: you told us to shut down DEI; now we no longer have the records you're requesting.

Group Agreement

Near-universal agreement that compelling disclosure of religious identity is un-American and legally dubious — but also that the funding mechanism gives the administration substantial coercive power regardless of what courts say at the lower level. The constitutional principle is clear; enforcement is the problem.

Key Themes & Strategic Insights

The Courts as Partial Guardrail

Bob's Assessment

The SCOTUS tariff ruling was a genuine defeat for the administration — but a narrowly grounded one, rooted in statutory text rather than a broad constitutional check on executive power. The administration will work around it. The more consequential test remains: will the Court curtail the unilateral firing of independent officials (the Lisa Cook / Jerome Powell question)? That case will reveal whether the judicial branch is a real guardrail or a speed bump.

The Extortion Architecture

A Recurring Pattern

Whether tariffs as budget substitutes, pardons as a for-profit service, or funding cuts as compliance enforcement — the administration has systematized coercion. John's summary: "It's an extortion racket throughout the whole administration." The legal victories at lower courts are real but insufficient; the administration has learned to inflict enough economic pain to force compliance before cases are resolved.

The Drone Math as Strategic Reality Check

When the Numbers Tell the Story

The $4M-missile-vs.-$35K-drone equation is not a talking point — it is a finite arithmetic problem. The U.S. cannot sustain a war of attrition at that cost ratio. The Ukrainians have already solved it at $1,000–$2,000. The fact that the U.S. military is asking them for help is simultaneously an embarrassment and a sign of institutional rationality surviving political incoherence.

The "Graveyard of Empires" Echo

Ralph & John's convergent view: History offers no template for the kind of regime change Trump is imagining. The Revolutionary Guard is not a government — it is an embedded social and economic institution. Bombing infrastructure leaves it intact. The peasant populations Trump imagines rallying to democracy have no experience of self-governance and every incentive to remain loyal to the only structure they know. We have been here before.
