Shakedowns

As the administration follows the organized crime playbook, this week brought several notable developments on the shakedown front:

The FCC approved the $8B Paramount-Skydance merger by a 2-1 vote, but only after CBS News paid a $16MM settlement, failed to renew Stephen Colbert's Late Show, and Skydance promised to end Paramount's DEI programs.

In a more explicit quid pro quo, Columbia University agreed to pay a $200MM fine to end an antisemitism investigation, plus an additional $21MM for employment discrimination claims. The university will punish 70 students who participated in pro-Palestinian rallies in exchange for restoring $1.3B in federal research funds.

What we're witnessing here is the emergence of a new model for regulating universities by the government.
— Law Professor David Pozen

Harvard continues to fight back, contending in federal court that the freezing of $2.6B in federal grants was "cooked up" and unconstitutional. The administration has also threatened to revoke Harvard's tax-exempt status and its ability to enroll international students.

If it weren't for me, the Market wouldn't be at Record Highs right now, it probably would have CRASHED

Immigration Enforcement

A record 56,000 immigrants are currently in ICE detention, with 72% having no criminal convictions. The DOD has allocated $1.26B for a 5,000-bed detention facility at Ft. Bliss, while FEMA is providing $608MM to states for detention center construction.

Migrants at a Miami immigration jail were shackled with their hands tied behind their backs and made to kneel to eat their food from styrofoam plates 'like dogs'
— The Guardian

A Marist poll found that 54% of respondents felt ICE has "gone too far" compared to 26% who found its actions "about right" and 18% who thought it hasn't "gone far enough."

Looking Ahead: 2026 Midterms

Republicans currently hold a precarious 219-214 House advantage (after recent deaths and resignations). With Democrats likely to win September special elections in AZ-07 and VA-11, the GOP margin for error will shrink to just two defections on floor votes.

Trump has openly called for further gerrymandering in Texas to gain 5 additional seats, despite Republicans already holding a 25-13 advantage in a state he won 56-42%. Any such scheme risks spreading Republican voters too thinly and could backfire in a wave election.
