Deposition of Jack Smith: Key Highlights

Date: Wednesday, December 17, 2025

Location: Rayburn House Office Building, Room 2237, Washington, D.C.

Committee: U.S. House Committee on the Judiciary

Time: 10:05 a.m. - 6:44 p.m.

Witness: Jack Smith, Former Special Counsel

Counsel: Lanny A. Breuer, Lindsey Hunt, Peter Koski (Covington & Burling)

Overview and Context

This deposition was conducted as part of Chairman Jordan's oversight of the Biden-Harris administration's Justice Department. The transcript includes an errata sheet with corrections made by Jack Smith.

Key Limitations on Testimony:
  • Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 6(e) restrictions on grand jury secrecy
  • Judge Cannon's January 21, 2025 order prohibiting disclosure of Volume Two (classified documents case) information
  • Restrictions on discussing interview transcripts, search warrant materials, business records, toll records, video footage, and other non-public information from the classified documents investigation

Smith's Assessment of the Cases

January 6 / Election Case

Smith testified that he believed the Special Counsel's Office had proof beyond a reasonable doubt that President Trump:

  • Engaged in a criminal scheme to overturn the results of the 2020 election
  • Attempted to interfere in the lawful transfer of power following the 2020 election
  • Had sufficient evidence to obtain and sustain a conviction

"We had a very strong case... if we went to trial we would prevail."

Classified Documents Case

Smith testified that he believed there was proof beyond a reasonable doubt that President Trump:

  • Willfully retained highly classified documents after leaving office in January 2021
  • Kept classified materials at his Mar-a-Lago social club
  • Had sufficient evidence for conviction on charges related to unlawful possession of classified documents

Prosecutorial Standards and Ethics

Robert Jackson's Standards

Smith was asked about and agreed with principles from Attorney General Robert Jackson's famous 1940 speech on prosecutorial ethics, including:

"The prosecutor has more control over life, liberty, and reputation than any other person in America."
"The most dangerous power of the prosecutor: that he will pick people he thinks he should get, rather than pick cases that need to be prosecuted."
"A prosecutor... seeks truth and not victims, who serves the law and not factional purposes, and who approaches his task with humility."

Smith affirmed he tried to live up to these standards during his time as Special Counsel.

Decision-Making Process

Open-Minded Investigation

  • Smith testified there was a prospect he might not bring indictments when first appointed
  • He did not have a preconceived outcome when taking the job
  • His team engaged in continuous debate about whether charges were justified
  • Final decisions to indict were made shortly before seeking indictments

"When I took this case, I did not know enough about the facts or the law to have a judgment about whether we should proceed in either case, both cases or neither case."

Consultations with Attorney General

  • Smith had several meetings with Attorney General Garland about both cases
  • They discussed legal and factual issues
  • The AG never told Smith he couldn't or shouldn't go forward
  • The AG could have blocked the indictments but chose not to
  • Smith regularly consulted with the Solicitor General

Controversial Issues Addressed

Congressional Toll Records

Major Point of Contention: The committee raised serious concerns about the Special Counsel's Office obtaining toll records from Members of Congress without their knowledge.

  • The House and Senate have Speech or Debate Clause protections under the Constitution
  • Committee members argued the subpoenas violated these protections
  • Smith disagreed with this interpretation
  • The DOJ Inspector General's Office was involved in serving some subpoenas, raising conflict of interest concerns
  • Smith testified that under current practice, judges would be notified if subpoenas involved Members of Congress, but this was not done at the time

Justice Manual and Public Statements

  • Questions were raised about whether Smith's definitive statements about Trump's guilt violated Justice Manual provisions
  • The Manual prohibits prosecutors from asserting guilt before a jury determination when a case is pending
  • Smith argued this restriction no longer applied after the cases were dismissed
  • His final report language was more definitive than Special Counsel Hur's or Mueller's reports

Smith's Professional Background

Experience: Nearly 30 years as a prosecutor

Career Highlights:

  • Public Integrity Section at the Department of Justice
  • Experience in prosecuting political figures throughout his career
  • International experience (including work at The Hague)
  • Specialized in complex investigations

Political and Timing Considerations

Election Timing Concerns

  • Questions were raised about the timing of indictments in relation to the 2024 election
  • The Special Counsel's Office closed down after Trump won reelection
  • DOJ election year sensitivity policies were discussed
  • Smith maintained his decisions were based on the facts and law, not political considerations

Restrictions and Compliance

What Smith Could NOT Discuss:

  • Grand jury proceedings (Rule 6(e) material)
  • Volume Two of the Special Counsel's report (classified documents case)
  • Non-public interview transcripts
  • Search warrant materials beyond what was publicly released
  • Business records, toll records, and video footage obtained by investigation
  • Attorney-client communications
  • Classified material details

What Smith COULD Discuss:

  • Information in the public record
  • Volume One of his report (election interference case)
  • Information made publicly available through authorized means
  • Materials released by DOJ to Congress (such as Mar-a-Lago search warrant emails released to Senator Grassley)

Key Participants

Committee Members Present: Jordan, Biggs, McClintock, Nehls, Kiley, Fry, Grothman, Knott, Onder, Harris, Fitzgerald, Gooden, Cline, Schmidt, Raskin (Ranking Member), Lofgren, Scanlon, Balint, Moskowitz, Goldman, Cohen, Neguse, Crockett, Johnson, and Jayapal.

Errata Sheet Corrections

The transcript includes numerous corrections made by Jack Smith, primarily involving:

  • Capitalization of proper nouns and titles (Special Counsel, District Court, Attorney General, etc.)
  • Clarification of specific statements and quotes
  • Correction of transcription errors
  • Technical legal term corrections

Conclusion

This deposition represents a significant moment in congressional oversight, with the former Special Counsel defending his prosecutorial decisions while navigating substantial legal restrictions on what he could disclose. The testimony reveals deep partisan divisions over the legitimacy of the investigations and prosecutions of President Trump, with fundamental disagreements about prosecutorial discretion, constitutional protections for Members of Congress, and the appropriate standards for public commentary by prosecutors about dismissed cases.
