Jon's Timeline: From Email to Investigation

Jon's Journey: From Email to Investigation

Click to expand full timeline

▼
In just 5 hours and 1 minute, a retired insurance worker's four-sentence email urging mercy for an asylum seeker triggered a federal investigation that would lead to home visits, subpoenas, and constant surveillance anxiety.
OCTOBER 30, 2024

The Email

Jon, a 67-year-old retiree in Philadelphia suburbs, reads Washington Post investigation about DHS trying to deport an Afghan asylum seeker. The immigrant, identified as "H," had begged federal officials to reconsider, telling them the Taliban would kill him if returned to Afghanistan.

Jon found prosecutor Joseph Dernbach's email address on Florida's bar association website through a basic Google search. His complete message: "Mr. Dernbach, don't play Russian roulette with H's life. Err on the side of caution. There's a reason the US government along with many other governments don't recognise the Taliban. Apply principles of common sense and decency."

Time to write: 5 minutes. Jon signed with his first and last name and pressed send, hoping his plea would make a difference.

5 HOURS, 1 MINUTE LATER

The Notification

Jon was watching TV with his wife when an email from Google appeared. He didn't tell his wife right away, worried she would panic.

"Google has received legal process from a Law Enforcement authority compelling the release of information related to your Google Account."

Type of legal process: "subpoena"
Authority: "Department of Homeland Security"

Jon read the message a second time, then a third. It could be fake, he thought, or a mistake. Or maybe it had something to do with that four-sentence email.

THE NEXT FEW DAYS

The Impossible Challenge

Google gave Jon seven days to file a motion in federal court to prevent them from releasing his information. But there were problems:

  • Google sent the notice from a "noreply" address
  • They didn't provide a copy of the subpoena
  • They didn't explain what information was requested
  • No phone number to reach them
  • No way to request more time

Jon called immigration lawyer Judi Bernstein-Baker, 80, who asked: "How do you challenge a subpoena you don't have a copy of?" She thought: Unless you're rich, nobody can find an attorney to go to federal court in seven days.

Jon tried to reach DHS through a maddening circuit of answering machines, dead numbers, and uninterested attendants. The federal court had no record of the subpoena. Through Reddit posts and news coverage, Jon worked out it was an administrative subpoena—requiring no judge or grand jury.

NOVEMBER 17, 2024

The Visit

9:30 AM. Through the window, Jon's wife saw them coming: "It's the police!" she screamed.

On his porch stood a local officer in uniform with two men in slacks and sport coats carrying DHS badges.

Jon, 5-foot-6, diabetic, with graying hair and a plodding British accent, tried not to panic. He'd seen videos of DHS encounters turning violent—even for women, teenagers, and old men. Inside, he could hear the dog yelping and his wife shouting, "Don't you have anything better to do?"

The questioning: The agents showed him a copy of his email to Dernbach. They wanted to "hear his side of the story." When asked how he knew Dernbach's email, Jon explained he found it through Google search. He showed them Google's notice—they seemed surprised, as if they hadn't seen it.

The threat assessment: The investigators agreed the email broke no law but pointed to mentions of "Russian roulette" and "Taliban." Perhaps the prosecutor felt threatened?

After 20 minutes: Jon asked if he'd be stopped at airports or put on a list. The men assured him: "Of course not. You have nothing to worry about."

NOVEMBER 21, 2024

The Revelation

22 days after the initial notification, Google finally provided Jon a copy of the subpoena (after a Washington Post reporter contacted them about his case).

What DHS Demanded:
  • Day, time, and duration of all online sessions since Sept 1
  • Every associated IP and physical address
  • List of each service used
  • Any alternate usernames and email addresses
  • Date account was opened
  • Credit card numbers
  • Driver's license number
  • Social Security number

Another revelation: Google informed Jon it had "not yet responded" to DHS's demand. He'd assumed they provided everything weeks earlier, before the agents visited. Google never explained the delay.

The gag request: On the subpoena's final page, DHS had asked Google not to tell Jon: "Any such disclosure will impede the investigation and thereby interfere with the enforcement of federal law." Google ignored that request.

The terrifying implication: If Google never sent the information, how did those federal agents track him down?

DECEMBER 2024

The Aftermath

Just before Christmas, Jon and his wife left for Puerto Rico to celebrate their 40th anniversary. At the airport outside San Juan, they waited at baggage claim until every other passenger had left.

"Is this a coincidence?" Jon asked his wife when their luggage finally arrived at the cruise ship.

In Jon's bag, a notice from TSA:
"Your bag was among those selected for physical inspection."
It did not explain why.

Legal response: ACLU agreed to represent Jon pro bono, filing a motion to quash in federal court on Monday, January 27, 2025. His attorneys accused DHS of violating the statute limiting administrative subpoenas to "immigration enforcement" and violating Jon's First Amendment rights.

Living with surveillance: Jon now wonders constantly: What if the U.S. government had investigated him in other ways? And what if it still was?

Jon's Background: A Lifetime of Activism

Born in England to a Jewish family, Jon grew up hearing how his mother, at 20, joined an intelligence service during the Holocaust to help Britain fight the Nazis.

  • 1978, Wales: While studying law and politics at Cardiff University, organized protest of Soviet oppression of Jews. Later traveled to USSR to visit ostracized families.
  • 1980s, Israel: Demonstrated against West Bank resettlement movement.
  • 1980s, Britain: Supported mine workers in dispute with PM Margaret Thatcher. Worked in British law representing social workers from London's poorest neighborhoods.
  • 1989, United States: Moved to Pennsylvania to raise a family near Independence Hall. Applied for citizenship after watching President George H.W. Bush sign Immigration Act of 1990.
  • Recent years: After retiring from insurance work, enrolled at Villanova University's certificate program to help immigrants navigate the legal system. Attended No Kings rally, occasionally wrote to lawmakers.
"He admired nothing more about the U.S. than the Constitution he'd studied before swearing his oath of allegiance. The rights it guaranteed made the country unlike any in the world, Jon thought, and he was proud to be part of it. Now, in his 27th year as a citizen, he was staring at his phone, terrified that the same country was trying to strip him of those rights."

It doesn't take that much to make people look over their shoulder, to think twice before they speak again. That's why these kinds of subpoenas and other actions — the visits — are so pernicious.

— NATHAN FREED WESSLER, ACLU ATTORNEY

Two separate administrative subpoenas demanding student records and information from the university.

Political speech and protest activity can trigger deportation proceedings—even for students legally in the country.

Harvard University Records

The Unprecedented Scope

Harvard's own attorneys called these subpoenas "unprecedented"—suggesting a scale or breadth beyond normal government requests.

Employment Records

Broad demands for employment information, potentially affecting large numbers of faculty, staff, or contractors.

Academic Freedom

Raises concerns about government intrusion into university operations and potential chilling effects on academic institutions.

SEPTEMBER 2024

Instagram ICE Raid Monitors

DHS used administrative subpoena to identify Instagram users posting about ICE raids in Los Angeles. Government dropped the case after ACLU filed 40-page legal challenge.

The Monitoring

Instagram users were tracking and posting about ICE enforcement activities in Los Angeles—documenting government actions in public spaces.

DHS's Argument

Federal attorneys claimed the accounts "invited scrutiny" by posting pictures of ICE agents' faces, license plates, and weapons—suggesting this public documentation was somehow threatening.

The Outcome

After ACLU filed a comprehensive 40-page legal challenge, DHS withdrew the subpoena entirely—suggesting the government knew it couldn't defend the surveillance in court.

This case demonstrates both the government's willingness to surveil those who document ICE activities AND the power of legal pushback—when resources are available to challenge these subpoenas, DHS often backs down.
DECEMBER 2024

Pennsylvania Facebook ICE Monitors

In Montgomery County, PA, DHS asked Meta to identify people behind Facebook and Instagram accounts tracking ICE raids. Government withdrew after legal challenge.

The Government's Claim

"John Doe, through his social media accounts, is threatening ICE agents to impede the performance of their duties."

The Evidence

Federal attorneys pointed to posts showing ICE agents' faces, license plates, and weapons—public information photographed in public spaces.

Pattern Recognition

Same playbook as LA case: claim documentation equals threats, withdraw when legally challenged. Shows systematic targeting of ICE monitors.

LATE 2024

Minnesota Hospital Workers (7,000 People)

DHS demanded detailed personal information about 7,000 workers in a Minnesota health system whose staff had protested ICE's intrusion into one of its hospitals.

The Trigger

Hospital staff protested ICE entering their facility—raising concerns about patient safety, medical ethics, and the sanctity of healthcare spaces.

The Scope

7,000 workers—an unprecedented dragnet affecting an entire health system's workforce, the vast majority of whom likely had nothing to do with the protest.

The Chilling Effect

Healthcare workers now face surveillance for advocating patient safety. The message: defend immigrants or vulnerable populations, and we'll investigate thousands of your colleagues.

How Administrative Subpoenas Work

Click to expand full details

▼
Administrative subpoenas are powerful legal weapons that require no judicial oversight, can be issued in minutes by mid-level officials, and give targeted individuals only days to mount a legal defense—often before they even receive a copy.

The Crucial Differences from Judicial Subpoenas

  • NO JUDICIAL OVERSIGHT: Unlike traditional subpoenas, administrative versions require no judge or grand jury approval—removing the constitutional check on government power.
  • ISSUED IN MINUTES: Federal officials throughout DHS, even in mid-level roles, can approve them with no external bureaucracy. Former investigators say they take "just minutes to write up."
  • SECRET BY DEFAULT: Government can request companies not notify users, though companies may ignore this request. Many users never learn they've been surveilled.
  • THOUSANDS ISSUED ANNUALLY: DHS not required to report numbers, but experts estimate tens of thousands per year—a surveillance apparatus operating in the shadows.
  • SEVEN-DAY CHALLENGE WINDOW: Users given only one week to file federal court motion to quash—often before receiving copy of subpoena, creating an impossible timeline.
  • NO RAMIFICATIONS FOR ABUSE: Even if courts find violations of constitutional rights, agency faces no requirement to stop the practice or face consequences.

Historical Context: How We Got Here

The U.S. government has issued administrative subpoenas for decades, but their use expanded dramatically—and became far more controversial—after September 11, 2001.

Post-9/11 Expansion

Justified as necessary for rapid response to terrorism threats, administrative subpoena powers were dramatically expanded across federal agencies.

Widespread Authority

A vast range of agencies—from FBI to Labor Department—can now deploy them for specific types of investigations, creating a patchwork of surveillance powers.

Previous Concerns

Even under previous administrations, the government faced accusations of overstepping laws and guidelines that restrict subpoena use.

"This vast administrative power has remained opaque even to those who receive these subpoenas and invisible to those it affects most." — Lindsay Nash, professor and researcher, Columbia Law Review

The Government's Defense

Proponents describe administrative subpoenas as critical tools allowing investigators to avoid protracted judicial reviews to obtain information quickly.

  • Speed Advantage: With no external bureaucracy, government can obtain phone, financial, and internet records in days instead of weeks or months.
  • Child Protection: Cited use case: identifying someone sexually exploiting a child without delays from judicial review.
  • Drug Trafficking: Tracking suspected drug traffickers who might flee during lengthy court processes.
  • Immigration Enforcement: DHS claims authority under laws governing immigration investigations.
DHS Assistant Secretary Tricia McLaughlin's statement on Jon's case: "This subpoena was part of a criminal investigation. Homeland Security Investigations has 'broad administrative subpoena authority' under the law."

Note: McLaughlin didn't say who was under criminal investigation, and DHS didn't answer questions about Jon's case or its broader use of administrative subpoenas.

The Critics' Concerns

Civil rights groups and privacy advocates argue that the lack of independent oversight and the secrecy with which subpoenas can be wielded threaten core democratic principles.

Jennifer Granick, ACLU Attorney:

"There's no oversight ahead of time, and there's no ramifications for having abused it after the fact. As we are increasingly in a world where unmasking critics is important to the administration, this type of legal process is ripe for that kind of abuse."

  • No Pre-Approval Check: Without judicial review, there's no neutral party evaluating whether the subpoena is justified, narrowly tailored, or constitutionally sound.
  • No Post-Issuance Accountability: Even when courts find abuse, there are typically no consequences for the officials who issued improper subpoenas.
  • Chilling Effect on Speech: Knowledge that criticizing government can trigger surveillance makes people "look over their shoulder, think twice before they speak again."
  • Disparate Impact: Only wealthy individuals or those with access to pro bono representation can effectively challenge these subpoenas in the seven-day window.
  • Secret Surveillance: If companies comply with non-disclosure requests, citizens can be surveilled without ever knowing—no oversight, no transparency, no accountability.

What They Can Demand

The scope of information accessible through administrative subpoenas is extraordinarily broad:

Internet Activity

Complete session history, all IP addresses, browsing patterns, service usage, account creation dates, alternate usernames

Financial Data

Credit card numbers, transaction histories, bank account information, payment patterns

Personal Identifiers

Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, passport information, physical addresses

Employment & Education

University records, employment histories, institutional affiliations, personnel files

Communications Metadata

Who you contact, when, how often—everything except the content itself (which requires different legal process)

Location Data

IP address history can reveal location patterns, movements, and associations over time

The investigators who questioned Jon told him DHS couldn't obtain emails, documents, photos, or other content with an administrative subpoena—but the breadth of metadata they can access creates a detailed portrait of someone's life, associations, and activities.

The Impossible Process

Jon's experience reveals how the system is "rigged to keep people in the dark":

  • Day 1: Google sends notification from "noreply" address—no way to respond with questions.
  • Day 1-7: Seven-day clock starts, but Google doesn't provide copy of subpoena or explain what information is requested.
  • No Extension Option: Google can extend deadline but never tells users this or provides way to request more time.
  • Court Has No Record: Administrative subpoenas don't go through courts, so calling the listed federal district yields no information.
  • Agency Unreachable: "Maddening, hours-long circuit of answering machines, dead numbers and uninterested attendants."
  • Rich vs. Poor Justice: "Unless you're rich, nobody can find an attorney to go to federal court in seven days."
  • No Copy Until Too Late: Jon received his subpoena copy 22 days after notification—only after Washington Post contacted Google.

There's no oversight ahead of time, and there's no ramifications for having abused it after the fact. As we are increasingly in a world where unmasking critics is important to the administration, this type of legal process is ripe for that kind of abuse.

— JENNIFER GRANICK, ACLU ATTORNEY

Tech Companies' Response & Transparency

▼
Tech companies sit at the crossroads between government surveillance and user privacy. Their policies on notification, transparency, and resistance vary dramatically—with direct consequences for Americans' constitutional rights.

Company Notification Policies

  • NOTIFY USERS (Unless Prohibited): Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Apple, Snap — alert users to administrative subpoenas unless legally prohibited or in extenuating circumstances like life-threatening situations
  • NOTIFY ONLY IF REQUIRED BY LAW: T-Mobile, TikTok — notify users only when legally mandated, not as default practice
  • REFUSED TO DISCLOSE POLICY: Verizon, AT&T — wouldn't tell Washington Post whether they provide any notice
  • NO RESPONSE: X (formerly Twitter) — did not reply to questions about notification policies

Google's Role in Jon's Case

Google's handling of Jon's subpoena reveals both the protection and limitations of company policies:

  • The Notification (Hour 5): Google alerted Jon to the subpoena five hours after DHS issued it—ignoring the government's request not to disclose. This notification was crucial: without it, Jon would never have known he was under surveillance.
  • The Catch-22 (Week 1): Notification came from "noreply" address with seven-day deadline to challenge—but no copy of the subpoena, no explanation of what data was requested, and no way to request more time.
  • The Delay (Week 3): Google didn't provide Jon the subpoena copy until 22 days later, and only after Washington Post contacted them—well past the seven-day challenge window.
  • The Protection (Ongoing): As of Jon's ACLU filing, Google had "not yet responded" to DHS's demand—protecting Jon's data even as the investigation continued.
  • The Silence (Throughout): Google never explained why it took 22 days to provide the subpoena copy, whether it could have extended the deadline, or how it decides when to comply versus resist.
Google spokesperson: "Our processes for handling law enforcement subpoenas are designed to protect users' privacy while meeting our legal obligations. We review all legal demands for legal validity, and we push back against those that are overbroad or improper, including objecting to some entirely."

Translation: Google does resist some subpoenas—but won't say which ones, how often, or under what criteria. Users are left in the dark about whether their data is being protected or surrendered.

The Transparency Problem

Large tech firms regularly publish "transparency reports" showing government data requests—but critical information is missing:

  • No Differentiation: Reports lump together judicial and administrative subpoenas despite fundamental differences in oversight and constitutional protection.
  • No Compliance Rates: Companies don't reveal how often they comply with versus resist administrative subpoenas.
  • No Criteria Disclosure: What makes a subpoena "overbroad or improper"? Companies won't say.
  • No User-Level Detail: Users can't find out if their specific data was requested or surrendered.
  • No Timing Information: How long do companies take to respond? When do they notify users? When do they provide subpoena copies? Unclear.
The result:

A surveillance system operating with minimal transparency, where users must trust that companies are protecting their rights—but have no way to verify those protections or hold anyone accountable when they fail.

The Seven-Day Trap

Even when companies notify users, the system is designed to prevent effective challenges:

The Timeline

Seven days to file federal court motion—an impossible deadline for most Americans without pre-existing legal representation.

The Cost

Federal litigation requires attorneys, court filings, legal research. "Unless you're rich," one lawyer noted, most can't mount a defense.

The Information Gap

Companies often don't provide subpoena copy in time to challenge it—users must defend against unknown accusations.

The Outcome

Most subpoenas go unchallenged. Only those with resources or lucky enough to attract ACLU attention can fight back.

Jon was fortunate: his case attracted media attention and ACLU representation. Most Americans targeted by administrative subpoenas have neither. Their data is surrendered without a fight, their surveillance happens without oversight, and they may never know their constitutional rights were violated.

Both Google and Meta received record numbers of subpoenas in the U.S. during the first half of 2024, as Trump began his second term. Google received 28,622 subpoenas — a 15% increase over the previous six months and the highest number since they began sharing data in 2012.

The Chilling Effect in Action

After Jon shared his story with fellow activists on a train to Philadelphia, one organizer realized the implications:

"I organize this weekly gathering... called 'We the People Wednesdays.' The group takes on a different topic each week and writes postcards to public officials. This is exactly the kind of thing we do. And we tell people to sign their names and Zip codes. If that's subject to surveillance, then anything could be."

This is how democracy dies — not with mass arrests, but with subtle intimidation that makes people think twice before speaking.
