SCOTUS has provided legal cover for presidential immunity and donor-class interests while procedurally blocking every progressive legislative workaround — the system using legal technicalities to crush working people, which is exactly what Platner argues Lincoln and FDR both correctly defied.

Platner's approach and its limits

Platner would initiate impeachment proceedings against at least two justices and apply federal judicial ethics standards immediately. He invokes FDR's court-packing threat as precedent: FDR never packed the court but the credible threat alone produced the "switch in time that saved nine."

The deterrent effect is real: impeachment proceedings create political cost and change judicial behavior even if removal never happens.

The hard limit: removal requires 67 Senate votes — a two-thirds majority never reached for a SCOTUS justice in US history. Term limits require a constitutional amendment or contested statute. The court can simply wait out a one-term Senate majority. This is the weakest link in Platner's toolbox precisely because SCOTUS was designed to be electorally insulated.

The fundamental tension

Operation Payday's conclusion is bleak: the apparatus has been specifically engineered to resist democratic correction. Every regulator disabled, every ethics filter removed, every enforcement unit gutted — not by accident but as a precondition for the wealth-extraction event. One senator with subpoena power is a significant but structurally insufficient response.

The Last Off-Ramp's counter is urgent: Platner may be the last candidate willing to name the mechanism before the next iteration arrives "smarter and meaner." His value is not only what he can do in the Senate — it is what naming the machine does to public understanding, which is the exact project that Operation Payday itself is engaged in. The scalpel is smaller than the wound. But it is a scalpel.
