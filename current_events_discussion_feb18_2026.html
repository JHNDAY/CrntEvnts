📰 Current Events Discussion

BOLLI Study Group • Zoom Session

📅 Date: Tuesday, February 18, 2026

⏰ Duration: ~104 minutes

📍 Format: Zoom (Remote)

🎯 Focus: Epstein files, presidential rankings, the shifting political landscape, and government incompetence chronicles

🎯 Session Overview

This densely packed session ranged across scandal, history, and political strategy. Sally Fleschner led off with a comprehensive accounting of names surfacing in the newly released Epstein files, sparking a lively debate about how to weigh mere "mention" against genuine complicity—with the group drawing a pointed parallel between Epstein's manipulative genius and a certain sitting president. George followed with a deep dive into presidential rankings, leaning heavily on the President's Day historian roundtable from Morning Joe, and the group spent considerable time on Carter's rehabilitation, the Lincoln–Trump bookend irony, and what character versus achievement actually means for a presidency.

John then steered into the evolving political landscape—MAGA's cultural decline, Trump's 40% approval, the Kennedy Center exodus, and a close comparative study of Jasmine Crockett versus James Tallarico as contrasting models for Democratic renewal in Texas, along with AOC's striking migration toward the mainstream. Larry, back after open-heart surgery, brought a poignant note on media disintegration and AI-generated misinformation. Sally closed with her new "Idiots in Government" feature, capped by an only-in-Trump-era story of an ambassador publicly attacking Belgium on social media. John wrapped with his signature "Adventures in Bad Taste"—the ongoing gilding of the White House.

🗂️ Sally: The Epstein Files

▼
Sally catalogued the cascade of names surfacing from the Epstein files and the group wrestled with how to weigh association versus genuine culpability

📋 The Name Parade

Sally noted the range of figures emerging from the files spanned the full spectrum of public life: royalty (Prince Andrew), entertainment (Woody Allen), finance (Howard Lutnick, Lawrence Summers), politics (Bill Clinton, Steve Bannon), tech (Peter Thiel), academia (Steven Pinker), media (David Brooks), and even spiritual leaders (Deepak Chopra, the Dalai Lama). What particularly struck Sally was seeing Noam Chomsky on the list—someone who "stood for something"—alongside his wife's blanket denials.

The group noted that some names came with highly specific, damning email exchanges, while others appeared only tangentially. Sally read out a selection of unredacted, disturbing messages to illustrate the nature of the correspondence.

⚖️ Weighing Association vs. Complicity

John's caution: Being mentioned in the files isn't automatically damning. Epstein ran in celebrity circles, and many names may appear tangentially. The Al Franken precedent—a senator railroaded over something minor compared to the actual Epstein activity—was held up as a warning against rush judgments.

Hal's framework: There's a crucial distinction between people who participated in the actual criminal activity, people who attended legitimate Epstein-adjacent academic or philanthropic events, and people who were simply manipulated by him. "Not everybody on the list is a pedophile."

Scott's analysis: Epstein was a real charlatan who drew in innocent people deliberately. He funded research institutions, talked science fluently, and—as an NBC News investigation showed—manufactured a "manipulative narrative" around his case, using figures like Chomsky to repair his reputation by association. Whether he was a government plant remains a live conspiracy theory.

🧠 How Did He Do It?

Linda's question: How did a college dropout become one of the most connected financiers on earth?

Sally's account: At 21, Epstein talked his way into teaching at Dalton (an elite NYC private school)—without a degree. There he befriended a senior Bear Stearns executive whose daughters attended, and leveraged that connection into finance. The attraction of his network, as Michael summarized: money, sex, and the status of being "accepted" by Epstein.

Hal's theory: He likely wasn't a genius—just a man who skimmed from money he controlled, moved assets between powerful people, and accumulated blackmail-level leverage along the way. George summarized it best: "It's not what you know, it's whom you know—plus he had smut on legions of people."

🪞 The Trump Parallel

John characterized Epstein as "this cunning manipulator who pretended to have great financial acumen and supernatural powers." George's immediate response: "That sounds a lot like number 47." The group laughed in recognition.

On the political calculus: while the files present a tangled bipartisan mess that demands care, John noted that "in the sense of bare-knuckles politics, this is bad for the Republicans"—and if it marginally helps Democrats retake the House, he's fine with that.

🇬🇧 The Profumo Precedent

Michael drew a parallel to the 1963 UK Profumo Affair: a Cabinet minister who lied to Parliament about his involvement with a woman simultaneously sleeping with a Soviet intelligence officer. The scandal cost the Conservative government the next election. Michael's question: why hasn't the Epstein equivalent produced comparable political fallout? Was it a mafia-style code of secrecy among the powerful?

🏛️ George: Presidential Rankings

▼
George dissected the latest presidential rankings and the President's Day historian roundtable from Morning Joe, probing what character vs. achievement really means across centuries of the presidency

📊 The Rankings Landscape

The two primary sources—CNN/Siena and the American Political Science Association—traditionally release updated rankings on President's Day. Notably, no new post-Biden rankings have appeared, which George suspects reflects corporate fear of triggering Trump retaliation.

The Top Tier: Lincoln (generally #1), FDR, and Washington occupy the summit—all three strong in both character and achievement. Teddy Roosevelt holds #4; Jefferson, Truman, and Eisenhower compete for 5–7. Obama ranks highly in the current era.

The Bottom Tier: Pierce, Andrew Johnson, and Buchanan—the three failed Civil War–era presidents—rank as failures (below 25 out of 100). Trump ties Pierce at #41 in the CNN rankings, just above Johnson and Buchanan.

🎙️ The Morning Joe Historian Roundtable

Ali Vitale called it "the Super Bowl of Historians." Three heavyweights, three frameworks:

  • Doris Kearns Goodwin — Character and moral qualities are paramount and synonymous with achievement. Her chosen greats: Lincoln, the two Roosevelts, and LBJ. Highlighted FDR's personal tragedy on Valentine's Day 1884 (losing both wife and mother in hours) as the crucible of his later empathy.
  • Douglas Brinkley — Achievement is the primary metric. Beyond Goodwin's picks, singled out Jimmy Carter for the Camp David Accords and JFK as "maybe the last president who inspired us to try to do great things." Attacked the Hall of Presidents plaques as "gobbledygook—looks like they were written by Trump."
  • Jon Meacham — Response to crisis defines greatness. "We don't elect statues or icons, we elect human beings—and perhaps we've taken the human being thing a little too far with this presidency." His focus: Washington's voluntary surrender of power, Lincoln's "team of rivals," FDR's crisis leadership.

🔄 The Carter Rehabilitation Debate

Ron's question: How much do historians weight the presidency itself versus the post-presidency? Carter seems to have built his legacy after leaving office.

George: Theoretically confined to the term; practically impossible to separate. Carter delivered the Camp David Accords and pioneered environmental policy, but was derailed by the Iran hostage crisis (helicopter crash) and high inflation. "Wrong place at the wrong time."

Sally (citing "King of Kings" by Scott Anderson): Carter actually negotiated the hostages' release—Reagan just got the credit when they physically departed on inauguration day. Much of the negative Carter narrative is propaganda driven by his status as a Washington outsider who never fit in.

George's counterpoint: Carter's sanctimonious self-righteousness was a turn-off, and he consistently went rogue on diplomatic missions rather than consulting the White House first. "Not a good team player."

📚 The Buchanan Paradox & The Lincoln–Trump Bookend

Buchanan was possibly the best-credentialed president in history (congressman, Secretary of State, ambassador to Russia and the UK) yet a catastrophic failure—classic Peter Principle, as Sally noted. George's grad-school roommate grew up in Lancaster and visited Buchanan's Wheatland mansion as a standard class trip, with the hope that Trump would rank below him. (Spoiler: on CNN's scale, they tied.)

John's closing note: By these historians' lights, the best and worst presidents are bookends of the Republican Party—Lincoln and Trump. "Those were very different Republicans, of course."

💭 Group Reflections on Rankings

Scott: Luck matters enormously. Character interacts with the moral fiber of the country—and "we're seeing that now." He floated an intriguing idea: rank the American people during each presidency, not just the president.

Michael: Americans expect too much from presidents—a superhuman who can simultaneously fix defense, domestic affairs, and the economy. Events make leaders as much as leaders shape events (citing Nixon's China opening alongside Watergate, Clinton's Oslo Accords alongside Monica Lewinsky).

George's rating-system caveat: A candidate could score nine 10s but one disqualifying zero—"a guy who was Epstein-like"—and still beat someone with eight 8.5s. Numbers don't always capture what matters most.

🗺️ John: The Shifting Political Landscape

▼
John's visualization-rich presentation tracked MAGA's cultural decline, Trump's approval slide, cyclical political charts, and the emerging Democratic stars trying to figure out how to win

📉 MAGA's Cultural Arc: From Revolution to Kid Rock

John shared a New Republic piece titled "The Hilarious Decline of MAGA's Brief Cultural Relevance," tracing the movement's trajectory from cultural juggernaut at the year's start to the spectacle of the Bad Bunny Super Bowl controversy being "countered" by Kid Rock. Trump's NYT-aggregated approval: 40%, disapproval 56%—his lowest point.

George: "We could view what's happening at the Kennedy Center as a metaphor for his presidency." Hillary Hahn was the latest classical musician to cancel; Trump is now threatening to close and renovate it entirely.

Scott's cultural framing: For decades the right projected "swamp" and "bubble" onto the left. Now the people deepest inside the right have to start thinking: "It's pretty swampy over here. We just have Kid Rock." He's watching for a mass cultural shift, not just a polling shift.

📈 John's Cyclical Politics Charts

John presented AI-assisted notional charts showing the cyclical swing between Republican and Democratic activist-government eras going back through FDR. Key dimensions tracked: government activism level, corruption index, and autocratic tendency.

Corruption was not new—Harding, Nixon both spiked—but Trump appears "probably way off the scale." The autocratic tendency dimension flagged not just Trump but G.W. Bush, Nixon, and even Woodrow Wilson (a Democrat).

Scott's challenge: The AI-generated numbers risk false precision—they can't capture how much worse Trump's second term is compared to historical precedents. Who chose those numbers? The tech bros owning AI have their own biases toward "acceptable speech."

John's closer: The H.L. Mencken quote, paraphrased: as voters get more and more what they want, "they'll finally realize their dream, and we'll have an absolute moron in the White House." George confirmed that's the substance of it.

🌟 Crockett vs. Tallarico: Two Models for Texas

Jasmine Crockett — Ferocious, sharp, books no foolishness, takes MAGA to task with evident relish. Excellent candidate by objective measure. But: how does that play not with the Northeast but with the middle of Texas?

James Tallarico — Young, telegenic, man of deep faith who speaks about it openly and without apology, equally open in criticizing Trump. His recent uncensored Colbert interview (posted on YouTube, not broadcast by CBS) raised $2–3M in small donations. Visiting every county in Texas. Hal called it "absolutely terrific—I can't believe he's not going to dominate that election." George compared him to "a less technocratic Pete Buttigieg."

Scott's caveat: It's also about how enemies define you. AOC in Texas would be "a disaster"—she's been defined as an "evil communist" by the right. The ideal candidate is one who won't get slaughtered by lies in the general.

🔄 AOC's Arc Toward the Center

A New York Times piece surprised John with its account of AOC's evolution: from outside agitator attacking the moderate wing, to coalition builder, mentor to junior colleagues, sought-after fundraiser—now supporting mainstream Democrats. Even whispers of a future presidential run.

John's read: she's understood that to win, you have to appeal to the center. "She's obviously pursuing that in a very aggressive way." Barack Obama was once an outside chance too.

🤔 On Kamala Harris

John was initially enthusiastic about Harris's campaign presentation but was "fairly discrediting" by her comments about Biden afterward—suggesting a lack of sincerity. He doesn't see a path for her in another run. The group largely moved past the topic without strong counterargument.

⚠️ Hal's Caution: The One-Incident Reset

Even granting the positive trend, Hal warned: "All it takes is one Trump incident." If Iran acts against US interests and Trump pounds on military response, the conversation resets entirely. Nobody's talking about Epstein files anymore. This remains the great X-factor in any optimistic projection.

📺 Larry's Return: Media & AI Misinformation

▼
Larry, returning from open-heart surgery, brought a sobering assessment of the information environment—podcasters as rabble-rousers, AI deepfakes, and the muzzling of mainstream media

💊 Welcome Back, Larry

George spotted Larry in the participant list and called on him mid-session. Larry's advice on his recent ordeal: "Don't have open-heart surgery, if you can avoid it." He's now in cardiac rehab. Sally and Beth volunteered to come visit. The group was visibly glad to have him back.

🎙️ The Podcast Misinformation Problem

Larry: newscasters have political bias, podcasters appear designed primarily to rabble-rouse. YouTube is flooded with content that makes him question accuracy. AI has made it worse by adding visual forgeries alongside verbal ones.

John concurred: the "BOMBSHELL NEWS" clickbait cycle is entertainment pretending to be journalism. He spotted an AI-generated fake of George Will making absurd statements—obvious to the politically literate but not to those with less baseline knowledge.

George added the structural dimension: Mainstream media is being muzzled simultaneously—Barry Weiss at CBS, Trump's litigation, Bezos defanging the Washington Post, the LA Times ownership doing the same. "That makes the podcasts you're mentioning yet more influential."

📜 The Yellow Journalism Long View

John placed this in historical context: William Randolph Hearst's early 20th century yellow journalism—"just lies and ridiculous things that a lot of people bought into." This may be part of the human condition, not a new crisis.

Larry's sobering counterpoint: "In terms of the entire US electorate, we are relatively few. There are many more people out there who don't know how to navigate through all this stew." John agreed: critical reading and reasoning remain the answer—but they're not universally distributed.

John on CBS: "CBS certainly isn't the CBS of Ed Murrow, that's for sure."

🤦 Sally: "Idiots in Government" Chronicle

▼
Sally launched a new recurring feature cataloguing government incompetence under the current administration, with four inaugural exhibits including what she called "probably one of the best ones I've ever found"

💡 The Concept

The premise: when idiots run the government, things will go wrong in spectacular, documentable ways. Sally proposed building a running log of incidents throughout the year and tallying them up at year's end as an assessment of the administration's performance. Beth suggested calling it "Believe It or Not."

📋 Exhibit A: El Paso — The Party Balloon Drone Scare

Someone decided that party balloons drifting near El Paso were cartel drones from Mexico and shot them down. The airport was closed. The incident required no further embellishment.

📋 Exhibit B: ICE Agents & the Contradictory Accounts

Two ICE agents pursued and shot a man in the leg. Their after-action reports were not only inconsistent with each other—they were both contradicted by video evidence. The men were indicted. The court then dismissed the case. Sally's verdict: "They weren't even smart enough to make their own accounts consistent with each other."

📋 Exhibit C: TSA Unpaid Workers

TSA workers are obligated to work but are not being paid. Sally's projection: within two weeks of non-payment, agents will start not showing up, producing catastrophic airport queue failures across the country.

📋 Exhibit D (The Feature): Ambassador Bill White and Belgium 🇧🇪

Trump appointed Bill White—a man with a shady résumé that included an abrupt job departure and a $1M fine for financial fraud—as Ambassador to Belgium. Ambassadors are explicitly trained NOT to interfere in local politics and to focus on trade promotion.

In Antwerp, some members of the Jewish community complained to the Belgian government about a mohel performing circumcisions without medical credentials, including the traditional post-circumcision oral suction practice (metzitzah b'peh). Belgium opened a formal investigation.

White, on his own initiative, posted on social media calling the Belgian investigation "ridiculous and anti-Semitic"—tagging Trump, Vance, and Rubio. Problems: (1) it was an investigation, not a prosecution; (2) it is spectacularly outside an ambassador's remit; (3) it triggered a formal summons from the Belgian government. He may be sent home.

Sally: "I can't believe how stupid this guy is. I think that should be right up on our list." The group agreed this was one for the ages.

✨ Adventures in Bad Taste: The Gilded White House

▼
The group's ongoing feature reviewing Trump's aesthetic transformation of public spaces—this week, the White House and its gold chandeliers

🏛️ Cabinet Room Then and Now

John walked through a New York Times visual comparison of the Cabinet Room from Obama to Biden to Trump—a study in aesthetic escalation. Three gold-antique chandeliers were added. George's verdict: "Chandeliers aren't even appropriate for professional meetings—they're more for dinner."

George on Secretary Rubio's portrait choices: "I like the way he described Polk as sort of a real estate guy, because of the war with Mexico."

🏰 The Ballroom: A Palace in the Making

Michael: The planned new White House ballroom, in square footage, will exceed the entire West Wing combined. Architects told him Trump can simply add floors to the West Wing to compensate. "Basically this is a total transformation into a monarch's palace. It's not the people's house anymore."

George: "A Turkish potentate's." John: There are organizations suing on architectural and historical preservation grounds—hopefully tying up the monstrosities in court throughout his administration.

✈️ Ron's Memory: Trump Shuttle & the Gold Toilet Seats

Ron recalled the Eastern Shuttle from LaGuardia to Boston—taken over by Trump, rebranded "Trump Airways / Trump Shuttle," name plastered on every surface, and famously gold toilet seats installed on the planes. "This is just part of the whole syndrome." George: "It's why it's called Exercises in Bad Taste."

Scott: "I have a sad thought—anybody I'd want to run for president would be mildly to majorly disgusted at the idea of living in that White House."

🤖 John's AI Toolbox & Upcoming Session

▼
John explained his AI-assisted visualization methodology and proposed a special off-cycle session focused on how he produces these documents

⚙️ How It Works

Scott asked directly: how does John produce the specific numbers in the cyclical charts? John described himself as "a certified practitioner of the black arts of AI." The process is a partnership: John drives, directs, and sets the conceptual framework; AI does the detailed production work that would otherwise take far too long. After generation, John reviews for coherence and accuracy.

Scott's concern (echoed by George): The AI-generated numbers risk underrepresenting how extreme the current moment is. Tech bros own the AI, and AI tends toward "acceptable speech"—it won't want to say this is "10 times worse than anything we've ever had" even if that's the accurate read.

John proposed an additional session (in the alternate week from regular meetings) focused on AI literacy for the group. He would spend ~30 minutes walking through how he uses AI to produce the visualizations, using the articles by Schumer (the viral AI piece) and Maureen Dowd already posted on the portal. Attendance optional.

George proposed Tuesday the 3rd (or 4th) for the next regular meeting, with the AI session to follow in the intervening week.

🏅 Opening Riff: Winter Olympics & Politics

▼
Pre-session small talk covering the 2025 Winter Olympics' lessons in sportsmanship—and the notable absence of same in contemporary politics

🎿 What the Olympics Shows You

George called the Olympics "interesting to watch what happens when people who expect to win, lose." He cited Ilia Malinin's collapse in the free skate despite being a near-certain gold medal favorite—and his remarkable openness with the media about it afterward. He also highlighted Mikaela Shiffrin finally winning after an eight-year medal drought, and the spirit of competitors in extreme sports cheering for their rivals.

John: "Well, [good sportsmanship is] always good to see, since it's disappeared from politics, right?" George: "In an asymmetric kind of way, John."

💬 On Bipartisan Missteps

John acknowledged that progressive Democrats made missteps that allowed Trump's return—a point George gently noted is "not a fairly biased view" when both sides are invoked. John agreed but maintained that progressives need to learn lessons from FDR and Lincoln about securing coalitions beyond their natural allies. "If you don't win, you can't do anything."

🔄 What is a SIG?

Ron asked a key clarifying question: does the group count against BOLLI's 10-week course limits? John: no. This is a SIG—a Special Interest Group—which means it can meet indefinitely without consuming formal course credits. "We can just keep meeting."

Next steps: Transition to every other week (Tuesday at 4 PM, pending confirmation). John to send email with confirmed date—tentatively Tuesday, March 3 or 4. The off-cycle AI session would occupy the intervening week for those interested.

💭 Session Reflection

This was a session defined by the group's characteristic blend of moral seriousness and dark humor. The Epstein discussion demonstrated the group's capacity to hold two truths simultaneously: the files represent a genuine moral catastrophe, and the rush to condemn by name alone carries its own dangers. The presidential rankings segment offered a longer historical view at a moment when that perspective is urgently needed—Lincoln and Trump as Republican bookends remains one of the sharpest observations of the year. John's political landscape presentation gave the group visual scaffolding for the cyclical nature of American politics, while the Texas Senate race discussion showed real engagement with the tactical question of how Democrats actually win rather than simply who is most righteous. Larry's return from open-heart surgery, and his immediate pivot to concerns about the information environment, was a reminder that the group's engagement is not casual—these are people who think about democracy's health even while recuperating. Sally's "Idiots in Government" feature promises to be a valuable running log; the Belgium ambassador story alone would have been worth the session. Adventures in Bad Taste closed on a lighter note, though Michael's "it's not the people's house anymore" lands heavier the more you think about it.
