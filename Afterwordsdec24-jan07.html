U.S. Military Intervention in Venezuela ▶
Trump Orders Military Operation to Capture Maduro
On January 3, 2026, President Trump announced that U.S. military forces had launched strikes across Venezuela and captured President Nicolás Maduro and his wife Cilia Flores in an unprecedented operation. NBC News reported that Trump had given Maduro a final ultimatum in a private phone call one week earlier, telling him to surrender. When Maduro refused, the operation launched at 10:46 PM ET on January 3.
"We're going to run the country right. It's going to be run very judiciously, very fairly."
— President Donald Trump at Mar-a-Lago press conference, January 3, 2026
Operation Absolute Resolve:
  • Planning: CIA team infiltrated Venezuela starting in August, tracking Maduro's movements
  • Casualties: 32 Cubans killed, including military personnel; few U.S. casualties
  • Aftermath: Vice President Delcy Rodríguez became acting president
  • Legal status: Maduro and Flores transported to New York to face narco-terrorism charges
  • Prior strikes: At least 35 vessels struck since September, killing 114 people
Brookings Institution analysis warned that the intervention epitomizes risks of American military action, noting Trump faces virtually no check on foreign policy and no experienced advisers to pivot to a plan B. The operation fits a pattern of Latin American interventions, most closely paralleling the 1989 Panama invasion.
Legal and Constitutional Concerns
New York City Bar Association condemned the action in the strongest terms, stating it violates both the U.S. Constitution and the War Powers Act of 1973, which requires Congressional approval for military actions except in defense of personnel or facilities. The operation also violates the UN Charter, a binding treaty the U.S. has ratified.
Democratic Senator Tim Kaine told NPR, "Maduro is terrible. But Trump put American servicemembers at risk with this unauthorized attack." Congressional Democrats criticized the lack of advance notice to Congress, while Republicans praised it as decisive action.
International and Domestic Reactions
Venezuelan opposition leader María Corina Machado, who recently won the Nobel Peace Prize, called for legitimately elected Edmundo González to assume the presidency. CBS News reported that González said the removal was "an important step but not enough," noting that only after releasing political prisoners and respecting the people's will can democratic transition begin.
International reaction was mixed. Argentina's President Javier Milei praised the operation, while the EU called for restraint and respect for international law. Iran demanded Maduro's release, calling it an "illegal act."

RFK Jr. Unveils Controversial Dietary Guidelines ▶
"End the War on Saturated Fats": New Food Pyramid Unveiled
On January 7, Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. released new U.S. Dietary Guidelines for 2025-2030, featuring an inverted food pyramid and endorsements of beef tallow and full-fat dairy. CBS News reported that Kennedy declared, "We are ending the war on saturated fats," while recommending olive oil, butter, or beef tallow for meal preparation.
"My message is clear: Eat real food."
— Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., White House briefing, January 7, 2026
Key Changes in New Guidelines:
  • Protein emphasis: Increased recommendation from 0.8 to 1.2-1.6 grams per kg of body weight
  • Full-fat dairy: Now prioritized over low-fat options (3 servings daily)
  • Cooking fats: Olive oil, butter, or beef tallow recommended
  • Ultra-processed foods: First-ever explicit warning to avoid highly processed foods
  • Added sugars: No amount recommended; maximum 10 grams per meal
  • Saturated fat limit: Remains at 10% of daily calories (unchanged from 2020)
Kennedy has long promoted his personal taste for saturated fats, including a March visit to a Florida Steak 'n Shake after the chain switched from vegetable oil to beef tallow. At a July meeting with governors, he promised the guidelines would "stress the need to eat saturated fats."
Scientific Community Raises Concerns
NPR reported that nutrition scientists expressed serious concern about the emphasis on saturated fats. "I would say that the evidence is moderate to high linking excessive saturated fat intake with negative outcomes," said Alison Steiber, Chief Mission Officer at the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics.
Scientific American noted that the American Heart Association recommends less than 6% of total calories from saturated fat, significantly stricter than the guidelines' 10% limit. Repeated studies have associated high saturated fat intake with higher LDL cholesterol, heart disease, weight gain, and diabetes.

Praise for Ultra-Processed Food Warning
The American Medical Association applauded the guidance for spotlighting ultra-processed foods, added sugars, and sodium. NBC News reported AMA President Dr. Bobby Mukkamala stating, "The Guidelines affirm that food is medicine and offer clear direction patients and physicians can use to improve health."
However, nutrition expert Marion Nestle, professor emerita at NYU, told media outlets the advice to limit ultra-processed foods is a major improvement, but "everything else is weaker or has no scientific justification." The 2024 advisory committee had recommended emphasizing plant-based proteins and reducing red meat, but the final guidelines emphasized meat and dairy instead.

CBS News Leadership Overhaul Under Bari Weiss ▶
Tony Dokoupil Named New CBS Evening News Anchor
On December 10, CBS News announced that Tony Dokoupil would become the new anchor of CBS Evening News, the first major move under newly appointed editor-in-chief Bari Weiss. Deadline reported that Dokoupil, 44, became the sixth anchor of the nightly broadcast in the last decade, beginning January 5, 2026.
The appointment comes after conservative commentator Bari Weiss was named editor-in-chief in October following Skydance's acquisition of CBS parent Paramount. Variety reported Weiss had considered Anderson Cooper and Bret Baier before selecting Dokoupil.
"We live in a time in which many people have lost trust in the media. Tony Dokoupil is the person to win it back."
— Bari Weiss, CBS News Editor-in-Chief
Recent CBS Evening News Anchors:
  • 2015-2017: Scott Pelley
  • 2017-2019: Jeff Glor
  • 2019-2024: Norah O'Donnell
  • 2025: John Dickerson and Maurice DuBois (co-anchors)
  • 2026-present: Tony Dokoupil
Dokoupil's Controversial Background
Dokoupil emerged as a favorite of Weiss after a September 2024 interview with author Ta-Nehisi Coates. Multiple outlets reported that Dokoupil told Coates his book "reads like the work of an extremist" during questions about Israel's right to exist. CBS executives initially said the interview did not meet editorial standards, but Shari Redstone, then chair of Paramount, called it a "model" of "civil discourse."
Weiss's The Free Press defended Dokoupil and published audio of the internal CBS call criticizing him. After Skydance's acquisition, Weiss purchased The Free Press before becoming CBS News editor-in-chief.

Epstein Files Release: Controversy and Redactions ▶
Justice Department Releases Epstein Files After Congressional Mandate
The Department of Justice released thousands of documents related to convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein following a congressional mandate with a December 19 deadline. CNN reported the files include over 300 gigabytes of data, papers, videos, photographs, and audio files from FBI investigations spanning decades.
The Epstein Files Transparency Act passed Congress in November with a 427-1 vote in the House and unanimous Senate approval, requiring all remaining unclassified documents to be released within 30 days of Trump signing it into law on November 19.
Timeline of Recent Releases:
  • November 12: House Democrats release emails showing Epstein wrote Trump "spent hours" with a victim
  • December 12: Democrats release photos showing Epstein with Trump, Clinton, and others
  • December 18: Additional 68 photos released from Epstein's estate (95,000 total obtained)
  • December 19: DOJ deadline; initial document dump with heavy redactions
  • December 24: DOJ announces discovery of over 1 million additional documents
Heavy Redactions Draw Criticism
Al Jazeera reported that at least 550 pages in the December 19 release were fully redacted, including a 119-page document labeled "Grand Jury-NY" and three consecutive documents totaling 255 pages. Many FBI interviews with Epstein's accusers and internal Justice Department memos remain unreadable.

Bipartisan criticism emerged quickly. Republican Rep. Thomas Massie posted that victims' lawyers knew of at least 20 names of men accused of sex crimes that should be in FBI FD-302 forms. Democratic Rep. Ro Khanna warned he and Massie would bring contempt proceedings if full disclosure wasn't forthcoming.
Photos Include Prominent Figures
Released photos show former Presidents Trump and Bill Clinton with Epstein at various times. CBS News reported images include Clinton in a hot tub, Clinton with Epstein in cultural garb, and Clinton with musicians Michael Jackson and Diana Ross on an airplane. Other photos show figures like filmmaker Woody Allen and former Trump adviser Steve Bannon.
A letter purportedly from Epstein to convicted sex offender Larry Nassar, containing crude references to Trump, was initially released but later declared fake by the Justice Department.
Million More Documents Discovered
On December 24, CNN reported the Justice Department announced discovery of over one million additional documents potentially related to the Epstein case. The department said it may need "a few more weeks" to review and release them, extending well beyond the original deadline.
NPR noted the incomplete release has fueled conspiracy theories, with Trump posting on Truth Social suggesting the DOJ should focus on releasing names of Democrats and "work on Election Fraud etc." White House frustration has mounted over the handling prolonging a story Trump has long been reluctant to discuss.

ACA Enhanced Subsidies Expire as Congress Deadlocks ▶
22 Million Americans Face Premium Increases as Subsidies Lapse
Enhanced premium tax credits for Affordable Care Act marketplace plans expired on December 31, 2025, after Congress failed to reach agreement on an extension. The Hill reported that approximately 22 million of the 24 million ACA marketplace enrollees receive the enhanced subsidies and will see their premiums more than double in many cases.
The enhanced subsidies were first enacted in 2021 as part of pandemic relief and extended through 2025 by the Inflation Reduction Act. They both increased subsidy amounts and eliminated the "subsidy cliff" that previously made households earning over 400% of the federal poverty level ineligible for any assistance.
114%
Average premium increase: from $888 in 2025 to $1,904 in 2026 (KFF analysis)
Who Will Be Hit Hardest:
  • Early retirees: 60-64 age group facing major increases
  • Middle-income households: Those just above 400% poverty level losing all subsidies
  • Small business owners: Self-employed individuals without employer coverage
  • Red state residents: 88% of enrollment growth since 2020 occurred in Trump-won states
Congressional Gridlock Despite Bipartisan Support
NPR reported that despite polling showing overwhelming public support for extending subsidies, Congress adjourned for the holidays without action. The standoff led to the longest government shutdown in U.S. history earlier in 2025, only ending when Democrats dropped their subsidy extension demands.
Senate Republicans voted down a Democratic three-year extension on December 11, with GOP Senator Bill Cassidy proposing health savings accounts instead. Republicans cited concerns about cost and fraud, and unwillingness to extend a pandemic-era program.

Enrollment Already Declining
CNN reported that state exchange directors say the expiration is already taking a toll. New enrollment is down, terminations are up, and more people are switching to less expensive bronze plans with higher deductibles and out-of-pocket costs.
At Your Health Idaho, new customers fell 22% from 2024, while twice as many customers terminated coverage. People who disenrolled cited affordability at three times the rate of prior years. About 59% of enrollees selected bronze plans for 2026, compared to 49% for 2025.
Extension Still Possible in 2026
NPR reported that Senator Peter Welch (D-VT) said bipartisan senators held talks this week to chart out a possible compromise. "A farmer in Vermont, their premium is going to go from $900 a month to $3,200 a month," Welch said. "So they're going to really face sticker shock."
Before the December recess, four House Republicans joined Democrats in signing a discharge petition forcing a vote on a three-year extension when the House returns. CNBC noted the subsidies could be extended retroactively to January 1, though logistical complications would slow implementation.
