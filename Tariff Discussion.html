1
Strategic Intent vs. Reality
▼
If tariffs are meant to be "reciprocal," why do they often exceed what other countries charge the US? Is this a negotiating tactic or flawed economic logic?
2
The "Liberation Day" Framing
▼
What does calling April 2nd "Liberation Day" reveal about how the administration views trade relationships? Is this rhetoric helpful or inflammatory for diplomacy?
3
Emergency Powers Usage
▼
Trump invoked the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) to justify these tariffs. Do trade deficits truly constitute a "national emergency," or is this an overreach of executive power?
4
Who Really Pays?
▼
If research shows only 0.7% of tariff costs were absorbed by Chinese exporters, with 99.3% passed to US consumers, how can the administration claim foreign countries are paying?
5
Regressive Tax Paradox
▼
Tariffs disproportionately hurt lower-income Americans who spend more on goods. How does this align with Trump's populist messaging about helping working-class Americans?
6
Revenue vs. Behavior Change
▼
Trump claims tariffs will generate $600 billion annually, but also says they'll bring manufacturing back to the US. Can you have both high tariff revenue AND successful reshoring? Aren't these goals contradictory?
7
Ally vs. Adversary Treatment
▼
Why are close allies like the UK and Japan facing significant tariffs while some countries get lower rates? What message does this send about US alliance priorities?
8
The China Exception
▼
China faces the highest rates (up to 125%), but the US is still heavily dependent on Chinese goods. Is this sustainable, or will it force uncomfortable economic adjustments?
9
WTO Paralysis
▼
The US has effectively disabled the World Trade Organization's dispute resolution since 2019. Without international arbitration, how should trade disputes be resolved?
10
Trade Deficit Obsession
▼
Why does the Trump administration view all trade deficits as inherently bad? Can you think of examples where trade deficits might actually benefit the importing country?
11
Manufacturing Renaissance Reality Check
▼
If tariffs successfully bring manufacturing back to the US, won't that likely mean higher prices AND potentially lower productivity compared to global supply chains?
12
Inflation vs. Growth Trade-off
▼
How do you balance the administration's growth promises against economists' warnings about stagflation (high inflation + slow growth)?
13
Midterm Election Calculus
▼
Consumer prices typically rise 6-12 months after tariff implementation. Could this timing impact the 2026 midterm elections?
14
Negotiation Theater
▼
Are the constantly shifting deadlines and rates genuine policy changes, or calculated pressure tactics? What does this unpredictability cost in terms of business planning?
15
Congressional Override
▼
Democratic resolutions to end the tariff emergencies were blocked by Republican majorities. Should Congress have more oversight over trade policy, or is this properly executive domain?
16
Precedent Setting
▼
If Trump can use emergency powers for trade deficits, what stops future presidents from using similar powers for climate change, inequality, or other broad issues?
17
Supply Chain Resilience
▼
Beyond costs, could these tariffs actually improve US economic security by reducing dependence on potentially hostile nations?
18
Global Trade System Evolution
▼
Are we witnessing the end of the post-WWII free trade consensus? What might replace it, and would that be better or worse for global prosperity?
💡 Facilitator Guidelines
For Moderators:
  • Start with factual clarification before opinions
  • Encourage citing specific examples or data
  • Ask "What evidence would change your mind?"
  • Focus on policy impacts, not personalities
Ground Rules:
  • Distinguish administration claims vs. research findings
  • Consider both short-term and long-term effects
  • Think beyond "US vs. other countries"
  • Examine winners and losers in each scenario
