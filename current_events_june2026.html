Two Key Primaries

One week before the runoff to determine the Republican Senate candidate for TX, Trump endorsed Ken Paxton who was leading Senator John Cornyn in the polls by about 10%. Paxton went on to win in a landslide by 64–36%. Cornyn has served for four terms and been a contender for majority leader. His colleagues in the Republican Senate caucus did not take his loss very well with outgoing NC Senator Thom Tillis saying "To call Paxton ethically challenged is to call Jeffrey Dahmer suffering from an eating disorder".

Turning to the CA gubernatorial primary, until Eric Swalwell dropped out of the race Democrats feared that two Republicans, Steve Hilton and Chad Bianco, could conceivably emerge from the jungle primary. With Swalwell out, his supporters apparently turned to Xavier Becerra and Tom Steyer. With 68% of the vote counted, Becerra leads with a 26.8% vote share followed by Hilton with 26.4%, Steyer with 21.1% and Bianco with 10.8%. The AP projects that Becerra will advance to the runoff and given that the late returns are favoring the Democrats, Steyer retains a modest chance to supplant Hilton as the second runoff candidate. All the Democrats combined are tallying 60% of the vote. This share, which is likely to increase, already exceeds the 58.5% of the vote Kamala Harris secured in the 2024 election.

Three Outrages Which Stood Out This Week

Trump apparently requires that his appointees are slavishly loyal to him and that they satisfy one of two criteria: (i) They look good on TV, preferably with experience at Fox News or on reality TV, or (ii) they were involved in real estate. Although it seemed impossible to lower the bar for his appointees, he has done so with his selection of Bill Pulte, the scion of a residential construction company, to serve as interim Director of National Intelligence. Although the law which established the position explicitly calls for "extensive national security expertise", Pulte has none whatsoever. In his roles as Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency and Chairman of Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae, however, Trump undoubtedly found his initiative to pursue trumped up mortgage fraud charges against Lisa Cook, Letitia James and Adam Schiff to be compelling. Pulte's selection prompted Zachary Wolf of CNN to write an article with the sub-title "People don't have jobs in the Trump administration so much as play roles".

From both a visual and cultural perspective, the construction of an octagon on the South Lawn of the White House to host a UFC fight card on June 14 which purportedly "celebrates" the nation's 250th birthday, but happens to fall on Trump's 80th birthday, is troubling. Analogizing this monstrosity to the permanence of the Eiffel Tower in Paris, Trump said "maybe we'll never take it down". Heather Cox Richardson reacted by writing "Trump appears to see politics as a dominance sport, much like...UFC".

Although not a Trump initiative per se, but consonant with his authoritarian instincts, what has happened at 60 Minutes this week brings into question how this 58-year-old program can endure. The seeds of its demise were planted last year when Skydance Media CEO David Elison hired Bari Weiss, who had no news background, as editor-in-chief for CBS News. Two months later, a story on the horrible conditions at CECOT in El Salvador was squelched by Weiss until the Trump administration had editorial input. Before Weiss's arrival, executive producer Bill Owens resigned because of his lack of editorial independence and then Anderson Cooper left earlier this month.

Last week, Weiss hired Nick Bilton, with a background as a journalist, screenwriter and author, but not in news per se, as executive producer. Two days later, interim executive producer Tanya Simon and correspondents Cecilia Vega and Sharyn Alfonsi, who produced the CECOT story, were fired. Somehow Bilton thought that it would be a good idea to host a bagel breakfast on Monday to introduce himself to the remaining staff and ease their qualms. Predictably the meeting didn't go well, especially after Scott Pelley, who has been at the show for 22 years, questioned Bilton's credentials, asked for an explanation of the three firings days earlier, and accused Weiss of "murdering" the show after "she was brought in to kill it". In response, Pelley was fired, leaving the show with only three of its formerly seven correspondents. Ben Rhodes, who served as a national security advisor under Obama, opined "This is how oligarch-authoritarian takeover of media happens".

Resistance

As the Republicans control both chambers of Congress, the Democrats are limited in what they can do to impose limits on Trump and his policies. They can voice their dissatisfaction, force uncomfortable votes for Republicans, and try to pass legislation, albeit subject to a veto, by securing the support of a handful of malleable Republicans, especially those facing competitive races in November. Late this week, they had two legislative successes:

  • On Thursday, The House voted 215–208, to block further strikes in Iran as four Republicans joined all Democrats for this symbolic victory.
  • During the Thursday/Friday vote-a-rama, the House passed a bill to provide $1B of security assistance and an $8B "loan" to Ukraine as well as increasing sanctions on Russia by a 226–195 vote with 18 Republicans voting along with all Democrats except Ilhan Omar.

However, the vote-a-rama also included a 52–47 vote in the Senate, with only Lisa Murkowski joining the Democrats to provide $70B of funding to ICE and CBP with no restriction on the $1.8B anti-weaponization fund after an earlier vote on a measure to permanently block the fund failed by one vote. At the same time, the House passed an Agriculture and FDA appropriations bill which included a $141 million reduction in the WIC nutrition program.

When we consider that this same Congress narrowly passed the Big Beautiful Bill last year and has largely let Trump govern unimpeded, let's paraphrase Mark Twain: "The accounts of my death Republicans voting to restrain Trump are greatly exaggerated".

In the absence of legislative curbs, the resistance to Trump and his programs has been concentrated in the courts (with the notable exception of the Supreme Court except regarding tariffs so far).

Two weeks ago, U.S. District Judge for TN Waverly Crenshaw Jr. in his ruling dismissing the human trafficking case against Kilmar Abrego Garcia cited AG Robert H. Jackson's 1940 address to U.S. attorneys when he said the worst abuse of power is when the prosecutor "picks people he thinks he should get, rather than pick cases which need to be prosecuted".

Then a week ago on Friday, there were three more decisions which blocked Trump excesses:

  • U.S. District Judge for D.C. Christopher Cooper ruled that the Trump co-branding of the Kennedy Center violated federal law. Now that the Center's chief counsel ordered the removal of all references to Trump, Christopher Bates opined that this process "could serve as a metaphor for how his presidency is going".
  • U.S. District Judge Leonie Brinkman in VA temporarily blocked any actions implementing the $1.8B anti-weaponization fund.
  • U.S. District Judge Kathleen Williams in Miami, responding to a motion filed by 35 former federal judges, reopened the $10B suit Trump filed against the IRS because the settlement could be "the product of collusion and is itself a fraud on the court".

Moving on to this week, on Tuesday, a 3-judge panel of the Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit ruled that the Pentagon policy banning transgender troops was illegal. And on Friday, U.S. District Judge for RI John McConnell Jr. ruled that the policy which halted asylum decisions and denied other immigration benefits to people from 39 countries was illegal.

Looking at a broader timeframe, Politico reports that cumulatively federal judges have ruled against ICE detention in 10,400 of 11,600 cases (90%). Moreover, several deportations, e.g. Kilmar Abrego Garcia's, have been stopped. The courts have ruled that the appointment of several U.S. Attorneys, e.g. Alina Habba in NJ, were illegal. They have also reversed the firings of some federal employees, prevented the shuttering/reduction of some federal agencies, e.g. Voice of America, called for the reinstatement of federal grants, overturned guidance forbidding diversity programs in schools, and have dismissed a number of vindictive prosecutions. There have been rulings against the administration in many other areas as well.

Notes and Quotes · Elections

In national polls, Gallup found that only 16% of respondents considered the state of the economy to be excellent or good, 34% rated it as being fair, and 49% considered it to be poor. The positive rating is the lowest since the midst of the pandemic in 2022. A CBS poll found that 54% of non-college Whites disapprove of Trump's job performance compared to 32% at the beginning of his administration.

Amber Phillips in a WAPO article suggested that Mark Kelly, Gavin Newsom and Josh Shapiro were the leading contenders for the 2028 Democratic nomination. An Emerson poll on this subject, which mostly reflected name recognition, was led by Pete Buttigieg at 18%, with Gavin Newsom at 16%, AOC at 11%, and Josh Shapiro and Kamala Harris both at 10%.

In noteworthy election results this week:

  • In the LA mayoral jungle primary, incumbent Democrat Karen Bass leads with 35% of the vote followed by former reality TV star and political newcomer Republican Spencer Pratt with 28.2% of the vote and Democrat Nithya Raman with 24.9%. Given that only 71% of the vote has been counted and that Raman has nearly halved Pratt's lead, there is a real chance that she could prevail. Referring to both the governor's and mayoralty races, Trump unsurprisingly wrote "There's BIG cheating by the Dumocrats in California...Why the vote counting delay?".
  • In the IA Republican gubernatorial primary, Trump-endorsed Randy Feenstra lost by 1% to political newcomer Zach Lahn who will run against Democratic state auditor Rob Sand. Democrat Josh Turek, their preferred candidate, will be running for the Senate against Rep. Ashley Hinson.
  • The NJ-07 Democratic primary to run for the seat formerly held by current governor Mikie Sherrill, a former Navy helicopter pilot and mother of four, was won by Rebecca Bennett, a former Navy helicopter pilot and mother of two, giving new meaning to the term "helicopter mother". She will run against Republican Thomas Kean, son of a former governor, who has been missing from Congress and hasn't voted in months.
  • 270 To Win polling has the battleground Senate races rated D+9 in NC, D+8 in OH, D+7 in ME, D+7 in GA, D+4 in AK, D+1 in IA, MI and TX even, and NB I+5. RealClear Polling has TX R+2, but MI D+7 if the party's preferred candidate Haley Stevens wins the August primary.
  • The ME polls do not yet fully reflect the revelation of Graham Platner's extramarital sexting and the disclosure by the NYT that three women whom he dated described toxic and unsettling relationships with him.
  • TX polling from a week ago has Talarico at +6% favorability with Paxton at −26%, although the polls have tightened since then.
  • The Democratic lead in the generic congressional ballot varies between 4 and 10% among various sources with the Silver Bulletin's 6.9% Democratic lead, comparable to where Democrats stood at this point in 2018, representative.
  • The Supreme Court overturned a lower court decision and reinstated the congressional map which will enable the Republicans to gain one House seat in AL, with the three liberal justices dissenting. Christopher Bates opined about the decision "The Supreme Court is just making it up".
  • On the other hand, the SC Senate followed the lead of their IN counterparts as twelve Republican senators joined with their Democratic colleagues to prevent the last minute adoption of a new congressional map which would result in James Clyburn losing his seat in Congress.

Iran War

The seeds for the Iran War were sown in 2018 when Trump withdrew from the JCPOA which took 20 months to negotiate and was signed in 2015 by China, France, Germany, Russia, the UK and the EU in addition to Iran and the U.S. It is hard to understand why this decision was made as Iran was largely in compliance with the agreement. Perhaps Frank Bruni hit upon the reason in this week's NYT Conversation when he wrote in a more general context "The Trump Administration destroys for the sake of destroying, to erase what its predecessors have done". Notwithstanding that his real estate colleagues with thin credentials, Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, were actually making progress toward an agreement in late February until Benjamin Netanyahu intervened and persuaded the over-confident Trump to go to war.

The war will be entering its 100th day tomorrow and continues to be a stalemate as Iran's leadership has leveraged its ability to block the Strait of Hormuz and willingness to let its population suffer. Meanwhile Trump vacillates in his public announcements and Truth Social posts between saying that a deal is near or expressing that he could care less if the war continues. And whenever it appears that the negotiators are making some progress, he torpedoes any settlement with impulsive posts. As he bloviates on TV and spews forth Truth Social posts at all hours, it must be clear to the Iranians that he really wants to end the war and that they hold the upper hand in this asymmetric conflict.

Here are what some pundits are saying:

  • Jen Rubin wrote "America under Trump is becoming the dispensable nation".
  • Christopher Bates described Trump's conduct of the war as the actions of "a very dangerous kid in a very dangerous candy store".
  • Daniel Drezner of the Fletcher School at Tufts wrote "It only took three months for Trump and his policy principals to realize that all the assumptions they had made going into the conflict were wrong".
