Topic I · Opening Exchange
Colombia at the Crossroads — Fran's Firsthand Account
The Three-Way Race and Its Outcome
Francesca Nannetti had just returned from Colombia the day before its presidential election, making her the session's on-the-ground witness. The field of candidates was winnowed to three serious contenders: Iván Cepeda on the far left (an ex-guerrilla and admirer of Maduro and Petro), Paloma Valencia on the center-right (a senator, described by Fran as well-qualified and a cousin of her late husband Mario), and De La Espriella on the far right (a lawyer with ties to figures of questionable ethics). No candidate cleared a majority, so the runoff pits Cepeda against De La Espriella — the two extremes — with Valencia eliminated.
The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy
Fran described asking voters on the ground how they felt about Valencia. The answer was dispiriting: many thought she was good but couldn't win — because she was a woman, because of this and that — and so didn't vote for her. "I said to them, that's a self-fulfilling prophecy. You're canceling your vote." Her youngest son, who worked on the campaign, concluded she had not run it well. Whatever the cause, the center-right is now out, and Valencia has thrown her support to De La Espriella.
The Stakes and Trump's Thumb on the Scale
Fran was unambiguous about the stakes: Colombia has been a longstanding ally of the United States, and a Cepeda victory could prompt significant capital and family flight — including her own family's business. Cepeda's recent edge: outgoing President Petro raised the minimum wage by 23%, likely swinging some voters left. Linda Fritz asked whether the Trump administration had weighed in. Fran confirmed: Trump has praised De La Espriella and previously froze Petro's financial accounts in a dispute. George noted the two finalists were roughly two points apart. The runoff is in approximately two weeks.
Colombia Runoff — The Two Finalists
Far Left
Iván Cepeda — ex-guerrilla, close ally of Maduro and current President Petro. Benefits from Petro's 23% minimum-wage hike. A Cepeda win, Fran said, would likely trigger significant family and business departures.
Far Right
De La Espriella — lawyer with ties to questionable clients. Trump has praised him publicly. Center-right candidate Paloma Valencia has directed her supporters toward him. Regional trend of rightward elections (Argentina, El Salvador) may favor his chances.
Topic II · Week in Review
Outrages Compounding — Iran, CBS, the Octagon, and Kennedy Center
George's Report: Two Days Later, Everything He Wrote Has Come True
George opened by noting the remarkable consonance between his pre-session written report and the subsequent news: Iran continuing to spiral, the Pulte nomination, and now Todd Blanch nominated as Attorney General — a man who declared, before his nomination, that he would do whatever the president wanted. George's verdict: Blanch makes Pam Bondi look like Robert H. Jackson. The California gubernatorial race, the LA mayoral contest (Spencer Pratt's political pratfall; Karen Bass now facing the progressive Democrat in the final round), and the ongoing spectacle of Trump at the Knicks game — booed to near-unanimity, then claiming it was mostly cheers — rounded out the week.
The CBS Massacre — Barry Weiss and the Bagel Breakfast
Three journalists were fired at CBS, and the new president — Barry Weiss, a print editor with no television background — attempted to soften the blow with bagels and cream cheese. Scott Pelley went ballistic and was subsequently fired as well. John noted the grim irony: CBS was Ed Murrow's network, and every reporter since has aspired to that standard. Now its leadership openly declares that their mission is to drive the news rather than report facts. John placed Weiss "in the gallery of villains, like the worst of the worst." George's counterpoint: Weiss was hired by David Allison to do exactly what she is doing. She is not a coincidence. Meanwhile, the Ellison family has the money and appetite to shield her. CBS viewership has fallen sharply — though 60 Minutes had been up 9% before the massacre.
The Octagon on the South Lawn
Trump is proposing a permanent UFC octagon on the South Lawn — cage fighters prepping at the Lincoln Memorial, then walking through the Oval Office. A lawsuit has already been filed: inappropriate use of public grounds, for-profit activity on public land. George added that Trump has been talking about extending the Lincoln Memorial all the way to the Potomac, and plans for a 250-foot Arc de Triomphe — which George called the Arc de Trump — that would permanently block the sightline from the Memorial toward Arlington National Cemetery. Hal's recurring observation: "You'll get worse. Every time."
Kennedy Center — His Name Coming Off
A court has ordered the Kennedy Center to remove Trump's name from all mentions. The Center has complied to the extent of removing it from the website and promotional materials; the physical building signage remains. John: "I think taking it off the actual building will do great damage to his psyche — at least I hope it does." George noted that a commentator described the deletion of all references as a metaphor for the entire presidency. The group suspected the administration would find ways to delay or resist the physical removal.
"There's no bar too low. He's going to drag us through the mud for the next four years. The only recourse is to keep supporting the institutions that oppose him, take the wins when you can, and keep as steady a course as you can — so that when we get past this, the country can start rebuilding." — John Day
Topic III · Data and Discussion
America's Demographic Reckoning — Birth Rates, Immigration, and the Numbers That Don't Add Up
Larry's Puzzle — and Its Resolution
Larry Schwirian had flagged a statistical puzzle: news reports indicated more American citizens were leaving the country than immigrants entering, while the fertility rate sits at roughly 1.6 — well below the 2.1 replacement threshold. Yet U.S. population still grew by approximately 0.8% in 2025. John helped him disentangle it: the confusion lay partly in the distinction between fertility rate (children per woman of childbearing age) and birth rate (births per thousand of total population), and partly in the fact that net immigration — even at suppressed levels under the current administration — still exceeds the net outflow of citizens. Mark Seliber's reading confirmed: total immigration inflow likely still exceeds outbound migration.
The Cell Phone Theory — and the Heterogeneity Problem
Michael Berger cited a Boston Globe article arguing that a principal driver of the birth rate decline since the 1980s–90s is the smartphone: people are on their phones at night rather than doing what produces children. He added a caution against clean national statistics: the U.S. is too heterogeneous for uniform projections. Maternal and infant mortality rates differ sharply across racial groups; women are having children later; in vitro fertilization and other technologies are changing the calculus. George noted that U.S. life expectancy (79) lags peer nations (82.7) — a gap attributable partly to this diversity of outcomes and uneven access to competent medical care, a problem likely to worsen with Medicaid restrictions.
Immigration as Economic Infrastructure
Larry flagged that in states like California and Texas, up to 40% of construction workers are immigrants, legal and illegal — and housing construction is already badly lagging demand. Hilary Kent argued the service industries most resistant to AI substitution will face severe workforce shortages. John made the broader case: one of the defining engines of American economic success over the past fifty years has been a relatively open immigration policy that drew people determined to work hard and move up. The share of major corporations now led by immigrants or their direct descendants is "non-trivial." George added that immigrant crime rates are significantly lower than those of native-born citizens — "notwithstanding what we hear from the right."
The Global Picture
Larry noted that declining fertility is a global phenomenon — not just American. Even India, which recently surpassed China as the world's most populous nation, now has a negative population growth rate. The sole major exception: the African continent. Sally observed that Europe has been grappling with low population for decades, and the policy response — increased immigration — has generated its own backlash. Her conclusion: people are simply going to have to work longer. "If you're going to live to 98, you shouldn't feel entitled to retire at 65."
Topic IV · Electoral Dilemma
The Plattner Question — Vote, Abstain, or Hold Your Nose?
The Dilemma Framed
Hilary Kent put the question to the group: would you vote for Plattner in Maine, given what has come out about him? The background: sexual texting, allegations of roughing up a girlfriend (which he denies), a troubling tattoo, and a recent article by a former campaign staffer painting him as a loose cannon. His Democratic primary opponent, Janet Mills, effectively dropped out. It is a near-foregone conclusion he wins the primary. The general election opponent would be Susan Collins.
The Group's Verdict — Divided
John polled the group. Six hands went up for Plattner. Sally, Beth Davis, and Larry said they would not vote at all. Hilary was a half — "probably wouldn't vote, not happy about it." Hal Shear made the most impassioned case for Plattner: he knows veterans who did three tours, came back with PTSD, and went through periods of alcohol and heroin dependency — and he doesn't believe those aberrations should define a person's fitness for office. Linda Fritz was unequivocal: "You gotta get rid of Collins." Sally's counter: the Democrats couldn't find someone more morally unblemished in a state like Maine? George's wry coda: "Hegseth and Trump have been down this road, and you don't even hear about it."
Plattner Vote — Group Positions
Vote for Plattner (6 hands)
Hal Shear, Linda Fritz, George, and others: character flaws from a difficult past should not override the strategic imperative of unseating Collins and flipping the Senate. "You have to win first." — Lincoln, paraphrased by John.
Abstain / Not Vote (3–4)
Sally, Beth, Larry: the pattern of behavior disqualifies him regardless of the opponent. "I just don't think they should be in office." Hilary: deeply torn, probably stays home. No one said they would vote for Collins.

The Thread That Ran Through Everything
From Bogotá to the Knicks game to a demographic reckoning to Maine's Senate race: this session circled a single question — how do societies choose when all the choices are bad?

Fran's Colombia story is a mirror for the American moment: when the sensible center is eliminated by a self-fulfilling prophecy of electability, voters are left choosing between extremes they distrust. The Plattner debate played out the same logic domestically — hold your nose and win, or preserve your integrity and cede the field to someone worse.

George's "Week in Review" supplied the drumbeat: CBS surrendered to power; the Senate voted against Trump in forms that cost nothing; the Kennedy Center name is coming off the website but probably not the building. The wins are real but narrow, the outrages accelerating. And the demographic data underneath it all — an aging country, a shrinking workforce, an immigration policy that is dismantling the very engine of renewal — suggest that the long-term costs of this moment extend well past 2028.

John's closing note: steady the course. Support the institutions. Take the wins. The country can start rebuilding — but only if it gets past this first.
