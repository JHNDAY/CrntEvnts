The Legal Authority Chain — How Presidents Can Impose Tariffs
✗
IEEPA (1977) — STRUCK DOWN
Emergency powers for genuine national security threats. No prior president used this for tariffs. Court: "regulate importation" ≠ power to tax.
No procedural limits — which is why Trump used it
✓
Section 232 (Trade Expansion Act 1962) — INTACT
National security tariffs — steel, aluminum, autos. Commerce Dept. investigation required. Time-tested in courts.
Requires national security finding — but broadly interpreted
~
Section 122 (Trade Act 1974) — Trump's new choice
Balance-of-payments authority. Used within hours of ruling. Limited to 150 days without Congressional approval. Global application.
150-day limit — Congress can override — being tested in courts
?
Section 301 (Trade Act 1974) + Section 338 (1930)
Used against China in Trump's first term. Requires investigation and findings of unfair practices. Section 338 almost never used — authority untested.
Procedural requirements add time — but courts have upheld broadly
March 12 Update: The Court's ruling is real — it struck down IEEPA as the legal vehicle. But Trump immediately replaced it with Section 122 tariffs (10%, now heading to 15%). On Mar 12, 24 states filed suit challenging the Section 122 tariffs as also illegal, arguing the required balance-of-payments deficit doesn't legally exist. If that suit succeeds, Trump would need to fall back further on Section 301 and 338 — both of which carry more procedural requirements. Whether that matters depends on whether courts continue to hold the line.
