02 Military Asymmetric Warfare: The US Is Running Out of Ammunition
DayFortessMcKnight
Summary
Discussion Questions
Supporting Details
The fundamental military economics of this war are upside-down: the US is spending millions to destroy thousands. Iran's drone arsenal is vast, cheap, and battle-tested. US Patriots are nearly depleted, produced at a trickle, and now being pulled from South Korea.
  • US Patriot missile: $4M+ each. Iranian Shahad drone: ~$35,000. The US is shooting down drones at a 100:1 cost ratio
  • The US had "a few hundred" Patriots at the start; most are now expended; production runs ~a dozen per year
  • Patriots are now being pulled from South Korea — alarming China and undermining confidence in US allies from Japan to Poland
  • Iran has enormous stockpiles of drones and ballistic missiles — dispersed, hardened, and practically inexhaustible at this scale
  • Ukraine (a US aid recipient) has developed a $1–2K Shahad-killer drone — better suited to this threat than anything the US has fielded
  • DARPA's micro-swarm concept (MIT's Alan Epstein, 15 years ago) is now being realized in Ukrainian battlefield innovation
  • US and Israel are simply not built — structurally or economically — for sustained attritional drone warfare
Q1
We're firing $4M missiles to destroy $35K drones — at near-zero stock remaining. Did no one in the planning process run this arithmetic before Feb 28? What does the absence of that calculus tell us about how this war was decided?
Intent: Focus on pre-war planning failure and civilian-military decision-making
Q2
Ukraine — which we arm — has already solved part of this problem with a $1–2K counter-drone weapon. Why is the most advanced military in the world learning battlefield innovation from a country under siege? What does this say about how the Pentagon prioritizes R&D?
Intent: Draw out the innovation / industrial base argument
Q3
Moving Patriots from South Korea weakens our deterrent vis-à-vis both North Korea and China simultaneously. Is there a point at which our military commitments in the Middle East structurally undermine our ability to honor our Pacific commitments?
Intent: Connect the regional to the global security picture
Q4
John Day suggested DARPA's micro-swarm concept from 15 years ago anticipated this problem — but wasn't pursued because it wasn't "big and shiny." Is the US military-industrial complex structurally incapable of investing in cheap, replicable solutions at scale?
Intent: Explore the political economy of defense procurement

Summary
Discussion Questions
Supporting Details

04 Historical Lens Airstrikes Have Never Once Produced Regime Change
FishmanMcKnight
Summary
Discussion Questions
Supporting Details
Political scientist Robert Pape has documented exhaustively that airstrikes to achieve regime change have a success rate of zero. The session treated this not as controversial but as established fact — and asked why the US keeps doing it anyway.
  • Robert Pape (U. Chicago) ran the regression across all modern cases: airstrikes to achieve political objectives have never worked
  • The mechanism is well understood: external bombardment converts internal opposition into national resistance — "us vs. them" shifts from regime vs. citizens to nation vs. invader
  • Historical cases adduced by the group: German bombing of London, US bombing of Hanoi, Iraq, Afghanistan
  • You can kill a leader — but the result is typically a more radical, more motivated successor with a unified population behind them
  • The new Iranian Supreme Leader is younger, more radical, and was personally motivated by the killing of his family
  • The US seems to have learned this lesson from the sugar-high of the Maduro operation in Venezuela — and misapplied it catastrophically
Q1
Pape's research is unambiguous and decades old. Why do US administrations — across parties — keep reaching for airstrikes as a political tool when the evidence against it is so clear? Is this ignorance, political pressure, or something else?
Intent: Explore the gap between academic knowledge and policy practice
Q2
The London Blitz, the bombing of Hanoi, shock-and-awe in Baghdad — the group listed these as analogues. Are there any cases where external military force successfully produced the political outcome the attacker sought? Does Iran fit any of those patterns?
Intent: Steel-man the opposing view before rejecting it
Q3
McKnight suggested the "sugar high" from toppling Maduro in Venezuela may have given the administration false confidence. How much does recent success at one type of operation distort decision-making for a fundamentally different one?
Intent: Examine the cognitive biases behind strategic miscalculation
Pape's Research — Referenced by McKnight
Robert Pape (University of Chicago) — "Bombing to Win" and subsequent work: airstrikes to compel political change have a success rate of zero across all documented modern cases
The mechanism: external attack triggers the "us vs. them" switch — even citizens who oppose a regime unite behind it against a foreign invader
McKnight: "It never works precisely because of what Bob made clear — you rally that population around even the unpopular leading group. Very simple, straightforward logic."
Historical Parallels Cited in Session
German bombing of London (WWII) — strengthened British resolve, did not produce surrender or regime change
US bombing of Hanoi (Vietnam War) — North Vietnam continued fighting; US withdrew; Hanoi reunified the country
Iraq (2003): shock and awe destroyed infrastructure; did not produce a stable, US-aligned political outcome
Afghanistan (2001–2021): twenty years of air superiority did not prevent Taliban restoration
"We keep frickin' doing it. And we probably got a sugar high from getting Maduro so quickly. This is not Venezuela. Very, very clearly not."
— Scott McKnight, 00:28:09

07 Global Strategy Beyond Oil: The Hidden Supply Chain Fractures No One Is Talking About
McKnightHalpinShearman
Summary
Discussion Questions
Supporting Details
The Strait of Hormuz is not just an oil chokepoint. It is a nexus for sulfur, fertilizer, LNG for Taiwan's chip fabs, and inbound food for the Gulf. The cascading effects reach batteries, semiconductors, and global food supply.
  • Sulfur: 92% of the world's sulfur comes from oil and gas refining. Sulfuric acid is essential to extracting copper, cobalt, and nickel — the core materials for EV batteries and data center infrastructure
  • Fertilizer: 33% of the world's fertilizer feedstock moves through Hormuz. Synthetic nitrogen fertilizer feeds roughly half the world's population — India, Brazil, and much of the Global South depend on it
  • Taiwan LNG → Chips: Qatar ships ~30% of Taiwan's LNG through Hormuz. Taiwan has ~one week of natural gas reserves. TSMC alone uses 10% of Taiwan's electricity. No gas → no power → no chips
  • Inbound food: Gulf nations are extreme food import-dependent — cars, computers, and food all flow in through Hormuz
  • Phosphorus: Bob Halpin added that phosphorus — on which industrial agriculture is completely dependent — has its largest global reserve in Morocco
  • Art Shearman's framing: "When are we going to realize that this global economy we've created is really very fragile?" The Ever Given in Suez was a warning. This is the test
Q1
If Taiwan's TSMC runs out of LNG and loses power, the global semiconductor supply chain stops. This is not a theoretical risk — it's a one-week countdown. Has the US government publicly acknowledged this? What's the contingency plan?
Intent: Make the Taiwan chip supply chain consequence concrete and urgent
Q2
33% of global fertilizer feedstock flows through Hormuz. A sustained closure means food price spikes in India, Brazil, and sub-Saharan Africa — nations that had nothing to do with this war. What are the geopolitical consequences of inadvertently destabilizing the Global South?
Intent: Broaden the frame beyond US/Iran to global humanitarian and political consequences
Q3
Art Shearman asked when we'll realize how fragile the global economy is. John Day offered a defense of globalization's achievements. Can both be true — and if so, what is the right policy response: deglobalize, or build resilience within globalized systems?
Intent: Draw out the globalization debate seeded in the session
Cascade Chain
Strait Closes → Sulfur (92% from oil refining) → Sulfuric acid → Copper, cobalt, nickel extraction → EV batteries and data centers disrupted
Strait Closes → Qatar LNG (30% of Taiwan's supply) → TSMC (10% of Taiwan's electricity) → Advanced chip production halts
Strait Closes → Fertilizer feedstock (33% of world supply) → Synthetic nitrogen → Food production shock in India, Brazil, Global South
Strait Closes → Inbound food, cars, computers to Gulf nations also disrupted — the region imports nearly everything it doesn't produce in oil
McKnight Summary Quote
"No gas, no power, no chips. We end up in this cascading effect. 33% of the world's fertilizer feedstock. Synthetic nitrogen is vital to about half the world's population food supply."

09 Energy Transition The Oil Crisis as Latent Accelerant for Clean Energy — If the Pain Sustains
HalpinShapiroMcKnight
Summary
Discussion Questions
Supporting Details
The session's closing frame: sustained high oil prices are the strongest possible economic signal toward EVs, solar, and grid decarbonization. But the political counter-force — the current administration's fossil-fuel lock-in — is formidable. The question is whether pain lasts long enough to overcome it.
  • $4–$5/gallon gas historically shifts consumer behavior toward EVs and hybrids — the economic signal is powerful if sustained
  • Electricity: only 4% is traded across borders — it is inherently local and sovereign, unlike oil which requires vast, vulnerable global supply chains
  • A greened, distributed grid can bring new resources online faster than oil pipelines — strategic flexibility that centralized fossil fuel systems cannot match
  • Decarbonization also defunds authoritarian petrostates: Russia, Iran, Gulf monarchies — McKnight's closing moral argument
  • The current administration's Energy Secretary frames renewables as "unreliable" — political capture by utilities and fossil fuel interests is the dominant counter-force
  • Dave Chosiad's explicit challenge: the group never heard the strongest version of the administration's case against the all-sources grid — that conversation was promised for next session
  • Battery-making, solar PV, EVs — industries born after globalization, never built domestically at scale; rebuilding them requires following before leading
Q1
Dave Chosiad explicitly asked the group to hear the strongest case for the administration's "reliability over renewables" position — and the session ran out of time. What IS the best version of that argument? Where does it succeed on its own terms, and where does it fail?
