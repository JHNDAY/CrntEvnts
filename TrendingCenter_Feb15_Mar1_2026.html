Oil markets convulsed and airspace across the Middle East closed.

02 Trump Administration Reversals & Growing Liabilities 50 mentions ▼
SCOTUS Strikes Down IEEPA Tariffs 6–3 (Feb 20)
The Supreme Court ruled 6–3 in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump that IEEPA does not grant the president authority to impose tariffs. The ruling invalidated the broad "reciprocal tariff" regime, though Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, and autos remain. The administration faces possible refund obligations of over $133 billion in collected duties.
Bloomberg Politico BBC
Trump Pivots to Section 122 — Raises Tariffs to 15%
Within 24 hours of the SCOTUS ruling, Trump signed an executive order imposing a new 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the 1974 Trade Act, then escalated to 15% via Truth Social, calling it "effective immediately." The tariff expires in 150 days. USMCA partners received partial exemptions; some imports from Canada, Mexico, and select Latin American countries were carved out.
Bloomberg Politico Mediaite
State of the Union Fact-Check Gap
Trump's Feb 24 SOTU — longest in history at nearly two hours — drew extensive fact-checking from NPR, PBS, and Bloomberg. Key disputes: Trump claimed 100% of jobs created were private-sector (misleading); trumpeted record oil production (EIA data shows 2026 production expected to drop); and characterized the "One Big Beautiful Bill" as the largest tax cut in history (it ranked 6th per Tax Foundation).
Bloomberg Politico BBC
Health Grant Cuts Blocked by Courts
Federal judges in New York and Illinois blocked HHS from rescinding $600 million in public health grants to Democratic-led states that had resisted Trump's immigration enforcement. Courts ruled the cuts violated the Constitution by retroactively conditioning already-approved congressional funding. Illinois AG Raoul's lawsuit called the cuts retaliatory and unconstitutional.
Bloomberg Politico BBC
Third-Country Deportation Policy Ruled Unlawful
A federal judge ruled in February that the administration's practice of deporting migrants to third countries — nations other than their home country — violates federal immigration law and due process. A Senate Democrats report showed the program cost up to $1 million per deportee, with millions paid to governments with documented human rights abuses.
Al Jazeera Politico Bloomberg
DOJ Accused of Spying on Congressional Searches of Epstein Files
During AG Bondi's House testimony, she was photographed holding a document titled "Jayapal Pramila Search History," revealing that the DOJ had logged members' specific searches of unredacted Epstein files in the DOJ reading room. Bipartisan outrage erupted. Speaker Johnson called it "inappropriate." Democrats launched a formal investigation into potential congressional surveillance.

03 ICE Operations 46 mentions ▼
Record Detention — 73,000 in Custody, 70%+ Without Criminal Record
By mid-February, ICE detention had grown 84% since January 2025, reaching 73,000 people — the highest ever. Over 70% have no criminal record. 2025 was the deadliest year for ICE detention in two decades, with 32 deaths; six more have died since January 2026. "At-large" street arrests increased elevenfold compared to the pre-Trump era.
Al Jazeera Bloomberg Politico
DHS Social Media Campaign Challenged for Misrepresenting Detainees
NPR analysis found that DHS and ICE social media accounts posted about more than 2,000 "worst of the worst" detainees, but investigation of 130 Minnesota cases found widespread inaccuracies — including posting the wrong person's photo. Experts noted immigrants generally commit fewer crimes than US citizens, calling the campaign a "distorted picture" of reality.
Al Jazeera Politico Bloomberg
56 Court Orders Violated — NJ Judge Demands Explanation
Government attorneys identified 56 court orders ICE had violated through mid-February 2026, including missed deadlines, prohibited transfers, and deportations after injunctions were issued. NJ District Judge Farbiarz ordered the government to explain its compliance procedures. Reuters found over 650 people charged with "catch-all" obstruction laws for peacefully monitoring ICE operations.
Al Jazeera Bloomberg Politico
Minnesota Drawdown — 700 Agents Pulled After Citizen Shootings
Border Czar Tom Homan announced the drawdown of 700 enforcement personnel from Minnesota following the January killings of US citizens Renée Good and Alex Pretti by ICE and CBP agents. Homan cited new cooperation with local jails. A Marquette poll found 60% of Americans disapprove of ICE's conduct, though 80% of Republicans still approve. A DOJ civil rights investigation into Pretti's death was opened.
Al Jazeera Politico Bloomberg
Administrative Warrants and Forced Home Entry Policy Challenged
ICE has been operating under a secret policy allowing agents to enter homes by force using administrative (non-judicial) I-205 warrants. The policy was not publicly disclosed for months. At least one US citizen was arrested in a mistaken home raid. Federal courts across the country in 2025–2026 challenged the constitutionality of this approach, setting up a likely SCOTUS confrontation.

07 Supreme Court & Other Legal Issues 30 mentions ▼
Learning Resources v. Trump — IEEPA Tariffs Struck Down 6–3
The Supreme Court's Feb 20 ruling that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs is the most consequential economic ruling of Trump's second term. The majority held that tariffs function as taxes under Congress's constitutional authority. Two Trump-appointed justices joined the majority. The $133 billion in collected duties may need to be refunded, with litigation expected to drag for years.
Bloomberg Politico BBC
Federal Judges Block Funding Cuts to Democratic States
By mid-February, courts had halted Trump's funding cuts or compelled resolution in more than half of 167 spending fights tracked by Bloomberg. Judges blocked $600 million in HHS grant rescissions to four Democratic states, extended an injunction protecting $10 billion in social service funding to five states, and ruled retroactive funding conditions unconstitutional.
Bloomberg Politico BBC
Immigration Due Process Battles Proliferate
Dozens of federal judges — including Trump appointees — ruled against the administration's efforts to limit due process for immigrants.
