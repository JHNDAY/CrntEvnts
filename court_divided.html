A Court Divided Against Itself

Jesse Wegman, Brennan Center for Justice · Read original in The New York Times ↗

Two justices. Two public outbursts. One apology — from the wrong person. As momentous rulings approach, a revealing glimpse of a court under institutional strain.

What Thomas ignored

The Progressive Era produced four constitutional amendments — including direct Senate elections and women's suffrage — each ratified by supermajorities. A triumph of democracy, not its undoing.

Conservative then & now The aggrieved ideologue and the pragmatic justice
Justice Clarence Thomas
Associate Justice, 1991–present  ·  Conservative wing
Thomas has spoken at length of his contempt for Yale Law School, his alienation from Washington social life, and a sense that the establishment has always treated him as an outsider — resentments he has carried onto the bench for three decades.
His jurisprudence is animated less by how a ruling affects ordinary Americans than by the internal logic of an originalist framework — and by a barely concealed score to settle with the progressive establishment he holds responsible for his confirmation ordeal.
Ideological grievance
Justice Sandra Day O'Connor
Associate Justice, 1981–2006  ·  Conservative swing vote
O'Connor's guiding question, in case after case, was a practical one: what are the real-world consequences for the people who will live under this ruling? Abstract doctrine mattered less to her than the human texture of the outcome.
The first woman on the court, she became its decisive center of gravity for a generation — not because she was unprincipled but because her conservatism was grounded in consequence rather than grievance. After retiring she founded iCivics to rebuild the democratic literacy the court increasingly seemed to take for granted.
Pragmatic consequence
⚡

The conservative tradition Thomas abandoned: O'Connor and Thomas served together for fifteen years and shared a party of appointment — yet their judicial temperaments could scarcely be more different. Where O'Connor asked what a ruling would mean for a rancher in Arizona or a student in Michigan, Thomas asks what the Founders would have said. Where she saw the court as an instrument of practical governance, he treats it as a platform for intellectual combat. O'Connor worried, in retirement, that the court was losing the public's trust. Thomas, by inviting a billionaire donor onto a national stage, seems indifferent to the concern.

Historical contrast When ideological rivals were still friends
Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg
Associate Justice, 1993–2020  ·  Liberal wing
In 2016, Ginsburg called Trump "a faker" with no consistency about him — openly prejudicial remarks about a sitting presidential candidate that she soon said she deeply regretted.
She apologized. Wegman notes the bitter irony: it is invariably the women on the court who find the courage to say they made a mistake.
Justice Antonin Scalia
Associate Justice, 1986–2016  ·  Conservative wing
In his Obergefell dissent, Scalia wrote he would "hide my head in a bag" before joining the majority's opinion — dismissing it as "the mystical aphorisms of the fortune cookie."
Withering mockery of colleagues was a Scalia signature. He never apologized — yet the court did not feel, as it does now, like a body coming apart at the seams.
♪

What the current court conspicuously lacks: Ginsburg and Scalia disagreed on nearly everything and sparred fiercely in print — yet they shared opera tickets, celebrated New Year's Eve together, and sustained a warm personal friendship across four decades on the bench. Scalia once said Ginsburg was his favorite colleague. Their rivalry was professional, not tribal. On today's court, reshaped by a decade of bare-knuckle confirmation politics, there is little evidence of comparable warmth across the ideological divide. The argument has curdled into something more personal — and the institution is paying the price.

The court's institutional moment

The Supreme Court is approaching near-record-low public approval ratings as it prepares to rule on major cases involving the administrative state. Wegman's verdict: one justice offered a court that would work for America — tough-minded, passionate, willing to admit error. The other gave us the court we have: smug, unapologetic, and gleefully divisive. The irony of the pattern holds — it is the women who say they made a mistake.
