Breaking News: A Senator Passes

Senator Lindsey Graham died overnight from a brief and sudden illness, occurring just after his 71st birthday on Thursday and following a meeting with President Zelensky in Kyiv on Friday. In an interview, former Missouri Democratic Senator Claire McCaskill offered a nuanced assessment of her former Republican colleague, lauding his loyalty to John McCain, his kindness when she entered the Senate in 2007, and his participation in the Gang of Eight immigration reform effort of 2013. She also highlighted his support for Israel and Ukraine, and his characteristically sunny disposition. Yet she acknowledged frustration with his conversion to MAGA politics. Despite these reservations, McCaskill emphasized that he was a good man and a dedicated public servant. Graham was in his 32nd year in Congress, having served four terms in both the House and Senate.

Trump Family Corruption: The Memecoin Windfall

Trump's 2025 financial disclosure indicated that he earned $635 million from the $TRUMP memecoin, with income arising only from transaction and licensing fees. Blockchain data released by Nansen on Monday reveals a stark reality: the million memecoin investors have collectively lost $3.8 billion to date. Much of this loss stems from a 97% decline in price from the coin's $75 peak. Undoubtedly, the Trump family and other insiders sold most or all of their shares before the price plummeted. This means that a substantial portion of the investors' $3.2 billion in lost value was essentially transferred to the Trumps and their associates—a textbook case of insider trading and market manipulation enriching the connected few.

Commenting on Trump's self-enrichment while in office, former New Jersey Governor Chris Christie remarked: "Here's another thing Trump learned from Putin during his first term...Trump and his family believe that they have license to take whatever they can." Earlier this week, additional revelations emerged that Jared Kushner and Trump's sons had received $13 billion from foreign governments—a staggering sum that raises profound questions about conflicts of interest and foreign influence.

America's 250th Anniversary: Pageantry and Dysfunction

During his July 4 speech, Trump declared: "Our warriors did not fight communism on battlefields around the world only to have that menace rear its ugly head right back here in America." The Guardian's David K. Smith captured the tone acidly: "It all made for the longest show on earth—the defeat of a king being celebrated by a would-be king." Historian Ron Chernow added pointedly: "I just can't imagine two human beings who are more dissimilar than George Washington and Donald Trump."

The Trump team and the RNC conducted focus groups testing the reversion to Joe McCarthy/Roy Cohn themes from 1953. Early results found the messaging energized the base but proved ineffectual with independents and younger voters. After the robust fireworks display, Washington D.C. briefly experienced the worst air quality in the world.

Aircraft Controversy: NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman had planned to fly his personal collection of fighter jets as part of a 9-hour parade of aircraft over the capital, inviting several administration officials. The FAA initially rejected the proposal. Isaacman overcame the objection by characterizing the planes as "public use aircraft," and Trump directed Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche to fly with Isaacman in his plane.

Funding Diversion: House Democrats accused the administration of diverting most of the congressionally-allocated $100 million funding for America 250 to Trump's Freedom 250 initiative.

Reflecting Pool Saga: The Reflecting Pool was drained again on Saturday—a recurring problem. Interior Secretary Doug Burgum stuck to Trump's narrative that vandalism was responsible. Former Olympic canoeist David Hearn pleaded not guilty on Thursday to felony destruction of property charges. His lawyer countered: "This indictment reflects the administration's efforts to scapegoat Davy and to shift blame for their own failures." At least six other individuals have been arrested on similar charges.

Commemorative Currency: Plans to include Trump's portrait on a commemorative $250 bill are underway. Simultaneously, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent announced that the long-proposed plan to include Harriet Tubman's image on the $20 bill, replacing Andrew Jackson's, has been scrapped.

NATO Summit in Ankara: Allies in Distress

On July 4, two days before departing for the NATO summit in Ankara, Trump spent 90 minutes on the phone with Vladimir Putin. This choice of meeting preparation—consulting with America's strategic adversary—raised eyebrows and prompted reflection on whether Trump's long-standing antipathy toward NATO has been shaped by the Russian leader.

Trump's NATO Record:

  • In 2018, he referred to the U.S. as "the schmucks that are paying for the whole thing."
  • During his 2024 campaign, when hypothetically asked what he would do if Russia attacked a NATO country that didn't "pay up," he responded: "No, I would not protect you...I would encourage them to do whatever the hell they want. You've got to pay."
  • In April, Trump said: "We never needed them. They needed us."
  • When meeting with NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte in April, he complained: "I just want their loyalty."
  • Last week, before his Putin call, Trump claimed our NATO support was "ridiculous...when the relationship is not reciprocal."

Defense Spending Reality Check: At Trump's behest, NATO countries have agreed to spend 5.0% of their GDP by 2035 (3.5% for core defense and 1.5% for infrastructure/security). NATO's latest press release on defense investment (2014-2026) shows 2026 figures:

  • Europe and Canada (31 countries): $777MM spending, $30.6T GDP, 2.53% ratio
  • United States: $1,033MM spending, $32.5T GDP, 3.17% ratio
  • Total: $1,810MM spending, $63.1T GDP, 2.86% ratio

The U.S. core defense spend of 3.17% ranks 8th among 32 members. Notably, nine NATO members spend a higher percentage of GDP: Lithuania (5.33%), Estonia (5.10%), Latvia (4.92%), Poland (4.68%), Greece (3.65%), Denmark (3.49%), Sweden (3.22%), and several others. Our key allies Germany (2.69%), the U.K. (2.56%), Canada (2.33%), Italy (2.30%), and France (2.22%) all spend less.

This metric, however, warrants contextualization. U.S. defense spending encompasses a worldwide presence—Asia, the Middle East, Latin America—and reflects decades of hegemonic commitments. Most NATO members' spending is exclusively for regional defense. NATO, founded in 1949 under Truman with the Marshall Plan, has successfully maintained international order for 80 years. Yet that post-WWII architecture now faces peril.

This would make Ukraine the third country licensed for production, joining Germany and Japan. However, Raytheon and Lockheed Martin have not been consulted, and the lead time and expense would be formidable.

The Platner Affair: Candidacy in Freefall

Graham Platner arrived on the political scene last year as a 40-year-old charismatic progressive populist Democratic candidate for Maine's U.S. Senate seat, challenging Republican incumbent Susan Collins. As with many meteoric rises, early promise gave way to troubling revelations: an SS-inspired Nazi tattoo, controversial Reddit posts calling himself a "psychedelics taking socialist," disparaging comments about women, and innuendo about problematic romantic relationships. Despite these liabilities, Platner's lead over Collins peaked at 9% in May and settled in the 7% range even after sexting allegations surfaced.

A New York Times article featuring allegations from three former girlfriends of volatile and toxic behavior sent tremors through his candidacy. Platner's response—admitting he had been a "bad boyfriend" and dismissively characterizing one accuser as a "lifelong GOP operative"—failed to contain the damage. His poll lead dropped to 2%.

The true collapse came on Monday when Jenny Racicot, one of the three women in the Times article and a self-described Democrat who dated Platner between 2019 and 2021, disclosed that he had arrived at her home drunk in 2021 after she texted him not to come over, and raped her. Though Platner denied the allegation, he said he was "taking the time to reflect" on his campaign. Thirty-nine of 47 Democratic senators urged him to end his candidacy by Wednesday morning—the deadline was critical, as Monday marked the ballot removal deadline, and emerging polls showed him trailing Collins by 5%.

What Jennifer Rubin characterized as "reflecting every ounce of his self-absorption," Platner refused to submit his withdrawal letter. By Thursday, he admitted "for the movement to succeed, it can't be me," but attempted to leverage influence in selecting his successor. Finally, on Friday, he formally submitted his withdrawal.

Commentary from New York Times Pundits:

  • Michelle Goldberg, admitting her own prior support: "If there's a lesson here, it might be about the importance of listening hard to the people telling you what you don't want to hear."
  • Frank Bruni concurred: "When you see lightning, you will soon see thunder."
  • Bret Stephens quipped: "I was about as shocked to hear about Platner's demise as Captain Renault was to find out that there was gambling at Rick's."
  • Bruni added: "For all of your just criticisms vis-à-vis Platner, they didn't end up debasing themselves as thoroughly as Republican officials did vis-à-vis Trump, whose Platner-plus storehouse of sins was accepted long ago."

Path Forward: Democrats must choose a replacement candidate by July 27 at a state convention in Bangor on July 25. The 601 participants will include 101 State Democratic Committee members and 500 delegates chosen through county nominating meetings. Candidates must submit 500 signatures with at least 50 from eight different counties. Leading contenders include former State Senate President Troy Jackson (progressive), Nirav Shah (former public health director), Secretary of State Shana Bellows (progressive), and Dan Kleban (founder of Maine Beer Company).

Despite the scandal, Nate Cohn's New York Times analysis suggests Platner's replacement may be a slight favorite over Collins, given her low approval rating and association with Trump, combined with Maine voters' 12-point preference for Democrats over Republicans.

Election Interference and Democratic Vulnerabilities

Corruption of Electoral Process: Trump's efforts to corrupt the midterms are advancing systematically. He refused to sign the bipartisan housing bill on June 29, citing lack of Senate progress on the SAVE voter suppression act. The bill became law at midnight Friday when the veto period expired. The DOJ's attempt to obtain personal information on Fulton County election workers from 2020 was blocked by Trump-appointed U.S. District Judge William Ray—an ominous warning to election workers in the upcoming midterms. Utah's Republican Lieutenant Governor Deidre Henderson described receiving threatening DOJ letters with "threats of criminal prosecution."

On Thursday, citing the Slaughter decision, the White House fired two Democrats on the bipartisan Election Assistance Commission and induced the remaining Republican to resign. The Commission certifies election equipment and ensures smooth state and local elections. Replacement will require time-consuming Senate confirmation. David Axelrod warned: "All the warning signs are flashing red...setting up Plan B—do whatever you need to do to win."

Midterm Democratic Strategy: Democratic strategist Jim Manley opined: "We truly have a chance to try and flip the Senate, but only if everything goes just exactly perfectly."

Michigan Democratic Primary: State Senator Mallory McMorrow withdrew from the Michigan Democratic Senate primary, leaving progressive Dr. Abdul El-Sayed and establishment Representative Haley Stevens. El-Sayed favors Medicare for All, calls Gaza suffering a genocide, and advocates abolishing ICE. Stevens supports a public option, is nuanced on Gaza, and champions the auto industry. With over 200,000 Arab Americans in Michigan (second only to California), the distinction matters. Polling now shows Stevens with a narrow 1% lead after trailing in the three-way race. The winner faces Mike Rogers, who lost to Elissa Slotkin by 0.3% in 2024. Notably, Jewish-American Senator Slotkin speaks fluent Arabic from her CIA service, while Arab-American El-Sayed does not.

Environmental Degradation

On Friday, the administration weakened the Endangered Species Act (1973) by restricting "harm" to protecting plants and animals while excluding habitat protection. They framed the previous definition as "regulatory intrusion that interfered with private property rights." Earthjustice attorney Kristen Boyles responded: "A presidential administration now claims that species protected by the Endangered Species Act shouldn't be safe from habitat modification which destroys where they live, raise their young, or search for food."

The same day, Trump appointed climate change skeptic Matthew Wielicki to oversee the government's flagship report on climate change impacts. The EPA fired environmental engineer Kip Tyler for "embarrassing the Agency" when he required a new fish farm to track microplastics.

World Cup Intrusion

Trump's intervention in the World Cup, drawing worldwide attention and marring America's role as host, demonstrated his compulsive need to inject himself into every arena. When star U.S. striker Folarin Balogun—who became a birthright citizen when his English/Nigerian mother went into premature labor while visiting relatives in New York in 2001—received a harsh red card against Bosnia (disqualifying him from the Round of 16 against Belgium), Trump intervened by calling FIFA President Gianni Infantino to rescind the suspension. Though Trump admitted not knowing what a red card was, Infantino complied.

The controversy evaporated the goodwill our team had generated, making America appear as ugly internationalists. The distraction may have affected team performance. On July 6, the U.S. played miserably against Belgium, losing 4-1. Christopher Bates opined: "Trump might be the world's most corrupt individual, while FIFA may be the most corrupt organization."
