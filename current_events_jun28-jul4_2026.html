Supreme Court: The Year in 6-3 Decisions

The conservative majority issued at least thirteen ideologically split 6-3 rulings this term—double last year's count. Key decisions: expanded presidential power to fire agency heads (Slaughter), allowed TPS rescissions and tightened asylum protections, upheld state laws banning trans girls from sports (Kavanaugh), struck down late-ballot counting restrictions (5-4, Barrett), and upheld birthright citizenship—though the margin is fragile. Justice Roberts authored more majority opinions than any other justice.

What Each Decision Means · The Pundits Weigh In

Slaughter (Executive Power): The term's blockbuster. The president can fire any Senate-confirmed official without cause. Sotomayor warned it "reshapes the government in a fundamental way."

TPS & Asylum: Opens mass deportations; closes the border to asylum seekers. ICE doubled detentions in the week following.

Birthright Citizenship: Upheld for now, but Kavanaugh's willingness to reconsider suggests this could flip with a single legislative nudge or one retired justice.

Ballot Counting & Chatrie: 5-4 wins for democracy: late ballots count; cell phone tracking requires warrants. Both fragile, both reversible.

Trans Sports & Party Spending: State laws restricting trans girls; unlimited party spending. The Court accelerates cultural and campaign finance realignment.

The Pundit Consensus: Fritze sees a Court doubling its partisan 6-3 splits. Adler notes this is a conservative Court, not a Trump Court—with limits. Reid asks the key question: "Are there limits on his power?" Slaughter answers: barely. Rubin says the Court has lost its nonpartisan credibility. Litman sees a systematic constitutional overhaul underway, with more radicalism coming.

The 250th Anniversary Spectacle

Trump commandeered the nation's semiquincentennial with Freedom 250 rallies (he headlined the July 4 event with 850,000 fireworks), a chaotic Reflecting Pool renovation that spiraled from $1.8M to $15M (Trump blamed saboteurs; it was algae and shoddy installation), and a sparse "Great American State Fair" running for 16 days at minimal attendance. Meanwhile, the West Wing ballroom project climbed from $200M to $600M and a proposed 250-foot Triumphal Arch would dwarf the Lincoln Memorial.

Claire Valdez ousted Dan Goldman; Brad Lander beat Dan Goldman; 32-year-old Daralizer Chevalier defeated Adriano Espaillat. In Colorado, 29-year-old socialist Melat Kiros upset 15-term progressive Diana DeGette in CO-1, citing Kiros's stance against arms sales to Israel as the deciding factor.

Supreme Court Decisions

The Court's 6-3 decision along ideological lines on Louisiana v. Callais written by Alito issued on April 29, which ruled that the second LA majority-Black district was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander whereas partisan ones are permissible, set the stage for a torrent of decisions deferred to the last six days of its 2025-26 term.

The Twelve Key Late Decisions

  • 6/25 · Mullin v. Doe: Permitted the administration to rescind TPS for about 350k Haitians and 6k Syrians. Roughly 1MM people from 15 other countries could become vulnerable. The 6-3 decision was written by Alito.
  • 6/25 · Mullin v. Al Otro Lado: Ruled that a petitioner must be present in the U.S. to formally request asylum. Migrants at the border have no legal means of directly crossing into the U.S. under any circumstances. The 6-3 decision was written by Alito.
  • 6/25 · Wolford v. Lopez: Struck down a HI gun law requiring explicit consent for individuals to carry guns onto private property open to the public. The 6-3 decision was written by Alito.
  • 6/25 · Monsanto v. Durnell: Blocked suits against Monsanto for not having included cancer warning labels on Roundup containers. The 7-2 decision, with Gorsuch and Jackson in dissent, was written by Kavanaugh.
  • 6/29 · Trump v. Slaughter: Permitted the president to fire Senate-confirmed agency heads without cause, overturning the Court's unanimous 1935 ruling on Humphrey's Executor. The 6-3 decision was written by Roberts. Sotomayor wrote the ruling "reshapes the government in a fundamental way" giving the president "a power unknown even to the English Crown against which the founders revolted."
  • 6/29 · Trump v. Cook: Permitted Lisa Cook to retain her position as a Federal Reserve Governor. The 5-4 decision, with Kavanaugh and Roberts concurring with the liberals, was written by Roberts, citing the need for fiscal policy to be insulated from politics. Thomas dissented, calling the ruling "incorrect."
  • 6/29 · Watson v. Republican National Committee: Upheld a MS law allowing ballots postmarked by election day but received up to four days later to be counted. This decision pertains to 29 other states. The 5-4 decision, with Roberts and Barrett joining the liberals, was written by Barrett. Alito warned of "lamentable consequences" in dissent.
  • 6/29 · Chatrie v. United States: Requires a reasonable search and usually a warrant for the government to obtain cell phone location data. The 6-3 decision was written by Kagan, joined by Roberts, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and the three liberals. Alito called the opinion an "irresponsible escapade."
  • E. Jean Carroll Appeal: The Court declined to hear the appeal of the $5MM jury verdict against Trump. Because the appeal required Trump to put the money plus interest into escrow, Carroll can expect to receive it soon.
  • 6/30 · Trump v. Barbara: Struck down an EO ordering federal departments not to recognize the citizenship of children born in the U.S. to illegal immigrants or travel visa holders. The 6-3 decision was written by Roberts, joined by Barrett, the three liberals and, in part, Kavanaugh, upholding the 1898 Wong Kim Ark decision. Alito and Thomas dissents were extraordinarily lengthy (11,600 and 27,500 words respectively).
  • 6/30 · NRSC v. Federal Election Commission: Struck down limits on how much political parties can spend on candidates. The 6-3 decision was written by Kavanaugh.
  • 6/30 · West Virginia v. B.P.J. and Little v. Hecox: Held that for Title IX purposes, "sex" refers to biological sex assigned at birth and upheld state laws preventing trans girls from playing girls sports. The 6-3 decision was written by Kavanaugh.

Key Takeaways

  • The TPS and asylum decisions will expedite deportations and greatly impede legal crossings at the southern border. ICE detained 10k individuals during five days after the rulings, double the previous number.
  • The birthright citizenship decision may be a pyrrhic victory. Kavanaugh's support for revocation can easily be secured legislatively.
  • The 5-4 decision allowing late-arriving ballots to be counted is fragile and can be overridden by state legislation.
  • John Roberts has been in the majority more than any other justice this term and usually writes the majority opinion. It is indeed the Roberts Court.

Decisions Condensed & The Pundit Response

The Twelve Decisions Distilled

  • Mullin v. Doe (TPS): Opens the door to mass deportations of roughly 1.3MM people across multiple countries. The regime can now rescind immigration protections with minimal legal constraint.
  • Mullin v. Al Otro Lado (Asylum): Closes the asylum process for people at the border. Migrants cannot apply without first being physically inside the U.S.—an nearly impossible catch-22.
  • Wolford v. Lopez (Gun Rights): Expands Second Amendment rights by striking down Hawaii's law requiring consent to carry in public spaces. Property owners lose authority over guns on their premises.
  • Monsanto v. Durnell (Product Liability): Shields corporations from liability for failure to warn of cancer risks. One of the few 7-2 decisions, with unexpected dissents from Gorsuch and Jackson.
  • Trump v. Slaughter (Presidential Power): The term's most consequential ruling. The president can now fire any Senate-confirmed official at will, upending 89 years of precedent and fundamentally altering the separation of powers.
  • Trump v. Cook (Federal Reserve): A rare 5-4 centrist win. Kavanaugh and Roberts sided with the liberals to preserve Fed independence—suggesting limits on executive overreach remain, barely.
  • Watson v. Republican National Committee (Ballot Counting): Upholds late-ballot counting protections in 30 states but by a fragile 5-4 margin. State legislatures can override this at any time.
  • Chatrie v. United States (Cell Location Data): A privacy victory. Warrants now required for cell phone tracking. One of the term's few pro-civil-liberties decisions, united an unusual coalition.
  • E. Jean Carroll (Appeal Decline): Trump's $5MM defamation judgment stands. The $83MM judgment for defamation of character looms closer as he cannot avoid escrow requirements on appeal.
  • Trump v. Barbara (Birthright Citizenship): Upheld the 14th Amendment's grant of citizenship to children born in the U.S., but the 6-3 margin is deceptively fragile. Kavanaugh's position suggests legislative change could easily tip the Court.
  • NRSC v. Federal Election Commission (Party Spending): Amplifies Citizens United by removing limits on party-to-candidate spending. Dark money flows accelerate.
  • West Virginia v. B.P.J. & Little v. Hecox (Trans Sports): State laws banning trans girls from girls' sports upheld. The 6-3 decision closes the door on Title IX protections for transgender athletes.

What the Pundits Saw

  • John Fritze (CNN): The sheer volume of ideologically split 6-3 decisions—13 this term versus 6 last year—signals a Court operating as a partisan instrument rather than a deliberative body. The doubling of such decisions is not statistical noise; it reflects the institutionalization of ideological division at the highest judicial level.
  • Jonathan Adler (William & Mary Law School): A crucial distinction: this was a conservative Court, not a Trump Court. The majority did not go as far as Trump wanted (e.g., Cook, Barbara, Chatrie). This suggests the conservatives have their own agenda, independent of presidential whim, and will not be subordinated to executive will.
  • Paula Reid (CNN): Her question—"Are there any limits on his power?"—cuts to the heart of Slaughter. The answer the Court gave is effectively no. The president can now dismiss any official at any time for any reason, eliminating one of the few structural checks on executive authority.
  • Jen Rubin (columnist): The Court's abandonment of institutional neutrality is complete. By aligning itself with the "failing MAGA movement," it has severed its claim to legitimacy as a coequal branch. The Court is no longer constraining power; it is consolidating it.
  • Harry Litman (legal analyst): The term's decisions are not isolated rulings; they constitute a coordinated constitutional overhaul. The Court is methodically dismantling the administrative state and executive constraints. The "balance" narrative in the press misses the forest: this Court is engaged in radical reconstruction, and more radicalism is coming.

The 250th Anniversary Spectacle

Trump loves spectacle, showy building and design projects, and, most of all, self-glorification. Beyond the purview of the 250th anniversary celebration, here is a partial list of his generally tasteless projects: ubiquitous gold leaf throughout the Oval Office; a replacement Rose Garden with Mar-a-Lago style patio; destruction of the East Wing and laying the foundation for a huge ballroom (cost escalated from $200MM to $600MM); plans for a 250-foot high Triumphal Arch at Memorial Circle; a special 250th anniversary passport featuring Trump's portrait; renaming proposals for the Kennedy Center and U.S. Institute for Peace; and the proposed planting of 47 maple trees in Lafayette Square.

Shortly after taking office, Trump signed an EO creating a task force to plan a Freedom 250 celebration designed to compete with the bipartisan America 250 initiative established by Congress in 2016.

The Reflecting Pool Saga

The project's cost has soared from the initial $1.8MM cost estimate to $15MM. When algae overran the Pool and blue polyurea coating chips came to the surface in mid-June, Trump blamed alleged saboteurs who he said poured in chemicals and cut a 300-foot slit on its floor. What appears to have really happened is that when large nano-bubblers were removed in preparation for the UFC spectacle on Trump's birthday, algae growth was enhanced and some of the blue polyurea coating which had been improperly installed chipped off. Social media references to "Sealant Team 6" and the "Strait of Warm Ooze" captured the comedy of errors. A Washington tour guide told The Guardian that Trump "has a contrary of the Midas touch which is everything he touches turns to crap." On Thursday a grand jury indicted former three-time Olympics canoeist David Hearn for vandalism after he stopped to inspect a loose piece of coating.

Freedom 250 Events

After mostly B-list performers dropped out in May when they learned of the highly partisan nature of the events, Trump decided to headline the 6/24 and 7/04 events on the National Mall, calling the 7/04 event with 850k fireworks "the most spectacular TRUMP RALLY of them all, a TRIBUTE TO AMERICA." PBS will air its 7/04 commemoration from Colonial Williamsburg instead.

The UFC fights at the White House, attended by all of Trump's immediate family and many donors, were described by Trump as "one of the most exciting days in the History of our fabled White House." According to Reuters/IPSOS polling, only 16% of respondents found the venue to be appropriate with 46% feeling otherwise. Rex Huppke suggested branding for the event would have been "Donald Trump's Sad Little Mandatory Birthday Party."

The Great American State Fair

A fourth Freedom 250 initiative is the 16-day "Great American State Fair" spanning from the U.S. Capital to the Washington Monument. At least 11 states—9 Blue and two Purple (CT, RI, MA, VT, ME, PA, NC, IL, WA, OR, and HI)—declined to participate. Trump opened the Fair on June 24 claiming "packed to the brim with at least 45k people" although NBCNews estimated attendance at roughly 1,000. Fair attendance has continued to be very sparse. The scaled down replica of the proposed victory arch is coming apart and oozing out yellow spray foam. Phillip Kennicott wrote that the "Great American State Fair feels more like a trade show for Christian groups, tourist boards and the military industrial complex."

Election News

New York Democratic Primaries

All three candidates endorsed by Mayor Mamdani prevailed. Claire Valdez defeated Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso in NY-07; Brad Lander defeated incumbent Dan Goldman in NY-10; and 32-year-old Daralizer Avila Chevalier defeated incumbent Adriano Espaillat in NY-13. The defeated candidates were hindered by an anti-incumbent mindset, their more open support of Israel, and Mamdani's endorsement of their opponents. Chevalier led pro-Palestinian rallies at Columbia in the wake of 10/07/23, voiced support for abolishing the police, and called Joe Biden a "rapist" in 2020.

By way of contrast, Cait Conley won the Democratic primary in NY-17 and will run against Republican incumbent Mike Lawler. Conley is a West Point graduate with master's degrees from Harvard and MIT, served in the Army for 16 years, and is endorsed by the JStreet PAC.

Colorado Primary Results

The NYC pattern replicated itself in the staunchly Democratic Denver-centered CO-1 primary as 29-year-old Democratic Socialist Melat Kiros defeated 15-term incumbent and staunch progressive Diana DeGette by 13%. Although Kiros supports Medicare for All and abolishing ICE, the main difference between the two candidates is that Kiros supports a moratorium on all arms sales to Israel and calls it an ethnocracy.

In statewide races, AG Phil Weiser defeated Senator Michael Bennett in the Democratic gubernatorial primary by 14%, Senator John Hickenlooper beat Julie Gonzalez by 6%, and Secretary of State Jena Griswold won the race to replace Weiser as AG by 25%.
