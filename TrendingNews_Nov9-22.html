Marjorie Taylor Greene Resignation (November 21)
Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene announced on Friday, November 21, that she would resign from Congress effective January 5, 2026, after Trump called her a "traitor" and withdrew his endorsement. In an 11-minute video statement, Greene blamed Trump's scorn on the Epstein files issue and excoriated the Republican Party for being "cast aside by MAGA Inc and replaced by Neocons, Big Pharma, Big Tech, Military Industrial War Complex." She refused to be a "battered wife" and stated she had "too much self-respect and dignity" to endure a primary against her. Greene did not give Speaker Johnson advance notice, and her resignation will shrink the GOP House majority to just 218 seats. Rep. Thomas Massie expressed support, writing "I'm very sad for our country but so happy for my friend Marjorie."
Trump's Break with Greene
After years as one of Trump's strongest defenders, Greene became one of his biggest critics over issues including the Epstein files, H-1B visas, foreign policy, healthcare, and tariffs. Trump branded her "wacky" and said he would endorse a challenger when she ran for reelection. When asked about her resignation, Trump told reporters, "I think it's great news for the country. It's great." Greene suggested Republicans will lose the 2026 midterms and Trump will be impeached when Democrats regain control.
Continued H-1B and Israel Divisions
The H-1B visa controversy and Israel policy divisions that emerged in early November continued to fracture MAGA. The split between tech billionaires (Musk, Thiel) who support H-1B visas and the America First base who oppose them remained unresolved. Similarly, divisions over Israel policy between pro-Israel Republicans and anti-Israel America First voices persisted.
Electoral Setbacks
Greene's announcement came as the Republican Party faced defeats in November elections up and down the ballot, with Democrats winning key state and local races where affordability was a central issue. This added to the sense of fracture within Trump's coalition.
Potential 2028 Presidential Run
Greene's resignation could set the stage for her to seek to replace Trump in the White House in 2028 and possibly become the next leader of the MAGA movement. However, she conceded her resignation might end not only her congressional career but also her role in MAGA entirely.

Trump Tariffs (45 mentions)
China trade deal, agricultural rollbacks, and $2,000 rebate proposal
▼
China Trade Deal Extension (November 10)
On November 10, the U.S. and China announced an extension of their trade deal through November 2026. China suspended export controls on rare earths (gallium, germanium, antimony, graphite), resumed purchasing U.S. agricultural products (12 million metric tons of soybeans in late 2025, 25 MMT annually 2026-2028), and suspended all retaliatory tariffs. The U.S. lowered fentanyl-related tariffs by 10 percentage points and maintained the suspension of heightened reciprocal tariffs. China also removed sanctions on various shipping entities and agreed to resume trade from Nexperia's facilities for critical legacy chips.
Expanded Agricultural Tariff Rollbacks (November 14-17)
Building on earlier rollbacks, Trump expanded tariff breaks on Brazilian goods including additional agricultural products. The November 13 executive order removed reciprocal tariffs from certain qualifying agricultural products not grown in sufficient quantities in the U.S. Trump cited substantial progress in trade negotiations (9 framework deals, 2 final agreements, 2 investment agreements) as justification. The U.S. and Switzerland agreed to lower Swiss import tariffs to 15% from 39%.
$2,000 Tariff Dividend Proposal
On November 9 and throughout the period, Trump floated issuing $2,000 "tariff dividends" to middle and lower-income Americans, funded by tariff revenue. He suggested checks would come "probably in the middle of next year, a little bit later than that." Budget experts noted the plan would cost $600 billion while tariffs only bring in $200-300 billion annually. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated Congress would need to approve the plan. Trump acknowledged US consumers are "paying something" for his tariffs.
Congressional Budget Office Deficit Revision
The CBO revised its estimate of tariffs' deficit impact, projecting they would shrink deficits by $3 trillion through 2035 (down from $4 trillion estimated in August). The agency based estimates on tariffs imposed between January 6 and November 15. Primary deficits would be reduced by $2.5 trillion over 11 years, with government borrowing costs expected to fall $500 billion.
Limited Price Relief for Consumers
Economists warned that tariff rollbacks won't lower grocery prices immediately because inventories purchased at higher tariff rates are still being sold. Federal Reserve Chair Powell noted "tariff-induced inflation will take a long time to become clear." Japan's economic growth contracted 1.8% annually in Q3 2025 due to Trump's tariffs hitting exports. The Tax Policy Center estimated tariffs would impose an average burden of $2,600 per household in 2026.
Copper Investigation Report Due
The Commerce Department's Section 232 national security investigation for copper imports, initiated February 25, had findings due on November 22, 2025.

Epstein Files (42 mentions)
Historic House and Senate vote forces DOJ release, Trump signs bill
▼
Trump's Reversal (November 16-17)
On Sunday November 16, Trump reversed course and urged House Republicans to "vote to release the Epstein files, because we have nothing to hide, and it's time to move on from this Democrat Hoax." His reversal came after it became clear the vote would succeed. Trump told reporters on Monday he would sign the bill, saying "I'm all for it" but maintained it was a "hoax" and Democrats were trying to "detract" from Republican success.
Historic House Vote (November 18)
The House voted 427-1 to pass the Epstein Files Transparency Act, with only Rep. Clay Higgins (R-LA) voting against. The discharge petition succeeded after Rep. Adelita Grijalva (D-AZ) became the decisive 218th signature following the end of the government shutdown. About a dozen Epstein survivors, including Virginia Giuffre's brother, watched from the gallery. Speaker Mike Johnson voted yes despite calling it a "political exercise" with "serious deficiencies."
Unanimous Senate Passage (November 18)
Less than three hours after the House vote, the Senate unanimously agreed to pass the measure without a formal vote. Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer offered a motion to pass the legislation as soon as received from the House; no senators objected. Senate Majority Leader John Thune said the House's 427-1 vote convinced them to move quickly, concluding "the bill was sufficient to accomplish what needed to be done."
Bill Provisions
The Epstein Files Transparency Act requires the DOJ to publicly release all unclassified records, documents, communications, and investigative materials related to Epstein and Ghislaine Maxwell within 30 days of Trump's signature. The bill includes exceptions for survivors' personal information and child sexual abuse material. It covers individuals named or referenced in connection with Epstein's criminal activities, civil settlements, immunity, plea agreements, or investigatory proceedings.
Survivor Advocacy and Political Dynamics
Epstein survivors spoke outside the Capitol urging lawmakers to vote yes, with several criticizing Trump for politicizing their fight for justice. The bipartisan effort was led by Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY) and Rep. Ro Khanna (D-CA), with crucial support from Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene. Trump does not need legislation to release the files and could order their release immediately. A White House official confirmed Trump will sign the bill "whenever it gets to the White House," though major loopholes may delay actual releases.

Supreme Court Cases (15 mentions)
Tariff case aftermath and ongoing litigation tracking
▼
Post-Oral Argument Status
Following the November 5 oral arguments in Learning Resources v. Trump, the Supreme Court is expected to issue a decision by year-end. The justices from both conservative and liberal wings expressed skepticism about Trump's authority under IEEPA to impose tariffs. The case remains the most consequential pending decision affecting Trump's tariff policy, with potentially "catastrophic consequences" for the administration if ruled against, according to Solicitor General D. John Sauer.
Administration's Alternative Plans
If the Supreme Court rules against Trump's IEEPA tariffs, experts expect the administration will seek alternative methods to carry out the trade agenda using other statutory authorities. Georgetown Law Professor Kathleen Claussen noted Trump "could piece together something very close to — if not identical to — what he's done so far" using powers explicitly granted by Congress.
Ongoing Litigation Tracking
Just Security's litigation tracker shows 234 active cases challenging Trump administration actions as of November, including challenges to tariffs, immigration policies, passport sex designation requirements, and other executive actions. The tracker documents 17 Supreme Court stays or orders to vacate lower court orders and 5 criminal prosecutions by DOJ (Rep. McIver, James Comey, John Bolton, Kilmar Abrego Garcia, Letitia James).
Senate Symbolic Vote on Tariffs
In October 2025, the US Senate voted 51-47 to nullify Trump's global "reciprocal" tariffs on more than 100 countries. The measure was largely symbolic as the House blocked tariff-related measures from floor consideration under a new rule.

Hollowing Out of the DOJ (12 mentions)
Continued resignations and prosecutorial exodus
▼
Continued Fallout from Eric Adams Case
The mass resignations from February 2025 over the Eric Adams case dismissal continued to reverberate through the DOJ. The "Thursday Night Massacre" or "Valentine's Day Seven" resignations set a precedent for career prosecutors refusing politically motivated orders. The event has been compared to Nixon's Saturday Night Massacre and highlighted concerns about quid pro quo arrangements using prosecutorial power.
Immigration Judge Crisis Deepens
Immigration courts remained severely understaffed with only 600 judges handling a 3.4 million case backlog, down from 700 earlier in the year after 80+ terminations. DOJ continued training military lawyers without immigration law backgrounds to serve as temporary judges. Defendants increasingly failed to appear at court hearings due to fear of ICE detention, automatically receiving removal orders with no appeal avenue.
Civil Rights Division at 70% Attrition
The Civil Rights Division maintained its 70% departure rate through November, with no signs of stabilization. Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon's vision to "turn the train around and drive it in the opposite direction" continued, focusing attorneys on Trump priorities rather than traditional civil rights enforcement. The administration had dropped or withdrawn from approximately 30 cases.
Southern District of Florida Russia Investigation Resignations
In early November, two prosecutors in the Southern District of Florida resigned after being assigned to investigate the origins of the 2016 Trump-Russia investigation. One stated continuing would "violate their ethical responsibilities." Over 30 subpoenas were issued targeting John Brennan, Peter Strzok, and Lisa Page, creating a "pins and needles" atmosphere in the office.

Revenge Litigation by Trump Administration (8 mentions)

Comey and James Cases Proceed
The prosecutions of James Comey and Letitia James, initiated earlier in 2025, continued through November. Both defendants maintained their vindictive prosecution defenses, arguing the cases were motivated solely by Trump's desire for revenge rather than legitimate legal grounds. More than 100 former DOJ officials continued to support their arguments in legal briefs.
Broader Pattern of Retaliation
The Trump administration's pattern of targeting perceived enemies continued, with investigations into Miles Taylor, Chris Krebs, and others who criticized Trump. Law firms that hired Trump opponents or were involved in litigation against him remained barred from federal business. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth continued firing military leaders perceived as insufficiently loyal.
Intimidation Effect on DOJ/FBI Workforce
Former officials noted the ongoing chilling effect on the DOJ and FBI workforce. Career employees remained reluctant to investigate potential abuses by the Trump administration due to fear of retaliation. The firings, demotions, and prosecutions from earlier in 2025 continued to deter officials from standing in the way of Trump's agenda.
