Minneapolis ICE Shooting: National Crisis Over Law Enforcement Use of Force ▶
Fatal ICE Shooting Sparks National Debate on Federal Law Enforcement Tactics
On January 7, 2026, NBC News reported that Immigration and Customs Enforcement agent Jonathan Ross fatally shot Renee Nicole Good, 37, a U.S. citizen, during an immigration enforcement operation in Minneapolis. The shooting occurred near East 34th Street and Portland Avenue, less than a mile from where George Floyd was killed in 2020.
Key Details:
  • Victim: Renee Nicole Good, 37-year-old U.S. citizen and mother
  • Location: South Minneapolis residential neighborhood
  • Federal Claims: DHS called it "domestic terrorism," claimed Good weaponized her vehicle
  • Local Response: Mayor Frey called federal claims "BS" after viewing video evidence
  • Investigation: FBI and Minnesota BCA investigating
CNN reported conflicting narratives between federal and local officials. Department of Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem characterized the incident as an "act of domestic terrorism," claiming the ICE officer was struck by Good's vehicle. However, Minneapolis Police Chief Brian O'Hara expressed being "very concerned" with the tactics used by federal immigration agents.
"This was a federal agent recklessly using power that resulted in somebody dying."
— Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey
Minneapolis Star Tribune identified the agent as Jonathan Ross and reported that five use-of-force experts questioned the decision to shoot at a moving vehicle, with some calling it a "bad shooting." The experts noted that most police departments discourage shooting at moving vehicles because deadly force is unlikely to stop the car and could jeopardize bystanders.
NPR covered the immediate aftermath, including protests where law enforcement deployed tear gas and pepper spray on demonstrators. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries called Good's death "an abomination — a disgrace."
President Trump defended the agent's actions on Truth Social, characterizing Good as a "professional agitator" who "violently, willfully, and viciously" ran over the officer. Vice President JD Vance echoed this characterization, according to CBS News, saying critics should be "ashamed of yourselves."
Sources: NBC News, CNN, City of Minneapolis, Star Tribune, NPR, CBS News, ABC News
Justice Department Crisis: Mass Resignations Over Minneapolis Shooting ▶
Senior DOJ Officials Resign in Protest Over Handling of ICE Shooting Investigation
MS NOW broke the story on January 12 that at least six leaders of the Justice Department's Civil Rights Division resigned in protest after Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon decided not to investigate the ICE officer's fatal shooting of Renee Good.
Resignations Include:
  • Criminal Section Chief and entire senior leadership team
  • Principal Deputy Chief
  • Deputy Chief and Acting Deputy Chief
  • Six federal prosecutors in Minnesota U.S. Attorney's office
CBS News reported that the departures represent the most significant mass resignation at the Justice Department since February 2025, when five leaders resigned rather than dismiss bribery charges against then-New York Mayor Eric Adams. The Civil Rights Division has lost approximately 75% of its staff in 2025.
"There is currently no basis for a criminal civil rights investigation."
— Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche
PBS NewsHour featured an interview with Carol Leonnig discussing the broader implications. The Minnesota federal prosecutors reportedly resigned both due to disappointment with how Main Justice handled the matter and concerns about being pushed to investigate Good herself rather than the officer.
Washington Post noted that multiple senior prosecutors in both Washington and Minnesota left their jobs amid turmoil over the Trump administration's handling of the shooting death, highlighting the unprecedented nature of an entire leadership team departing simultaneously.

Venezuela Oil Strategy: Skepticism from Energy Executives ▶
Trump's Venezuela Vision Meets Industry Reality Check
On January 9, President Trump convened oil executives at the White House to discuss investing in Venezuela's oil infrastructure. CNBC reported that major oil company CEOs expressed significant reservations about rapid investment in the country.
"If we look at the legal and commercial constructs and frameworks in place today in Venezuela, today it's uninvestable."
— ExxonMobil CEO Darren Woods
CNN Politics described Trump's meeting as ending "without any major commitments from companies to invest billions of dollars." Woods bluntly told officials that Venezuela presents "an unacceptable risk," citing the country's history of seizing Exxon's assets twice since 2006.

CBS News reported that ConocoPhillips CEO Ryan Lance called for restructuring the entire Venezuelan energy system, including state-owned oil company PDVSA. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent acknowledged that "big oil companies who move slowly, who have corporate boards, are not interested."
Only Chevron, currently operating in Venezuela through joint ventures, expressed readiness to increase production. Al Jazeera quoted Chevron Vice Chairman Mark Nelson saying the company could increase liftings by 100% "essentially effective immediately" and boost production by 50% in 18-24 months.
CNN Business analysis highlighted that industry sources were "unlikely to dive headfirst into Venezuela" due to the uncertain situation on the ground, the oil industry's state of disrepair, and Caracas' history of seizing U.S. oil assets. Energy Secretary Chris Wright acknowledged that reaching the $100 billion investment target "does indeed take time."
Sources: CNBC, CNN Politics, CBS News, Al Jazeera, CNN Business, ExxonMobil
Federal Reserve Independence: Criminal Investigation of Chair Powell ▶
DOJ Launches Unprecedented Investigation Into Fed Chair
On January 11, CNBC reported that Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell disclosed he was under criminal investigation by the Department of Justice, receiving grand jury subpoenas related to his Congressional testimony about the Fed's $2.5 billion headquarters renovation project.
"The threat of criminal charges is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the President."
— Fed Chair Jerome Powell
CNN Business characterized the probe as taking "Trump's fight with the Fed to a whole new level," noting it sends a "chilling message" to Powell and whoever might next lead the Federal Reserve. Powell's term as chair ends in May 2026, though his term as a Fed governor extends until January 2028.
Investigation Context:
  • Stated Focus: Powell's June 2025 Senate testimony about Fed building renovations
  • Powell's View: Investigation is pretext to pressure Fed on interest rate cuts
  • Trump's Response: "I don't know anything about it" (NBC News interview)
  • Political Reaction: Republican Senator Thom Tillis vows to oppose all Fed nominees until resolved
Axios noted the investigation "ostensibly concerns the central bank's $2.5 billion renovation project, but functions as an unprecedented escalation of the president's effort to influence the central bank over interest rates." The Fed cut rates three times in the second half of 2025, but Trump has consistently demanded more aggressive rate reductions.
CNBC reported Trump's subsequent comments calling Powell either "incompetent" or "crooked" and saying "that jerk will be gone soon." JPMorgan Chase CEO Jamie Dimon warned that anything undermining Fed independence "is probably not a great idea."
PBS NewsHour covered the bipartisan backlash, with Republican Senator Lisa Murkowski stating after speaking with Powell that "the administration's investigation is nothing more than an attempt at coercion."
Al Jazeera documented Trump's escalating attacks on Powell throughout 2025, including calling him "a stupid person," "a very incompetent man," and claiming Powell has "no personality, no high intelligence, no nothing."
Sources: CNBC, CNN Business, Axios, Federal Reserve, NBC News, PBS NewsHour, Al Jazeera, ABC News
