CBS: Built by Murrow,
Tarnished by Weiss

The Rightward Lurch That Nobody Watched — CBS bets the Tiffany Network on a MAGA audience it cannot win; meanwhile Jimmy Kimmel is exiled, returns in triumph, and the invisible hand of the remote control renders its verdict.
Act I & Act II — Two Networks, Two Wagers, Two Very Different Bills
CBS Discovers That Genuflecting to Power Has a Nielsen Price
By Our Increasingly Former Correspondent · CBS Desk

When David Ellison completed his acquisition of Paramount in late 2025 — sweetened by a remarkable $16 billion settlement that resolved the sitting president's lawsuit over a 60 Minutes edit — and installed former Free Press provocateur Bari Weiss as editor-in-chief of CBS News, the strategic logic was at least legible, if not wise.

The theory: American media had overcorrected left. Half the country distrusted the legacy press. An entrepreneurial shakeup — a little MAGA oxygen, a touch of contrarianism — might lure Fox's slightly less feral viewers to the Tiffany Network.

"The goal is not to deliver the news so much as it is to drive the news." — Bari Weiss, in a memo to producers. The market apparently preferred the old arrangement.

What followed was a rather spectacular miscalculation. Under new anchor Tony Dokoupil, CBS Evening News opened 2026 with 4.17 million viewers — already 23% below the 5.4 million Norah O'Donnell had commanded a year prior. By mid-March, the number had slipped below 3.83 million: the lowest first-quarter audience of the 21st century. Meanwhile ABC's World News Tonight was drawing 8.48 million — more than double CBS — and NBC's Nightly News was actually up 8% in the coveted 25-54 demo.

The attempted pivot also produced what might charitably be called editorial adventures: a 60 Minutes segment about El Salvador's CECOT mega-prison was abruptly pulled; a town hall featuring Erika Kirk — widow of the recently assassinated conservative activist Charlie Kirk — drew viewership 11% below the show's typical time-slot numbers. The audience, it seems, was not pining for these particular innovations.

Kimmel: Exiled by Disney, Restored by Viewers, Vindicated by Nielsen
By Our Late-Night Triumphalist Correspondent · ABC Desk

In September 2025, ABC briefly suspended Jimmy Kimmel following his on-air comments about the political circumstances surrounding Charlie Kirk's assassination. The suspension was indefinite. The chastened host would learn his lesson.

He did not learn his lesson. He returned six days later. What happened next is the kind of television story that belongs in case studies: Kimmel's comeback episode drew 6.26 million viewers — nearly four times his usual audience and the highest-rated regularly scheduled episode of his show in more than a decade. With three-day delayed viewing, the number swelled to 8.6 million, the best late-night share in 30 years, dating to the final episodes of Johnny Carson. His YouTube monologue logged 17 million views in 24 hours.

"His audience is gone." — Donald Trump, on Jimmy Kimmel. The president would later receive a thank-you from Kimmel for the inadvertent marketing assistance.

The story did not end there. For the full year 2025, Jimmy Kimmel Live! was the only network late-night show to grow its total audience — up 14% year-over-year, with a 4% gain in the 18-49 demographic. By March 2026, he had won three consecutive weekly ratings victories in the demo. Every other major late-night competitor had declined.

Disney, for its part, responded to this triumph by cutting Kimmel's budget. One is reluctant to place too much faith in corporate wisdom at this juncture in history.

Case Study in Print — The Washington Post: A Masterclass in Subscriber Attrition
Three Acts of Capitulation,
Three Waves of Cancellation
By Our Increasingly Former Subscriber · Democracy-Dies-In-Darkness Desk

If CBS is the television chapter of this story, The Washington Post is the print edition — and its collapse has been, if anything, more methodically documented and more brutal in its arithmetic. Jeff Bezos, who purchased the paper in 2013 for $250 million with apparent democratic idealism, managed in the span of eighteen months to preside over losses exceeding what he paid for it.

The Post's capitulation arrived in three distinct acts, each with its own subscriber exodus, as if the readership were staging an orderly, if furious, departure.

Act I — The Withheld Endorsement (October 2024). Eleven days before the presidential election, Bezos decreed that the Post would not endorse Kamala Harris — breaking with decades of practice. Former editor Marty Baron called it a "betrayal of the Post's core principles." The result: approximately 250,000 digital subscribers cancelled, roughly 10% of the paper's subscriber base, overnight.

Act II — The Opinion Section Purge (February 2025). Bezos announced the opinion pages would henceforth publish only pieces supporting "personal liberties and free markets." Opinion editor David Shipley resigned immediately. Within two days, more than 75,000 additional digital subscribers had cancelled. Columnist Ruth Marcus, ending a 40-year tenure, departed after the publisher killed her column dissenting from Bezos's edict. A further 100,000 subscribers followed.

"It took decades to build the Post into a jewel of journalism. Bezos seemed to take that role seriously — until it became too costly, and perhaps too inconvenient." — Washingtonian Magazine, March 2026

Act III — The Newsroom Massacre (February 2026). The Post laid off more than 350 journalists — 44% of the newsroom. Within weeks, another 60,000 subscribers cancelled. Print subscribers, which had stood at 130,000 in 2023, fell below 100,000 for the first time in 55 years. The paper had lost $277 million over three years: $77M in 2023, $100M in 2024, $100M in 2025 — more than the entire purchase price.

The web traffic numbers are perhaps the starkest metric of all. At its January 2021 peak — the Capitol attack — the Post drew 22.5 million daily active users. By 2024, that had collapsed to 2.5–3 million daily users: less than a quarter of the peak, a 75% evaporation of digital audience. The Post quietly stopped publishing its traffic figures after a 60% decline in monthly traffic.

The management's stated ambition, presented in a 2025 PowerPoint, was to reach 200 million paying subscribers — roughly 80 times its then-current base — including, specifically, "firefighters in Cleveland." One person who saw the presentation told Washingtonian that it convinced them to resign on the spot.

The Market's Verdict, Rendered in Remote Controls
The free market of viewership has adjudicated, without sentiment or ideology, a principle that any first-year media economist might have warned against: brand loyalty is not transferable to a brand you have just publicly repudiated.

CBS viewers did not tune in to Edward R. Murrow's old network because they lacked alternatives. They tuned in because the network meant something. When the network's new management announced — by settlement, by editorial suppression, by the hiring of its new EIC — that it had concluded its old meaning was a liability, the viewers updated their priors accordingly.

Kimmel's audience, meanwhile, demonstrated the equal and opposite corollary: viewers who feel their loyalty reciprocated — that the entertainer they watch has not been traded away for good standing with the powerful — will show up in numbers that make the Nielsen system look briefly alive again.

The corporations bent the knee. The audience did not follow. This is, in retrospect, unsurprising — and in prospect, entirely instructive.
Post Scriptum — The First Time CBS Did This
Wires, Lights & Stomach Aches:
Paley Sidelines Murrow, 1954–1958
A Note from Antiquity, Rendered Painfully Relevant

It is one of the enduring ironies of American broadcast history that the network now being dismantled in the name of commercial viability was once the network that invented broadcast journalism — and then, the first time that journalism became commercially inconvenient, dismantled that too.

Edward R. Murrow and William S. Paley had been, by multiple accounts, genuine friends. Paley had recruited Murrow, supported his wartime London dispatches, and was said by biographer Joseph Persico to be one of the few people in CBS history who counted Murrow as a true personal confidant. When Murrow launched See It Now in 1951 — a documentary program of a kind television had never seen — Paley was proud of it.

Then Murrow used it to take down Senator Joseph McCarthy.

The March 9, 1954 broadcast, assembled from McCarthy's own footage and words, was devastating. McCarthy's career began its terminal slide. But within CBS, the reaction was rather cooler. The network had not promoted the broadcast in advance; executives quietly suggested Murrow had overstepped "editorial objectivity." Murrow had become, in the language of corporate management across all eras, a possible liability to the company's business opportunities.

"I don't want a constant stomach ache every time you cover a controversial subject." — William S. Paley to Edward R. Murrow, in the confrontation that ended See It Now, 1958.

The retreat was gradual. See It Now's hard edge softened in its final seasons into admiring profiles of artists — Louis Armstrong, Marian Anderson — safer waters. Murrow's program ideas went unrealized. His contract negotiations stalled. When the final confrontation came in Paley's office in 1958, Murrow asked directly whether Paley intended to destroy the program. Paley's answer, famously, was that he did not want a stomach ache. See It Now aired its last broadcast — a documentary about postwar Germany — on July 7, 1958.

Three months later, Murrow gave what became known as the "wires and lights in a box" speech before the Radio and Television News Directors Association in Chicago. It is among the most prescient addresses in the history of American media, and it cost him whatever remained of his standing at CBS:

"We are currently wealthy, fat, comfortable and complacent. We have a built-in allergy to unpleasant or disturbing information. During the daily peak viewing periods, television in the main insulates us from the realities of the world in which we live. If this state of affairs continues, we may alter an advertising slogan to read: Look now, pay later."
— Edward R. Murrow, RTNDA, Chicago, October 15, 1958

Paley felt Murrow was biting the hand that fed him. Producer Fred Friendly later said the Chicago speech did more damage to their relationship than the McCarthy broadcast itself. Murrow left CBS in January 1961, recruited by President Kennedy to run the U.S. Information Agency, and died of lung cancer in 1965 at 57.

The parallel to 2026 is not subtle. CBS is again choosing comfort over controversy — though this time the stomach ache is being avoided in the opposite ideological direction, and the commercial logic is, if anything, less defensible. Paley was protecting a profitable status quo from a journalist who threatened it. Ellison and Weiss are abandoning a profitable status quo in pursuit of an audience that does not want them and will not come.

Murrow's ghost, one imagines, finds this less than encouraging.

The CBS Trajectory — A Study in Institutional Memory
1954 Murrow takes down McCarthy. CBS does not promote the broadcast. Executives wince.
1958 Paley cancels See It Now. "No more stomach aches." Murrow's journalism career at CBS winds down.
1971 60 Minutes — Murrow's spiritual heir — begins its run as the most-watched news program in TV history.
2025 Weiss suppresses a 60 Minutes segment on El Salvador's CECOT prison for "political" reasons. History notes the symmetry.
2026 CBS Evening News posts its lowest Q1 audience of the century. The stomach ache, it turns out, was the point.
