SpudCell

A cell that was built, not born — and what it means to feed, grow, reproduce, and evolve from scratch
Reported July 1, 2026 Carl Zimmer & Marco Hernandez, NYT University of Minnesota · Kate Adamala Lab
  • Minnesota researchers synthesized "SpudCell," a cell built from scratch that feeds, grows, reproduces, and competes for food.
  • Assembled from ~100 proteins/molecules and just 36 genes — far fewer than any natural organism.
  • Cells split via a novel, non-natural division mechanism, and a mutant strain outcompeted the original over five generations.
  • Can't yet build its own ribosomes, so lineages fail after 5–10 generations without help.
  • Not patented — creators founded the nonprofit Biotic to share the recipe openly with the field.

Scientists have long dreamed of discovering the alchemy by which chemicals can be turned into life. On July 1, 2026, a team at the University of Minnesota, led by synthetic biologist Kate Adamala, announced it had taken a major step toward that vision: simple cells, built from scratch, that feed, grow, reproduce, and compete with one another for food.

Named SpudCell for its potato-like appearance, the cell was assembled from roughly a hundred proteins and molecules, 36 genes borrowed from a virus and E. coli, and membrane-forming building blocks that spontaneously enclosed the mixture into bubbles. Many of those bubbles ended up with the right ingredients to start carrying out the chemical reactions seen in real cells.

It is not, by Adamala's own account, unambiguously alive — it can't yet build its own ribosomes, the molecular factories that make proteins, and depends on scientists to supply them. But it does more than any synthetic cell before it, and rather than patent the work, Adamala and Stanford's Drew Endy have founded a nonprofit, Biotic, to build an open research community around it.

How the cell came together
1990s

Venter's team starts stripping a genome down

Craig Venter's team began studying a microbe with fewer than 1,000 genes, eventually stripping it to 525 essential genes — the "top-down" approach to minimal life.

2016

A third of the "essential" genes remain a mystery

John Glass's team, continuing Venter's work, reported it didn't know what a third of the 525 essential genes were doing. A decade later, 56 of them are still unexplained.

Recent years

Adamala tackles division from the bottom up

Rather than mimic the natural protein ring that pinches cells in two, Adamala's team found that proteins simply accumulating on a membrane created enough bending pressure to split it — a year of experiments to make work reliably.

Build phase

Broth, bubbles, and 36 genes

The team mixed roughly 100 proteins and molecules with 36 genes for basic jobs like copying DNA, then added membrane building blocks that spontaneously formed bubbles engulfing the soup.

Feeding & growth

Slurping and fusing

SpudCells drew small molecules through surface channels, and fused with food-loaded "feeder" bubbles for larger nutrients too big to pass through — growing large enough to divide within hours.

Reproduction

A special protein forces the split

A protein added to the flask latched onto the cell surface and forced it to bend inward until it split in two — with each new cell continuing to grow.

Evolution test

Mutants outcompete the originals

A mutant strain, engineered to bind food bubbles more tightly, was mixed 50-50 with the original and, over five generations, pulled ahead — evidence of Darwinian competition in a wholly synthetic system.

Milestone achieved
Ongoing limitation

The ribosome problem persists

SpudCells carry the genes for ribosomes but the parts don't come together on their own. Fed ready-made ribosomes instead, cells can keep making proteins for five to ten generations before failing as ribosomes degrade.

Deep dives
Why 36 genes, and what they still don't explain
Two roads have converged on the question of minimal life. Craig Venter's team went top-down, stripping a natural microbe's genome to 525 essential genes and still not knowing what a third of them did in a 2016 paper. SpudCell went bottom-up instead — combining lifeless molecules until a cell-like system emerged, arriving at 36 genes borrowed from a virus and E. coli for jobs like copying DNA.
"There are still significant tasks that every cell has to do that we don't know."

— John Glass, synthetic biologist, J. Craig Venter Institute

The two approaches ask different questions of the same mystery: Venter's tells you what a natural cell can't lose without breaking; Adamala's tells you what a minimal set of parts can build from nothing. Neither yet has a complete parts list.

The ribosome problem — SpudCell's central limitation
A ribosome is the molecular factory that turns genetic instructions into proteins — and it is, so far, the one major piece of cellular machinery SpudCell cannot build for itself. The cells carry the genes needed to construct ribosomes, but for reasons not yet understood, the parts don't spontaneously assemble inside the cell.
"I don't want to say it dies, but it stops working."

— Kate Adamala, on what happens as ribosomes degrade across generations

For now, Adamala's lab feeds SpudCells ready-made ribosomes — a workaround with an expiration date. Solving self-assembly is one of Biotic's top open-source priorities, alongside enabling the cells to divide indefinitely rather than degrading after five to ten generations.

Is it alive? Why scientists are hedging
SpudCell feeds, grows, reproduces, and — in the five-generation competition experiment — even shows a rudimentary capacity to evolve under selective pressure. By most functional measures, that's a strong claim to being alive. Yet Adamala remains careful about the label, and her caution reflects a real scientific gap, not false modesty: a cell that depends on borrowed ribosomes is not yet self-sustaining.
"It's a cell that was built, not born. It's constructed, but it does what cells do."

— Drew Endy, synthetic biologist, Stanford University

Roseanna Zia, a computational biologist at the University of Missouri uninvolved in the work, called the competition result "the shake-the-ground accomplishment" — less because it proves SpudCell is alive, and more because it shows synthetic cells can now be pitted against one another to evolve toward more sophisticated versions.

Biotic, open science, and the dual-use question
Rather than patent SpudCell, Adamala and Endy founded Biotic, a nonprofit Endy estimates will spend hundreds of millions of dollars over the next decade, with hundreds of scientists expected to join and easier recipes and shared ingredients planned to lower the barrier to entry.
"We can have these conversations now, as opposed to waiting for somebody else to do it, and then we're just all reacting."

— Drew Endy, on safeguarding the research openly rather than after the fact

The tradeoff is real: today's SpudCells can only survive a few generations on a special lab diet, but future, hardier versions raise the possibility of misuse — potentially even as a weapon, by Endy's own acknowledgment. Endy's bet is that an open, well-organized research community is better positioned to prevent that outcome than a closed or purely commercial one would be.
