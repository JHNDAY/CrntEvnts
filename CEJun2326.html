Topic I · Week in Review
"Everything He Touches Turns to Crap" — A Catalog of Outrages
Two Quotes That Stuck
The session opened with two lines someone in the room had read that week. A Guardian tour guide at the Reflecting Pool, surnamed LeBron, on Trump: "the contrary of the Midas touch — everything he touches turns to crap." And Giorgia Meloni, asked about a viral photo, replying that Italians don't beg — a pointed rebuke buried in a polite quote. The framing that took hold for the session: Trump is oddly deferential to adversaries and rough on allies, and this week's news, item by item, bore out a "reverse Midas" theme the room kept returning to.
An Iran Deal Signed at Versailles
The new Iran agreement was signed at Versailles — a choice of venue at least one voice in the room found tasteless ("garish," "cruddy mirrors") and historically tone-deaf, drawing comparisons to a 1973 visit there and to the fatigue of over-gilded palaces generally (the Hermitage, Hearst Castle — "one old gilded ornament after another"). On substance, the case made in the room was that the new deal closely tracks the JCPOA that Trump withdrew from in 2018, but is worse on three counts: a $300 billion fund of uncertain provenance; sanctions relief that, unlike the JCPOA's partial relief, doesn't carve out Iran's ballistic missile program, human rights record, or support for militant proxies. The verdict offered: "the deal was a catastrophe."
The Week's Smaller Outrages
Maureen Dowd's column dubbed Trump "the Creature from the Green Lagoon" over a monument-repair controversy: a no-bid contractor, paid roughly seven times market cost, now claiming to have discovered a 300-foot crack. On election integrity, the room weighed Trump's renewed claims of California fraud against a University of Virginia study finding 42% of congressional districts gerrymandered nationally (Texas and California chief among the practitioners) — with 32 districts sitting in a 5–10% Cook competitiveness band, 22 of them Republican-held, meaning a strong blue-wave year could put a meaningful bloc of seats in play. Miles Taylor, the DHS chief of staff whose memo had criticized the first Trump term, called the UFC event at the White House "America's Roman moment."
Obama's Library, the Knicks, and the Contrast on Display
The Obama Presidential Library opening drew all three living ex-presidents and their spouses — conspicuously not the fourth. Stevie Wonder and Bruce Springsteen performed, a contrast drawn against Trump's struggling efforts to book "over-the-hill" entertainers for a July 4th event, many of whom have since canceled. And at the Knicks' championship celebration — their first title in 53 years — Trump was booed nearly unanimously from behind bulletproof glass alongside the "universally loathed" owner James Dolan, then told reporters the crowd was "mostly cheers."
California's Governor Race and the 2028 Bench
In California's special election, Democrats took roughly 62% of the vote to Republicans' 38% in a close multi-way Republican field (Steve Hilton, Xavier Becerra, Tom Steyer). Pollster Sarah Longwell — an ex-Republican now in "the loyal opposition" — offered her lab-built 2028 candidate: John Ossoff. The table also weighed Josh Shapiro and a long-shot Jack Schlossberg (currently polling third — "I'd like to see him lose"), with several noting that several leading prospects are Jewish, a fact that cuts both ways given how exposed they are on Israel with parts of the base.
Bob Fishman: Ossoff Up Close
Bob Fishman reported attending two small-donor fundraisers for Ossoff in the Boston area over the past two years: "very impressive, very measured" — comparable, he said, to Massachusetts Congressman Jake Auchincloss. Ossoff is running in a red Georgia environment against a Trump-endorsed former football coach, John Collins, and money notwithstanding, Bob thinks he's well positioned. Bob also noted, half-wistfully, that he wished Auchincloss had been the one running against Ed Markey rather than sitting that race out — a contest Bob would have found more interesting, even if harder to call.
The Auchincloss Question — Centrist or Just Pragmatic?
It was reported that Auchincloss wisely declined to challenge Markey, having watched what happened to Joe Kennedy a few years earlier, and that there's a progressive groundswell building to challenge him in two years — with AIPAC and Israel showing up explicitly in that conversation. The group split mildly on the "centrist" label: one view in the room called him center or center-left; Bob countered that Auchincloss's district (Fall River, Taunton) is nothing like Brookline or Newton, so the labels apply differently depending on the lens. Hal pushed back hardest, declining to vote for Auchincloss over his record on Israel but otherwise calling him "very competent," citing his work reopening the Bosnian caucus.
Favorability, and an Aside on Nixon
Favorability numbers were cited: Obama at 57%, Trump at 34%, Biden at 30%. On Nixon, the comparison cut an unexpected way — Nixon, it was argued, "had some sense of shame," where Trump has none; Nixon delivered the China opening, the EPA, and the HMO Act, flawed but not "bereft of any sense of governance" the way Trump is. The exchange closed on a brief, good-natured soccer detour about Bosnia's World Cup qualifying match and who at the table would root for whom if Bosnia met the U.S.
Topic II · Breaking, Mid-Session
Massachusetts: Rent Control Struck Down, and a Mayor Picks Sides
The Rent Control Ballot Question, Killed for Mentioning Religion
Mid-discussion, Bob Fishman's phone delivered an alert: the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court had ruled the rent control ballot question unconstitutional, keeping it off the November ballot — news Hal had also caught in that morning's paper. The reasoning amused the table: the measure exempted religious institutions that provide housing from its rent-control provisions, and because it named "religion," the court tossed it. It was noted that Attorney General Andrea Campbell had reportedly said the drafters hadn't expected that clause to be read as a religious-establishment problem — a costly oversight that a more careful legal review might have caught. The same court session also struck down a related tax ballot question.
Boston's Mayor Wades Into State Legislative Races
Michael Berger flagged something he called unusual: Boston's mayor actively endorsing state senate and house candidates to advance a commercial-versus-residential property tax shift — raising the commercial rate, lowering the residential one. He couldn't recall a Boston mayor previously campaigning this directly for statehouse candidates to push a personal policy agenda.
Topic III · A Group Exercise
Political Typology — BOLLI Takes the Pew Poll
The Setup
George had circulated the Pew Research political typology quiz a few weeks earlier. Rather than just discuss it, a private group version was built so BOLLI members could take the poll and see, anonymously, where the room landed on the spectrum — a deliberate nudge toward the group's recurring theme: try to see issues from angles other than one's own.
The Results — Skewed, Predictably
Eight people had taken the poll so far. The BOLLI group's results skewed heavily toward "Loyal Liberals" (five of eight), with the rest spread toward "Ardent Centrist Left" types and a single outlier landing closer to "pragmatic and polite right." Bob, unable to read the small type on screen, asked for the category labels to be read aloud; the room worked through the underlying questions — bigger vs. smaller government, whether officials run for office to serve or for power — prompting a tangent on Lord Acton's "power corrupts; absolute power corrupts absolutely," with the obvious caveat about applying it to someone already fully corrupted.
Michael's Objection
Michael Berger pushed on one question's framing: it's not really about why officials run for office (everyone promises plenty), but about how many actually keep those promises once elected. The reply that came back leaned on a familiar asymmetry: it's much easier to do harm in office than to do good — gutting USAID took weeks; passing benevolent legislation takes years.
Topic IV · Israel, Iran, and the Hazards of Democracy
Suez in Reverse, Two New Death-Penalty Laws, and a Question Nobody Asks
The Suez Echo
Sally had circulated an article — by a writer unfamiliar to the room, published on the pro-Palestinian outlet Mondoweiss — drawing a parallel between the current Iran ceasefire negotiations and the 1956 Suez Crisis, with Israel and the U.S. cast in the roles of Britain and France: heavy on hubris, light on consultation with allies. It was noted that the irony cuts differently this time: Suez was a military success undone by political failure (Eisenhower forced a withdrawal); the current episode, the group suggested, may be closer to a military failure dressed up as a deal — especially given reports that negotiators were reportedly making real progress just a day or two before the war started.
Hal's Question Nobody Asks
Hal Shear named a pattern he finds telling: politicians — even Massachusetts politicians — voice "questions about Israel" but rarely pair that criticism with an affirmation that Israel has a right to exist and that its people aren't owed annihilation. He was careful to acknowledge the 1948 land taken from Arab residents had a rationale, and that millions of people now live with that history — but said he's "thunderstruck" that the basic affirmation of Israel's right to exist almost never gets said out loud alongside the criticism.
Two New Israeli Laws
The conversation turned to two recent Knesset laws. In March, a death-penalty statute targeting West Bank Palestinians accused of killing an Israeli or denying Israel's right to exist — tried in military courts with a 96% conviction rate, no unanimity requirement, executions televised within 90 days, relaxed evidentiary rules (trials without the accused present, confessions obtained under torture admissible), and applying to Palestinians only — Jewish Israelis are exempt regardless of where they live. In May, a parallel law authorizing the death penalty and public trials for anyone involved in the October 7th attacks, covering terrorism, murder, sexual violence, and genocide charges, to be heard in a Jerusalem military court — reportedly already sweeping in at least one detainee whose connection to the attack was simply having been present as a photographer. Both laws passed with near-unanimous Knesset votes; absent members, the group speculated, were likely Israeli-Arab representatives. A widely shared video showed Itamar Ben-Gvir popping champagne on the Knesset floor to celebrate.
Israel's Two New Death-Penalty Laws
March — Terrorist Death Penalty
Targets West Bank Palestinians for killing Israelis or denying Israel's right to exist. Military court, 96% conviction rate, no unanimity required, execution within 90 days, televised. Confessions under torture admissible; applies to Palestinians only.
May — October 7th Law
Covers anyone involved in the Hamas-led October 7th attacks — terrorism, murder, sexual violence, genocide charges. Jerusalem military court, separate rules from ordinary criminal trials. At least one detainee swept in reportedly present only as a photographer.
No Constitution, and a Supreme Court Under Siege
Both international and Israeli human rights groups have condemned the new laws as undemocratic. The group noted Israel, like Britain, has no written constitution — relying instead on a patchwork of Basic Laws and "reasonableness" doctrines. Hal raised the deeper worry: Israel's Supreme Court is currently the last institutional check against these laws, and if Netanyahu's coalition wins the election the Knesset's dissolution has now triggered for October, the Court itself could be gutted — "the final nail in the coffin." Sally predicted Netanyahu wins again; nobody at the table offered a confident alternative, beyond the observation that no opposing candidate currently on offer represents more than "a kinder, gentler version of Netanyahu."
Beth and Michael: the Numbers Underneath
Beth cited polling she's followed for two to three years showing roughly 80% of Israel's Jewish population (excluding Arab citizens) supporting their government's conduct in Gaza and Lebanon — though she was careful to note no poll yet exists on the new execution laws specifically, since they're too new. Michael Berger filled in the structural picture: Israel's population is approaching 10 million, roughly 20% Arab; the current government is the most right-wing nationalist since 1948; the defense minister has pushed to carpet-bomb southern Lebanon as was done in Gaza; the Knesset has now dissolved itself, with elections set for October; and Israel's economy and currency have both suffered under extended reservist call-ups. Michael's bottom line: Israel "has a right to exist — it doesn't have a right to do what it's doing at the moment," citing roughly 4,000 civilian deaths in Lebanon against Hezbollah's own cross-border missile fire, which he called disproportionate without excusing Hezbollah.
"Both Israel and the United States are suffering what I'd call the hazards of democracy. Trump was duly elected. Netanyahu was elected, many times over, by the citizens of Israel. And yet both of these politicians, with a fairly narrow base, have gotten into power — and once they're there, they pursue very undemocratic policies." — Heard around the table
A Note of Decency, Before Moving On
Two counterweights from that week's Globe were offered: a Scottish visitor's viral TikTok confessing she'd expected to find America cold and unwelcoming and instead "only encountered kindness," and a story from Kansas, of all places, where a university band turned out to play the Algerian national anthem for newly arrived immigrants. The framing offered, borrowing Lincoln: "the better angels of our nature" are still there, "just being severely challenged."
Topic V · A Month in the Making
The Hollowing of the DOJ, and a Hard Question: Can Anyone Steelman the Other Side?
The Pattern
The room reopened a topic it has circled for about a month: competent career prosecutors leaving or being forced out of the Justice Department, replaced by loyalists; a "fantastic wave" of resignations; criminal cases dropped; vindictive prosecutions becoming routine. The long-standing legal norm of "presumption of regularity" — that courts default to trusting DOJ representations — is visibly eroding, with federal judges increasingly pushing back rather than deferring.
Two Cases in the News
A New York Times piece by David French was cited, describing a judge who discovered the DOJ had submitted redacted grand jury transcripts, threw out the case, and ordered the government to pay damages to the wrongly charged. A second, related thread: 31 cases alleging improper grand-jury use tied to voter-roll purges, of which 9 have been resolved — all against the government — with a majority of those rulings coming from judges appointed by Republican presidents. A structural complication the group flagged: even when judges issue sanctions, the marshals tasked with enforcing them work for the DOJ itself, and aren't reliably carrying them out.
The Challenge: Find One Defensible Argument
A deliberately hard exercise was put to the room: looking at a list of the administration's stated justifications against the critics' rebuttals, could anyone marshal a genuine argument for the administration's side — not because they believed it, but as an exercise in intellectual honesty? The table largely came up empty. The Mayor Adams case came closest to a real comparison point, alongside a counter-example from the other direction: the slow-moving Biden classified-documents case, where the argument made was that the delay itself, not any double standard, was the real story — "if I had taken those documents, they would have arrested me immediately."
Is "Weaponization" Even a Legal Term?
The group worked through where the word "weaponization" — newly prominent after a $1.7 billion settlement — actually comes from. Consensus formed that it isn't a defined legal term at all, more a political label retrofitted onto what would otherwise be called a frivolous-lawsuit claim. The discussion turned historical: a long-standing norm, traced informally to the Roosevelt era, that the Justice Department operates with independence from direct presidential prosecutorial demands — a norm Roosevelt himself tested from the other direction when his own party declined to back his Supreme Court-packing plan.
Housekeeping · Logistics & the Ballot
Sawyer Street, Open Primaries, and an Ad From "Jenny, the Neighbor"
A Massachusetts Jungle-Primary Question
A proposed ballot question would bring California-style open primaries to Massachusetts state offices: one combined primary regardless of party, top two finishers advancing, no separate party primaries. The group worked through the mechanics and the worry that drove California's version — too many same-party candidates splitting a primary field and locking the general election into two members of one party. A side discussion floated layering ranked-choice voting on top, which most agreed plays well with mathematicians and poorly with the average voter ("it sounds like cheating" even when it isn't). The group also flagged a gubernatorial-race ad running heavy rotation, in which a woman named "Jenny" vouches for a candidate as a fellow Democrat — without disclosing she is also his neighbor.
Sawyer Street Is Coming
The best information in the room: BOLLI's move to the new Sawyer Street facility is likely after August 1st, meaning this was very possibly the group's last in-person session at Turner Street for a while. The new building will be physically nicer but, for this coming term, still single-mode — Zoom or in-person, not both at once. True hybrid capability is planned for Sawyer Street eventually, just not certain to be ready immediately. The hybrid kit the group used today belongs to Ali Kerm, who is taking it along on sabbatical — so future in-person sessions may be back to a single fixed camera unless other equipment turns up.
Sidebar · The AI Literacy SIG
36 Signed Up, and a Cautionary Tale From a Songwriting Course
Where the SIG Stands
Responding to Aaron's question about an "AI consortium" course he'd heard about, the room got an update on the AI Literacy SIG: a Zoom session followed two weeks later by an in-person one, with prompt-writing as the entry skill — "how to write a prompt that returns high-quality information." Thirty-six people have signed up so far. The plan is to have every participant try the same exercise, then send in their results to be aggregated into one HTML visualization showing the range of careers, interests, and approaches across the group.
Closing Tangent — Approval Ratings and the Hur Report
As the session ran past the hour, the conversation circled back to approval numbers — Trump in the low-to-mid 30s, a figure the group compared to Nixon's own post-resignation nadir before his rehabilitation over time. That comparison pulled in a sharper one: the criticism that "everyone around" a declining president chooses to lie rather than disclose — raised here regarding Robert Hur's February report questioning Biden's memory and the fitness concerns it documented, and Merrick Garland's decision to release it. The recording ends mid-exchange, with someone noting that "Kent took stuff, also" before the transcript cuts off.
The Thread That Ran Through Everything
Two democracies, two leaders elected on narrow margins, both governing as if democracy were a one-time hurdle rather than an ongoing constraint — that was the spine of the day. Israel's new execution laws and America's hollowed-out DOJ are different instruments aimed at the same target: institutions that exist to slow leaders down, being dismantled by leaders who won fair elections and then decided fairness was optional going forward.

The group's own exercises pushed against that drift in small ways — taking the Pew typology poll seriously enough to look at its flaws, trying (and largely failing) to steelman an administration neither side could defend, building an AI literacy curriculum on the bet that learning to ask better questions of a machine is, in its own modest way, a civic skill.

And the closing thread — the Scottish visitor surprised by American kindness, the Kansas band playing an Algerian anthem — wasn't naivety. It was the same argument made twice: that the better angels are still there, under real pressure, and that noticing them is not a substitute for the work of holding institutions to account, but a reason to keep doing it.
