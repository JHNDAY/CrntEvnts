The Core Case
What Frey Argues
Claim I · Paideia
Liberal learning has intrinsic, not instrumental, value
The Greeks called it paideia; the Germans, Bildung. It is the cultivation of the highest human capacities for their own sake — not for wages or credentials. The question "What is it for?" is itself the error. The encounter with greatness is a good, full stop.
Even a bad person who reads Plato has gained something real.
Claim II · Hierarchy
Shakespeare is better than Grisham — and saying so is not undemocratic
There is a hierarchy of goods, but the Great Books are not luxury goods. They require no $30,000 microscope. Dostoevsky is cheap. The hierarchy is real; the access restriction is a political choice, not a natural fact.
Community colleges teach Great Books. Leisure is not a class privilege.
Claim III · Community
Liberal learning requires a community, not just a curriculum
The secret at Tulsa was not the texts — Homer or Hannah Arendt. It was the shared encounter: students going back to the dorm to argue Plato's Symposium at midnight. Aristotle's triad: wisdom, virtue, friendship. Remove the third and the first two wither.
Wisdom is sought in community. It is not delivered by lecture.
Claim IV · Studiousness
"Studiositas" — cultivated attention — is the endangered capacity
The Latin root is not "hitting the books." It is training the desire to know so that it orients toward the good rather than toward TikTok for five hours. The enemy of liberal learning is not ignorance — it is distraction at scale.
The machine offers infinite content. The student must choose depth.
The Headwinds
Why It Fails Anyway
  • Hyper-specialization: faculty refuse to teach outside their research silo
  • Ideological capture from both left (canon as hegemony) and right (credential fetishism)
  • Universities as businesses: ROI drives every decision
  • State legislation (Oklahoma, Ohio, Utah) mandating "workforce alignment" review
  • The research university is not structured to reward general education
The Crucial Distinction
Wrong Case vs. Right Case

✓ The Right Case
Humanities give you "soft skills" AI can't replace — communication, empathy, collaboration
Humanities cultivate the capacity to define what we are aiming for — the question machines must never answer

Connection · What A.I. Kant Do
The Kantian Echo
Frey's "wrong case / right case" distinction maps precisely onto Kant's means/ends framework. The categorical imperative forbids treating rational beings as mere instruments. The AI workforce argument treats the humanities as an instrument — Kant would say this violates the humanity it claims to serve. What machines categorically cannot do: set ends, not means. Define what we are aiming for, not how to get there efficiently. Dowd's Turing pivot is the same move: the question is not "can machines think?" but "who decides what thinking is for?"
Kant's Formula of Humanity
"Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only." — Groundwork, 1785. Frey's argument restated: do not outsource the project of becoming a person to a machine that has no concept of persons.
Aristotle's School-as-Leisure
The Greek root of "school" (skholē) is leisure — not idleness, but the space set aside to cultivate the highest parts of ourselves. A university structured around workforce readiness has, etymologically, abolished school.

The Unanticipated Reversal
CS Down. Liberal Arts Up.
The March 2026 Current Events session surfaced a labor market inversion: AI is automating away the jobs that displaced the humanities. The very students Frey was told were impractical — philosophy, literature, history majors — are now being hired as prompt engineers, AI output evaluators, and research synthesizers. The empirical vindication is arriving on a timeline that makes Frey's experiment look prophetic.
Frey's Ambivalence

Frey explicitly resists the "AI makes humanities employable again" argument — it is, she says, the wrong case restated in new vocabulary. The right case is not that liberal arts majors will get jobs. It is that without their cultivation, no one — employed or not — will remain capable of deciding what the jobs should be for.

For Discussion
The Open Questions
Question I
Does the labor market vindication help or hurt Frey's argument?
If liberal arts graduates are now in demand, does that strengthen the case for humanities education — or does it prove that Frey was right to resist the workforce framing? Can the right answer survive being confirmed for the wrong reasons?
Question II
Is AI a clarifier or a destroyer of the humanities project?
Frey: "A.I. just makes it very clear" that outsourcing thought is outsourcing humanity. But AI also writes passable essays, summarizes Plato, and composes music. What does it mean to cultivate capacities that machines can simulate?
Question III
Who actually has the power to restore liberal learning?
Frey's program succeeded by every measure — and was dismantled anyway. Political intervention (Sasse, Rufo) risks culture-war capture. Market pressure rewards STEM. Individual teachers change individual students. Is that enough?
Frey's answer: it has to be. Begin with general education reform.
BOLLI Resonance

The BOLLI AI Literacy course has been practicing Frey's right case from the first session: not "how to use AI for productivity" but "what it means to think alongside a machine — and why the human half of that partnership requires cultivation." The Ed Murrow protocol, the immersive reading of Dorothy Parker, the Turing/Kant/Dewey arc: each is an argument, in practice, that the encounter with serious text is not optional infrastructure for AI literacy — it is the thing itself.

The Synthesis
Where Douthat Pushes Back — and Why It Matters
Douthat's Move
Work is a zone of human interaction — Cicero knew this
Douthat refuses to fully accept the work/leisure binary. Cicero argued that humane learning prepares you for civic participation. Preparing students to navigate a corporate environment, manage producers, collaborate across difference — this is not a betrayal of the liberal tradition. It is one of its oldest applications.
The humanities prepared Romans to govern. They can prepare us to work.
The Resolution
The instrumental use is tolerable; the instrumental defense is fatal
Frey concedes the point, partially. The problem is not that humanities graduates find work. The problem is that when deans and politicians justify humanities funding exclusively through workforce outcomes, they have already surrendered the argument — and will lose the funding the moment the workforce numbers change.
Defend on human grounds. Accept workforce outcomes as a bonus, not a rationale.
The Lineage
A School of Thought in Four Moments
Socrates & Plato — Athens
The examined life is the only life worth living. Paideia as formation of the soul — not for the agora, but for the person capable of the agora.
Cicero — Rome
Liberal learning as civic preparation. The orator-statesman who can speak, reason, and persuade across the full range of human affairs. Humane learning as republican infrastructure.
Kant — Königsberg
Rational autonomy as the ground of human dignity. Never treat persons as means only. The machine that defines human ends has violated the categorical imperative — and abolished the human.
Frey — Tulsa / 2026
Liberal learning as the last institutional practice of the question "what are we aiming for?" In the age of A.I., this question is no longer academic. It is the only question that remains entirely ours.
