The Collapse No One Saw Coming

For more than a decade, the career advice was almost laughably simple: study computer science, learn to code, and prosperity would follow. Universities obliged, doubling CS enrollment — from 51,696 bachelor's degrees in 2013–2014 to 112,720 by 2022–2023.[1] The machine was working exactly as designed, right up until the moment it wasn't.

In 2025, the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's data revealed something close to a statistical paradox: CS graduates were registering 6.1% unemployment — nearly double the 3.2% rate of philosophy majors, and worse than journalism graduates at 4.4%.[1] Art history majors, for so long the punchline of every STEM-vs.-humanities debate, came in at just 3.0%.

How did this happen? Two forces converged simultaneously. First, generative AI tools capable of writing, debugging, and scaffolding code arrived in force. Second, major tech companies that had over-hired aggressively in 2021–2022 began shedding payroll: over 100,000 tech workers lost jobs in 2025, following more than 150,000 layoffs in 2024.[1] New graduates walked into a saturated market competing against experienced engineers and AI systems simultaneously.

A Stanford Digital Economy Study tracking payroll records from ADP — America's largest payroll processor — documented the split with unusual precision. Employment for young and older software developers moved in lockstep until late 2022. Then the lines diverged sharply. By July 2025, employment for developers aged 22–25 had fallen nearly 20% from peak.[2] Senior developers, who carry years of contextual judgment that AI cannot easily replicate, were largely unaffected. The study suggests AI is particularly adept at replacing exactly what computer science programs teach: the coding syntax and basic algorithms of a classroom education.[3]

Why Humanities Skills Resist Automation

The puzzle of why liberal arts skills are proving surprisingly durable in an AI economy resolves once you understand what AI can and cannot do. Generative AI excels at codified, repeatable, digitally tractable tasks — writing boilerplate code, summarizing documents, generating images from text. What it lacks is the capacity for genuine contextual judgment, moral reasoning, empathy, and the navigation of ambiguous human situations.

McKinsey projects that by 2030, demand for social and emotional skills in the United States will rise by 14%, as employers seek graduates who can bring genuine human understanding to complex challenges.[4] LinkedIn's 2023 Workplace Learning Report identified management, communication, leadership, research, and analysis as the most persistently sought-after skills across all industries — the very competencies a liberal arts education is designed to cultivate.[4]

Deloitte's 2025 Global Gen Z and Millennial Survey found that younger generations, despite being digital natives, place greater value on soft skills — empathy, leadership, adaptability — than on technical credentials in an AI-driven workplace.[4] Employers are discovering that in a world where any team member can now generate code in seconds, the scarce resource is the person who can decide what to build, why it matters, and who it might harm — and communicate that persuasively to stakeholders across a table.

Perhaps most strikingly, Information Week published analysis in early 2025 arguing that liberal arts graduates may actually become the best programmers of the AI era — not because they can code, but because they can direct AI to code.[5] The future of software development, the analysis suggested, belongs to adaptable, big-picture thinkers who can guide AI tools, understand nuanced problems, and construct meaningful solutions — traits more reliably cultivated by wrestling with Tolstoy and Thucydides than by memorizing sorting algorithms.

The Prompt Engineer Is a Humanist

New job titles are arriving in force: AI Ethicist, Prompt Engineer, AI Product Strategist, Responsible AI Specialist, Conversational Designer. Each one requires a combination of technological literacy and humanistic depth. The person evaluating whether an AI-generated medical summary is safe to show a patient is not primarily doing software engineering. They are doing moral philosophy.

Emerging roles in AI ethics and cultural data analysis are increasingly attracting humanities graduates, according to educational observers.[6] The Chronicle of Higher Education's 2025 webinar on workforce preparation captured the shift in a single line: employers want to see not just what graduates know, but how they think, learn, connect, adapt, and lead.[7]

David Meerman Scott, writing in January 2025, frames this as the triumph of adaptability over specialization: in a world where entire industries are disrupted every few years, being narrowly trained for a single task is increasingly a liability, while being broadly trained to think, learn, and adapt is increasingly an asset.[8] That, in a single sentence, describes what four years of reading Montaigne and arguing about Kantian ethics is supposed to do.

As Inc. Magazine noted in July 2025, 65% of employers cite soft skills as the top gap among new graduates.[9] Where do you learn empathy, the ability to probe the deep weirdness of people, and comfort with change and difficult trade-offs? By wrestling with the long, messy, beautiful history of human art and thought — in other words, by studying the humanities.

The Mirror Argument
How This Very Document Proves the Point
What Produced This Visualization

This document was not coded by a programmer. It was produced by a person — a retired data analyst and educator with a Ph.D. in statistical physics and decades of experience in research and analysis — who directed an AI to write it. The human contributor supplied the central argument, the framing metaphor, the editorial sensibility, the thematic structure, and the judgment about which evidence to include. The "labor" of writing HTML was done entirely by Claude.

What Skills Were Actually Required

The skills that made this possible were not programming skills. They were the ability to identify a meaningful intellectual argument, to structure an inquiry, to evaluate sources critically, to communicate a complex idea with clarity and editorial purpose, and to direct an AI collaborator with sufficient precision to produce something useful. These are the exact skills a liberal arts education is designed to develop.

The Inversion in Practice

A decade ago, creating an interactive HTML visualization required either coding skills or an expensive developer. Today, it requires the ability to think clearly, write well, and communicate with an AI. The person who cannot code but who can reason, argue, and direct has gained a powerful capability. The person who can only code has lost a monopoly. This is the role reversal, illustrated in real time.

The Deeper Irony

The AI that produced this HTML — Claude — was itself built by engineers and researchers who combined deep technical knowledge with philosophy, ethics, linguistics, cognitive science, and policy thinking. The most consequential AI systems of this era were designed by teams that included humanists precisely because building beneficial AI requires exactly the judgment that humanistic education cultivates. The humanities didn't just survive the AI age. They helped create it.
