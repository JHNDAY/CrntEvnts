As voters approach the 2026 midterms and 2028 presidential election, a new exhaustive survey reveals the daunting challenges Republicans and Democrats face in uniting disparate factions within their own parties. The Pew study — conducted every five-or-so years since 1987 — reimagines how Americans cluster around ideas absent the constraint of two political parties.

"What if people didn't automatically have political parties and you just identified groups based on their political values?" — Jocelyn Kiley, Director of Research on U.S. Politics, Pew Research Center

The Tuned-Out Middle

Beyond the eight partisan groups lies a significant bloc of less politically engaged Americans whose moderate or mixed views often determine general election outcomes. The most enthusiastic supporters of each party hold the most extreme views, while this broader swath — less engaged, more moderate — tends to decide who wins.

Electoral Outlook
What This Means for 2026 & 2028

For the 2026 Midterms: Loyal Liberals and the No Apologies Right are most likely to say it really matters which party wins control of Congress. The Left-Out Left, despite low 2024 turnout, shows 59% saying the midterms "really matter" — and only 5% approve of Trump. Democrats appear well-positioned.

The Pragmatic & Polite Right — who voted Trump +14 in 2024 — now give him only 36% approval. Their disenchantment is a significant variable in competitive districts.

For the 2028 Presidential Race: Leftward Progressives make up only 14% of the Democratic Party but have driven much of its agenda. A winning nominee will likely need support from the more moderate Order & Opportunity Left and Left-Out Left — groups with different priorities.

On the Republican side, Faith First Conservatives and the No Apologies Right together make up more than 4 in 10 Republicans — potentially enough to choose the next nominee. But their preferences diverge from the broader electorate: both groups say Trump has been the best president in 40 years, while the Unconventional and Pragmatic Right favor Ronald Reagan.
