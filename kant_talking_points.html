What A.I. Kant Do
Points to bring to the table — not conclusions to hand out
Read the column · NYT Opinion · May 16, 2026
Dowd's Claim

After decades of STEM supremacy, tech leaders are now insisting that empathy, critical thinking, and humanistic understanding are the skills AI cannot replicate — the very qualities that will define human value in an AI-saturated world. But is this a genuine reckoning, or guilt management?

The Distinction at the Heart of the Piece
What A.I. Can Do — Comprehend
  • Code, compute, synthesize at speed
  • Answer black-or-white questions reliably
  • Mirror back information on demand
  • Excel at every STEM domain simultaneously
  • Generate "cool reason" — logical, consistent, fast
  • Replace jobs that require pattern-matching at scale
vs.
What A.I. Kant Do — Apprehend
  • Feel the weight of Kant's Sublime or Camus's absurdity
  • Navigate mystery, obscurity, and the unanswerable
  • Generate empathy from lived experience
  • Sit with cognitive strain without reaching for an answer
  • Make meaning from suffering, loss, and ambiguity
  • Do stand-up comedy — "the last job AI will get"
Source: Drew Lichtenberg, Shakespeare Theatre Company — "The cool reason of A.I. comprehends, but the seething imagination of art apprehends."
Questions to Bring to the Table
On the Reversal
For decades we were told: learn to code or fall behind. Now we're told the English major wins. Should we believe the same people who got it wrong the first time?
CS enrollment at Stanford has declined for the first time in 20 years. Is that a signal — or a blip?
On Cognitive Fitness
Cal Newport warns that people who stop reading hard books and writing with effort are "frying their brains." Do you feel that pull in your own daily life?
"The people who are reading hard books… are comfortable with cognitive strain. These are the people with real value."
On the Skeptic's Challenge
Leon Wieseltier says tech taught us that all knowledge can be reduced to information — "press a button, you got your answer." Has it changed how you think?
Humanities depts are still shrinking. Reading scores still falling. Does the tech world's sudden enthusiasm for the humanities feel real?
On Meaning Without Work
Rob Reich imagines a post-AI world where humans devote themselves to "artisanal, meaning-seeking projects." Does that sound like paradise — or purposelessness?
We went from letters to phone calls to texts to emojis. What's next — and who decides what counts as meaningful?
On Curiosity
Mark Cuban says curiosity is "the greatest skill you can have in an A.I. universe." But AI is exceptionally good at satisfying curiosity instantly. Does that make us more or less curious?
Daniela Amodei (Anthropic): hiring for people who are "compassionate and curious." What does curiosity look like when answers are always one prompt away?
On the Humanities
Students are turning to Kant, Nietzsche, and Camus — "hungry for difficult texts with no clear answers." Is that what you'd expect from a generation raised on Google and TikTok?
Dowd's student source found them "particularly into" the Analytic of the Sublime and existential nausea. The harder the text, the more the draw.

The Sharpest Cut
"The whole humanistic mentality of mystery, obscurity, patience, beauty — it's the opposite of what this technology has inculcated." And: "Press a button, you got your answer. So the whole idea that knowledge requires struggle, time, and discomfort — that's what AI has erased from the culture." Wieseltier's charge is not that AI is dangerous. It's that it's already won, and the tech world's sudden embrace of the humanities is an alibi.
Dowd's Own Doubts — Worth Sitting With

Departments still shrinkingLiberal arts and humanities programs continue to close, graduate enrollments are being slashed, and reading scores keep falling. The supposed renaissance isn't showing up in the data yet.

"The End of the English Major"The New Yorker declared it three years ago. A Texas study ranked liberal arts at the bottom of programs that pay off. The market hasn't gotten the memo.

Wishful thinking?Dowd asks directly: are the lords of the cloud engaging in wishful thinking, or guilt-ridden self-absolution — knowing society will "turn against them in big ways"?

Letters → calls → texts → emojisEach transition narrowed the bandwidth. The humanities revival, if real, runs against the grain of every cultural trend of the past 30 years.

Postscript — The Better Question
The Original Question — Refused
"The original question, 'Can machines think?' I believe to be too meaningless to deserve discussion."
— Alan Turing · "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" · 1950
The Better Question — 75 Years On

Can machines assist people in thinking — more deeply, more curiously, and more humanely than they could alone?

Turing sidestepped the unanswerable — "Can it think?" — and asked instead what machines could do in the presence of a human interlocutor. Dowd's column is haunted by the same move: the question is not whether AI can apprehend Camus or feel the Sublime, but whether it can help us do so with more rigor, more wonder, and more patience than we'd manage on our own. That is the wager the BOLLI AI Literacy Course is placing — every session.
