Elections

Democratic strategy for 2026/2028, gerrymandering effects, polling shows Democrats ahead by 2.5-5%

In addition to campaigning on crime/immigration issues, the Republicans surely will also attack wokeness, DEI and other issues which play to their base. The Democrats must decide how to best respond to the Trump administration's malfeasance and rebut their attack lines without overdoing their criticism or getting too defensive. To this end, former Clinton adviser Doug Sosnik suggested a three-pronged approach for winning in 2026 and 2028:

  1. (Develop) a narrative of defining Trump and the Republicans for what they're doing or not doing to help the people in this country;
  2. Articulate a coherent narrative about the future and appeal to the middle class and particularly people in this country, the 70% who don't have a four-year college degree;
  3. (T)he long-term solution for our party is going to sink-or-swim based on who we nominate for president and whether or not they win".

Getting down to mechanics, in looking back at the 2020 and 2024 elections, Ron Brownstein characterized the new swing voters to be the 60 MM people who voted in either 2020 (158 MM total presidential votes) or 2024 (155 MM total presidential votes), but not both times. Even allowing for newly eligible voters in 2024 who couldn't vote in 2020, 60 MM is still a formidable number. In this regard and echoing Doug Sosnik, Andrew Tanenbaum wrote "Trump won in 2024 by making big gains among low-propensity, low-information, working-class voters".

A complicating factor in the House elections is the Republican gerrymander in TX, which has been finalized, and potential Republican gerrymanders in IN, MO (Where the governor has convened a special session), OH and possibly FL (which may have already reached its gerrymander saturation point). Although the TX gerrymander should net 3-5 seats for the Republicans, it could be negated by the CA gerrymander, which needs approval as a ballot initiative and leads by 16% and 22% in the two early polls. Nate Cohn has estimated that if all of the above five gerrymanders go through without any further offsetting Democratic gerrymanders, the Democrats would have to win the national 2026 House vote by 2.3% or more to regain control. This is certainly achievable as the Real Clear Politics polling average, which may have a small Republican bias, currently has the Democrats ahead by 2.5% and CNBC has them ahead by 5%.

Looking forward to the 2030 census, it has been estimated that the Republicans could net seven additional seats due to population shifts. This may be overstated because of transplants from other states who may retain their party preference as has notably been the case in GA as it is becoming a purple state.

Although it is little talked about, there already is a national gerrymander in the Senate because the Republicans have more low population states, each of which gets two Electoral Votes for its senators as do the more populous states.
