Federal judges across the country are accusing Justice Department lawyers of a lack of candor — and questioning a trust that has steadied the courts since Watergate.

The Issue
A growing number of federal judges are now publicly accusing Justice Department lawyers of misleading the court — eroding the long-standing assumption that the government's word can be taken on faith.
The Core Question
When judges can no longer assume the government is telling the truth, what slows down — and who pays for it?

Context, Not Authority
The Two Sides
What the Judges Say

Department lawyers are breaking the duty of candor — withholding facts, redacting records, and making claims at odds with their own emails. The trust that lets courts move quickly is being spent down.

What the Department Says

Attacks on its attorneys' integrity are "outrageous and unjustified"; it will keep defending the President's agenda with respect for the rule of law. Officials have, in turn, branded the critics "rogue" and "activist" judges.

Why It Matters
The Framework
The Presumption of Regularity

Since Watergate, courts have assumed government lawyers are honest — a shortcut, codified in the Justice Manual, that lets the federal system run swiftly because the executive branch's word is trusted. Withdraw it, and every routine case slows.
Judge Patricia Wald's standard — the FIVE C's:
competence · credibility · civility · consistency · candor

Drill-Down — Available, Not Required

Is this misconduct, pressure, or direction from Washington? A Harvard law professor points to a depleted department: a public hiring push, lawyers borrowed from Homeland Security and the military, fewer and less-qualified applicants, and crushing immigration caseloads. His warning — the longer it runs, the fewer good lawyers stay, and even routine work gets harder once judges stop trusting you.

For the Group
Three Thresholds
  • Is this a story of a few dishonest lawyers, an institution under strain, or political direction from the top — and does the distinction change the remedy?
  • When the rebukes come from judges appointed by both parties, how much weight should we give the "activist judges" framing — on either side?
  • The presumption of regularity is fast but unverified trust. If courts must now check the government's word, what is the cost — and is it worth paying?
