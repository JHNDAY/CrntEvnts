Thirteen Reasons the Iran War Was a Mistake

Richard Haass, former president of the Council on Foreign Relations and director of policy planning at the State Department under President George W. Bush, offers a systematic dissection of why the February 28 strikes on Iran represent a strategic error — one driven by political ambition rather than military necessity.

Points 1–2
War Classification & Legal Status
01
War of Choice
The United States had other policy options available. Diplomacy appeared to have promise in preventing Iran from developing nuclear weapons, and increased economic pressure had the potential to precipitate regime change over time. This was not a forced response — it was a decision.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference Reporting confirms U.S. intelligence assessed Iran posed no direct threat to the American mainland for at least a decade — yet the White House proceeded anyway, driven by lobbying from Israel's Netanyahu and Saudi Crown Prince MBS.
02
Preventive, Not Preemptive
Iran posed no imminent threat to vital U.S. interests. It was not on the verge of becoming a nuclear weapons state, nor was it about to use existing weapons against the U.S. This distinction matters enormously: preemptive war is legally defensible; preventive war is not. A world where nations freely conduct preventive strikes would be a world of endless conflict.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference The intelligence callout in WaPo's "Intelligence Disconnect" section confirms this directly: U.S. agencies assessed Iran was "unlikely to pose a direct threat to the U.S. mainland within the next decade" — yet the decision to strike proceeded.
"A world in which countries believe they have the right to undertake preventive strikes against those they judge to be threats would be a world of frequent conflict. That is why such actions have no standing under international law." — Richard Haass, Point 2
Points 3–5
The Regime Change Objective: Military vs. Political
03
Political Goal ≠ Military Outcome
Regime change is political, not military. Force can destroy and kill, but it cannot on its own install a new government. A viable alternative and enabling conditions are required. Gaza and Hamas show that regimes absorb enormous punishment and survive. Every day the Iranian regime stands will be framed as its own victory.
04
Ouster ≠ Successful Change
Even if the clerical regime fell, security forces are best positioned to fill the vacuum — not a democratic alternative. Those forces would likely continue Iran's existing foreign policy objectives that the U.S. finds objectionable. Removing a leader is not the same as transforming a state.
05
Decapitation Unlikely to Decide
Targeting leadership ("decapitation") may have occurred with Khamenei's death, but is unlikely to prove decisive. The regime has institutionalized itself over nearly 50 years, and had weeks of warning to improve succession planning as military tension rose.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference WaPo confirms Khamenei was killed in a strike timed to a leadership meeting — yet Iran's Assembly of Experts immediately moved to name a successor, validating Haass's institutionalization argument.
Points 6–8
Preparation, Precedent & the Libya Parallel
06
No Opposition in Waiting
The Trump administration called for regime change without preparing the conditions for an alternative to succeed. The Iranian political opposition is not united, not functioning as a government-in-waiting, and not positioned to accept defections or provide security in a power vacuum.
07
History Requires Boots on the Ground
Successful regime change historically requires a physical, on-the-ground presence. Germany, Japan, Panama — and failures in Iraq and Afghanistan — all bear this out. An occupation of Iran is inconceivable given the country's size and capacity for resistance.
Historical Parallels Cited by Haass
🇩🇪🇯🇵
Germany & Japan (1945)
Successful regime change required full occupation, years of presence, and massive resources. The conditions in Iran bear no resemblance.
🇱🇾
Libya (2011)
Western air power ousted Gaddafi but no follow-through ensued — leaving the country in chaos. Haass calls this the closest recent parallel to Iran.
🇮🇶
Iraq (2003)
Another preventive war of choice in the Middle East. Cost the U.S. enormously. Haass directly parallels the 2003 decision to the current Iran strikes.
↗ WaPo note WaPo's reporting confirms no Congressional authorization was sought — echoing the same constitutional controversies that surrounded Iraq in 2003.
08
Maximum Goal, Minimum Means
The administration chose the most ambitious foreign policy objective — regime change — while deploying limited means. It rejected a narrower goal (degrading nuclear and missile capabilities alone) in favor of the maximalist aim. The Libya parallel looms large: air power achieved ouster but not order.
Points 9–11
Strategic Contradictions Within the Trump Doctrine
09
Contradicts Trump's Own Doctrine
This is an administration that has shown no interest in regime change or democracy promotion elsewhere, nor an appetite for costly foreign ventures — precisely what Trump promised to end. Why Iran, why now? No clear evidence the regime was on the edge of collapse.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference WaPo's reporting reveals the driver: private lobbying by Netanyahu and MBS — not a new Iranian provocation. The "why now" may be explained by geopolitical pressure rather than strategic necessity.
10
The Armada Trap
Assembling a massive military presence — Trump's "armada" — after verbal threats failed created its own pressure to act, since forces couldn't be held at high readiness indefinitely. The means of policy drove the ends: the decision to attack. This is the reverse of how policy should be determined.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference WaPo's timeline confirms weeks of military buildup preceded the strike. The armada's presence was a known precursor — validating Haass's point that military deployment created its own momentum toward action.
11
Wrong Theater for U.S. Priorities
The U.S. has opted yet again for a massive commitment in the Middle East — at odds with the administration's own National Security Strategy and with the reality that the greatest challenges to U.S. interests lie in Europe and the Indo-Pacific. Putin and Xi are both, likely, pleased.
"The means of policy played a large role in determining the ends of policy, namely, the decision to attack. This is obviously the reverse of how policy should be determined." — Richard Haass, Point 10
Points 12–13
Consequences & Overconfidence
12
An Unprepared Public & Base
The American people were not prepared for this war. Trump did not use his State of the Union address to make the case for striking Iran. A prolonged war will unsettle markets, spike energy prices, and potentially trigger attacks on American installations and personnel worldwide.
↗ WaPo Cross-Reference WaPo's "Global Disruption" consequence card confirms immediate effects: Dubai International Airport — the world's busiest international hub — closed following Iranian retaliation, and markets began bracing for energy shock amid Strait of Hormuz concerns.
13
Overconfidence from Prior Cheap Wins
Last year's bombing of Iranian nuclear sites and the Venezuela intervention may have convinced Trump that ambitious ends are achievable with limited means at low cost. History warns otherwise. Regime change is easier called for than carried out — and while only one side begins a war, it takes two to end it. Iran now votes on how big and how long this becomes.
